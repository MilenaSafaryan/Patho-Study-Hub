Key Terms & Definitions

Start here
Micturition
Voiding — coordinated reflex + voluntary bladder emptying, governed by the pontine micturition center.
Detrusor muscle
The bladder's smooth muscle wall; parasympathetically driven contraction empties the bladder.
Urgency urinary incontinence (UUI)
Involuntary leakage with/after a sudden urge — from detrusor overactivity.
Stress urinary incontinence (SUI)
Leakage with ↑ intraabdominal pressure (cough, lift, laugh) without a bladder contraction.
Overflow urinary incontinence (OUI)
Continuous dribbling from an overfull bladder — outlet obstruction or detrusor underactivity.
Enuresis
Involuntary urination during sleep in a child ≥5 years old.
Neurogenic bladder
LUT dysfunction from disrupted nervous system control of micturition.
Detrusor sphincter dyssynergia (DSD)
Detrusor contraction occurring simultaneously with involuntary sphincter contraction.
Vesicoureteral reflux (VUR)
Retrograde urine flow from bladder into the upper urinary tract, from an incompetent ureterovesical junction.
Ureteropelvic junction obstruction (UPJO)
Blockage where the ureter meets the renal pelvis — the most common cause of neonatal hydronephrosis.
Ureterocele
Congenital cystic dilation of the distal ureter.
Urothelial carcinoma
~90% of bladder cancers; arises from the transitional epithelium lining the urinary tract.
Cystitis
Bladder inflammation, most commonly bacterial (lower UTI).
Ureteral colic
Sharp, spasmodic, waxing/waning pain from a stone lodged in the ureter.
Anatomy & Micturition Physiology

Three-tier neural control

Pontine micturition center: coordinates internal sphincter relaxation + bladder contraction. Cerebral cortex: voluntarily INHIBITS the process via conscious external sphincter control. Parasympathetic (pelvic nerve, S2-S4): senses bladder stretch, drives reflex detrusor contraction. Somatic (pudendal nerve): voluntary external sphincter control. Sympathetic fibers mainly handle bladder blood flow/pain, NOT the core voiding mechanism.

Internal vs. external sphincter

Internal: proximal urethra, detrusor muscle fiber convergence — involuntary. External: distal urethra, skeletal muscle from the pelvic floor — voluntary, maintains continence at normal resting pressure.

The voiding sequence

Bladder filling → stretch receptors fire → afferent signal to spinal cord → reflex parasympathetic return → detrusor contraction ("micturition contraction"). Voluntary control (~350-400 mL) inhibits this via CNS-driven external sphincter contraction until the appropriate time; voiding then requires voluntary abdominal muscle contraction + simultaneous external sphincter relaxation.

Normal capacity & aging changes

Normal adult capacity: 300-500 mL; urge develops at 150-250 mL; typical voiding interval every 4-5 hours; normal residual <50-100 mL. With aging: capacity ↓ to 200-350 mL, voiding every 3-4 hours, nocturia becomes common.

Types of Incontinence

Scale

~25 million affected in the US; 61.8% of AFAB adults vs. 19.3% of AMAB adults. NOT a normal part of aging — though age-related bladder changes (↓ capacity/contractility, ↑ overactivity/residual volume, ↓ estrogen effects on detrusor/urethral muscle) contribute.

Requirements for continence

Physiologic (functional nervous system + intact bladder/urethral function) AND cognitive (ability to recognize/react to urge + motivation) — disruption of EITHER can cause incontinence.

ISD vs. urethral hypermobility — both cause SUI, different mechanisms: ISD is a loss of intrinsic urethral muscle/mucosal tone (from neuromuscular damage, often post-surgical) that normally keeps the urethra closed. Urethral hypermobility is a support problem — the pelvic floor/connective tissue can't hold the urethra and bladder neck in position, so they can't fully close under increased pressure. UPP testing specifically distinguishes between these two.
Incontinence Diagnosis & Treatment

Diagnostic workup

History (urinary symptoms, UTI symptoms, bowel changes, prolapse/prostate symptoms, fluid intake, meds) + physical exam (pelvic floor, palpable bladder, masses, vaginal atrophy, prolapse, prostate) + voiding diary. Urinalysis for ALL patients; urine culture only if hematuria/pyuria/bacteriuria present; PSA if prostate enlargement suspected; bladder stress test for suspected SUI. Routine imaging NOT indicated for uncomplicated UI.

Treatment escalation ladder

1) Modify contributing factors (treat underlying conditions, adjust meds, environmental access) + lifestyle changes (weight loss, smoking cessation, reduce caffeine/alcohol/carbonation). 2) Behavioral: pelvic floor muscle training (Kegels, PT, biofeedback) for SUI/UUI; bladder training (scheduled voiding, urgency suppression). 3) Pharmacologic: antimuscarinics, intravaginal estrogen, α-blockers/5α-reductase inhibitors, mirabegron (β3-agonist) for UUI. 4) Surgical: bulking agents, slings, artificial sphincter, urethropexy.

Supportive management (catheters, pads) is a last resort, not first-line — each option creates its own complication risk (catheters: UTI + calculi from urinary stasis; pads: skin breakdown, dermatitis, infection). Catheterization purely for caregiver convenience is explicitly inappropriate. The core message for patients: leakage is NOT a normal part of aging.
Enuresis

Definitions

Monosymptomatic: nocturnal incontinence only, no other LUTS. Primary: never achieved consistent nighttime dryness. Secondary: develops after ≥6 months of dryness. Nonmonosymptomatic: nighttime enuresis PLUS daytime LUTS (urgency, frequency, hesitancy, holding maneuvers) — reflects broader bladder dysfunction.

Prevalence & natural history

~15% at age 5, 10% at age 7, 5% at age 10 — resolves spontaneously at ~15%/year as children get older.

Five contributing mechanisms

Maturational delay, genetic predisposition (family history common), nocturnal polyuria (↑ evening fluid, ↓ ADH response/production), detrusor overactivity (circadian rhythm defect in detrusor inhibition), small functional bladder capacity, and disturbed sleep arousal (bladder-brain connection dysfunction in the pontine tegmentum — why the child doesn't wake up).

Associated conditions

Constipation, fecal incontinence, ADHD, autism spectrum disorder, neurodevelopmental delay, disturbed sleep — worth screening for when enuresis is the presenting complaint.

Neurogenic Bladder

Three types

Detrusor overactivity: involuntary contractions during filling. Detrusor underactivity: weak/short contractions → incomplete emptying. DSD: detrusor contracts WHILE the sphincter also involuntarily contracts — a genuine coordination failure, not just weak or overactive muscle alone.

Etiology-specific patterns worth knowing

MS: demyelination → EITHER overactivity (storage symptoms) OR areflexia (voiding symptoms), depending on lesion location. Parkinson: BOTH storage and voiding symptoms. Spinal cord injury: lesion PROXIMAL to sacral cord → overactivity/spasticity; lesion AT sacral cord/cauda equina → areflexia. Diabetes: hyperglycemia-driven oxidative stress alters detrusor structure/function directly.

Management splits cleanly by symptom type: storage dysfunction → antimuscarinics, β3-agonists, desmopressin, or (second-line) onabotulinumtoxinA/neuromodulation. Voiding dysfunction (incomplete emptying) → intermittent catheterization is the mainstay, not drugs.
Vesicoureteral Reflux

Mechanism

Normal: ureters enter at an oblique angle, travel 1-2 cm under the bladder mucosa (intravesical tunnel) before opening — bladder filling pressure compresses this tunnel shut. VUR: SHORTENED tunnel → lateral displacement + valve incompetence → retrograde urine flow, sometimes reaching the kidney.

Epidemiology

~1% of children; 16% of infants with hydronephrosis; 30-45% of children with febrile UTIs. Genetic predisposition: 27% risk in siblings, 36% in children of an affected parent. Most common in children <2 (majority resolve spontaneously with growth).

The real danger of VUR is what it enables: recurrent UTI/pyelonephritis → renal scarring → hypertension → potentially ESKD. VUR itself often resolves; the downstream infection-driven kidney damage is the actual long-term threat, which is why prompt UTI treatment matters as much as the reflux grade itself.
Grading (I-V) by VCUG determines management: grade I-II + toilet-trained → watchful waiting + prompt UTI treatment. Grade III-IV or not toilet-trained → daily antibiotic prophylaxis until spontaneous resolution or surgical correction. Breakthrough UTIs on prophylaxis → surgical candidacy.
Ureteropelvic Junction Obstruction

Scale & laterality

~1 in 1000 children — the MOST common cause of neonatal hydronephrosis. Left-sided in ~2/3 of cases. Mostly congenital, though acquired UPJO occurs.

Intrinsic vs. extrinsic causes

Intrinsic (most common): abnormal smooth muscle/connective tissue development causing narrowing/kinking at the UPJ. Extrinsic (most common cause overall for this category): an aberrant/accessory renal artery crossing and intermittently compressing the UPJ.

Downstream mechanism: inefficient drainage → ↑ ureteric/pelvic pressure → progressive pelvis dilation → efferent arteriole vasoconstriction → ↓ renal blood flow → ↓ GFR → tubular dilation, glomerulosclerosis, inflammation, fibrosis. Treatment for symptomatic/declining-function cases: pyeloplasty (resect the narrowed segment, reattach ureter to renal pelvis).
Ureteral Ectopy & Ureterocele

Ureteral ectopy

Ureter opens somewhere outside the normal bladder trigone location — from delayed/failed separation of the ureteral bud from the mesonephric duct. Commonly linked to duplex collecting systems. ~1 in 2000 children. Distinctive presentation: if the ectopic opening is distal to/bypasses the external sphincter, the patient has continuous incontinence and is "never dry" despite otherwise NORMAL bladder function and complete emptying — a genuinely unusual incontinence pattern worth recognizing.

Ureterocele

Congenital cystic dilation of the distal ureter — intravesical/orthotopic (entirely within bladder) vs. extravesical/ectopic (extends into urethra). ~1 in 500 people; duplex-system and ectopic types are most common. Ectopic ureteroceles can be large enough to prolapse through the urethral meatus. Most common presentation: UTI signs/symptoms.

Both are often found alongside OTHER genitourinary anomalies (duplicate ureters, duplex kidneys) and sometimes broader congenital syndromes (tracheoesophageal fistula, cardiac defects, anorectal malformations) — a reminder to look for associated anomalies once one congenital urinary finding is identified.
Bladder Cancer

Scale

6th most common US cancer (~82,300 new cases/2023); average diagnosis age 73; 5-year survival 77.9% overall. Urothelial carcinoma = ~90% of cases (papillary vs. flat subtypes). Rarer types: squamous cell (1-2%, invasive), adenocarcinoma (~1%, invasive), small cell (<1%, neuroendocrine), sarcoma (very rare).

Smoking dominates risk

Smoking causes ~HALF of all bladder cancers; smokers are 3x more likely to develop it. Mechanism: inhaled carcinogens enter bloodstream → filtered/concentrated in urinary tract → prolonged urothelial contact. Occupational exposure (painters, machinists, printers, hairdressers, truck drivers) via aromatic amines/hydrocarbons is the other major environmental category.

Two distinct molecular pathways

Non-muscle-invasive: hyperplastic flat/papillary lesions from FGFR3, HRAS, chromosome 9 deletion mutations. Muscle-invasive: tumor suppressor deactivation (TP53, RB1, PTEN) → flat dysplasia → carcinoma in situ. These are genuinely different molecular routes, not just different severity levels of the same process.

Presentation & the diagnostic trap

Painless hematuria — intermittent, gross or microscopic — is the classic presenting symptom. Irritative symptoms (dysuria, frequency, urgency) especially with CIS; obstructive symptoms if tumor is near the bladder neck/urethra. Diagnosis is often DELAYED because symptoms overlap with UTI, cystitis, prostatitis, and stone passage — unexplained hematuria in an adult should be treated as urothelial cancer until proven otherwise.

Survival drops sharply with stage: Stage 0 (noninvasive) 97% 5-year survival → Stage II (muscle-invasive) 71% → Stage IV (metastatic) 8%. Non-muscle-invasive disease has LOW progression risk but HIGH recurrence risk — meaning lifelong surveillance is required even after apparently successful treatment of early-stage disease.
Urethritis & Cystitis

Urethritis

Most common organisms: N. gonorrhoeae, C. trachomatis, M. genitalium. Up to 40% ASYMPTOMATIC. Diagnosis: discharge on exam, urethral swab Gram stain, or urinalysis leukocyte esterase/WBCs + NAAT for pathogen ID. Sexual contacts must be evaluated/treated; repeat NAAT at 3 months given high reinfection rates.

Cystitis — why it's so much more common in females

Shorter urethra, shorter anus-to-urethra distance, easier periurethral bacterial attachment. Males are protected by longer urethral length, drier periurethral environment, and antibacterial prostatic secretions. E. coli causes 75-95% of uncomplicated cases.

Uncomplicated vs. complicated vs. recurrent UTI

Uncomplicated: bladder-confined, immunocompetent, nonpregnant, no anatomic abnormality. Complicated: anatomic/functional/metabolic condition raising treatment-failure risk. Recurrent: ≥2 infections in 6 months or ≥3 in 1 year.

Diagnosis & first-line treatment

Pyuria present in almost ALL acute cystitis cases — its ABSENCE strongly suggests an alternative diagnosis. Nitrites indicate significant bacteriuria but a negative result doesn't exclude it. First-line: nitrofurantoin 100mg BID x5 days, TMP-SMX 160/800mg BID x3 days, or fosfomycin 3g single dose. Avoid antibiotics with >20% local resistance rates.

Recurrence prevention: ↑ fluid intake to 2-3 L/day, avoid spermicides, void after intercourse, wipe front-to-back once, shower over bath, fragrance-free soap, vaginal estrogen for postmenopausal patients. These are genuinely evidence-informed behavioral interventions, not just generic advice.
Lower Urinary Tract Calculi

Ureteral calculi

≤5mm: ~68% spontaneous passage probability. UPJ and ureterovesical junction are the classic lodging points for larger stones. Ureteral colic: sharp, spasmodic, waves of 20-60 minutes — upper ureter obstruction refers to flank/umbilical pain; distal ureter obstruction refers to groin/testicle/labium.

Size-based management (mirrors Chapter 27's kidney stone logic)

≤5mm, no infection/AKI: conservative — observation, hydration, NSAIDs (specifically chosen because they REDUCE ureteral smooth muscle tone, directly treating the spasm mechanism). 5-10mm: medical expulsive therapy — α-blockers, calcium channel blockers, antispasmodics (relax ureteral wall, reduce peristalsis frequency). >10mm: surgical (shockwave lithotripsy, ureteroscopy).

Bladder (vesical) calculi — three origins

Migrant: formed upstream, migrated down. Primary: form IN the bladder without underlying pathology — metabolic (low urinary phosphate, high calcium oxalate/uric acid/ammonia excretion) + nutritional (vitamin/mineral deficiency, low protein) factors. Secondary: from obstruction/stasis (BPH, neurogenic bladder, catheters, bladder inflammation).

Composition & treatment nuance

Adults: mostly uric acid (contrast with kidney stones, which are mostly calcium oxalate). Children: mostly calcium oxalate/phosphate. Uric acid bladder stones specifically respond to increased water intake, low-purine diet, AND urinary alkalinization with potassium citrate — a dissolution option not available for most other stone types.
