Key Terms & Definitions

Start here
Homeostasis
Maintaining internal conditions in a stable state around a narrow "set point," via negative feedback loops.
Milieu intérieur
Claude Bernard's term for the stable internal environment necessary for survival, independent of the external environment.
Allostasis
The dynamic process of achieving stability through change — varying physiologic parameters to match resources to environmental demands.
Stressor
Anything that throws the body out of allostatic balance (Sapolsky's definition).
Stress response
The body's effort to restore allostatic balance after a stressor (Sapolsky's definition).
General adaptation syndrome (GAS)
Selye's model of the body's nonspecific response to any stressor, in three stages: alarm, resistance, exhaustion.
Alarm stage
The fight-or-flight stage of GAS — hypothalamus activates the SNS and HPA axis in response to a stressor.
Resistance (adaptation) stage
The GAS stage where the body mobilizes glucose, fatty acids, and amino acids to manage an ongoing stressor.
Exhaustion stage
The GAS stage where prolonged stress causes glucocorticoid dysregulation, loss of resistance, and "diseases of adaptation."
HPA axis
Hypothalamic-pituitary-adrenal axis — CRH → ACTH → cortisol; the slower hormonal arm of the stress response.
Sympathetic-adrenal-medullary (SAM) system
The fast-acting arm of the stress response — SNS activation triggering adrenal medulla release of catecholamines.
CRH (corticotropin-releasing hormone)
Released by the hypothalamic paraventricular nucleus (PVN); activates both the SNS and the anterior pituitary (ACTH release).
ACTH
Adrenocorticotropic hormone, released by the anterior pituitary in response to CRH; stimulates the adrenal cortex to release cortisol.
Cortisol
The primary glucocorticoid; mobilizes glucose/protein/fat stores, suppresses inflammation and immune function, slower onset but longer duration than catecholamines.
Aldosterone
The primary mineralocorticoid; promotes sodium reabsorption/potassium excretion in the kidney, expanding fluid volume and raising blood pressure.
Allostatic load
The cumulative physiologic "wear and tear" cost of repeated or chronic allostatic responses.
Allostatic overload
When adaptation mechanisms are overwhelmed or inadequate, producing pathophysiologic states across multiple body systems.
Resilience
The capacity to adapt successfully to stress/adversity, preventing disease and mitigating allostatic load.
Maladaptation
Ineffective, inadequate, or inappropriate change in response to altered circumstances.
Coping
A behavioral adaptive response to a stressor; can be functional (health-promoting) or dysfunctional (maladaptive, e.g., overeating, substance use).
Distress
The experience of perceiving an inability to cope with a stressor — further escalates catecholamine and glucocorticoid release.
Habituation
Adapting to a repeated stressor by training the hypothalamus to react less forcefully to it (e.g., no longer noticing airport noise).
Tend-and-befriend response
An alternative to fight-or-flight, thought to be more common in females, mediated by oxytocin and endogenous opioids — produces a calming, affiliative response to stress.
Psychoneuroimmunology
The field studying interactions among the nervous, endocrine, and immune systems, especially regarding stress and its effects on health.
Homeostasis

Word origin & core idea

From Greek homeo ("similar") + stasis ("standing still") — maintaining internal conditions stable around a narrow ideal set point.

Claude Bernard (19th century)

Described the milieu intérieur — the idea that vital physiologic mechanisms exist to maintain a uniform, constant internal environment necessary for survival, independent of the external environment. Disease occurs when the body fails to respond appropriately to threats to this stability.

Walter Cannon (1932, The Wisdom of the Body)

Coined "homeostasis" formally — each biochemical/physiologic variable (temperature, O2, sodium, calcium, glucose) is held within a narrow set-point range via negative feedback loops that sense and correct deviations.

Examples of homeostatic systems

Baroreceptor response to BP changes; ADH release in response to serum osmolality changes; hypothalamic thermoregulation; central chemoreceptor response to PaCO2; parathyroid response to serum calcium.

Homeostasis is a necessary starting concept, but has been criticized as too simple to capture the full complexity of how organisms actually maintain stability — which is exactly the gap allostasis was developed to fill.
Allostasis

Word origin & core idea

From Greek allo ("variable"/"different") + stasis. Sterling and Eyer (1988) — the ability to successfully adapt to challenges by varying internal parameters to match environmental demands, rather than holding a single fixed set point.

Why fixed set points aren't enough

Different circumstances require different set points — respiratory rate must rise during exercise or pneumonia. Allostasis is brain-orchestrated: parameters are continuously reevaluated and readjusted to match resources to situational needs.

The shock example — a key illustration of trade-offs: in shock, blood flow to the brain and heart is preserved by reducing perfusion to the kidneys, skin, and GI tract. A change that's detrimental to one system (kidneys not making urine) supports survival of the organism as a whole. This is the essence of allostasis — stability achieved through change, not by holding everything constant.
Allostasis is now understood to apply broadly — not just to physical stimuli, but to social interactions, discrimination/racism, low socioeconomic status, childhood trauma, mass violence exposure, social media pressure, pandemics, environmental pollutants, climate uncertainty, and everyday work/life demands.
Defining Stress

Why "stress" is genuinely ambiguous

Used to mean a causal factor ("they're experiencing stress"), a state of tension ("I feel stressed"), AND a bodily response — all at once, across health, psychology, and sociology literature.

Sapolsky's clarifying distinction

Stressor: anything that throws the body out of allostatic balance. Stress response: the body's effort to restore that balance. This pairing is precise and commonly tested.

The stress response is meant to be helpful, at least short-term — it becomes damaging specifically when repeatedly activated or when it fails to switch off.
Selye & the General Adaptation Syndrome

The serendipitous discovery (1930s)

Selye injected rats with varied hormonal/tissue extracts expecting different responses per substance — instead found the same three changes every time: adrenal cortex enlargement, lymphatic organ shrinkage (thymus, spleen, lymph nodes), and bleeding peptic ulcers.

The generalization

The same triad appeared with completely different noxious stimuli (temperature extremes, surgery, forced exercise) — leading Selye to conclude this was a nonspecific response to any demand. He coined "stressors" for the causative agents and "general adaptation syndrome (GAS)" for the response pattern.

Modern critique of Selye's model: too simplistic — the body does NOT produce identical responses to all stressor types; hormone release patterns differ by stressor type/severity/duration. Also, Selye initially focused only on physical/biological stimuli; by the 1970s, researchers (including Selye) recognized that perception of a stimulus matters, and responses can be behavioral as well as physiologic.

The Alarm Stage — HPA Axis & SAM System

This is the fight-or-flight response (from Cannon's original work) — two parallel cascades from the same starting point, one fast, one slower.

Mind Map — Two Arms of the Alarm Stage

Trigger

The hypothalamus continuously monitors internal/external environment; when it senses a stressor threatening homeostasis (physical or emotional, positive or negative — arguing, an infection, running for a bus, even winning the lottery), it activates GAS.

Why there are two parallel pathways

PVN-released CRH does double duty: it directly activates the SNS (fast catecholamine release) AND stimulates the anterior pituitary to release ACTH (slower cortisol release via the adrenal cortex) — the HPA axis and SAM system run in parallel, not in sequence.

Once the pituitary is activated, the alarm stage progresses into the resistance stage.
Resistance & Exhaustion Stages

Resistance (adaptation) stage

SNS + adrenal medulla + adrenal cortex work at full force, mobilizing glucose, free fatty acids, and amino acids — elevated via cortisol and catecholamines. These resources fuel the brain and heart, and amino acids double as building blocks for later repair.

Outcome of resistance

If the stressor resolves, the organism returns to a steady state — though the new baseline may sit at a different set point than before (consistent with allostasis, not classic fixed-point homeostasis).

Why the alarm stage can't persist: if it did, the body would suffer wear and tear, permanent damage, and even death — this is the direct rationale for why the resistance stage must take over, and ultimately why prolonged stress without resolution leads to the exhaustion stage's dysregulation and "diseases of adaptation."
Catecholamines — Norepinephrine & Epinephrine

Release pathway

Cerebral cortex/limbic system (hippocampus, amygdala) appraise a stimulus as stressful → relay to hypothalamus → norepinephrine released directly by sympathetic neurons AND from the locus ceruleus (brainstem); epinephrine (+ some norepinephrine) released from the adrenal medulla into the bloodstream.

Stimulus-dependent balance

Cold exposure → SNS/norepinephrine response dominates. Emotional distress or acute hypoglycemia → greater adrenal medulla/epinephrine response. Note: the stress response can occur even without consciousness (comatose/sedated patients).

Norepinephrine specifically: primary vasoconstrictor of vascular smooth muscle, reduces gastric secretion, inhibits insulin secretion, dilates pupils. Epinephrine specifically: enhances myocardial contractility, ↑ heart rate/venous return/cardiac output, bronchodilation, ↑ glycogenolysis, inhibits insulin secretion. Both also influence proinflammatory cytokine production (IL-6, TNF-α).
Cortisol & Aldosterone

Cortisol — the basics

Lipid-soluble, so it crosses cell membranes to bind cytosolic/nuclear receptors — present in nearly every tissue. Released by the adrenal cortex in response to ACTH (itself triggered by CRH). Onset is slower than catecholamines, but duration is longer.

Negative feedback

Cortisol binds receptors on the hypothalamus and anterior pituitary to suppress further CRH/ACTH release — a classic negative feedback loop keeping cortisol within range.

Cortisol's protein/glucose logic

Anabolic in the liver (↑ protein synthesis) but catabolic in muscle/lymphoid/adipose/skin/bone — releasing amino acids for the liver to use. Stimulates gluconeogenesis (6–10x increase in amino-acid-to-glucose/ketoacid conversion) and preserves glucose for neurons by limiting uptake elsewhere.

Chronic elevation is the problem

Prolonged high cortisol → glucocorticoid receptor desensitization/down-regulation on immune cells → progressively fewer anti-inflammatory effects over time, and can eventually flip to proinflammatory effects.

Catecholamine-cortisol synergy/antagonism

Catecholamines help release ACTH (supporting the HPA axis); cortisol supports adrenal medulla epinephrine synthesis (via the PNMT enzyme) and blood pressure/cardiac output maintenance. But in skeletal muscle, catecholamines actually oppose cortisol's catabolic effect.

Aldosterone

The primary mineralocorticoid. SNS activation → renin-angiotensin system → aldosterone release (fluid volume depletion also triggers this directly). Acts on kidney distal tubules/collecting ducts: reabsorbs sodium, excretes potassium — water follows sodium osmotically, so ECF volume and BP both rise. Angiotensin II itself is also a potent vasoconstrictor, reinforcing catecholamine-driven BP elevation.

Endorphins, Enkephalins & Immune Cytokines

Endogenous opioids

Endorphins, enkephalins, dynorphins, endomorphins — small peptides produced in the CNS and elsewhere, released in response to stress, exercise, chocolate, laughter, massage, acupuncture. "Endorphin" = endogenous + morphine; raises pain threshold, produces sedation/euphoria, and can attenuate SNS activation by CRH during acute stress.

Immune system connections both ways

Immune cells (B/T lymphocytes, granulocytes, macrophages, monocytes) produce endogenous opioids in response to stressors/CRH/cytokines/catecholamines, and carry opioid receptors that modulate immune activity. Conversely, activated immune cells release proinflammatory cytokines that can enhance pain — a bidirectional loop.

IL-1 (from macrophages) can itself affect hypothalamic CRH production, and leukocytes can produce ACTH — the nervous, endocrine, and immune systems are genuinely intertwined, not three separate boxes.
Stress-related microbiome disruption has been linked to MS, diabetes, psoriasis, IBS, depression, ADHD, and rheumatoid arthritis — an expanding area connecting stress physiology to a wide range of seemingly unrelated conditions.
Sex Hormones — Estrogen, Testosterone & DHEA

Cortisol's reproductive suppression

Excess stress/cortisol suppresses GnRH, LH, estradiol, and progesterone release — inhibiting female reproduction and affecting premenstrual symptoms. Estradiol, in turn, down-regulates brain glucocorticoid receptor binding.

Androgens oppose glucocorticoid effects

Testosterone and DHEA oppose cortisol's catabolic effects on bone, and its impact on lymphoid tissue/inflammatory cytokines/leukocytes. DHEA counteracts glucocorticoid-associated depressive symptoms; testosterone has antidepressant/antianxiety, mood-elevating effects.

Testosterone drops with stress

Illness, surgery, strenuous exercise, and academic stress all significantly reduce circulating testosterone. Combined with vasopressin, testosterone enhances BP/HR reactivity and amplifies fight-or-flight.

Tend-and-befriend

Oxytocin (modulated by estrogen) and endogenous opioids are thought to produce a calming effect during stress — giving rise to the idea that females may show a "tend-and-befriend" response rather than classic fight-or-flight in some situations.

Growth Hormone, Prolactin & Oxytocin

Growth hormone

Anabolic (↑ protein synthesis, bone/muscle growth), ↑ lipolysis, ↓ carbohydrate use peripherally. Normally cyclic/nocturnal, highest in adolescence. Rises acutely with intense stress; but chronic stress activation eventually decreases GH secretion — the mechanism behind stunted growth in children under prolonged chronic stress.

Prolactin

Released with physical/psychological stress, exercise, inflammation, sexual activity, pregnancy, and suckling (even in males). Suppresses ovulation; roles in lactation, metabolism, bone homeostasis, and immune regulation (lymphocytes have prolactin receptors); may be neuroprotective for hippocampal neurons under stress. Requires more intense stimuli to rise than catecholamines/cortisol do.

Oxytocin — the "tend-and-befriend hormone"

Produced during childbirth, lactation, and sexual activity (both sexes); promotes bonding/social attachment. Moderates the stress response — reduces HPA/sympathetic activation and perceived anxiety. Stronger effects/higher concentrations in females (estrogen interaction). Early adverse life events → lower lifetime oxytocin, potentially contributing to obesity risk since oxytocin normally decreases food intake.

Real study cited: preteen girls who heard their mother's voice on the phone after a lab stressor had higher urinary oxytocin and lower salivary cortisol than those who only texted — hearing the voice specifically buffered the stress response.
Adaptation, Coping & Resilience

Adaptation

The biopsychosocial process of adjusting physiology, morphology, and behavior to new/altered internal or external circumstances.

Resilience

The capacity to adapt to adversity, achieving a successful outcome — "coping and flourishing either 'in spite of' or 'because of' adversity." Prevents disease and mitigates allostatic load; involves the ability to resist, recover, or redirect after trauma.

What influences resilience

Genetics, epigenetic changes from early trauma, social support/coping mechanisms, positive outlook/sense of control, sense of purpose, grit, prior learning, lifestyle (exercise, diet, sleep), and religious/spiritual practice. Also influenced by cortisol itself and neural proteins like BDNF (brain-derived neurotrophic factor, important for brain plasticity).

Maladaptation & dysfunctional coping

Maladaptation: ineffective/inappropriate change. Coping is functional if it resolves the situation or feelings; dysfunctional if it doesn't. Dysfunctional examples: excessive eating, alcohol use, smoking, substance abuse — these directly contribute to atherosclerosis, hypertension, type 2 diabetes, and metabolic syndrome.

Perception, Distress & Habituation

Perception shapes response

The same stimulus (e.g., a loud "bang") can be interpreted very differently depending on context (car backfiring vs. gunshot) — the interpretation, not just the stimulus itself, drives the adaptive response.

Distress specifically

The experience of perceiving an inability to cope — this perception itself further activates catecholamine and glucocorticoid release, escalating allostatic load and worsening preexisting conditions (e.g., an asthma patient without an inhaler becomes even more short of breath from the distress itself).

Habituation — training the hypothalamus to react less

Repeated, ignored exposure to a stressor (e.g., airport noise) reduces the hypothalamic response over time, preventing inappropriate GAS triggering. Desensitization techniques (biofeedback, guided imagery, mindfulness, yoga, meditation) shift dominant brain waves from beta to alpha/theta (more relaxed states) — documented to enhance immune function and reduce inflammation with 20–30 min/day practice.

Conditions shown to benefit from desensitization approaches: migraine, chronic back pain, hypertension, IBS/IBD.
Allostatic Overload & Illness

When adaptation mechanisms are inadequate or total load is excessive, homeostasis can't be regained — the result spans psychiatric, endocrine, and inflammatory disease categories.

Mind Map — Four Ways Allostatic Load Accumulates

Common stress-linked disorders

Hair loss, burnout, mouth sores, insomnia, asthma, heart palpitations, tics, tension headaches, muscle-contraction backaches, digestive disorders, irritable bladder, menstrual irregularity, erectile dysfunction.

Physical indicators of high stress

↑ BP, ↑ muscle tension, ↑ pulse/respiration, sweaty palms, cold extremities, fatigue, tension headache, GI upset, appetite/weight change, ↑ catecholamines, hyperglycemia, restlessness, insomnia.

Behavioral/emotional indicators

Nonspecific anxiety, depression, increased substance use, eating/sleeping/activity changes, mental exhaustion, low self-esteem, irritability, low motivation, poor concentration/judgment, absenteeism, accident-proneness.

Cardiovascular link

Cortisol receptors are dense in abdominal fat — excess cortisol drives abdominal fat accumulation, and released free fatty acids raise cardiovascular risk. Catecholamines/cortisol together promote atherosclerosis, hypertension, dysrhythmias, sudden cardiac death, and stress-induced cardiomyopathy; catecholamines also increase platelet activity (clotting) and serum lipids.

The inflammation bridge to cardiovascular disease: stress → proinflammatory cytokines (IL-1, IL-6, TNF-α) → triggers CRP production → CRP is itself an established cardiovascular disease marker. This gives a direct mechanistic link between psychological stress and heart disease, not just a correlational one.

Cognitive/CNS effects (psychoneuroimmunology)

Stress mediators normally support learning, memory, and autonomic regulation. But chronic over/under-activity can cause hippocampal atrophy (impaired memory) alongside amygdala hypertrophy/remodeling (increased fear, anxiety, mood disorders).

Inflammation and cognitive decline

Elevated IL-1 and IL-6 (inflammation markers tied to stress) have been linked to memory loss, cognitive decline, and the development of Alzheimer disease — connecting allostatic overload directly to neurodegenerative risk.
