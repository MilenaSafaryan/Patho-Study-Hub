Key Terms & Definitions

Start here

These terms anchor everything else in the chapter. Type in the box to filter.

Gallbladder
A distensible sac (~30–50 mL capacity) that concentrates and stores bile, connecting via the cystic duct to the common hepatic duct.
Common bile duct
Formed by the union of the cystic and common hepatic ducts; ~3 inches long, runs behind the duodenum, ends at the ampulla of Vater.
Ampulla of Vater
The complex structure where the common bile duct and main pancreatic duct terminate together into the duodenum.
Bile
A fluid made of water, electrolytes, bile acids, pigment, cholesterol, and phospholipids; aids fat digestion and excretes waste (bilirubin, toxins, cholesterol).
Bile salts (bile acids)
Primary (cholic, chenodeoxycholic) and secondary (deoxycholic, ursodeoxycholic, lithocholic) acids that solubilize lipids in bile.
Micelle
A cluster formed by hydrophilic/hydrophobic bile salt molecules that surrounds and solubilizes lipids like cholesterol.
Lecithin
A phospholipid secreted in bile that incorporates into micelles to greatly enhance cholesterol solubility.
Enterohepatic circulation
The recycling of bile acids: absorbed in the terminal ileum, carried by portal circulation back to the liver, and re-secreted into bile (2–3 times/day on average).
Sphincter of Oddi
The muscular sphincter at the ampulla of Vater that controls bile (and pancreatic juice) flow into the duodenum; contracted during fasting to promote gallbladder filling.
Exocrine pancreas
The portion of the pancreas that secretes digestive juice (enzymes and proenzymes) into the intestinal tract via the pancreatic duct — over 1 L/day.
Endocrine pancreas
The portion of the pancreas (islets) that secretes insulin, glucagon, and somatostatin into the bloodstream.
Acinar cells
Pancreatic exocrine cells arranged in glandular acini that produce digestive juice, draining into the main pancreatic duct.
Proenzyme (zymogen)
An inactive enzyme precursor, e.g. trypsinogen, that is only activated after reaching the duodenum — a key safeguard against autodigestion.
Cholecystokinin (CCK)
A small-intestinal hormone that stimulates pancreatic enzyme release and gallbladder contraction.
Secretin
A small-intestinal hormone that regulates pancreatic juice (bicarbonate) secretion.
Acute pancreatitis
An inflammatory process of the pancreas ranging from mild to life-threatening, caused by premature activation of digestive enzymes and autodigestion of the gland.
Autodigestion
The self-destructive process in which prematurely activated pancreatic enzymes digest the pancreas's own tissue.
Lipase & amylase
Pancreatic digestive enzymes; serum levels rise in acute pancreatitis. Lipase is more specific and stays elevated longer.
Sentinel loop
A distended loop of small bowel near the pancreas seen on plain abdominal x-ray in pancreatitis, from local ileus.
Ranson criteria
A widely used scoring system to predict the clinical course/severity of acute pancreatitis.
Acute necrotizing pancreatitis
Severe pancreatitis with widespread pancreatic tissue death; high risk of progressing to infected necrosis, with high morbidity/mortality.
Pancreatic pseudocyst
A fluid collection in/near the pancreas, often communicating with the pancreatic duct; lacks an epithelial lining (unlike a true cyst) — the most common localized complication of acute pancreatitis.
Pancreatic ascites
Usually painless, often massive fluid accumulation from a persistent leak in the main pancreatic duct.
Necrosectomy
Surgical or endoscopic debridement of dead (necrotic) pancreatic tissue.
Cholelithiasis
The presence of gallstones within the gallbladder; most are cholesterol stones, a minority are pigment stones.
Biliary colic
Temporary, self-limited RUQ pain from a stone transiently obstructing the cystic duct, often triggered by a fatty meal; resolves once the stone dislodges or passes.
Cholecystitis
Inflammation of the gallbladder, usually from a gallstone persistently obstructing the cystic duct (acute calculous cholecystitis); unlike biliary colic, pain is constant and accompanied by systemic signs of inflammation.
Murphy sign
Inspiratory arrest when the examiner palpates the right subcostal area during a deep breath — a classic bedside sign of acute cholecystitis.
Choledocholithiasis
A gallstone lodged in the common bile duct, causing obstructive jaundice and putting the patient at risk for cholangitis or pancreatitis.
Cholangitis
Bacterial infection of the bile ducts, usually from bile stasis behind an obstructing stone; a potentially life-threatening emergency requiring urgent drainage.
Charcot triad
Fever/chills, RUQ pain, and jaundice — the classic presentation of acute cholangitis.
Reynolds pentad
Charcot triad plus hypotension and altered mental status — signals severe, decompensating cholangitis.
Courvoisier sign
A palpable, nontender, distended gallbladder in a jaundiced patient — suggests malignant biliary obstruction (e.g., pancreatic head cancer) rather than gallstones.
Chronic pancreatitis
Irreversible, progressive fibrosis and destruction of pancreatic tissue from repeated inflammatory injury, leading to loss of both exocrine and endocrine function.
Pancreatic exocrine insufficiency (PEI)
Inadequate digestive enzyme output from a damaged pancreas, causing malabsorption, steatorrhea, and fat-soluble vitamin deficiency.
PERT
Pancreatic enzyme replacement therapy — oral enzyme supplements taken with meals to treat exocrine insufficiency.
CA 19-9
A tumor marker sometimes elevated in pancreatic cancer, used to support diagnosis and monitor treatment response (not used alone for screening).

Anatomy & Physiology of the Pancreaticobiliary System

Foundations

The pancreaticobiliary system links the liver, gallbladder, bile ducts, and pancreas into one controlled delivery system for digestive secretions — all draining through a single shared gateway, the ampulla of Vater.

Structure of the Pancreaticobiliary System

Components

Gallbladder + cystic duct; intrahepatic, hepatic, and common bile ducts; and the endocrine + exocrine pancreas. Together, the extrahepatic biliary tree and gallbladder form a controlled bile-delivery system.

Gallbladder

A distensible sac of ~30–50 mL capacity; connects via the cystic duct to the common hepatic duct, forming the common bile duct.

Common bile duct

~3 inches long; runs behind the duodenum and terminates at the ampulla of Vater — the shared drainage point for bile and pancreatic juice.

Pancreatic duct

Travels proximally through the pancreas, branching dorsally to drain the tail, and also empties at the ampulla of Vater.

Embryology — liver/biliary tree

Hepatic diverticulum forms from the primitive foregut at week 3–4. By week 5, three buds appear: cranial (hepatoblasts → liver), caudal (→ gallbladder, joins the hepatic duct via the cystic duct), and basal (→ ventral pancreas). Ducts start hollow, solidify into cords, then re-canalize.

Embryology — pancreas

The dorsal pancreas forms from a separate outcropping on the opposite side of the foregut from the hepatic diverticulum; the ventral pancreas forms from the basal bud of the hepatic diverticulum. The two fuse.

Fun clinical fact: During the second trimester the fetus produces bile, which gives fetal meconium its color.
Physiology of Bile

Composition

Mostly water and electrolytes, low protein; organic solutes are mainly bile acids, pigment, cholesterol, and phospholipids.

Bile acids

Primary: cholic and chenodeoxycholic acids. Secondary (made by colonic bacteria): deoxycholic, ursodeoxycholic, and lithocholic acids.

Functions of bile

Aids lipid digestion, and transports waste (especially bilirubin), immunoglobulin A, toxins, and cholesterol into the gut for disposal or reabsorption.

Drainage pathway

Bile canaliculi → canals of Hering → ductules → interlobular ducts → septal ducts → right/left lobar ducts → common bile duct.

Fasting vs. feeding cycle:

StateWhat happens
FastingSphincter of Oddi is contracted → bile flows into the gallbladder (only ~half is stored; the rest flows to the duodenum). Within 4 hours, up to 90% of bile's water is absorbed, concentrating sodium, bile salts, and electrolytes.
First morning mealHormonally + neurally regulated gallbladder contraction releases the concentrated bile into the duodenum.

Mind Map — Enterohepatic Circulation of Bile Salts

Recycling rate

Bile acids are reabsorbed and re-secreted on average 2–3 times per day. Losses in stool are balanced by new hepatic synthesis of bile salt from cholesterol.

Micelles & cholesterol solubility

Bile salts are hydrophilic on one end, hydrophobic on the other — they aggregate into micelles around lipids. Micelles alone don't stabilize cholesterol well; lecithin incorporated into the micelle core greatly boosts cholesterol solubility.

Gallstone link: When cholesterol concentration in bile is too high for the available micelle/lecithin capacity, it precipitates out of solution — this is the basic mechanism behind cholesterol gallstone formation.
Functional Anatomy of the Pancreas

Two organs in one

Endocrine: secretes insulin, glucagon, and somatostatin into the bloodstream. Exocrine: secretes >1 L of digestive juice daily into the digestive tract.

Embryologic origin

Formed from the fusion of two organs — a dorsal and a ventral pancreas.

Microscopic structure

Lobular, arranged into exocrine glands; pancreatic juice is secreted into glandular acini, which drain into the main pancreatic duct.

Islet distribution

Pancreatic islets (hormone-producing areas) are more abundant in the tail of the pancreas than in the body or head.

What's in pancreatic juice

Active enzymes ready to work immediately (e.g., amylase, lipase) and proenzymes that need activation in the duodenum (e.g., trypsinogen).

Hormonal control

Release during a meal is controlled by cholecystokinin (CCK) and secretin, both secreted from the small intestinal mucosa.

Key mechanism to remember: When this regulation is deranged and enzymes activate prematurely inside the gland instead of the duodenum, the result is acute pancreatitis.

Disorders of the Gallbladder & Biliary Tree

Gallstones are extremely common — roughly 10–20% of US adults have them — but most stay silent for life. The clinical picture only escalates when a stone actually blocks something, and where it blocks determines which of four overlapping conditions you're dealing with.

Cholelithiasis (Gallstones)

Two stone types

Cholesterol stones (majority, >80%): from cholesterol supersaturation of bile overwhelming the micelle/lecithin solubilizing system (see above). Pigment stones (dark brown/black): form when bile contains excess bilirubin, often linked to hemolysis, biliary infection, or parasitic infestation.

How common

About 10–20% of US adults (roughly 20 million people) have gallstones; most never become symptomatic.

Classic risk factors — the "4 F's" (sometimes 5)

Female, Fat (obesity), Forty (increasing age), Fertile (multiparity/pregnancy) — sometimes a 5th Fair (fair-skinned/Northern European ancestry) is added. Estrogen raises biliary cholesterol secretion, which is why risk rises with female sex, pregnancy, and estrogen-containing medications.

Other risk factors

Metabolic syndrome (obesity, insulin resistance, type 2 diabetes, hypertension, hyperlipidemia); rapid weight loss; Crohn disease or ileal resection (impairs bile salt reabsorption); prolonged fasting, TPN, or immobility (biliary stasis from reduced gallbladder stimulation); and genetic predisposition.

Most gallstones are silent

The majority are found incidentally on imaging and never cause symptoms — treatment is only pursued once a stone actually causes a problem.

Treatment principle

Asymptomatic stones: generally observed, no treatment needed. Symptomatic stones: laparoscopic cholecystectomy is the definitive treatment. Dietary fat restriction can reduce symptom triggers while awaiting surgery.

The Biliary Obstruction Spectrum: Colic → Cholecystitis → Choledocholithiasis → Cholangitis

These four conditions are often confused because they're all downstream of the same problem — a gallstone — but where the stone lodges and how long it stays there changes everything about severity and urgency.

Mind Map — Same Gallstone, Four Different Pictures

ConditionPain patternKey distinguishing features
Biliary colicCrampy RUQ pain, often after a fatty meal; despite the name "colic," pain is usually steady rather than truly colicky, and it resolves as the stone dislodgesNo fever, no systemic illness — the gallbladder isn't inflamed, just transiently obstructed
Acute cholecystitisConstant RUQ pain, may radiate to right shoulder/scapula, worse with deep inspirationPositive Murphy sign (inspiratory arrest on RUQ palpation); fever, nausea/vomiting, systemically unwell — unlike colic, the gallbladder is now truly inflamed
CholedocholithiasisRUQ/epigastric painJaundice, dark urine, clay-colored stools; labs show elevated direct bilirubin and alkaline phosphatase — the stone has moved into the common bile duct itself
CholangitisRUQ pain as part of the triadCharcot triad: fever/chills + RUQ pain + jaundice. Severe cases add hypotension and altered mental status (Reynolds pentad) — bile stasis has allowed bacterial infection to take hold and can ascend toward the liver
Cholangitis is the true emergency here. Charcot triad (or worse, Reynolds pentad) signals bacterial infection in an obstructed biliary system — this needs urgent antibiotics and biliary decompression (typically ERCP), not just symptom control.

Diagnosis across the spectrum

RUQ ultrasound is the first-line imaging test for all four conditions — look for stones, gallbladder wall thickening (>0.4 cm), pericholecystic fluid, or bile duct dilation. MRCP or ERCP add detail when duct-level involvement is suspected.

Treatment by condition

Biliary colic: supportive care (fluids, antiemetics, analgesia) + elective laparoscopic cholecystectomy to prevent recurrence. Cholecystitis: IV antibiotics + laparoscopic cholecystectomy, ideally within 72 hours of admission. Choledocholithiasis: ERCP or surgical stone extraction. Cholangitis: broad-spectrum IV antibiotics plus urgent biliary drainage (ERCP is now the mainstay, largely replacing surgical duct exploration).

Disorders of the Pancreas

Pancreatitis

Pancreatitis is common and can be debilitating — acute (a single inflammatory episode) or chronic (recurring flare-ups). This section covers acute pancreatitis in depth.

Acute Pancreatitis

Acute pancreatitis ranges from mild/inconveniencing to severe/life-threatening. It affects 5–35 per 100,000 people annually in the US. The core process, however it starts, is the same: enzymes get activated inside the pancreas instead of the duodenum, and the gland starts digesting itself.

Mind Map — Three Pathways to Acute Pancreatitis

Most common US causes

Biliary tract disease, hypertriglyceridemia, and ethanol (alcohol)-associated pancreatitis.

Alcohol's role

Up to 66% of first pancreatitis cases are linked to alcoholism, though the exact mechanism is unclear (possible: increased exocrine secretion, sphincter of Oddi contraction, direct acinar toxicity). Many "acute" alcoholic cases may actually be flare-ups of unrecognized chronic pancreatitis — though up to 40% show no chronic changes on pathology.

Clinical Manifestations

Classic presentation

Steady, boring pain in the epigastrium or left upper quadrant, gradually increasing, often radiating through to the back. Accompanied by nausea/vomiting.

Exam findings

Exquisite tenderness on palpation; bowel sounds reduced but not absent; possible abdominal distention; fever usually low-grade initially.

Severe disease

Circulatory instability, respiratory insufficiency, and shock.

Diagnosis

Serum enzymes

Lipase and amylase rise together in the first 12 hours. Lipase is more specific and stays elevated longer — it's the preferred test.

Other labs

↑ AST/ALT; marked ↑ alkaline phosphatase and bilirubin suggests biliary obstruction (e.g., gallstones). Also: leukocytosis, hyperlipidemia, hypocalcemia, ↑ CRP.

Imaging

Plain x-ray: ileus pattern or "sentinel loop." Ultrasound: convenient bedside view, but limited by bowel gas. CT: gold standard — shows edema, necrosis, abscess, cysts. MRCP: for detailed ductal anatomy in select cases.

Severity prediction

Ranson criteria (often paired with CT scoring) predict clinical course; patients with many risk factors need early ICU monitoring. Pancreatic necrosis on contrast CT is an especially important finding.

Differential diagnosis includes perforated peptic ulcer, acute cholecystitis, and mesenteric vascular disease — many other conditions can also raise serum amylase (see table below), so amylase alone doesn't confirm pancreatitis.

Treatment

Mild-to-moderate disease

Conservative: withhold oral feeding, NG suction if significant ileus, careful IV fluid replacement (D5NS or lactated Ringer). Parenteral analgesics (narcotics used carefully re: sphincter of Oddi, though no agent is strictly contraindicated). Usually resolves in 3–7 days; improving lipase/CRP tracks clinical improvement.

Severe disease

May cause multisystem organ dysfunction — needs ICU-level support. Rapid catabolism → TPN usually indicated beyond a few days. Also: calcium for hypocalcemia, magnesium correction, glycemic control.

Fatal complications to watch for

Acute respiratory failure (often with ARDS), acute renal failure, acute intra-abdominal sepsis — may need mechanical ventilation or hemodialysis.

Acute necrotizing pancreatitis

Severe pain + toxicity signs + CT evidence of necrosis. Bacterial infection is the key driver of poor outcomes. Empiric broad-spectrum antibiotics (e.g., carbapenems) if febrile/septic; aspirate fluid collections for culture. Indiscriminate antibiotic use is not helpful and adds side-effect risk.

Surgical last resort: Abscess/hemorrhage may need percutaneous drainage or surgery. Necrosectomy (debridement) or pancreatectomy are the main surgical options — technically challenging, high risk, referred to experienced surgeons, and generally a last resort.
Localized Complications of Acute Pancreatitis

Pancreatic pseudocyst — the most common localized complication

A fluid collection in/near the pancreas, often communicating directly with the pancreatic duct. No epithelial lining (unlike a true cyst) — that's what makes it a "pseudo"cyst. Presents as abdominal fullness/mass and tenderness; can develop acutely or subacutely during recovery.

Pseudocyst complications

Infection (called an infected pseudocyst, distinct from a pancreatic abscess), spontaneous rupture, or hemorrhage.

Pseudocyst management

Percutaneous, endoscopic, or surgical drainage (external or internal — usually into stomach or bowel). Current treatment of choice, if available: endoscopic gastrostomic passage + stent placement.

Pancreatic ascites

From a persistent leak in the main pancreatic duct — usually painless, often massive. Fluid can track into unusual spaces (pleural space, mediastinum). Diagnosed via ultrasound/CT + aspiration (checking amylase level). Managed conservatively with prolonged parenteral nutrition; endoscopic ductal stenting may help.

Other complications to know

  • Common bile duct obstruction
  • Portal or splenic vein thrombosis
  • Peptic ulcer disease
  • Chronic fistula formation

Chronic Pancreatitis

Researched from credible sources

Where acute pancreatitis is a single inflammatory event, chronic pancreatitis is the scar tissue left behind after repeated ones — permanent, progressive, and eventually taking both the exocrine and endocrine pancreas down with it.

Etiology, Manifestations & Treatment

What's different from acute pancreatitis

Chronic pancreatitis is irreversible — repeated inflammatory episodes cause progressive fibrosis, permanently destroying normal pancreatic tissue and both its exocrine (digestive enzyme) and endocrine (insulin) functions.

Leading cause

Alcohol use is the single most significant risk factor in adults. Average age at diagnosis is 35–55; men are affected roughly 1.5–3x more often than women.

Mind Map — The Classic Triad of Chronic Pancreatitis

Pain pattern

Epigastric pain radiating to the back, classically worsened by eating and relieved by leaning forward. Most patients present with recurrent acute pancreatitis episodes before progressing to constant chronic pain — though 10–20% of patients are painless.

The "90% rule"

Symptoms of exocrine insufficiency (steatorrhea, malabsorption) typically don't appear until roughly 90% of pancreatic function is already lost — this is why the disease is often silent for a long time before diagnosis.

Diagnosis

Contrast-enhanced CT is generally the first imaging choice; MRI, MRCP, or endoscopic ultrasound may be used when CT is inconclusive, especially early in the disease. Unlike acute pancreatitis, serum amylase/lipase are often normal in chronic disease because there's simply not enough functioning pancreatic tissue left to release much enzyme.

A diagnosis that should prompt a cancer workup

Unexplained worsening of chronic pancreatitis symptoms — especially with a new pancreatic duct stricture — should raise suspicion for pancreatic cancer, given the overlap in risk factors and presentation.

Treatment — lifestyle & pain

Alcohol and tobacco cessation are foundational. Small, frequent, low-fat meals reduce symptom triggers. Pain management (including antidepressants for neuropathic-type pain) is often needed; roughly half of patients eventually require surgery for refractory pain.

Treatment — exocrine & endocrine insufficiency

PERT (pancreatic enzyme replacement therapy) taken with meals is the mainstay for steatorrhea and malabsorption, alongside fat-soluble vitamin (A, D, E, K) supplementation. Diabetes arising from pancreatic damage is managed with an endocrinology referral; insulin is used cautiously, since these patients often also lack glucagon reserve and are prone to hypoglycemia.

Pancreatic Cancer

Researched from credible sources

Pancreatic cancer is notorious for being diagnosed late — by the time it causes obvious symptoms, it has often already spread. Location within the pancreas is the single biggest factor in how (and how early) it's noticed.

Risk Factors, Presentation & Prognosis

Risk factors

Smoking, older age, male sex, obesity, chronic pancreatitis, longstanding diabetes mellitus, and hereditary factors (familial pancreatic cancer syndromes, BRCA mutations, hereditary chronic pancreatitis).

Where it arises

About 60–70% arise in the head of the pancreas, 20–25% in the body/tail, and the rest are diffuse/multifocal. This location matters enormously for how the disease presents.

Why location changes everything: Head-of-pancreas tumors sit right next to the common bile duct, so they tend to cause obstructive jaundice early — while the tumor is still small. Body/tail tumors have no such early warning system, so they're typically found later, once they've grown large enough to cause pain or have already spread.
SymptomWhy it happens
Painless jaundice (classic for head-of-pancreas tumors)Tumor compresses the common bile duct, blocking bile flow before pain develops — often the earliest presenting sign
Epigastric or back painTumor invasion of surrounding tissue or the celiac/splanchnic nerve plexus (retroperitoneal invasion) — back radiation is a worrying sign of local spread

Diagnosis

CT or MRI for initial imaging and staging; endoscopic ultrasound with fine-needle aspiration or ERCP for tissue diagnosis. CA 19-9 is a tumor marker that can support diagnosis and track treatment response, but isn't reliable enough for screening on its own.

Prognosis

Sobering: overall 5-year survival is in the single digits (roughly 4–10% by various estimates), rising to somewhat better odds (~15–20%) for the minority found early enough for surgical resection. More than half of patients already have distant metastasis at diagnosis.

Treatment

Surgical resection (e.g., the Whipple procedure for head-of-pancreas tumors) offers the only realistic chance at cure, but is only possible in a minority of patients whose disease is caught early and localized. Chemotherapy (and sometimes radiation) is used both alongside surgery and as the primary treatment for unresectable or metastatic disease.
