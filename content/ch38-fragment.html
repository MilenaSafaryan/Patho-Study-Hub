Glisson capsule
The connective tissue capsule covering the liver, itself covered by visceral peritoneum.
Portal triad
The bundle of portal vein, hepatic artery, and bile duct branches that travel together through the liver.
Hepatic sinusoids
Capillary-like spaces where hepatic artery and portal vein blood mix, lined by endothelial cells and Kupffer cells.
Kupffer cells
Phagocytic macrophages lining the hepatic sinusoids.
Portal hypertension
Elevated portal venous pressure from obstruction to blood flow through the liver; a central event in many liver diseases.
Hepatocellular failure
Loss of the liver's biochemical/synthetic functions, producing jaundice, bleeding, ascites, encephalopathy, and more.
Hepatic encephalopathy
Brain dysfunction from abnormal protein/ammonia metabolism due to liver failure.
Fetor hepaticus
A characteristic breath odor from decreased hepatic detoxification.
Caput medusae
Dilated periumbilical veins seen with portal hypertension and collateral blood flow.
Jaundice (icterus)
Yellow discoloration from elevated bilirubin, due to biliary obstruction or decreased bilirubin synthesis/excretion.
Anti-HAV IgM / IgG
IgM = acute hepatitis A infection; IgG = past infection or vaccination, implying immunity.
HBsAg
Hepatitis B surface antigen; implies active infection, detectable 2–6 weeks post-infection.
HBsAb (IgG)
Hepatitis B surface antibody; implies resolution of infection and immunity.
HBeAg
Hepatitis Be antigen; implies ongoing infection with high infectivity.
Anti-HCV
Antibody to hepatitis C antigens; does NOT indicate immunity, and may be falsely positive/negative.
Cirrhosis
The irreversible end stage of chronic liver injury: diffuse fibrosis surrounding regenerative nodules, with permanently altered blood flow and function.
Primary biliary cholangitis (PBC)
Autoimmune small-duct biliary cirrhosis, associated with antimitochondrial antibodies.
Primary sclerosing cholangitis (PSC)
Autoimmune large-duct biliary disease with recurrent cholangitis and biliary scarring, strongly linked to ulcerative colitis.
Steatohepatitis
Fatty liver with significant inflammation; alcoholic or nonalcoholic (NASH).
Mallory bodies
Intracellular inclusions seen in hepatocytes in alcoholic hepatitis.
Hereditary hemochromatosis (HH)
Autosomal recessive HFE gene mutation causing excessive iron absorption and deposition in organs.
Wilson disease
Autosomal recessive ATP7B gene mutation causing excessive copper accumulation in the liver and other organs.
Kayser-Fleischer rings
Brownish corneal rings seen on slit-lamp exam, characteristic (but not required) for Wilson disease diagnosis.
NAPQI
N-acetyl-p-benzoquinone imine — the toxic metabolite of acetaminophen that causes hepatic necrosis when glutathione is overwhelmed.
Kernicterus
Brain injury (basal ganglia/thalamus staining) from unconjugated bilirubin crossing the immature neonatal blood-brain barrier.
Physiologic jaundice of the newborn
A harmless, self-limited (≤2 weeks) neonatal jaundice from immature bilirubin conjugation/transport.
Reye syndrome
A pediatric syndrome of fatty liver infiltration and encephalopathy after a viral illness, linked to aspirin use.
α1-Antitrypsin deficiency
Autosomal recessive enzyme-inhibitor deficiency (PiZZ variant) causing liver disease and emphysema.
Gilbert syndrome
A very common, benign autosomal dominant condition causing mild unconjugated hyperbilirubinemia.
Crigler-Najjar syndrome
Rare autosomal recessive disorder of absent/reduced bilirubin conjugation; type I is fatal without transplant, type II is milder.
Progressive familial intrahepatic cholestasis (PFIC)
Rare autosomal recessive disorder of bile salt excretion causing severe jaundice, pruritus, and malabsorption.
Alagille syndrome
Autosomal dominant condition with a paucity of intrahepatic bile ducts plus bony/cardiac malformations.
Biliary atresia
Extrahepatic ductopenia — a congenital or acquired obliteration of the extrahepatic bile ducts; a common serious birth defect.
Kasai procedure
A hepatoportoenteric connection surgery for biliary atresia; usually a bridge to eventual liver transplant, not curative.
Ischemic hepatitis
Liver injury from hypotension/reduced perfusion, common in older patients with cardiovascular disease.
Unconjugated (indirect) bilirubin
Fat-soluble bilirubin bound to albumin, not yet processed by the liver; can cross the blood-brain barrier, cannot be excreted in urine.
Conjugated (direct) bilirubin
Water-soluble bilirubin after hepatocyte processing (glucuronidation); excretable in bile and urine.
Prehepatic jaundice
Jaundice from excess bilirubin production (usually hemolysis) overwhelming a normal liver's conjugation capacity; unconjugated hyperbilirubinemia.
Hepatic (hepatocellular) jaundice
Jaundice from damaged hepatocytes unable to properly conjugate and excrete bilirubin; typically a mixed (both unconjugated and conjugated) picture.
Posthepatic (obstructive) jaundice
Jaundice from blocked bile flow after the liver has already conjugated bilirubin; conjugated hyperbilirubinemia, with dark urine and pale/clay-colored stools.
Urobilinogen
A colorless bilirubin breakdown product formed by colonic bacteria; most becomes stercobilin (colors stool), some is reabsorbed and excreted in urine.
Hepatic venous pressure gradient (HVPG)
The clinical measurement used to diagnose portal hypertension; normal is 1–5 mmHg, clinically significant portal hypertension is ≥10 mmHg.
Fulminant hepatic failure
Acute liver failure with rapid-onset hepatic encephalopathy and severe coagulopathy within days to weeks, in a person with no prior chronic liver disease.
Hepatic encephalopathy
Reversible neurologic dysfunction from the liver's failure to clear neurotoxins (especially ammonia) from portal blood.
HBeAg / Anti-HBe
Markers of hepatitis B viral replication activity; HBeAg-positive indicates high infectivity, seroconversion to anti-HBe suggests lower viral activity.
Sustained virologic response (SVR)
Undetectable hepatitis C viral RNA 12 weeks after finishing treatment — the standard definition of a hepatitis C cure.
Direct-acting antivirals (DAAs)
Modern oral hepatitis C medications that directly target viral proteins; cure over 90–95% of patients in as little as 8–12 weeks.
Hepatocellular carcinoma (HCC)
The most common primary liver cancer, arising almost exclusively in the setting of chronic hepatitis B/C infection or cirrhosis of any cause.
Alpha-fetoprotein (AFP)
A tumor marker sometimes elevated in HCC, used alongside ultrasound for surveillance in high-risk patients — imperfect sensitivity/specificity on its own.

Structure & Function of the Liver

Foundations

The liver is the largest parenchymal organ (~1500 g), tucked in the right upper quadrant beneath the diaphragm. Its dual blood supply and interwoven roles as a digestive, endocrine, hematologic, and excretory organ explain why liver disease produces such a wide range of symptoms.

The sections below marked "Researched from credible sources" extend this guide beyond your original chapter upload — sourced from StatPearls/NCBI Bookshelf, AASLD, Merck Manual, AAFP, Cleveland Clinic, and similar credible clinical references, in the same structure and depth as the rest of the guide.
Anatomy & Blood Supply

Basic anatomy

Right upper quadrant, beneath the diaphragm; divided into right and left lobes, further subdivided by blood supply and biliary drainage patterns. Covered by the Glisson capsule, then visceral peritoneum.

Bare area

The region of the liver in direct contact with the diaphragm, not covered by peritoneum — demarcated by the suspensory hepatic ligaments.

Mind Map — Dual Blood Supply & Circulation Pathway

Portal triad

Portal veins + hepatic arteries + bile ducts travel together, branching throughout the liver.

Sinusoids

Where hepatic artery and portal vein blood mix, surrounding sheets of liver cells (hepatic plates); lined by endothelial cells and Kupffer cells (phagocytic macrophages).

Portal hypertension: Any obstruction to blood flow through the liver raises portal venous pressure proximal to the blockage — a central pathophysiologic event in many liver diseases (varices, splenomegaly, ascites, caput medusae).
Functions of the Liver

The liver simultaneously acts as a digestive, endocrine, hematologic, and excretory organ — with so much functional redundancy that more than 80% of the liver can be destroyed before life is threatened.

General Manifestations of Liver Disease

Because of the liver's huge reserve capacity, symptoms only appear once injury is significant/diffuse, or strategically placed enough to block biliary or vascular flow. Nearly every manifestation traces back to one of a few root mechanisms.

Jaundice & Bilirubin Metabolism

Researched from credible sources

Jaundice is one symptom with three completely different root causes — and figuring out which one you're looking at is mostly a matter of tracking where bilirubin gets stuck on its way out of the body.

Bilirubin's Journey, and the Three Types of Jaundice

Where bilirubin comes from

Produced from the breakdown of heme (mostly from aged red blood cells). This initial form is unconjugated (indirect) bilirubin — fat-soluble, bound to albumin in the blood, and unable to be excreted in urine.

What the liver does to it

Hepatocytes take up unconjugated bilirubin and attach glucuronic acid to it (via the enzyme glucuronosyltransferase), producing water-soluble conjugated (direct) bilirubin, which can be excreted into bile.

Mind Map — Bilirubin's Path from Blood to Bathroom

Posthepatic (obstructive)Bile flow blocked after conjugation — gallstones, strictures, pancreatic/biliary tumorsConjugated (direct) hyperbilirubinemiaDark ("cola-colored") urine + pale/clay-colored stools (loss of stercobilin)

What happens to bilirubin in the gut

Conjugated bilirubin reaching the colon is converted by bacteria into urobilinogen. About 80% becomes stercobilin, which colors stool brown. About 20% is reabsorbed into the portal circulation (enterohepatic circulation) — most goes back to the liver, a small amount is excreted by the kidneys as urobilin, contributing to urine's normal color.

Why fractionated bilirubin testing matters

Checking conjugated vs. unconjugated bilirubin (a "split bilirubin") is the key lab step for sorting prehepatic from hepatic/posthepatic causes — this, plus liver enzymes (AST/ALT/ALP) and the clinical picture, usually narrows the differential quickly.

Clinical threshold: Visible jaundice (scleral icterus, skin yellowing) generally doesn't appear until bilirubin exceeds about 3 mg/dL — levels can be creeping up on lab work well before it's visible to the eye.

Portal Hypertension

Researched from credible sources

Portal hypertension is the pressure-cooker mechanism behind most of the dramatic complications of chronic liver disease — nearly everything in the "hepatocellular failure" table earlier that mentioned portal hypertension traces back to this single problem.

Mechanism & the "ABCDE" of Complications

Definition

Elevated pressure in the portal venous system — normal portal pressure is about 5–10 mmHg; portal hypertension is generally defined as a hepatic venous pressure gradient (HVPG) above 5 mmHg, with ≥10 mmHg considered "clinically significant."

Where the blockage can be

Prehepatic (e.g., portal vein thrombosis), hepatic (by far the most common — cirrhosis of any cause), or posthepatic (e.g., right-sided heart failure, Budd-Chiari syndrome).

Two mechanisms driving the pressure up

(1) Increased resistance to flow through a fibrotic, distorted liver, and (2) increased splanchnic blood inflow from vasodilation — both push more blood against a narrower pipe.

What happens to the extra pressure

Blood reroutes around the liver through collateral vessels (portosystemic anastomoses) — this is the origin of varices, caput medusae, and hemorrhoids seen in advanced liver disease.

Mind Map — "ABCDE" of Portal Hypertension

The most dangerous complication

Acute variceal hemorrhage — typically sudden, painless, and potentially massive upper GI bleeding from ruptured esophageal or gastric varices.

Hypersplenism

Congestive splenomegaly traps blood cells, causing thrombocytopenia, leukopenia, and/or anemia — often the first clue to portal hypertension on a routine CBC.

Hepatic encephalopathy connection

Portosystemic shunting lets toxins (especially ammonia) that would normally be cleared by the liver bypass it entirely and reach the brain directly — this is a major contributor to hepatic encephalopathy in cirrhotic patients.

Treatment

Nonselective beta-blockers reduce portal pressure and lower bleeding risk; endoscopic band ligation treats active/high-risk varices. For refractory ascites or recurrent variceal bleeding, a TIPS (transjugular intrahepatic portosystemic shunt) procedure or surgical shunt can decompress the portal system. Ultimately, treating the underlying liver disease is the only way to address the root cause.

Acute Viral Hepatitis

Infection

"Viral hepatitis" usually means hepatitis A, B, or C. Hepatitis D is a defective virus that needs HBV coinfection to cause disease, and hepatitis E resembles hepatitis A but is rare in the US. Serology is essential — clinical presentation alone usually can't tell them apart.

Mind Map — Hepatitis B Serologic Markers, in order of appearance

Hepatitis A (detailed)

Transmission & course

RNA virus spread fecal-orally; 2–7 week incubation. May be asymptomatic/anicteric (especially children) or cause jaundice (majority of adults). Generally self-limited; rare fulminant/fatal cases, especially with preexisting chronic hepatitis B/C.

Icteric prodrome

Malaise, anorexia, nausea, low-grade fever, RUQ pain → jaundice lasting ~2 weeks on average. Two uncommon prolonged forms: prolonged cholestasis and relapsing hepatitis.

Diagnosis

Anti-HAV IgG = past infection/vaccination (immunity). Anti-HAV IgM = acute infection. Highly reliable within weeks of exposure.

Treatment

No specific treatment changes the course — supportive care (rest, nutrition), avoid alcohol/acetaminophen/other hepatotoxins.

Active immunization

Recommended for all children age 12 months–15 years, and adults in risk groups (travelers, chronic hep B/C, institutional residents, certain sexual practices). IM dose + booster at 6–12 months → lifelong immunity in ≥98%. Effective even with advanced chronic liver disease. Added to routine childhood schedule by ACIP/CDC in 2006.

Passive immunization

For anti-HAV-negative exposed persons: pooled human immunoglobulin, given IM within 2 weeks of exposure (plus active vaccination). Passive immunity lasts 4–6 months and doesn't reduce vaccine effectiveness.

Hepatitis B (In Depth)

Researched from credible sources
Chronic Infection, Phases & Treatment

Why HBV matters long-term

Unlike hepatitis A, HBV can become chronic (5–10% of adult infections, but much higher — up to 90% — if acquired at birth or in early childhood). Chronic HBV is a leading global cause of cirrhosis and hepatocellular carcinoma.

Reading the serology over time

HBeAg positive = actively replicating virus, high infectivity. Seroconversion to anti-HBe = viral replication has typically slowed, generally a favorable sign. HBsAg loss + anti-HBs appearance = functional cure/resolved infection.

Who needs treatment

Not every chronic HBV carrier needs antiviral therapy — decisions are based on viral load, ALT levels, and evidence of liver damage (fibrosis/cirrhosis) rather than HBsAg positivity alone.

Treatment options

Oral nucleos(t)ide analogs (e.g., tenofovir, entecavir) suppress viral replication long-term with a high resistance barrier; pegylated interferon is an alternative with a finite treatment course. Treatment usually suppresses rather than cures the virus — lifelong therapy is often needed.

Prevention remains the biggest win: the HBV vaccine (part of routine childhood immunization) is highly effective, and this is why chronic HBV is becoming rarer in vaccinated populations even as it remains a major burden globally.

Hepatitis C (In Depth)

Researched from credible sources
The Chronicity Problem, and Why Treatment Changed Everything

The defining feature

More than 80% of hepatitis C infections become chronic — by far the highest chronicity rate of any hepatitis virus — making it a leading cause of cirrhosis, liver failure, and hepatocellular carcinoma worldwide.

Why it went undiagnosed for so long

Acute HCV infection is frequently asymptomatic or produces only mild, nonspecific symptoms, so most people don't know they're infected until years later when liver damage is already underway.

Direct-acting antivirals (DAAs) — the game changer

Modern oral DAA regimens (targeting HCV proteins directly) cure over 90–95% of patients in just 8–12 weeks, with far fewer side effects than the older interferon-based regimens. This is one of modern medicine's clearest treatment success stories — HCV went from a chronic, often progressive disease to a functionally curable one.

What "cured" means

Success is measured as sustained virologic response (SVR) — undetectable HCV RNA 12 weeks after finishing treatment. SVR is considered a virologic cure.

Cure doesn't erase existing damage: patients who achieve SVR after developing cirrhosis still need ongoing HCC surveillance — clearing the virus stops new damage but doesn't reverse fibrosis that's already there.

Hepatitis D & E (In Depth)

Researched from credible sources
The Two Less Common Hepatitis Viruses

Hepatitis D — the "why" behind its severity

HDV is a defective virus that can only replicate using HBV's surface antigen — it literally cannot infect someone without HBV present. Coinfection (getting both at once) tends to resolve like typical acute hepatitis. Superinfection (HDV landing on top of existing chronic HBV) is much more dangerous — high rates of progression to chronic disease and a notably higher risk of fulminant hepatic failure than HBV alone.

Hepatitis E — usually mild, with one major exception

Fecal-oral transmission, generally self-limited like hepatitis A. The major exception: HEV infection during pregnancy carries a substantially elevated mortality risk, particularly in the third trimester — a detail worth remembering since it doesn't fit the "usually mild" pattern of the rest of the disease.

Neither HDV nor HEV has a dedicated approved antiviral treatment in widespread use — management for both remains largely supportive, which is exactly why preventing HBV (and thereby preventing HDV) through vaccination is so valuable.

Cirrhosis

End-stage liver injury

Cirrhosis is the shared final pathway for many kinds of chronic liver injury — chronic hepatitis, NASH, metal storage diseases, alcoholic liver disease, and toxic hepatitis all converge here: diffuse fibrosis surrounding regenerative nodules, with permanently altered blood flow and function.

Mind Map — Causes of Biliary Cirrhosis

Primary Biliary Cholangitis (PBC)

What it is

The prototypical small-duct biliary obstruction, from continuous inflammation of the bile ducts.

Diagnosis

Usually positive antinuclear and antimitochondrial antibodies. With typical antibody results in the right clinical setting, liver biopsy may not be necessary.

Treatment

Ursodeoxycholic acid (UDCA/ursodiol) is the only approved treatment and may delay progression to end-stage disease. Methotrexate/colchicine sometimes added; obeticholic acid and bezafibrate are newer investigational options for noncirrhotic patients. Supportive care includes fat-soluble vitamin supplementation.

Prognosis

Most patients eventually progress despite treatment; liver transplantation is usually the only route to prolonged survival.

Biliary Flukes

Geography

Common in Russia, Japan, and Southeast Asia; immigrants from endemic areas remain at risk.

Life cycle & transmission

Opisthorchis and Clonorchis (Asia): acquired by eating raw fish carrying larval cysts. Fasciola hepatica (worldwide sheep/cattle areas): acquired by eating fecally contaminated watercress/aquatic plants.

Diagnosis

Serial stool exams for parasitic ova; serologic testing often helpful.

Treatment

Antiparasitic agents (praziquantel, mebendazole, albendazole) eliminate live worms, but do not necessarily prevent recurrent cholangitis and other consequences.

Primary Sclerosing Cholangitis (PSC)

Association

Autoimmune large-duct disease; 80% of PSC patients have coexistent ulcerative colitis, while only 3–5% of UC patients develop PSC.

Manifestations

Recurrent cholangitis episodes with progressive biliary scarring/obstruction; bacterial sepsis can accompany bouts and is a major cause of morbidity/mortality.

Diagnosis

Majority have positive perinuclear ANCA (not pathognomonic). Primary diagnosis via ERCP/MRCP showing a "beaded and atrophic" biliary tree; biopsy sometimes done for staging or suspected small-duct PSC.

Treatment

UDCA often used but without strong proof of benefit. Immunosuppressants and anti-TNF therapy haven't shown clear efficacy. Endoscopic stricture relief is palliative only — liver transplantation is the only truly effective option.

Cancer risk: Recurrent cholangitis of any cause leads to cirrhosis, and PSC patients are predisposed to cholangiocarcinoma.

Alcoholic Liver Disease

Spectrum of injury

Alcoholic liver disease progresses through overlapping stages — fatty liver, hepatitis, and cirrhosis can each occur alone or together in the same patient.

Fatty Liver & Steatohepatitis (Alcoholic and NASH)

Fatty liver

Fat accumulates in liver cells because more fat is delivered than can be metabolized, or from a cellular fat-metabolism defect. Asymptomatic, no inflammation.

Steatohepatitis

Fatty liver plus significant inflammation. Commonly alcohol-related, but can also result from diabetes, obesity, protein malnutrition, TPN, or drugs — in which case it's called NASH (nonalcoholic steatohepatitis).

Findings

Usually mild/asymptomatic, often found incidentally on imaging or biopsy. Mildly abnormal transaminases; hypertriglyceridemia common (occasionally >1000 mg/dL). Occasionally: liver enlargement, abdominal discomfort, portal hypertension.

Treatment

Alcoholic: stop alcohol + appropriate nutrition. NASH: weight loss, aggressive diabetes/hyperlipidemia control; vitamin E shows possible benefit.

Progression risk: An estimated 3–15% of untreated NASH patients eventually develop progressive fibrosis and cirrhosis.
Alcoholic Hepatitis

Pathology

Active inflammation of the centrilobular liver region — hepatocyte necrosis, neutrophilic infiltration, and Mallory bodies (intracellular inclusions). Often follows a binge well above a chronic alcoholic's usual intake.

Severity range

Mild to very severe. Worst cases: hepatomegaly, fever, acute liver failure, encephalopathy — plus possible complication by acute alcohol withdrawal/delirium tremens. Mortality up to 33% in severe cases.

Diagnosis

History of heavy alcohol use + labs. AST markedly higher than ALT strongly suggests a toxic (not viral) cause. Also check viral serologies, acetaminophen level, ceruloplasmin/ferritin to rule out other causes.

Treatment

Nutrition is key: thiamine 100 mg/day + multivitamin routinely; check/replenish B12 and folate. Elevated PT/INR → subcutaneous vitamin K. Corticosteroids (prednisolone) for severe disease with declining function/coma — mild-moderate cases don't benefit and get supportive care only. Pentoxifylline is an alternative if steroids can't be used (efficacy unproven).

Toxic & Metal Storage Liver Disorders

Iron, copper, drugs

Two genetic metal-overload diseases — hemochromatosis (iron) and Wilson disease (copper) — cause liver damage through excess metal accumulation. Acetaminophen overdose is the classic toxic/drug cause worth knowing in depth.

Mind Map — Hemochromatosis vs. Wilson Disease

Hereditary Hemochromatosis (HH)

Genetics

Autosomal recessive; mutant HFE gene (usually C282Y) causes uncontrolled GI iron absorption. In European populations ~1 in 10 are carriers, 0.5% homozygous. US homozygosity: 0.44% White, 0.027% Hispanic, 0.014% African American; very low in Asians.

Penetrance

Despite high gene prevalence, clinical disease occurs in less than 1% of homozygotes — incomplete penetrance plus environmental factors.

Iron burden

Advanced disease may involve 20 g+ of body iron (vs. normal ~1 g in males, 0.3 g in females), depositing mainly in liver, pancreas, and heart.

Sex difference

Much less common in females (1:5 to 1:10 female:male ratio) due to menstrual iron loss and possibly endocrine factors.

Secondary hemochromatosis

From large iron ingestion or repeated transfusions (e.g., sickle cell disease, thalassemia, sideroblastic anemias). Historical example: Bantu siderosis (beer fermented in iron pots).

Manifestations

Liver usually affected first (hepatomegaly, ↑ enzymes). Also: diabetes, hyperpigmentation/bronze skin, polyarthritis, hypogonadism, heart failure. Advanced disease → fibrosis/macronodular cirrhosis (major cause of death). Splenomegaly common; portal hypertension less common than other liver diseases.

Cancer risk

HCC develops in ~30% of hemochromatosis patients — exclusively in those with cirrhosis. Early diagnosis and treatment are critical.

Diagnosis

↑ plasma iron and transferrin saturation; serum ferritin dramatically elevated (often several thousand μg/L; normal 10–200 μg/L). Confirmed by HFE genetic analysis. Biopsy in select cases shows periportal iron deposition. Should prompt family screening.

Treatment

Reserved for ferritin >1000 μg/L (asymptomatic low-iron-load patients have near-normal life span). Mainstay: weekly phlebotomy of 500 mL until hematocrit <37%, then maintenance phlebotomy every 2–3 months. Alternative: deferoxamine (iron chelator), less efficient, needs adequate renal function.

Prognosis

Excellent if caught early (prevents heart/liver disease), but diabetes may still develop, and iron removal doesn't reverse hypogonadism or arthritis. Transplant available for irreversible cirrhosis without disqualifying heart involvement.

Wilson Disease (Hepatolenticular Degeneration)

Genetics

Rare autosomal recessive disorder; mutation in the ATP7B gene → copper retention in the liver + impaired incorporation into ceruloplasmin. Most patients are compound heterozygotes.

Presentation age

Any time before age 30, with hepatic dysfunction or neuropsychiatric illness.

Hepatic manifestations

More common in children — hepatomegaly, fatty infiltration, ↑ liver enzymes. Can range from acute hepatitis progressing to fulminant failure, to insidious macronodular cirrhosis with portal hypertension.

Neurologic manifestations

Movement disorder or rigid dystonia, sometimes primarily psychiatric (e.g., acute psychosis). Patients presenting neuropsychiatrically virtually always have occult compensated cirrhosis already.

Other manifestations

Renal tubular acidosis (Fanconi-like syndrome), cardiomyopathy, hypogonadism, vitamin D-resistant rickets, arthritis.

Diagnosis

Kayser-Fleischer rings on slit-lamp exam (absence doesn't exclude diagnosis) + low ceruloplasmin + elevated 24-hr urinary copper = diagnostic combination. Penicillamine-challenge urinary copper helps ambiguous cases. Liver biopsy for copper is technically demanding, not routine. Genetic screening for relatives when the mutation is known.

Treatment — diet

Eliminate copper-rich foods (organ meats, shellfish, nuts, chocolate, mushrooms); filter high-copper water.

Treatment — chelation

Trientine is the preferred oral chelator (fewer side effects than D-penicillamine, the older option). Zinc is another alternative. Side effects of trientine: mild anemia, gastritis, transient neurologic worsening.

Good news: Early treatment produces marked improvement and protects against liver/neurologic disease — even advanced cases can see some functional recovery. Treatment is lifelong; noncompliance leads to definite progression. Ammonium tetrathiomolybdate is sometimes used for severe neurologic disease (doesn't cause the early neurologic worsening seen with penicillamine). Liver transplant has a limited role for end-stage disease.
Acetaminophen Poisoning

Acetaminophen overdose caused 39% of acute hepatic failure cases in a US multicenter study — the single most important toxic cause of liver failure to know cold.

Mind Map — Acetaminophen Toxicity Pathway

Toxic dose

Acute ingestion of at least 140 mg/kg body weight can overwhelm glutathione conjugation and expose the liver to toxic NAPQI, causing hepatic necrosis.

Who's at risk from smaller repeated doses

Children, the elderly, patients with preexisting liver disease, people who abuse alcohol, and those taking other hepatotoxic drugs.

Safe levels

Significant liver damage is rare if serum levels are below ~150 μg/mL at 4 hours and ~37 μg/mL at 12 hours post-ingestion — but clinicians should reference a published nomogram and monitor serial levels rather than rely on rules of thumb.

Screen for co-ingestion

Should also screen for other hepatotoxic drugs, such as tricyclic antidepressants.

Fulminant Hepatic Failure

Researched from credible sources

Where cirrhosis is liver failure that takes years to arrive, fulminant hepatic failure is the opposite — a previously healthy liver collapsing within days to weeks. It's rare, but it's one of the few true liver emergencies.

Definition, Causes & Management

Defining criteria

Rapid-onset hepatic encephalopathy plus severe coagulopathy (elevated INR), developing within roughly 8 weeks of the first symptoms, in a person with no preexisting chronic liver disease. Also called acute liver failure (ALF).

Most common causes

Acetaminophen overdose is the single leading cause in the US (see the Acetaminophen Poisoning section above). Other major causes: viral hepatitis (especially B, and A in susceptible patients), other drug reactions, autoimmune hepatitis, Wilson disease, and acute fatty liver of pregnancy.

Why encephalopathy is the hallmark

Severe coagulopathy often actually appears *before* encephalopathy develops — clinicians watch INR closely as an early warning sign, since the transition to hepatic encephalopathy marks a sharp turn toward a much more dangerous course.

Cerebral edema

A dangerous complication in severe cases — brain swelling from the same toxin buildup (especially ammonia) driving the encephalopathy, contributing to raised intracranial pressure.

Timing determines urgency: Rising INR above 2.0, or the onset of any grade of hepatic encephalopathy, should prompt transfer to a center capable of liver transplantation — waiting until the patient is unstable makes safe transfer far more dangerous.

Cause-specific treatment

N-acetylcysteine for acetaminophen toxicity; antivirals for HBV; penicillin for Amanita mushroom poisoning; immediate delivery for acute fatty liver of pregnancy; chelation therapy for Wilson disease. Identifying the cause quickly matters because some of these have specific, time-sensitive antidotes.

General management & prognosis

Intensive care support (often including management of cerebral edema, coagulopathy, and infection risk) while awaiting either spontaneous recovery or transplant. Emergency liver transplantation is the only definitive treatment when the liver won't recover on its own — survival has improved substantially with modern critical care, but this remains a life-threatening condition.

Hepatocellular Carcinoma (HCC)

Researched from credible sources

HCC is the endpoint many chronic liver diseases in this guide are quietly building toward — which is exactly why "surveillance" is such a recurring word in liver disease management.

Risk Factors, Surveillance & Treatment

The dominant risk factors

Chronic hepatitis B is the leading global risk factor for HCC (it can cause HCC even without cirrhosis being present first). Chronic hepatitis C and cirrhosis of any cause (alcohol, NASH, hemochromatosis, autoimmune, etc.) are the other major drivers.

Other contributing factors

Alcohol use, obesity, and diabetes/NASH are increasingly important risk factors as viral hepatitis rates fall in some populations — this shift explains why HCC incidence hasn't dropped as much as hepatitis vaccination/treatment success might suggest.

Who gets screened

All patients with cirrhosis, regardless of cause, plus certain chronic HBV carriers even without cirrhosis (e.g., older patients, those with a family history of HCC). This ties directly back to the risk factors above — surveillance follows the risk, not just a diagnosis of "liver disease" in general.

Surveillance method

Abdominal ultrasound every 6 months, often paired with serum AFP (alpha-fetoprotein). Neither test alone is perfect — ultrasound sensitivity for early HCC is only moderate, and AFP can be falsely elevated by inflammation or pregnancy — but together they catch meaningfully more early-stage cancers than either alone.

Why early detection matters so much

HCC found early (small, localized, in a patient with preserved liver function) can potentially be cured with resection, ablation, or transplant. Found late, treatment options narrow considerably — this is the entire rationale behind biannual surveillance in at-risk patients.

Treatment by stage

Early/localized: surgical resection, liver transplantation, or local ablation (e.g., radiofrequency ablation) — transplant has the added benefit of treating both the tumor and the underlying cirrhosis at once. Intermediate stage: transarterial chemoembolization (TACE) to cut off the tumor's blood supply. Advanced/metastatic: systemic therapy (targeted agents, immunotherapy).

The throughline for this whole guide: nearly every chronic liver condition covered here — hepatitis B/C, alcoholic cirrhosis, hemochromatosis, Wilson disease, biliary cirrhosis, NASH — converges on the same two endpoints: cirrhosis and, downstream of that, HCC risk. That's the real reason so much of hepatology is about surveillance and early intervention rather than waiting for symptoms.

Liver Disease in Infants & Children

Pediatric

Pediatric liver disease includes childhood versions of adult diseases plus conditions unique to this age group — many present at birth or shortly after.

Neonatal Jaundice & Kernicterus

Physiologic jaundice

Harmless, lasts ≤2 weeks; caused by immature bilirubin conjugation/transport plus increased gut reabsorption of bilirubin.

When it's NOT physiologic

Bilirubin >5 mg/dL on day 1, >10 mg/dL on day 2, or >13 mg/dL at any time — should trigger evaluation for hemolytic disorders, Crigler-Najjar syndrome, hypothyroidism, congenital pyloric stenosis, sepsis, resorbing hematomas, etc.

Breastfeeding link

Breast-fed babies have higher hyperbilirubinemia rates — β-glucuronidase in breast milk increases unconjugated bilirubin reabsorption in the gut. Breastfeeding can be paused temporarily if significant hyperbilirubinemia occurs.

Kernicterus

Brain injury from hyperbilirubinemia — immature blood-brain barrier lets free unconjugated bilirubin enter the brain. Yellow staining of damaged tissue occurs mainly in the basal ganglia and thalamus. Most likely mechanism: premature apoptosis. Most affected infants die; survivors often have cerebral palsy, movement disorders, intellectual disability.

Risk factors for kernicterus

Premature birth, neonatal jaundice, especially hemolytic disease of the newborn. Drugs that displace bilirubin from albumin make it worse.

Treatment

Phototherapy ("bili-lights," 450-nm wavelength) converts unconjugated lipid-soluble bilirubin into water-soluble photoisomers excretable by the kidneys. Less commonly: phenobarbital (↑ UDPGT) or exchange transfusion. Early recognition generally prevents catastrophic outcomes.

Infectious Hepatitis in Children

Hepatitis A

Usually mild/asymptomatic in children; prevalence inversely correlates with sanitation quality. Supportive treatment; universal vaccination recommended after age 1.

Hepatitis B

Common childhood disease globally; vertical transmission from HBsAg-positive mothers is the most common route. Children show more immune-complex features (arthritis, fever, papular acrodermatitis — a rash not seen in adults — renal disease, hematologic complications). Chronic infection risk is much higher after neonatal/childhood infection → higher long-term cirrhosis/HCC risk.

Hepatitis B prevention

HBIG (passive) within 12 hours of birth for infants of HBsAg-positive mothers. Active HBV vaccine series at birth, 1 month, and 6 months. Same prophylaxis for children of high-risk/unscreened mothers or other HBV exposure. Universal childhood HBV vaccination recommended by the US Public Health Service since 1991.

Hepatitis C, D, E

HCV: less commonly spread vertically; blood product screening has reduced childhood risk. HDV: can spread intrafamily as an HBV coinfector. HEV: especially virulent in adolescents/young adults, high mortality in pregnant females; the most common cause of childhood hepatitis in endemic regions.

Other pediatric hepatitis causes: Systemic viruses (EBV, CMV, herpesvirus, adenovirus) can cause biochemical/clinical hepatitis. Neonatal hepatitis is part of the TORCH syndrome (CMV, herpesvirus, varicella, Toxoplasma, syphilis) — encephalitis/retinitis may accompany with lifelong effects. Hepatomegaly + elevated transaminases in a newborn should prompt a congenital infection workup.
Reye Syndrome

Setting

Primarily a childhood disease (rare adult cases); occurs shortly after a viral illness like influenza or chickenpox.

Presentation

Nausea and vomiting rapidly progressing to coma. Significant hepatocyte mitochondrial dysfunction (exact mechanism unknown). Fatty liver infiltration with severe dysfunction: encephalopathy, coagulopathy, elevated hepatocellular enzymes. Mortality up to 40%.

Aspirin link

Strongly associated with aspirin use during the preceding viral illness (though other drugs may contribute) — this is why aspirin is contraindicated for childhood viral illness.

Treatment

Supportive; if the child survives, recovery is generally complete. Liver transplant reserved for irreversible disease.

Congenital & Metabolic Liver Disease

α1-Antitrypsin deficiency

Autosomal recessive; α1-antitrypsin normally inhibits enzymes like elastase/collagenase from damaging tissue. The PiZZ variant (α1-ATZ protein) is the most common pathologic form, causing both liver and lung disease (centrilobular emphysema, pancreatic insufficiency, cirrhosis). Defective protein accumulates in the liver as diagnostic granules.

α1-Antitrypsin — treatment

Pooled human α1-antitrypsin infusion for adults >18 with severely low levels; lung transplant for end-stage lung disease; liver transplant often precluded by coexisting lung/other issues; gene therapy under active investigation.

Cystic fibrosis

Autosomal recessive CFTR gene mutation (chromosome 7); needs mutations in both copies. 1 in 3,000 White births, 1 in 15,000 African American births. Besides lung disease: pancreatic insufficiency, intestinal obstruction, gallstones, neonatal giant cell hepatitis, bile duct obstruction, biliary cirrhosis.

Cystic fibrosis — treatment

CFTR modulators and inhaled DNase (genotype-dependent agent selection); vaccinations and prophylactic antibiotics to prevent complications.

Wilson disease and hemochromatosis are also single-gene liver diseases relevant here — see their dedicated sections above.

Cerebrotendinous xanthomatosis

A steroid hydroxylase deficiency causing premature atherosclerosis and encephalopathy — generally does NOT include liver disease. Chenodeoxycholic acid treatment shows marked improvement in children.

X-linked adrenoleukodystrophy (ALD)

A peroxisomopathy (peroxisomes handle final β-oxidation steps in bile acid synthesis) causing progressive neurologic dysfunction and adrenal insufficiency. Hematopoietic cell transplant is emerging as treatment of choice in early stages; "Lorenzo's oil" (glycerol trioleate + trierucate) is a controversial dietary therapy.

General principle for inborn errors of metabolism: Neonatal-onset inborn errors are usually fatal without immediate treatment; those presenting later may respond to specific therapy. Liver/bone marrow transplant can help select conditions. Diagnosis should always prompt family history investigation and genetic counseling.
Disorders of Bilirubin & Bile Acid Transport

Mind Map — Bilirubin/Bile Acid Disorders by Severity

Gilbert syndrome

Very common (~10% of US White population), entirely benign, autosomal dominant; mild unconjugated hyperbilirubinemia from decreased bilirubin glucuronidation. Important to recognize so patients aren't over-evaluated.

Crigler-Najjar syndrome

Rare autosomal recessive. Type I: near-total absence of conjugation → high unconjugated bilirubin crosses the immature BBB → usually fatal (kernicterus) or causes irreversible damage; rare lifesaving cases treated with transplant after phototherapy/plasma exchange. Type II: some conjugating capacity remains, enhanced by phenobarbital; rarely causes encephalopathy — patients can live normally.

Progressive familial intrahepatic cholestasis (PFIC)

Rare autosomal recessive bile salt excretion defect → severe jaundice, pruritus, malabsorption. PFIC type I (Byler syndrome) traces to a single-gene mutation in an Amish kindred. UDCA helps some children; biliary diversion surgery can relieve symptoms by reducing the bile acid pool. Once uniformly fatal, but liver transplant can now normalize bile acid synthesis and growth in select patients.

Cerebrotendinous xanthomatosis

A bile acid synthesis defect (steroid hydroxylase deficiency) — distinct from the transport defects above — causing premature atherosclerosis/encephalopathy but generally not liver disease.

Other rare, generally fatal-in-infancy bile transport disorders exist (North American Indian childhood cirrhosis, Aagenaes/cholestasis-lymphedema syndrome) — all linked to single-gene mutations under active molecular investigation.
Intrahepatic & Extrahepatic Cholestatic Conditions

Definition

Congenital intrahepatic cholestasis = cholestatic disease confined to the liver, with a normal extrahepatic biliary system.

Neonatal hepatitis

One cause of intrahepatic cholestasis — can be idiopathic, viral, or secondary to an inborn error of metabolism.

Alagille syndrome

The most common inherited intrahepatic cholestasis — autosomal dominant, incomplete penetrance/expressivity. Paucity of intrahepatic bile ducts plus bony and cardiovascular malformations. Slowly progressive; untreated may cause pruritus, hypercholesterolemia, xanthomas, and vitamin E deficiency neurologic complications. UDCA as a bridge; liver transplant is the current treatment of choice.

Biliary atresia (extrahepatic ductopenia)

Congenital or acquired (autoimmune, or chronic transplant rejection). Common birth defect: 1:10,000–1:15,000 live births — more common than intrahepatic cholestatic conditions. Progressive cholestasis: pruritus, malabsorption with growth retardation, fat-soluble vitamin deficiency, hyperlipidemia, eventual cirrhosis with portal hypertension; some children get recurrent bacterial cholangitis.

Diagnosis

Distinguishing biliary atresia from idiopathic neonatal hepatitis can be tricky, but liver biopsy findings are usually characteristic. Cholangiogram assesses for a correctable obstruction.

Treatment — Kasai procedure

Creates a hepatoportoenteric connection allowing bile drainage. Not usually curative, but "buys time" for the child to grow until liver transplant is appropriate.

Liver Disease in Older Adults

Baseline aging changes

Liver size and blood flow decrease with age, but this has little functional significance. Drugs metabolized via hepatic blood flow or the cytochrome (mixed-function oxidase) system may have prolonged half-life, requiring dose adjustment. Routine liver enzyme tests are not changed by aging itself.

Hepatocellular carcinoma (HCC)

In the US, usually results from years of alcohol or chronic viral hepatitis injury — more common in older people. Prognosis is dismal.

Ischemic hepatitis

Associated with underlying cardiovascular disease and hypotension episodes (surgery, sepsis) — more common in older patients. Rapid rise in transaminases, prolonged PT. Recovery can be rapid; prognosis depends on the underlying disorder. Rarely a cause of death by itself.

Right heart failure

Can cause passive hepatic congestion with ascites and (rarely) liver failure. In developing countries, constrictive pericarditis and uncorrected rheumatic valvular disease remain significant causes of intractable ascites — worth considering in the differential.

Metabolic liver disease

Rarely presents for the first time in geriatric patients. Hemochromatosis in females often surfaces after menopause (new-onset diabetes, heart failure, arthritis, cirrhosis, or HCC).

Autoimmune liver disease

Typically seen in young-to-middle-aged people, but autoimmune chronic active hepatitis can cause "cryptogenic" cirrhosis in older females. PBC is uncommon late in life. PSC can affect older patients with long-standing ulcerative colitis, even years/decades after colectomy.

Alcohol-related disease

Because alcohol abuse typically starts early in life, older patients carry the cumulative injury of years of exposure.
