Key Terms & Definitions

Start here
Nociception
The physiologic mechanisms involved in the pain phenomenon; divided into four stages — transduction, transmission, perception, modulation.
Nociceptor
A free nerve ending that transduces noxious (painful) stimuli into neuronal action potentials.
Transduction
Converting a painful stimulus into a neuronal action potential at the sensory receptor.
Transmission
Movement of action potentials along neurons from the peripheral receptor to the spinal cord and then to the brain.
Perception
What occurs when the brain receives pain signals and interprets them as painful.
Modulation
The complex mechanism by which synaptic transmission of pain signals is altered — can enhance or suppress the pain signal.
Prostaglandins
Inflammatory chemical mediators formed from arachidonic acid via cyclooxygenase; sensitize nociceptors, lowering the pain threshold.
Cyclooxygenase (COX)
The enzyme that converts arachidonic acid into prostaglandins; blocked by NSAIDs.
Aδ fibers
Myelinated, fast-conducting sensory fibers carrying sharp, stinging, well-localized pain.
C fibers
Unmyelinated, slow-conducting sensory fibers carrying dull, aching, poorly-localized pain; make up the majority of pain fibers.
Dorsal root ganglion
Location of the cell bodies of primary sensory pain neurons, just outside the spinal cord.
Tract of Lissauer
The pathway by which afferent pain fibers spread up and down 2–3 spinal segments after entering the dorsal horn.
Substantia gelatinosa
Laminae II and III of the dorsal horn — a key region of multiple synaptic connections where pain signals can be modulated (enhanced or blocked).
Lamina V
A spinal cord region receiving convergent input from both somatic and visceral pain fibers — thought to explain referred pain.
Referred pain
Pain from a visceral organ that is perceived at the body surface, from convergence of visceral and somatic afferents in lamina V.
Substance P
A key neurotransmitter/neuropeptide involved in synaptic pain transmission in the spinal cord.
Glutamate
The excitatory neurotransmitter carrying the nociceptive message from primary afferent fibers to secondary neurons; binds NMDA receptors.
NMDA receptor
A postsynaptic glutamate receptor whose activation is thought to induce "synaptic memory" of pain signals.
Wind-up
Sensitization of spinal cord neurons from excessive/repeated C-fiber stimulation, so even mild stimuli become painful — a possible mechanism in chronic pain development.
Spinothalamic tract
The main ascending pathway carrying pain/temperature signals from the spinal cord to the thalamus; divided into lateral (pain/temperature) and anterior (crude touch) divisions.
Lateral spinothalamic tract
Carries precisely-localized pain/temperature signals to the ventral posterolateral (VPL) thalamic nucleus, then to the primary somatosensory cortex.
Anterolateral (paleospinothalamic) system
Carries poorly-localized pain to intralaminar thalamic nuclei, responsible for the emotional/affective aspects of pain rather than precise location.
Somatosensory cortex
The primary cortical region (postcentral gyrus) that processes the sensory-discriminative qualities of pain — location, quality, intensity.
Gate control theory
Melzack and Wall's 1965 theory that a "gate" mechanism in the spinal dorsal horn can be closed by non-painful sensory input or descending brain signals, reducing pain transmission.
Periaqueductal gray (PAG)
A midbrain region central to descending pain inhibition; activated by the cortex to suppress incoming pain signals at the dorsal horn.
Rostroventromedial medulla (RVM)
Receives signals from the PAG and sends output down the spinal cord to activate endogenous opioid release, suppressing pain transmission.
Endogenous opioids
The body's natural pain-relieving peptides — β-endorphins, enkephalins, and dynorphins — acting at μ, δ, and κ receptors.
Nociceptive pain
Pain from actual/threatened tissue damage activating nociceptors; subdivided into somatic (sharp, localized) and visceral (dull, diffuse) pain.
Neuropathic pain
Pain from a lesion or disease of the somatosensory nervous system itself; described as burning, shooting, or electric-shock-like.
Nociplastic pain
Pain from altered nociceptive processing/sensitization without clear evidence of tissue or nerve damage (e.g., fibromyalgia); the newest IASP pain category (2017).
Acute pain
Pain from a specific injury/disease with a predictable trajectory, typically resolving within days to a few months as healing occurs.
Chronic pain
Pain persisting beyond the expected healing time, generally defined as lasting 3 months or longer; often involves centralized/maladaptive nervous system changes.
Numeric Rating Scale (NRS)
A 0–10 self-report pain intensity scale — the most common adult pain assessment tool.
FLACC scale
An observational pain scale (Face, Legs, Activity, Cry, Consolability) for infants/young children or patients unable to self-report.
PQRST
A pain assessment mnemonic: Provocation/palliation, Quality, Region/radiation, Severity, Timing.
WHO analgesic ladder
A stepwise pain management framework moving from non-opioids, to weak opioids, to strong opioids as pain severity increases.
Defining Pain

Why it's hard to define

Pain is a subjective experience, which makes defining and assessing it genuinely difficult — there's no objective external measure.

Two classic definitions

The International Association for the Study of Pain frames it as an unpleasant sensory and emotional experience tied to actual or potential tissue damage. McCaffery's clinically useful framing: pain is whatever the person experiencing it says it is, whenever they say it's happening.

"The fifth vital sign": because unrelieved pain is physiologically harmful (affecting recovery rate, healthcare costs, and postoperative morbidity), pain assessment/management now has formal accreditation standards (The Joint Commission) — it's treated as a core vital sign, not an optional add-on.
Nociception — the Four-Stage Overview

It's clinically useful to break pain physiology into four stages, because each stage is a potential point of intervention — this is the organizing idea behind the whole topic.

Mind Map — The Four Stages of Nociception

Transduction

Where it happens

Most pain begins in the periphery when nociceptors (free nerve endings) are stimulated. Found in skin, muscle, connective tissue, the circulatory system, and abdominal/pelvic/thoracic viscera.

How stimulation occurs

Either direct damage to nerve endings, or release of chemicals at the site of injury.

Chemical mediators of pain

K+, H+, lactate, histamine, serotonin, bradykinins, acetylcholine, substance P, and prostaglandins. Some are released directly from tissue injury; others are produced as part of the inflammatory response.

The mechanism

These chemicals alter the membrane potential of the pain receptor — if depolarization is sufficient, action potentials are generated, and transmission (stage 2) begins.

Prostaglandins & NSAIDs — Why This Mechanism Matters Clinically

This is the pathway that explains why NSAIDs work — one of the most clinically relevant mechanisms in this whole section.

Mind Map — Prostaglandin Formation & the NSAID Target

What prostaglandins do

Sensitize (lower the threshold of) nociceptive fibers — stimuli that wouldn't normally cause pain start producing it.

How NSAIDs work

NSAIDs (including aspirin) prevent prostaglandin production by inhibiting cyclooxygenase — blocking the enzyme, not the initial tissue injury itself.

Transmission — Aδ vs. C Fibers

The two primary sensory fiber types carrying nociceptive impulses have essentially opposite characteristics — speed vs. proportion, sharp vs. dull. This contrast is the single most testable fact in the transmission stage.

FeatureAδ FibersC Fibers
StructureMyelinatedUnmyelinated
Proportion of pain fibers~10%~90% (majority)
Stimulus sourceThermal, mechanicalPolymodal (mechanical, thermal, chemical)
SpeedFast, 5–10 m/secSlow, 0.6–2 m/sec
Pain qualitySharp, stinging, cutting, pinchingDull, burning, aching

Aδ fibers — the "first pain"

Fast-traveling; give the initial sharp, highly-localized response to tissue injury.

C fibers — the "second pain"

Slower, poorly-localized, dull/aching quality that lingers after the initial sharp pain fades. These project to brain areas evoking emotional responses like displeasure and anxiety — this is why most pain "feels bad" emotionally, not just physically.

The Spinal Cord Pathway — Where Pain Signals Get Processed

Mind Map — Pain Signal Entry & Routing Through the Dorsal Horn

Lamina I

Receives excitatory input from Aδ/C fibers carrying cutaneous pain from small, localized skin areas; many of these neurons cross the cord to activate the anterolateral tract.

Laminae II-III (substantia gelatinosa)

A hub of multiple synaptic connections — this is where pain transmission can be modulated by other sensory input or CNS activity, either enhancing or blocking the signal.

Lamina V — referred pain

Receives both somatic (mechanical/thermal/chemical) and visceral afferent input. This convergence is the likely explanation for referred pain — why pain from an internal organ is felt at a surface location.

Laminae VI-VIII

Receive sensory input from muscles, joints, and visceral afferent fibers.

Why the tract of Lissauer matters clinically: because afferent fibers spread up/down 2-3 spinal segments before synapsing, it explains why reflex postural adjustments (like withdrawing a painful body part) can involve muscles at nearby, not just the exact same, spinal level.
Glutamate, NMDA Receptors & Wind-Up

Neurotransmitters at play

Substance P, excitatory amino acids (glutamate), GABA, cholecystokinin, and calcitonin gene-related peptide all participate in spinal cord synaptic pain transmission.

Glutamate's specific role

Carries the nociceptive message from primary afferent fibers to secondary neurons, binding NMDA receptors on the postsynaptic neuron.

Wind-up: NMDA receptor activation is thought to create a kind of "synaptic memory" of pain. Excessive or repeated C-fiber stimulation sensitizes spinal cord neurons so that even mild stimulation becomes painful — this phenomenon is called wind-up, and it's considered an important mechanism in how chronic pain syndromes develop.

Clinical implication

Drugs that inhibit glutamate production may impede the wind-up response — meaning pain can potentially be controlled before this synaptic memory develops, which is part of the rationale for treating pain early and proactively rather than waiting until it's severe.

Stage 3 — How the Brain Turns a Signal Into "Pain"

Once transmission carries the nociceptive signal up the spinal cord, it has to actually reach — and be processed by — the brain before anyone "feels" pain. This stage is carried almost entirely by one structure: the spinothalamic tract.

Mind Map — The Spinothalamic Tract, Periphery to Cortex

The lateral division — "where and what"

Small-receptive-field neurons project to the ventral posterolateral (VPL) thalamic nucleus, then to the primary somatosensory cortex — this is what allows precise localization and discrimination of a painful stimulus.

The medial division — "how bad and how upsetting"

Neurons with large, complex receptive fields terminate in the intralaminar thalamic nuclei, projecting to the anterior cingulate cortex, insula, and prefrontal cortex — responsible for the emotional/affective and cognitive dimensions of pain (why pain feels unpleasant, not just detectable).

Why this two-pathway split matters clinically: it explains why pain has both a sensory-discriminative component (sharp, in my left hand) and a separate emotional component (this is awful) — and why the two can be affected independently, which is part of why purely sensory-blocking treatments don't always relieve the distress of pain.
There's no single "pain center" in the brain — functional imaging shows painful stimuli activate a distributed network including the primary and secondary somatosensory cortex, insula, anterior cingulate cortex, and prefrontal areas.

Modulation

Stage 4 — How Pain Signals Get Turned Up or Down

Modulation is the reason the same injury can feel different depending on context — it's the nervous system's built-in volume control for pain, operating at the level of the spinal cord itself.

Gate control theory (Melzack & Wall, 1965)

Proposed a "gate" mechanism in the spinal dorsal horn: activity in large-diameter, non-painful sensory fibers (touch, pressure) can inhibit ongoing transmission from small-diameter pain fibers at that same spinal level — effectively "closing the gate" on the pain signal before it travels further.

Why rubbing an injury helps

This is the physiologic explanation for why rubbing a bumped knee reduces pain — large-fiber touch input partially blocks small-fiber pain transmission at the same dorsal horn synapse. It's also the basis for TENS units (transcutaneous electrical nerve stimulation).

The theory's other big contribution

Gate control theory was the first to propose that the brain doesn't just receive pain signals passively — it can send information back down to the spinal cord to suppress incoming pain. This "descending modulation" concept, radical when proposed with no supporting evidence in 1965, turned out to be correct.

How descending inhibition works

Cortical/limbic activity engages the periaqueductal gray (PAG) in the midbrain, which relays to the rostroventromedial medulla (RVM). RVM neurons send signals down the spinal cord to the dorsal horn, triggering release of endogenous opioids that suppress the incoming nociceptive signal — the brain telling the spinal cord "I'm not listening to this right now."

This is how morphine works: opioid drugs act at these same descending-pathway receptor sites (PAG, RVM, dorsal horn), essentially amplifying the body's own natural pain-suppression system rather than doing something entirely separate.
Endogenous Opioids

The three families

β-endorphins (bind mainly μ receptors), enkephalins (bind mainly δ receptors), and dynorphins (bind mainly κ receptors) — the body's own morphine-like pain relievers.

Where they act

At multiple levels: the dorsal horn of the spinal cord (reducing neurotransmitter release from the first-order neuron and hyperpolarizing the second-order neuron), the brainstem (PAG/RVM), and higher brain regions involved in emotional pain processing.

Serotonin and norepinephrine are also released via descending pathways and contribute to pain modulation — this is part of why some antidepressants (which affect these same neurotransmitters) have legitimate pain-relieving effects independent of mood.

By Mechanism, and By Duration

The IASP recognizes three mechanistic categories of pain — this is the modern classification framework (nociplastic pain was formally added in 2017).

TypeCauseTypical quality
NociceptiveActual/threatened damage to non-neural tissue, activating nociceptorsSomatic: sharp, well-localized. Visceral: dull, diffuse
NeuropathicLesion or disease of the somatosensory nervous system itself (nerve damage)Burning, shooting, electric-shock-like, tingling; sensitive to touch/temperature
NociplasticAltered nociceptive processing/central sensitization without clear tissue or nerve damage (e.g., fibromyalgia)Widespread, often with hypersensitivity to normally nonpainful stimuli

Nociceptive — somatic vs. visceral

Somatic: from skin/musculoskeletal tissue — sharp, easy to pinpoint. Visceral: from thoracic/abdominal/pelvic organs — dull, diffuse, harder to localize (this ties directly back to the lamina V convergence and referred pain discussed under Transmission).

Acute vs. chronic

Acute: tied to a specific injury/disease, predictable trajectory, resolves as healing occurs (generally within 3–6 months). Chronic: persists beyond expected healing time (generally ≥3 months); often involves maladaptive neuroplasticity and impaired descending modulation — the nervous system itself changes, not just the original injury.

Clinically important overlap: pain doesn't always fit neatly into one box. Neuropathic pain, though usually chronic, can start acutely and later become chronic. Chronic pain of any type can eventually "centralize" — the threshold for pain perception drops throughout the nervous system, so the classification that fit at onset may not fit months later.

PQRST — the structured interview approach

Provocation/palliation (what makes it better/worse), Quality (sharp, dull, burning), Region/radiation (where, does it spread), Severity (intensity rating), Timing (onset, duration, pattern).

Multidimensional tools

The Brief Pain Inventory and the PEG tool (Pain, Enjoyment, General activity) go beyond intensity alone to capture functional impact — useful because a modest drop in pain score can still reflect meaningful improvement in daily function and quality of life.

Acute pain often comes with objective physiologic clues (tachycardia, tachypnea, elevated BP, diaphoresis) from sympathetic activation — but their absence doesn't mean pain isn't present, especially in chronic pain, where these signs habituate away even though the pain persists.

Pain Management

Pharmacologic & Nonpharmacologic Approaches

WHO analgesic ladder — the stepwise framework

Originally developed for cancer pain, now applied broadly: start with non-opioid analgesics for mild pain, add/step up to weak opioids for moderate pain, and use strong opioids for severe pain — adjusting up or down based on ongoing reassessment rather than a fixed starting point.

Non-opioid analgesics

Acetaminophen and NSAIDs (ibuprofen, aspirin) — recall from the Transduction section that NSAIDs work by blocking cyclooxygenase, preventing prostaglandin-mediated nociceptor sensitization at the source.

Monitoring concerns

Acetaminophen: watch for hepatotoxicity, especially with chronic use or liver impairment. NSAIDs: GI bleeding risk (give with food), plus renal and cardiovascular considerations with long-term use.

Opioids & adjuvants

Opioids act at the same μ/δ/κ receptors as the body's endogenous opioids described under Modulation. Adjuvant agents (anticonvulsants, certain antidepressants) are often preferred specifically for neuropathic pain, targeting nerve-level mechanisms rather than nociceptor-level ones.

Nonpharmacologic strategies

TENS units (direct clinical application of gate control theory), heat/cold therapy, massage, acupuncture, relaxation/distraction techniques, physical therapy — many of these work by engaging the same large-fiber gating or descending-modulation mechanisms covered earlier, not as a separate "alternative" pathway.

Why combining approaches works

Because pain is modulated at multiple points (peripheral transduction, spinal gate, descending inhibition, cortical perception), combining a peripherally-acting drug (NSAID) with a centrally-acting one (opioid) and a nonpharmacologic modality (TENS, positioning) can be more effective than maximizing any single approach alone — each targets a different stage of the same four-stage nociception pathway covered in this guide.
