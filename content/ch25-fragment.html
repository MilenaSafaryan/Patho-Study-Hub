Key Terms & Definitions

Start here
pH
The negative logarithm of hydrogen ion concentration; normal arterial blood pH is 7.35–7.45.
Bicarbonate buffer system
The primary extracellular buffer — HCO3⁻ (base) and H2CO3 (weak acid) in equilibrium; normal ratio 20:1.
Volatile acid
Carbonic acid — the only acid excretable as a gas (CO2 + H2O) by the lungs.
Nonvolatile (metabolic) acid
Any acid other than carbonic acid (e.g., lactic acid, ketoacids); excreted only by the kidneys.
Compensation
A physiologic response that moves pH toward normal by deliberately creating an imbalance in the OTHER acid system — doesn't correct the underlying problem.
Titratable acidity
H+ excreted in urine while bound to filtered buffers like phosphate.
Ammonium trapping
Renal tubular cells produce ammonia (NH3), which combines with H+ to form NH4+ — a way to excrete H+ without acidifying the urine excessively.
Acidosis
A process tending to decrease blood pH (make it more acidic) — a process, not necessarily a measured low pH.
Acidemia
An actual measured decrease in blood pH.
Alkalosis
A process tending to increase blood pH.
Alkalemia
An actual measured increase in blood pH.
Metabolic acidosis
Relative excess of any acid except carbonic acid — from increased acid or decreased base (bicarbonate).
Respiratory acidosis
Excess carbonic acid from impaired CO2 excretion (hypoventilation).
pH Basics & Three Lines of Defense

The stakes

Normal blood H+ concentration: ~40 nmol/L → pH 7.40. Normal range: 7.35–7.45 (wider in infants/children). pH <6.9 → likely death. pH >7.8 → likely death. This is an extraordinarily narrow tolerance for a system the body defends aggressively.

What each lab value tells you

PaCO2 = respiratory (carbonic acid) component. HCO3⁻ = renal/metabolic component. pH = the NET result of both systems plus any imbalance and compensation. Clinically measured pH is BLOOD pH — it doesn't necessarily reflect intracellular or CSF pH.

Mind Map — Three Defenses, Three Timescales

Buffers

The four buffer systems

Bicarbonate (extracellular fluid — the MOST important one), phosphate (intracellular fluid + renal tubular fluid), hemoglobin (inside RBCs), protein (intracellular fluid + blood). All work within SECONDS — the body's first line of defense.

The 20:1 ratio — the single number to memorize

Normal pH requires exactly 20 bicarbonate ions for every 1 carbonic acid molecule (Henderson-Hasselbalch equation; pKa = 6.1 for carbonic acid). This ratio — NOT the absolute values of either component — is what determines pH. This is the key insight behind how compensation works throughout the rest of the chapter.

Mechanism: excess metabolic acid → bicarbonate takes up the H+ → becomes carbonic acid → carbonic anhydrase converts it to CO2 + H2O → exhaled. Deficit of metabolic acid → carbonic acid releases H+ instead, opposing the pH rise.
Respiratory Contribution

The core mechanism

Cells produce CO2 continuously; CO2 + H2O = carbonic acid. Exhaling CO2/H2O is functionally excreting carbonic acid. Chemoreceptors sense PaCO2, PaO2, and pH → adjust rate/depth of respiration accordingly. Requires the ENTIRE chain to work: chemoreceptors, brainstem respiratory neurons, motor nerves, respiratory muscles, chest wall, airways, lungs, pulmonary circulation.

The volatile-acid distinction — why this matters

Carbonic acid is the ONLY volatile acid (excretable as gas). Lactic acid, ketoacids, and other metabolic acids are nonvolatile — the lungs literally cannot excrete them directly. When a nonvolatile acid excess triggers hyperventilation, it removes carbonic acid (helping the RATIO/pH) without touching the actual excess acid — this is exactly why it's called compensation, not correction.

StimulusRespiratory responseResult type
↑ PaCO2, ↓ pHHyperventilationCorrection
↓ PaCO2, ↑ pHHypoventilationCorrection
↓ pH from excess metabolic acidHyperventilationCompensation
↑ pH from metabolic acid deficitHypoventilationCompensation
Correction vs. compensation — the key distinction: when carbonic acid itself is the problem, the respiratory response CORRECTS it directly. When a metabolic (non-carbonic) acid is the problem, the respiratory response only COMPENSATES — it changes the ratio favorably without removing the actual offending acid. Respiratory compensation begins within MINUTES.
Renal Contribution

What the kidneys can and can't do

Kidneys excrete ANY acid EXCEPT carbonic acid (the lungs' job). Age effects: infants excrete MORE bicarbonate in urine (less effective acid excretion); older adults also have less efficient renal response to a large acid load — both ends of the age spectrum are more vulnerable to metabolic acid-base problems.

Proximal tubule mechanism

For every H+ secreted into the tubular fluid, ONE bicarbonate ion moves into the interstitial fluid (reabsorption) — nearly all filtered bicarbonate is reclaimed this way. Net H+ excretion only happens AFTER all filtered bicarbonate has been reabsorbed.

Mind Map — How the Kidney Excretes Acid Without Destroying the Urine

Why the ammonia trick matters

Ammonium ions (NH4+) don't easily cross back into the blood, and — critically — only FREE hydrogen ions contribute to urine acidity, not H+ bound in NH4+. This lets the kidney excrete large amounts of H+ without making urine dangerously acidic. Ammonia production ramps up specifically when more H+ needs to be excreted.

Renal compensation for carbonic acid imbalances

Kidneys can't excrete carbonic acid directly, but they CAN adjust metabolic acid excretion/bicarbonate reabsorption to shift the 20:1 ratio favorably. ↑ Carbonic acid → kidneys ↑ metabolic acid excretion + ↑ HCO3⁻ reabsorption. ↓ Carbonic acid (prolonged) → kidneys ↓ metabolic acid excretion, letting metabolic acid accumulate to compensate. Takes SEVERAL DAYS to be fully effective — much slower than respiratory compensation.

HCO3⁻ concentration is the direct readout of metabolic acid-base status: ↓ HCO3⁻ = relative metabolic acid excess (used up buffering); ↑ HCO3⁻ = relative metabolic acid deficit (relative base excess).
The Four Primary Disorders

Process vs. actual measured state

Acidosis: a PROCESS tending to lower pH. Acidemia: pH is ACTUALLY measured low. Alkalosis: a process tending to raise pH. Alkalemia: pH is actually measured high. This distinction matters because a person can have an acidotic PROCESS occurring while their measured pH is normal (compensated) or even alkalemic (mixed disorder).

The four disorders, by mechanism

Metabolic acidosis: ↑ metabolic acid or ↓ bicarbonate. Respiratory acidosis: ↑ carbonic acid (hypoventilation). Metabolic alkalosis: ↓ metabolic acid or ↑ bicarbonate. Respiratory alkalosis: ↓ carbonic acid (hyperventilation). All four can be reasoned directly from the 20:1 bicarbonate:carbonic acid ratio — whichever direction pushes that ratio away from 20:1 defines the primary disorder.

Metabolic Acidosis

Two mechanisms

↑ Acid: ketoacidosis (diabetic, starvation, low-carb diet, alcohol-related), severe hyperthyroidism, burns, shock, hypoaldosteronism, drug effects (metformin lactic acidosis, propofol infusion syndrome, SGLT2i-related normoglycemic ketoacidosis), tissue anoxia/lactic acidosis, oliguric AKI/ESKD, acid ingestion (salicylates, methanol, ethylene glycol). ↓ Base: severe/prolonged diarrhea, GI fistula draining intestinal/pancreatic secretions, intestinal decompression, renal tubular acidosis.

Why diarrhea causes metabolic acidosis specifically

Intestinal fluid is rich in bicarbonate (from pancreatic secretions) — diarrhea removes this base directly from the body, dropping the bicarbonate side of the 20:1 ratio.

Clinical manifestations: headache, abdominal pain, CNS depression (confusion → lethargy → stupor → coma) — from decreased CSF/interstitial brain fluid pH altering protein structure/enzyme activity. Severe cases: tachycardia, ventricular dysrhythmias (myocardial intracellular acidity), ↓ contractility — potentially fatal. Death from brainstem dysfunction typically below pH 6.9.

Compensatory response

Hyperventilation (low pH stimulates chemoreceptors → brainstem ventilatory neurons → ↑ rate/depth). Doesn't remove the metabolic acid, but DOES shift the bicarbonate:carbonic acid ratio favorably by lowering the carbonic acid side.

ABG signature

Uncompensated: ↓ HCO3⁻, ↓ pH, normal PaCO2. Compensated: ↓ HCO3⁻ (primary), ↓ PaCO2 (compensation), pH low-normal or still low depending on degree of compensation.

Respiratory Acidosis

Three etiology categories

Impaired gas exchange: type B COPD, end-stage type A COPD, bacterial/COVID pneumonia, severe asthma, pulmonary edema, ARDS. Impaired neuromuscular chest function: Guillain-Barré syndrome, chest injury/surgery (pain limits breathing), hypokalemic muscle weakness, severe kyphoscoliosis, respiratory muscle fatigue. Impaired brainstem control: respiratory depressants (opioids, barbiturates, benzodiazepines).

Acute-on-chronic respiratory acidosis

Chronic respiratory acidosis is common in type B COPD; if an acute respiratory infection develops on top of it, the acidosis can worsen further — a clinically important compound scenario, not just an academic label.

Why neurologic symptoms are often MORE prominent than in metabolic acidosis: carbonic acid (CO2 + H2O) crosses the blood-brain barrier much more easily than most metabolic acids. Manifestations: headache (cerebral vasodilation → ↑ CSF pressure → possible papilledema), tachycardia, cardiac dysrhythmias, blurred vision, tremors, vertigo, disorientation, lethargy, somnolence. Severe cases: peripheral vasodilation → hypotension, worsened if dysrhythmias are also present.

Compensatory response

↑ Renal metabolic acid excretion — takes SEVERAL DAYS to become effective (much slower than the respiratory compensation for metabolic acidosis). As more metabolic acid is excreted, less bicarbonate is consumed in buffering, so bicarbonate concentration RISES, favorably shifting the ratio.

ABG signature

Uncompensated: ↑ PaCO2 (primary), ↓ pH, normal HCO3⁻. Compensated: ↑ PaCO2 (primary), ↑ HCO3⁻ (compensation), pH low-normal or normal depending on degree of compensation.

The pattern to internalize: the PRIMARY disorder's own lab value moves first and furthest from normal. Compensation shows up as the OTHER value moving in the SAME direction to help pH — so in compensated states, BOTH PaCO2 and HCO3⁻ are abnormal, just in the same direction (both up or both down), which is what distinguishes compensation from a second, independent primary disorder (mixed acid-base imbalance, where they'd move in ways that don't resolve to a single consistent story).
Acute conditions (asthma attack, PE) show a NORMAL HCO3⁻/PaCO2 for the non-primary value — renal compensation hasn't had time (days) to kick in yet. Chronic conditions (COPD) show a fully shifted compensatory value, since there's been time for the slower system to respond.
