Key Terms & Definitions

Start here
Neoplasia
"New growth" — abnormal cellular growth; used loosely as a synonym for tumor.
Cancer
The term reserved specifically for malignant neoplasms.
Anaplasia
Lack of differentiated features in a cancer cell — greater anaplasia correlates with more aggressive malignancy.
Carcinoma
Malignant tumor of epithelial origin — accounts for ~90% of human cancers.
Sarcoma
Malignant tumor of mesenchymal origin (nerve, bone, muscle, tendon, fat).
Proto-oncogene
A normal cellular gene coding for proteins involved in growth/differentiation/apoptosis/signaling; becomes an oncogene when mutated to gain function.
Oncogene
The mutant, overactive/overexpressed form of a proto-oncogene.
Tumor suppressor gene
A gene that normally inhibits cell proliferation; loss of function is the most common mutation type in cancer development.
Driver mutation
A DNA abnormality that actively contributes to malignant proliferation.
Passenger mutation
A random, coexisting DNA change that plays no role in tumorigenesis.
Carcinogen
An agent/substance capable of inducing cancer.
Co-carcinogen
A substance that causes cancer only in combination with other chemicals/compounds.
Initiation
The first step of carcinogenesis — mutations that activate proto-oncogenes/inactivate tumor suppressor genes.
Promotion
The stage where a mutant cell proliferates, often driven by inflammation, hormones, or nutritional factors.
Progression
The stage where proliferating cells develop full malignant behavior via accumulating genetic instability.
Telomerase
An enzyme that rebuilds chromosome telomeres, allowing indefinite division — reactivated by most cancer cells to escape normal replicative limits.
Warburg effect
Cancer cells' preference for glycolysis (even with oxygen present) over oxidative phosphorylation, despite glycolysis producing far less ATP per glucose.
E-cadherin
A key epithelial cell-to-cell adhesion molecule; its loss enables carcinoma cells to detach and invade.
Epithelial-to-mesenchymal transition (EMT)
A developmental program hijacked by cancer cells to gain invasive, apoptosis-resistant, stem-cell-like properties.
Cancer stem cells (CSCs)
A small, self-renewing, undifferentiated tumor cell subpopulation implicated in tumor growth, metastasis, and treatment resistance.
Angiogenesis
New blood vessel formation; tumors can't exceed ~2 mm without it.
VEGF
Vascular endothelial growth factor — the key signal tumor cells produce (often in response to hypoxia) to trigger angiogenesis.
PD-L1 / PD-1
An immune checkpoint pair — cancer cells express PD-L1 to bind T-cell PD-1 receptors, suppressing cytotoxic T-cell activation and evading immune destruction.
Cachexia
Cancer-related weight loss, muscle wasting, and generalized weakness — affects 60–80% of cancer patients.
Paraneoplastic syndrome
A symptom complex at a site distant from the tumor, not explained by direct tumor effects — often from ectopic hormone/cytokine secretion.
Leukopenia
Decreased circulating white blood cells — major cause of infection-related morbidity/mortality in cancer patients.
Thrombocytopenia
Decreased circulating platelets; <20,000/mm³ is associated with spontaneous hemorrhage risk.
Benign vs. Malignant — Characteristics & Anaplasia

The core distinction

Malignant tumors can kill the host if untreated; benign tumors generally cannot — though location matters (a benign tumor pressing on the brain or blocking an airway/vessel can still be life-threatening).

Anaplasia

Lack of differentiated features — indicated by variable cell size/shape, enlarged nuclei, abnormal mitoses, bizarre giant cells. More anaplasia = more aggressive malignancy. But regardless of histologic appearance, actual invasion or metastasis confirms malignancy.

Invasiveness — evidence that tumor cells have penetrated local tissue, lymphatics, or blood vessels — is the key feature suggesting malignant potential for metastasis to distant sites.
Tumor Terminology

The naming rule

-oma = benign. Carcinoma = malignant, epithelial origin. Sarcoma = malignant, mesenchymal origin (nerve, bone, muscle, tendon, fat). Example: benign glandular tumor = adenoma; malignant version = adenocarcinoma.

Exceptions to memorize

Lymphoma, hepatoma, and melanoma are all highly malignant despite the "-oma" suffix — don't be fooled by the naming convention. Leukemia = malignant growth of white blood cells.

Hallmarks of Cancer (Hanahan & Weinberg, 2000)

A framework for the "antisocial" behaviors cancer cells acquire to escape normal growth control:

The eight hallmarks

  • Sustained proliferative signaling
  • Escape from apoptotic signals
  • Inactivation/evasion of growth suppressor signals
  • Replicative immortality
  • Invasion and metastasis
  • Proangiogenic signaling (build their own blood supply)
  • Reprogrammed energy metabolism (aerobic glycolysis)
  • Evasion of immune surveillance/destruction

The normal rules cancer cells break

Normal cells: proliferation rate matched to death rate, require survival signals from the environment, only proliferate with space + mitogen signals available, and respond to apoptotic instructions. Cancer cells ignore essentially all of these constraints.

Epidemiology & Risk Factors

Scale

9.7 million deaths worldwide (2022). 88% of US diagnoses occur after age 55. Lifetime risk: ~40/100 US males, ~39/100 US females. 5-year relative survival: 70% (White), 65% (Black) — but this doesn't distinguish cured vs. relapsed/still-in-treatment, and doesn't account for poverty/nutrition/access-to-care effects.

The preventability figure

At least 40% of cancer deaths may be preventable — key modifiable factors: tobacco, alcohol, obesity, physical inactivity, nutrition, sun exposure, and certain viral exposures (anogenital, head/neck, liver cancers).

Tobacco, Nutrition & Lifestyle Factors

Tobacco — the single largest preventable cause

~30% of all cancer deaths. Linked beyond lung cancer to pancreas, bladder, kidney, mouth, esophagus, cervix, and blood/marrow cancers. Adult smoking prevalence: 42% (1965) → 11% (2023) — but 10% of middle/high schoolers reported tobacco use in 2023, with 7.8% using e-cigarettes (largely unregulated, early data suggests toxin/carcinogen content).

Initiators vs. promoters (carcinogen types)

Initiators cause genetic damage (e.g., polycyclic aromatic hydrocarbons, nicotine derivatives — both found in tobacco smoke). Promoters spur mutant cells to proliferate — tobacco smoke contains both types.

Obesity & nutrition

Obesity is an independent risk factor for endometrial, esophageal, and liver cancer among others — and worsens recurrence/mortality risk even after diagnosis. ACS recommends a plant-based diet, limiting red/processed meat, controlling calories, limiting alcohol.

Alcohol's multiple mechanisms

Linked to breast, esophageal, laryngeal, liver cancer. Mechanisms: direct upper-GI cell damage, impaired hepatic detox capacity, ↑ estrogen levels (breast cancer promotion), and insulin-secretion effects (insulin is a growth factor for many tissues).

Antioxidants — a clear negative result worth knowing: despite a plausible mechanism (free radicals damage DNA → antioxidants should help), large clinical trials of β-carotene, vitamins E/C, selenium, retinol, zinc found no convincing benefit for cancer prevention from supplements. Whole food sources remain reasonable; supplements are not proven protective.
Microbiome: alterations are increasingly linked to cancer development, especially GI tract and lung cancers. Inflammation-driven intestinal permeability is an area of active research as both a risk factor and a potential prevention/treatment target.
Driver vs. Passenger Mutations

By diagnosis, a tumor already has billions of mutated cells

Only some mutations are driver mutations (actively contribute to malignant proliferation) — many others are passenger mutations (random, coexisting, no functional role).

Two broad gene categories

Proto-oncogenes: cancer arises from gain-of-function (overactivity). Tumor suppressor genes: cancer arises from loss-of-function (underactivity) — and loss-of-function mutations are actually the MOST common mutation type in cancer overall.

Malignant transformation generally requires mutations in a combination of these growth-regulatory genes — plus, indirectly, DNA-repair genes and apoptosis-regulating genes (see Chapter 4) also contribute to cancer risk when they malfunction.
Proto-Oncogenes & Oncogenes — Four Signaling Categories

Most proto-oncogenes code for components of the SAME proliferation-signaling relay — just at different points along it.

Mind Map — Where Proto-Oncogenes Act in the Signaling Relay

Growth factors (mitogens)

Normally cells don't make enough growth factor to stimulate their OWN proliferation — signals must come from the environment. Mutant proto-oncogene overproduction shifts this to self-stimulated (autocrine) growth. PDGF is classically oversecreted in glial/connective tissue cancers.

Receptors

Mutations can cause inappropriate receptor expression, excess normal receptors, abnormally high mitogen affinity, or receptors active even without any growth factor bound. HER2 overexpression: 15–20% of breast cancers, up to 30% of esophageal/GE junction cancers.

Cytoplasmic signaling — the RAS example

RAS proteins are G proteins active only when GTP-bound; they normally self-inactivate by hydrolyzing GTP. A RAS mutation can prevent this hydrolysis → the protein stays persistently active → inappropriate proliferation signaling. RAS mutations occur in up to 30% of all human cancers.

Transcription factors

Normally sequestered until appropriate signals release them. Mutations can cause overproduction or disable the normal "keep in check" mechanisms. MYC abnormalities found in lung/breast cancer, leukemia, neuroblastoma.

From Proto-Oncogene to Oncogene — Four Activation Routes

The four mechanisms

  • Retroviral insertion of an oncogene into the host cell
  • A mutagenic event changing the proto-oncogene's own structure/function
  • Damage/loss of the regulatory DNA sequence controlling proto-oncogene expression
  • Chromosome replication error causing extra gene copies (amplification)

Viral oncogenesis — three distinct routes

(1) Direct interruption of growth-control/apoptotic pathways. (2) Viral genome incorporation disrupting gene regulation (HIV, human T-cell leukemia virus). (3) Indirect — chronic inflammatory immune response causes tissue damage/cancer even in UNinfected neighboring cells.

Multistep Carcinogenesis — Initiation

Mind Map — Initiation → Promotion → Progression

Key experimental proof of "multistep"

RAS oncogene alone → anchorage independence, but NOT tumor formation in vivo. MYC oncogene alone → immortalized cells in culture, but still NOT tumor formation. RAS + MYC together → fully malignant. Single mutations are insufficient.

Carcinogen categories

Some cause cancer "as is." Co-carcinogens only cause cancer combined with other substances. A third category requires metabolic activation (e.g., hepatic enzyme processing) before becoming carcinogenic.

Known carcinogens worth knowing by category: UV/ionizing radiation, HPV and hepatitis B/C viruses, asbestos, alcohol (oral/pharynx/breast/digestive tract-liver), tobacco (respiratory + digestive + GU + cervical), alkylating agents (hematologic), vinyl chloride (liver/brain/lymph/hematologic).
Promotion

Inflammation as a promoter

Inflammatory cells (macrophages, mast cells, neutrophils, T/B lymphocytes) supply the tumor microenvironment with growth factors, survival factors (limiting apoptosis), proangiogenic factors, and matrix-modifying enzymes. They also release reactive oxygen species that are actively mutagenic — accelerating malignant genetic evolution.

Hormonal promotion — estrogen

More lifetime menstrual cycles (early menarche, late first pregnancy, no breastfeeding, late menopause) → higher breast/uterine/ovarian cancer risk, via cumulative estrogen exposure. Estrogen is a promoter but is NOT itself carcinogenic — it doesn't cause mutations directly. Antiestrogen drugs (tamoxifen, aromatase inhibitors) exploit this relationship therapeutically.

Hormonal promotion — testosterone

A growth factor/promoter for prostate tissue — therapeutic testosterone blockade can shrink prostate tumors and produce temporary remission, supporting the causal relationship.

Immune evasion during promotion

Highly immunogenic cancer cells are usually eliminated by a competent immune system — hence higher cancer rates (especially virally-induced) in immunocompromised patients. Less immunogenic cells survive; cancer cells can also actively suppress immunity via cytokines, or via PD-L1 binding T-cell PD-1 receptors (exploiting a normal autoimmunity-prevention checkpoint) and up-regulating CTLA-4 on T cells.

Progression

Genetic instability drives evolution

Proliferating cells undergo chance mutations creating varied phenotypes; cells whose phenotype confers a growth advantage proliferate preferentially — an ongoing selection process. Cancer karyotypes become increasingly bizarre over time (extra/missing chromosomes, misplaced fragments).

Telomerase reactivation

Fetal cells replicate ~80 times in culture; older-donor cells only 20–30 times (telomere shortening limits division). Most cancer cells reactivate telomerase during malignant transformation, escaping this limit — a key route to replicative immortality. Additionally, most cancers are p53-deficient, letting them escape apoptosis despite major DNA derangement.

The Warburg effect: most cancer cells rely on glycolysis for energy even WHEN oxygen is available (not just under hypoxia) — despite glycolysis yielding only 2 ATP vs. 34 ATP from oxidative phosphorylation per glucose. This inefficient-looking choice actually gives a survival advantage in the low-oxygen environments that develop as tumors outgrow their blood supply.
Invasion Mechanism

The basement membrane crossing steps

(1) Loss of cell-to-cell adhesion (classically, loss of E-cadherin in carcinomas). (2) Binding to matrix components (laminin) via tumor cell laminin receptors. (3) Release of proteases/collagenases to digest the basement membrane. (4) Ameboid movement through the resulting rift — repeated at the vessel wall to enter circulation, and again at the target tissue to exit.

Cancer stem cells (CSCs)

A small self-renewing, undifferentiated subpopulation — contribute to continued growth, metastatic seeding, and treatment resistance (can be quiescent, evading chemo, then repopulate the tumor). First discovered in solid tumors in 2003.

Epithelial-to-mesenchymal transition (EMT)

A normal embryonic/wound-healing/fibrosis program hijacked by cancer cells to gain invasiveness, apoptosis resistance, and stem-cell-like qualities — seen in glioblastoma, colon cancer, and melanoma research.

After entering circulation, tumor survival is NOT guaranteed — fewer than 1 in 10,000 circulating cancer cells survives to form a distant tumor. Micrometastases can also lie dormant for years (sometimes suppressed by the primary tumor itself) before "waking up," which is why late recurrence happens even after apparently successful primary tumor removal (breast cancer, melanoma).
Patterns of Spread & Tumor Biomarkers

Blood-borne spread

Sometimes explained by circulatory anatomy (colon cancer → liver via the portal vein), but many patterns reflect tumor cell "homing" via chemotactic signals and integrin/adhesion molecule interactions — poorly understood but under active study.

Lymphatic spread — more predictable

Nodes immediately draining the tumor site are colonized first, then spread proceeds contiguously node-to-node. Hodgkin disease is noted for especially orderly lymphatic spread.

Angiogenesis

The 2mm rule

Tumors can't grow beyond ~2 mm diameter without inducing their own blood supply — many tumors stay small/nonvascular for years before triggering angiogenesis, though some are proangiogenic even at the premalignant stage.

VEGF mechanism

Tumor cells produce VEGF (often triggered by hypoxia) → stimulates vascular endothelial cell proliferation/migration → new (often abnormal, distorted, excessively branched) vessels form within the tumor.

Warning Signs

Adult warning signs (CAUTION)

Change in bowel/bladder habits; A sore that doesn't heal; Unusual bleeding/discharge; Unusual bleeding between periods; Thickening or lump; Indigestion/difficulty swallowing; Obvious change in wart/mole/sore; Nagging cough or hoarseness.

Pediatric warning signs (CHILD CANCER)

Unexplained weight loss; headaches with early-morning vomiting; increased bone/joint/back/leg pain; lumps/masses; excessive bruising/bleeding/rash; constant infections; whitish color behind pupils (leukocoria); persistent nausea/vomiting; constant tiredness/pallor; sudden vision changes; recurrent unexplained fevers.

Cachexia, Immune Suppression & Bone Marrow Effects

Cachexia (60–80% of patients)

Weight loss + muscle wasting + weakness. Drivers: anorexia (from tumor/immune cell toxins, food aversions, early satiety), PLUS a paradoxically high metabolic rate despite poor intake — driven largely by TNF and other immune cytokines mobilizing fat/protein stores for hypermetabolic cells.

Immune suppression

Cancer cells actively secrete immunosuppressive substances; patients show reduced T/B cell populations and poor vaccine response. Worse immune status = worse prognosis, since cytotoxic T cells/NK cells are needed to detect and destroy cancer cells.

Bone marrow suppression — the classic triad:

CytopeniaClinical significance
Anemia↓ RBCs → fatigue, ↑ HR, ↑ RR (from reduced O2-carrying capacity); from marrow invasion, poor nutrition, chemo, or bleeding
Leukopenia↓ WBCs → major cause of infection-related morbidity/mortality; infections often from the patient's own endogenous flora, hard to prevent or treat effectively
Thrombocytopenia↓ Platelets → hemorrhage risk; <20,000/mm³ associated with spontaneous bleeding
Growth factor support: erythropoietin (Epogen) for anemia, granulocyte-stimulating factors (filgrastim) for leukopenia — blood product transfusion is used more often in cancer patients than in any other medical condition.
Paraneoplastic Syndromes

Definition & frequency

Symptom complexes at a site distant from the tumor, not explained by direct tumor effects — occur in up to 20% of cancer patients; more common in middle-aged/older patients; most common in lung, lymphatic, ovarian, and breast cancers.

Three classic examples

(1) Hypercalcemia — from tumor production of parathyroid hormone–related protein; unexplained hypercalcemia = cancer until proven otherwise. (2) Cushing syndrome — from ectopic ACTH secretion. (3) SIADH (hyponatremia/water overload) — from ectopic ADH secretion.

Small cell lung cancer is the classic culprit for BOTH ectopic ACTH and ectopic ADH paraneoplastic syndromes — worth memorizing this specific association. Also note: hypercalcemia from actual bone metastasis is an expected finding, NOT a paraneoplastic syndrome — the distinction is whether the mechanism is a distant ectopic hormone effect vs. direct tumor tissue destruction.
Cancer Therapy & Screening

Precision medicine trend

Next-gen sequencing enables "tissue-agnostic" basket trials — treating based on a specific molecular finding (e.g., PIK3CA mutation, NTRK fusion) regardless of whether the tumor originated in lung, breast, etc.

Pathogen-targeted prevention

HPV and hepatitis B vaccination reduce future cancer risk. Treating hepatitis C can reduce hepatocellular cancer risk AND cause regression in certain non-Hodgkin lymphomas with concurrent HCV. Treating H. pylori produces remission in up to 70% of a specific non-Hodgkin lymphoma type.

Surgery

Main treatment for localized solid tumors; curative in many cases, but surgery alone is curative in only a minority since undetectable micrometastases are often already present — hence adjuvant therapy (radiation/chemo/immunotherapy/hormonal) commonly follows. Neoadjuvant therapy (before surgery) can shrink tumors for a less aggressive surgical approach.

Radiation therapy

Used for unresectable tumors, residual local disease after surgery, symptom palliation, or prophylaxis (e.g., whole-brain radiation for small cell lung cancer's high brain-met risk). Works mainly by triggering apoptosis via DNA damage (base damage, strand breaks, cross-links) — TP53-mutant tumors are often less responsive. Given in fractionated doses since tumors are heterogeneous (cells cycling at different rates/phases) and a single tumoricidal dose would also kill normal tissue.

Cure vs. 5-year survival: cure means eradication of ALL cancer cells — a different concept from the 5-year survival statistic. Regional lymph node involvement still carries good cure potential with appropriate therapy; widespread multi-organ invasion shifts treatment goals toward remission, life prolongation, or palliation rather than cure.
Screening age ranges worth knowing (average risk, ACS guidelines): Breast — mammogram starting 40–45, annual or biennial after 55. Colon — starting age 45. Cervical — starting age 25 (primary HPV testing). Lung — ages 50–80, 20 pack-year history, current smoker or quit within 15 years, annual low-dose CT. Prostate — shared decision-making starting age 40–50 depending on risk category.
