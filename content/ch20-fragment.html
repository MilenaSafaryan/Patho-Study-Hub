Key Terms & Definitions

Start here
Shock
Insufficient oxygenated blood delivery to the microcirculation, causing tissue hypoxia and cellular dysfunction.
Cardiogenic shock
Inadequate cardiac output from heart disorders despite sufficient vascular volume.
Obstructive shock
Circulatory blockage disrupting cardiac output (e.g., massive PE, tamponade).
Hypovolemic shock
Shock from lost blood volume — hemorrhage or excessive extracellular fluid loss.
Distributive shock
Shock from greatly expanded vascular space via inappropriate vasodilation, without true volume loss.
Lactate
The product of anaerobic pyruvate metabolism; >5–6 mmol/L signals significant tissue hypoxia and serves as a shock severity marker.
Reperfusion injury
Oxygen free radical formation when blood flow is restored to previously ischemic tissue.
Reactive oxygen species (ROS)
Unstable molecules (superoxide, peroxide, hydroxyl radicals) that attack membranes, denature proteins, and damage DNA.
SIRS
Systemic inflammatory response syndrome — a widespread inflammatory response to infectious or noninfectious insults.
Sepsis (2016 definition)
Life-threatening organ dysfunction caused by a dysregulated host response to infection.
Septic shock (2016 definition)
Sepsis requiring vasopressors to maintain MAP ≥65 mmHg, with lactate >2 mmol/L despite adequate fluid resuscitation.
Bacteremia
Viable bacteria present in the bloodstream.
Shock index
Systolic BP ÷ heart rate; normal 0.7–0.9, >1.0 signals concern.
ARDS
Acute respiratory distress syndrome — refractory hypoxemia, ↓ pulmonary compliance, noncardiogenic pulmonary edema.
DIC
Disseminated intravascular coagulation — widespread microvascular clotting consuming platelets/clotting factors, causing simultaneous thrombosis and bleeding.
Acute tubular necrosis (ATN)
Ischemic necrosis of renal tubular epithelial cells from prolonged hypoperfusion.
MODS
Multiple organ dysfunction syndrome — altered function in ≥2 organ systems, requiring intervention to maintain homeostasis.
Four Types of Shock — Overview

The four mechanistic categories

Cardiogenic: heart disorders → inadequate CO despite normal volume. Obstructive: mechanical blockage disrupts CO (often grouped with cardiogenic since the final pathway is the same — failure of the heart to pump). Hypovolemic: lost blood/fluid volume. Distributive: expanded vascular space from inappropriate vasodilation, no true volume loss.

Historical framing

1895, John Collins Warren: shock as "a momentary pause in the act of death" — still a strikingly accurate, high-mortality condition despite modern advances in understanding and management.

Impaired Tissue Oxygenation

Mind Map — From Hypoxia to Cell Death

Why anaerobic metabolism is so costly

Aerobic metabolism: pyruvate → citric acid cycle → up to 36 ATP. Anaerobic: citric acid cycle inhibited, pyruvate converts to lactate instead — net gain of only 2 ATP per glucose. This isn't just "less efficient," it's the mechanism that starts the whole downstream cascade of pump failure.

The ion pump failure cascade

ATP-dependent pumps normally push Na+/Ca2+ back out against steep electrochemical gradients. Without ATP: Na+/water accumulate intracellularly (hydropic swelling) and free intracellular calcium rises — triggering a cascade that further impairs energy production AND plasma membrane integrity. Cell death from oxygen deprivation: minutes to several hours, depending on tissue metabolic rate.

Reperfusion Injury & Cytokines

Reperfusion injury mechanism

Restoring O2 to ischemic cells generates ROS (superoxide, peroxide, hydroxyl radicals, singlet oxygen) — unstable molecules that attack membranes, denature proteins, damage DNA. Mitochondria are especially vulnerable: rising intracellular lactate increases outer mitochondrial membrane permeability, letting more ROS in, worsening ATP production failure and triggering necrosis or apoptosis.

Immune cells as a second ROS source

Neutrophils recruited to injured tissue are ANOTHER source of free radicals — meaning cellular injury can continue and progress long after the original hypoxic insult has resolved. This is why shock damage isn't simply "reversed" once perfusion is restored.

Key cytokines/mediators worth knowing by function: TNF-α and IL-1 (major septic shock mediators — inflammation, neutrophil activation, vascular failure); IL-6 (fever, acute phase proteins); IL-10 (anti-inflammatory, may suppress shock); nitric oxide and adrenomedullin (vasodilators); endothelin, norepinephrine, angiotensin II (vasoconstrictors); vasopressin and cortisol (improve vascular responsiveness to catecholamines); histamine (↑ permeability, blocked by heparin's antihistamine action); protein C (inhibits thrombosis); heat-shock proteins (protect protein structure, inhibit apoptosis). These represent active therapeutic targets in a notoriously hard-to-treat syndrome.
Microcirculatory Dysfunction

Normal autoregulation fails

Healthy tissue matches blood flow to metabolic need across a wide BP range. In shock, this fails — producing abnormal flow distribution: some capillary beds are underperfused/hypoxic while others are excessively dilated/overperfused.

Homogeneous vs. heterogeneous patterns

Low-CO shock (hypovolemic/cardiogenic): homogeneous reduction in flow through arterioles/capillaries/venules, tracking directly with the degree of CO reduction. Distributive shock (e.g., septic): heterogeneous — some capillaries closed, others wide open with high venule flow; microcirculatory dysfunction correlates POORLY with systemic hemodynamics — a genuinely different, harder-to-monitor pattern.

The resulting "oxygen debt" can be estimated clinically via serum lactate, CRP, degree of metabolic acidosis, and central venous oxygen saturation (SvO2) — all reflecting the same underlying switch to anaerobic metabolism in oxygen-deprived tissue.
Cardiogenic Shock

Etiology

Most common cause: MI with >40% LV myocardium dysfunction/loss. Others: RV MI, end-stage cardiomyopathy, papillary muscle dysfunction, free wall rupture, congenital defects. Key distinguishing lab feature: HIGH LV preload — this is what separates cardiogenic from hypovolemic shock (both have low CO, but opposite preload).

The compensatory paradox

SNS activation (↑ HR, ↑ SVR) and RAAS activation (↑ resistance, ↑ preload) are triggered to raise CO — but BOTH increase myocardial workload/O2 demand, which can precipitate FURTHER cardiac damage and a progressive CO decline. The compensatory mechanisms actively worsen the underlying problem.

Clinical progression

Initially: SNS maintains BP despite falling CO. As compensation fails: systolic BP drops, diastolic rises (from SNS), pulse pressure narrows. HR >100. Cool, clammy skin (vasoconstriction). Coarse crackles (pulmonary edema), S3 summation gallop.

PCWP and ABG thresholds

PCWP normal <12 mmHg; cardiogenic shock typically >15 mmHg. Chronic HF patients may not develop pulmonary edema until PCWP reaches 20–40 mmHg (adapted threshold). ABG progression: initial respiratory alkalosis (hyperventilation) → respiratory acidosis + hypoxemia as edema worsens, which further impairs myocardial function — a self-reinforcing decline.

Pharmacotherapy — the treatment tension

Positive inotropes (NE, dobutamine, dopamine, milrinone/amrinone) increase contractility/CO but ALSO increase myocardial O2 demand — potentially worsening ischemia. Dobutamine has minimal α-activity (mainly ↑ contractility, may even ↓ resistance — caution in hypotension). Vasodilators (nitroprusside, nitroglycerin) reduce afterload/preload to cut workload.

Mechanical assist devices

IABP: inflates in diastole (↑ coronary perfusion), deflates before systole (vacuum effect ↓ afterload) — temporary, restricts mobility. Impella: catheter-based VAD through the aortic valve into the LV, pumps 2.5–5.0 L/min directly into the aorta. ECMO: full heart-lung bypass, shown to outperform IABP post-cardiac-arrest/in cardiogenic shock. VAD: longer-term, often bridge-to-transplant; can sometimes allow enough cardiac recovery for removal.

Compensatory mechanisms are generally INEFFECTIVE in obstructive shock — deterioration can be rapid. Treatment is entirely about relieving the specific obstruction: needle decompression/finger thoracotomy for tension pneumothorax, pericardiocentesis for tamponade, thrombolysis/embolectomy for massive PE. This is fundamentally different from other shock types where supportive/pharmacologic management plays a bigger role while the underlying cause resolves on its own timeline.
Etiology, Classification & Shock Index

Sources of volume loss

Internal: internal hemorrhage, long bone fracture, interstitial fluid leakage. External: external hemorrhage (most common overall cause), burns, severe vomiting/diarrhea, diuresis.

Key distinguishing lab feature

LOW preload (opposite of cardiogenic shock's high preload) — this is the fastest way to tell the two apart when CO is low in both.

Shock index = systolic BP ÷ heart rate. Normal: 0.7–0.9. >1.0 signals concern — a single calculated number that can flag deterioration before it's obvious on individual vital signs alone, useful for tracking trends over time in acute hemorrhage.
Capillary refill test: normal is <2 seconds; prolonged refill appears starting in Class II and persists through more severe classes — a quick bedside check that tracks with classification severity.
Fluid Resuscitation

Colloids — largely out of favor

Albumin: no benefit over crystalloids. Dextran/hetastarch: no benefit AND increased mortality in hemorrhagic shock. Not recommended for hypovolemic shock generally, unless significant interstitial edema is present.

Crystalloids — first-line for non-anemic hypovolemia

Isotonic solutions (lactated Ringer, normal saline) preferred over hypotonic — they stay in the extracellular space and expand blood volume more effectively.

When significant anemia is present, blood products are the treatment of choice — not crystalloids. Excessive crystalloid administration DILUTES circulating hemoglobin, platelets, and clotting factors, and room-temperature crystalloid can itself impair the clotting cascade, contributing to DIC during massive transfusion scenarios.

Massive transfusion protocol (MTP)

Generally defined as ≥4 units PRBCs in 1 hour. Ratio: 1 unit PRBC : 1 unit FFP : 1 pack platelets — maintains clotting factors and prevents coagulopathy. After 4 units PRBCs, check ionized calcium — PRBC anticoagulant citrate binds plasma calcium, affecting BOTH myocardial contractility and coagulation, so calcium chloride may need IV replacement.

General principle

"Replace what's being lost" — blood loss gets blood products, clear fluid loss gets crystalloid. Vasoconstrictors are a secondary option only when BP stays low despite adequate volume replacement — pharmacologic agents are not primary therapy for pure hypovolemia.

Anaphylactic Shock

Mechanism

Type I IgE-antibody reaction on mast cells/basophils → antigen cross-links receptors → release of histamine, leukotrienes, bradykinins, prostaglandins → bronchoconstriction, peripheral vasodilation, ↑ capillary permeability. Anaphylactoid reactions: same clinical picture, but mast cell degranulation WITHOUT IgE involvement (radiocontrast, opioids, muscle relaxants, temperature extremes, transfusion reactions).

Why it becomes shock

Massive peripheral dilation → blood pools peripherally (functionally a hypovolemic-like state even with NORMAL total blood volume) + fluid leakage into interstitium → ↓ preload → ↓ CO.

Presentation timeline

Onset: 2–30 minutes post-exposure (occasionally hours later). Progression: anxiety, ↑ HR/RR → hypotension, urticaria, pruritus, angioedema, sense of impending doom → bronchoconstriction (wheezing, cyanosis) + laryngeal edema (hoarseness, stridor).

Treatment priorities

Remove antigen if possible; airway management (intubation if needed); epinephrine is essential (stabilizes mast cells + raises BP); bronchodilators for bronchospasm; IV fluids to fill the expanded vascular space; vasopressors as needed; steroids (slow onset — adjunct, not first-line); antihistamines (reduced effectiveness once mediators are already released).

Lifetime risk: 1–3% in the US. >1/3 of cases have unidentified triggers. Fatality rate ~1% of episodes, mostly associated with antibiotics and nut allergies — but response to prompt treatment is usually rapid with good outcomes.
Neurogenic Shock

Mechanism

Vasomotor center depression (brain trauma) or interrupted sympathetic fibers (spinal cord injury, high spinal anesthesia, drug overdose) → loss of baroreceptor reflex pathway → profound arteriolar + venous vasodilation → peripheral pooling → ↓ venous return → ↓ CO → hypotension.

Position-dependence — a distinctive feature

Horizontal: venous return may be adequate, BP sufficient. Upright: gravitational peripheral pooling causes severe BP/CO drop → syncope. This position-sensitivity is fairly unique to neurogenic shock among the shock types and directly informs management (leg elevation, slow position changes, compression stockings).

Prognosis tracks the underlying neurologic injury: head trauma-related shock has a poor prognosis; spinal cord injury-related shock often resolves as spinal reflexes return over the following weeks — genuinely different trajectories despite similar acute presentation.
Septic Shock

Scale

Sepsis: ~49 million cases, >11 million deaths worldwide; ~25% mortality. Septic shock specifically: mortality approaching 60%. High-risk groups: very young, elderly, immunocompromised, debilitated/malnourished, those with invasive lines/catheters/surgery.

2016 definitions (updated from older criteria)

Sepsis: life-threatening organ dysfunction from a dysregulated host response to infection. Septic shock: sepsis requiring vasopressors to maintain MAP ≥65 mmHg with lactate >2 mmol/L despite adequate fluid resuscitation. Note: patients on vasopressors may NOT appear hypotensive when perfusion abnormalities are actually measured — a diagnostic subtlety.

Mind Map — Endotoxin to Systemic Inflammation

Organism profile & entry points

Gram-negative (E. coli, Klebsiella, Enterobacter, Serratia, Pseudomonas, Proteus) — endotoxin/LPS in cell wall (O antigen + R core + toxic lipid A) is the classic trigger. Also gram-positive (Staph aureus/epidermidis, Strep pneumoniae) and fungi (Candida). Common entry: genitourinary tract, also GI, respiratory, skin.

Downstream cascade activation

Macrophage cytokines activate neutrophils/platelets → PAF, free radicals, proteolytic enzymes, arachidonic acid metabolites (thromboxane A2/B2 → pulmonary vasoconstriction/bronchoconstriction/platelet aggregation; prostacyclin → vasodilation/hypotension). Complement (C5a, C3a) → microemboli, endothelial damage. Mast cell histamine → permeability/edema. Coagulation cascade → thrombi. Kinin system → bradykinin → vasodilation/permeability.

The hyperdynamic paradox — unique to early septic shock: high CO (8–12 L/min common) with warm extremities, YET the patient is hypotensive — because ↓ SVR and ↓ venous return outpace the compensatory CO increase. Even this elevated CO may be inadequate to perfuse the abnormally expanded vascular bed. If uncorrected, myocardial global ischemia can develop → systolic dysfunction → a MIXED septic + cardiogenic shock picture.
ARDS, DIC, Acute Renal Failure & MODS

ARDS

Most commonly associated with septic shock. Refractory hypoxemia + ↓ pulmonary compliance + noncardiogenic pulmonary edema (normal preload — distinguishes from cardiogenic pulmonary edema). Mortality with ARDS complicating shock: 34–64% — but the primary cause of death is MULTIPLE ORGAN FAILURE, not the hypoxemia itself. Mechanism: neutrophils migrate to pulmonary capillaries even from distant ischemic tissue → proteolytic enzymes/ROS/inflammatory mediators → leaky capillaries → protein-rich exudate floods interstitium/alveoli; type II pneumocyte damage → surfactant deficiency → alveolar collapse.

DIC

Immune activation of the clotting cascade → widespread microvascular clots (ischemic damage) + consumption of platelets/clotting factors (bleeding risk) — simultaneously. Labs: ↓ platelets, ↓ fibrinogen, ↑ D-dimer, ↑ PTT/PT. Clinical: acral ischemia (fingers/toes — pain, pallor, poor refill), organ failure from clot obstruction, oozing IV sites, bleeding incisions, hematuria/hemoptysis; spontaneous intracranial hemorrhage is a particularly severe complication.

Acute renal failure / ATN

Afferent arteriole vasoconstriction → ↓ glomerular blood flow/hydrostatic pressure/GFR. Hypoxic tubular cell damage after 15-20 minutes of acute ischemia → necrosis. Rising BUN/creatinine, urine output falls toward zero, unresponsive to fluids/diuretics, tubular epithelial cell casts in urine. Potentially REVERSIBLE but needs dialysis support; recovery begins 1-2 weeks post-injury, can take up to a year to complete.

MODS

Primary MODS: multiple organ injury directly from the initial insult (e.g., trauma). Secondary MODS: develops days-weeks after the primary insult, associated with SIRS — sepsis/septic shock is the most common cause.

MODS mortality scales sharply with organ count: 2 organ systems affected → 54% mortality. 5 organ systems → 100% mortality. This isn't a gradual slope — it's a steep, almost binary-feeling escalation that underscores why early intervention before multi-organ involvement is so critical.
