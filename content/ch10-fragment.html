Key Terms & Definitions

Start here
Autoimmunity
The immune system recognizing "self" as foreign and mounting a tissue-damaging response — a failure of self-tolerance.
Self-tolerance
The immune system's learned ability to not attack the body's own cells; involves both central and peripheral mechanisms.
Central tolerance
Elimination/suppression of self-reactive T and B cells during development in the thymus and bone marrow.
Peripheral tolerance
Backup mechanisms (clonal deletion, anergy, Treg suppression) controlling self-reactive lymphocytes that escaped central tolerance.
Regulatory T cells (Tregs)
CD4+CD25+FOXP3+ cells that suppress autoreactive T and B cells via inhibitory cytokines (IL-10, TGF-β) and CTLA-4.
Molecular (antigenic) mimicry
A theory of autoimmunity where pathogen epitopes resemble self-antigens closely enough that the immune response against the pathogen also attacks self tissue.
Hidden/sequestered antigen theory
Self-antigens normally isolated from immune surveillance (cornea, testicles, brain) become exposed after tissue damage and are attacked as foreign.
Epitope spread
Recurrent infection/organ damage causes the immune system to develop multiple new autoantibodies over time.
Hypersensitivity
A normal immune mechanism triggered inappropriately, excessively, or with undesirable effects; four types (I–IV).
IgE
The principal antibody mediating type I hypersensitivity; binds mast cell/basophil Fc receptors.
Mast cell degranulation
Release of preformed (histamine, heparin, enzymes) and newly-formed (leukotrienes, prostaglandins) mediators triggered by IgE-antigen cross-linking.
Anaphylaxis
A life-threatening, systemic type I hypersensitivity reaction.
Primary immunodeficiency
An immune deficiency not attributable to another cause — congenital or acquired (e.g., HIV/AIDS).
Secondary immunodeficiency
Immune impairment resulting from another disorder or treatment (chemotherapy, malnutrition, stress).
SCID
Severe combined immunodeficiency — genetic disorders causing absent/dysfunctional T cells (± B cells/NK cells), leaving both cellular and humoral immunity impaired.
DiGeorge syndrome (22q11.2 deletion)
A T-cell disorder from a hypoplastic/aplastic thymus due to a chromosome 22q11.2 deletion.
Wiskott-Aldrich syndrome
An X-linked disorder (WASp gene) causing B-cell dysfunction, microthrombocytopenia, and eczema.
Selective IgA deficiency
The most common B-cell PID — lack of serum IgA; often asymptomatic but linked to autoimmune/allergic conditions.
Excessive vs. Deficient Immune Responses

Mind Map — Two Broad Categories of Immune Disorder

Autoimmunity vs. hypersensitivity — a key distinction

Autoimmunity describes the CAUSE (immune attack on self). Hypersensitivity describes the MECHANISM of injury (how the damage happens) — which may or may not involve autoimmunity. Most autoimmune reactions occur via type II or III hypersensitivity mechanisms, so many autoimmune diseases are simultaneously classified as hypersensitivity reactions.

Worked examples of the overlap

Myasthenia gravis = autoimmune disease AND type II hypersensitivity. Immune complex glomerulonephritis = autoimmune disease AND type III hypersensitivity. But hypersensitivity to a foreign antigen (bee venom) is NOT autoimmune — the self/foreign distinction is what separates the two labels.

Autoimmune disorders range from organ-specific (MS → CNS; type 1 diabetes → pancreatic β-islet cells) to organ-nonspecific (SLE, Sjögren syndrome — multiple organs involved).
Self-Tolerance

Central tolerance

Occurs in the thymus (T cells) and bone marrow (B cells) — the thymus is principally responsible for eliminating/suppressing aggressive self-reactive T lymphocytes during development.

Peripheral tolerance — the backup system

A small number of self-reactive T cells always escape thymic control. Peripheral tolerance catches them via clonal deletion, anergy (T-cell intrinsic), and extrinsic control by Tregs (primarily CD4+CD25+FOXP3+).

How Tregs actually work: CD4+ T cells binding self-antigen on specialized APCs get triggered to express FOXP3, converting them into inhibitory Tregs. These Tregs secrete IL-10 and TGF-β, and express CTLA-4, which binds/blocks the B7 coreceptor on other immune cells — making those cells harder to activate. This is the same CTLA-4 checkpoint molecule cancer cells exploit (see Chapter 7).
Autoimmune disease develops when self-tolerance is lost — resulting in dysregulation of pro/anti-inflammatory mediators, cytokines, CD4+/CD8+ T cells, B cells, and dendritic cell regulatory enzymes.
Theories of Autoimmunity

No single theory fully explains autoimmunity — several proposed mechanisms, each with supporting (and limiting) evidence.

Molecular (antigenic) mimicry

Pathogen epitopes resemble self-antigens closely enough to be attacked by cross-reactive lymphocytes. Classic example: streptococcal M protein cross-reacts with human lysoganglioside → rheumatic heart disease. Another example: EBV peptide cross-reacts with a self-protein (Sm) in SLE patients.

Hidden/sequestered antigen theory

Self-antigens in immune-privileged sites (cornea, testicles, brain — areas not drained by lymphatics) were never "seen" during tolerance development. Tissue damage exposes them later, triggering attack. Challenged by: cardiac surgery/transplant patients universally develop anti-heart autoantibodies but do NOT develop myocarditis — showing tissue damage + microbe exposure isn't sufficient on its own.

Adjuvant/bystander effect

An infection activates immune cell receptors → proinflammatory cytokine release → activates PREEXISTING autoreactive T/B cells that had escaped thymic deletion. Unclear if this is nonspecific or Toll-like-receptor-specific.

Epitope spread

Recurrent infection/organ damage → immune system develops MULTIPLE autoantibodies over time (spreading beyond the original target).

Thymus gland defect theories

Impaired T-cell maturation/differentiation (decreased hormone secretion, or failure to expose developing T cells to ALL self products) → unrecognized self products get attacked later. Complicated by newer evidence that not all T cells actually mature in the thymus.

Treg dysfunction theory

Decreased Treg numbers/activity fail to repress autoreactive lymphocytes. Some Tregs lose FOXP3 and convert into effector T cells. A FOXP3 mutation itself can directly impair CD4+ T-cell development/function → autoimmune inflammation. TH22 cells (a newer subset) also drive chronic inflammation/autoimmunity via IL-13, IL-22, TNF production.

Serum vs. cell transferability

Types I, II, and III can all be transferred via serum (antibody-mediated). Type IV can ONLY be transferred via cells (T cells) — not serum. This single fact is a fast way to identify Type IV on an exam.

Type II has two subtypes

IIa: antibody injures/kills the target cell (classic cytotoxic). IIb: antibody STIMULATES the cell instead of injuring it (e.g., Graves disease — TSH receptor antibodies stimulate rather than destroy thyroid cells).

Type IV's four subtypes (a-d): IVa = CD4+ TH1 cells activating macrophages. IVb = CD4+ TH2 cells with eosinophil involvement (eosinophilic asthma). IVc = cytotoxic CD8+ T cells using perforin-granzyme B (Stevens-Johnson syndrome). IVd = T-lymphocyte-driven neutrophilic inflammation.
Type I Hypersensitivity — Etiology & Pathogenesis

Genetic component

Strong hereditary link to IgE responsiveness — both the ability to respond to a specific allergen AND general IgE-production capacity. Positive family history remains a strong predictor despite unclear specific causal genes.

Timing — why it's called "immediate"

Signs/symptoms typically appear 15–30 minutes after allergen exposure. Requires prior sensitization — repeated exposures are usually needed before significant IgE builds up.

Mind Map — Type I Hypersensitivity Cascade

Mast cells — the principal effector

Found throughout loose connective tissue; each covered with up to 500,000 IgE receptors, filled with granules of vasoactive/proinflammatory mediators (especially histamine).

Environmental amplifiers

Tobacco smoke and pollution can increase mucosal permeability, enhancing allergen entry — which in turn increases IgE levels and IgE responsiveness over time.

Histamine & Other Mediators

Preformed vs. newly formed mediators

Preformed (stored in granules): histamine, heparin, proteolytic enzymes, cytokines, chemotactic factors. Newly formed (made during degranulation): superoxide, prostaglandins, thromboxanes, leukotrienes, bradykinin, interleukins.

H1 vs. H2 receptors — largely opposing effects

H1 (most active on mast cells): ↑ vascular permeability, vasodilation (flushing), urticaria, bronchoconstriction, ↑ mucus/pruritus, ↑ gut permeability. H2: smooth muscle RELAXATION in lower airways, ↑ gastric acid secretion, and at high concentration actually INHIBITS inflammatory cells (↓ degranulation, ↓ neutrophil chemotaxis).

H3 & H4 receptors

H3: brain, spinal cord, sensory neurons (postganglionic cholinergic nerves in lung bronchi). H4: immune cells (dendritic cells, eosinophils, T cells, monocytes, macrophages, NK cells) and hematopoietic tissue — strongly involved in chemotaxis/inflammation.

Other key mediators

Kininogenase/tryptase activate the kinin pathway; C3 activates complement via the alternative pathway; heparin decreases clotting; leukotrienes cause smooth muscle contraction + ↑ vascular permeability. IL-9 from mucosal mast cells is elevated in food-mediated hypersensitivity specifically.

Type I — Clinical Manifestations, Treatment & Prevention

Spectrum of severity

Mild: urticaria, seasonal rhinitis, eczema, mild bronchoconstriction. More severe: throat tightening, localized edema, wheezing, tachycardia. Most severe: anaphylaxis — classically from bee stings, seafood, or peanuts.

Common triggers to recognize

Medications (penicillin, NSAIDs, contrast media), biologics (antisera, insulin, vaccines), latex, pollen/mold/dander/dust mites, insect venom (Hymenoptera), foods (peanuts, tree nuts, shellfish, eggs, milk), and irritants (smoke, diesel exhaust, perfumes).

Primary vs. Secondary Immunodeficiency

Primary

Not attributable to another cause — congenital or acquired (HIV/AIDS is a primary ACQUIRED immunodeficiency). Congenital PIDs occur in ~1 in 58,000 infants, often sex-linked.

Secondary

A consequence of another disorder or treatment — malnutrition, hyperlipidemia, cancer chemotherapy, or biopsychosocial stress (e.g., postsurgical immune suppression).

First clinical clue for most immunodeficiency disorders: signs/symptoms of infection. A growing body of genetic evidence suggests many people carry inborn immunity errors of variable clinical significance — changing how immunodeficiency is understood as a spectrum rather than all-or-nothing.
Severe Combined Immunodeficiency (SCID)

Nearly all SCID types share absent/dysfunctional T cells (impairing both cellular AND humoral immunity, since B cells need T-cell help) — but B-cell and NK-cell involvement varies by genetic defect.

Clinical presentation

Ill within 3 months of age: failure to thrive, persistent oral thrush, persistent diarrhea, severe Candida diaper dermatitis, recurrent severe infections, lymphadenopathy, hepatosplenomegaly. High mortality untreated, usually before age 1.

Susceptible pathogens

Candida albicans, Pneumocystis jiroveci, CMV, herpesvirus, and common childhood diseases (varicella, measles) — opportunistic and severe because they can't mount an effective response even when B cells are technically present.

Management: protective isolation, strict handwashing, avoid other children pre-discharge, CMV-negative breastfeeding only. Curative therapy: hematopoietic stem cell transplant (cord blood or bone marrow, ideally HLA-haploidentical sibling). Also: chemotherapy conditioning, long-term immunoglobulin replacement (q2-3 weeks), enzyme replacement, gene therapy. Newborn screening now exists in 23 states + DC + Navajo Nation given the urgency of early detection.
Wiskott-Aldrich Syndrome

Genetics

X-linked (males only) — WASp gene mutation, Xp11.23. WASp is involved in cytoplasmic signaling, immune cell function, and actin cytoskeleton regulation in hematopoietic cells.

Lab pattern

↓ Mature peripheral B cells; ↓ IgM, ↑ IgE and IgA, normal-to-low IgG (from increased antibody catabolism); low/absent isohemagglutinins; low platelets (splenic destruction from abnormal platelet size/shape).

Classic triad: microthrombocytopenia + eczema + increased infection/malignancy susceptibility. Particular vulnerability to polysaccharide-encapsulated bacteria (S. pneumoniae, H. influenzae) — pneumonia, meningitis, otitis media, sepsis. Can develop bleeding (bloody diarrhea, cerebral hemorrhage) and systemic autoimmunity. Untreated average age at death: 3.5 years.
Treatment: antibody replacement + antibiotics; splenectomy, bone marrow/stem cell transplant, gene therapy as definitive options.
DiGeorge Syndrome (22q11.2 Deletion)

Genetics & mechanism

Autosomal dominant, chromosome 22q11.2 deletion (usually maternally inherited) — most common microdeletion syndrome (~1/4000 live births, equal sex distribution). Causes a hypoplastic/aplastic thymus → variable T-cell deficiency; B cells usually normal unless T-cell loss is severe enough to also impair B-cell function (SCID-like presentation).

The classic triad

Immunodeficiency (75%) + congenital cardiac anomalies (75%) + hypoparathyroidism (50%).

Other features

Hypothyroidism, esophageal atresia/reflux, urogenital anomalies, growth restriction, developmental delay, psychiatric/behavioral issues, microcephaly, dysmorphic facial features (mandibular hypoplasia, short forehead, low-set ears).

Treatment

Thymic or bone marrow transplant for reestablishing T-cell populations in severe cases; targeted management of cardiac defects, hypocalcemia/seizures, and psychiatric issues.

Chronic Mucocutaneous Candidiasis Disease (CMCD)

Genetics

Autosomal dominant gain-of-function STAT1 mutation → impaired/absent IL-17 (or IL-17F) → selective cell-mediated immunity deficiency specifically against C. albicans and S. aureus.

Clinical picture

B-cell and T-cell function are otherwise NORMAL except for this specific inability to respond to candidal infection — causing persistent/recurrent skin, nail, and mucous membrane infections.

Treatment: antifungal/antibiotic therapy to reduce infection severity/scarring; gene therapy under investigation.
Selective IgA Deficiency

The most common B-cell PID

Incidence varies by ancestry: 1/600 (White populations) vs. 1/2600 (Asian populations). Mechanism: plasma cell apoptosis or failure of IgA-bearing lymphocytes to mature into plasma cells. B-cell count is normal, but B cells don't respond properly to IL-4/6/7/10. IgM, IgG, and T-cell numbers are all normal.

Clinical picture

Often completely asymptomatic. When symptomatic: respiratory/GI/GU infections. High rate of associated allergic, vascular, endocrine, and autoimmune disease (IBD, celiac, SLE, chronic active hepatitis) — and a tendency to develop anti-IgA antibodies.

Critical transfusion consideration: because these patients can develop anti-IgA antibodies, exogenous IgA replacement is contraindicated, and when blood products are needed, washed/packed RBCs with volume expanders must be used instead of standard products — to avoid exposing the patient to IgA and triggering a reaction.
