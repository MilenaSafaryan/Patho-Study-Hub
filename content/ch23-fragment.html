Key Terms & Definitions

Start here
Restrictive lung disease
Decreased lung expansion from parenchymal, pleural, chest wall, or neuromuscular causes; ↓ TLC, VC, FRC, RV.
Idiopathic pulmonary fibrosis (IPF)
Chronic interstitial lung disease of the elderly from chronic epithelial injury, aging, and genetic predisposition.
Honeycomb lung
The radiographic end-stage appearance of IPF fibrosis.
Sarcoidosis
A systemic disease of unknown cause producing noncaseating granulomas, most often in lymph nodes/lung.
Pneumoconiosis
Parenchymal lung disease from chronic inhalation of inorganic dust (coal, silica, asbestos).
ARDS
Acute hypoxemia + noncardiogenic pulmonary edema + reduced lung compliance from alveolar-capillary membrane damage.
NRDS
Neonatal respiratory distress from surfactant deficiency in premature infants.
L/S ratio
Lecithin/sphingomyelin ratio in amniotic fluid; ≥2:1 indicates fetal lung maturity.
Spontaneous pneumothorax
Air in the pleural space without trauma — primary (no underlying disease) or secondary (existing lung disease).
Tension pneumothorax
Air accumulates under pressure, unable to escape — mediastinal shift, medical emergency.
Transudate
Low-protein/LDH pleural effusion from hydrostatic/oncotic pressure imbalance (e.g., heart failure).
Exudate
High-protein/LDH pleural effusion from increased capillary permeability (e.g., infection, malignancy).
Kyphoscoliosis
Combined posterior (kyphosis) and lateral (scoliosis) spinal curvature restricting lung expansion.
Flail chest
Free-floating chest wall segment from rib fractures at two sites, moving paradoxically with breathing.
Ghon tubercle
The calcified, fibrotic granuloma marking primary TB infection — the pathologic hallmark of TB.
Miliary tuberculosis
Disseminated TB from hematogenous spread, producing diffuse small nodular densities.
Mantoux (PPD) test
Intradermal tuberculin skin test; induration size interpreted against risk category to assess TB exposure.
What Makes a Disease "Restrictive"

The shared physiologic signature

↓ TLC, ↓ VC, ↓ FRC, ↓ RV — greater volume decrease = greater severity. ABGs: ↓ PaO2 with normal/↓ PaCO2 → respiratory alkalosis (a useful contrast with obstructive disease's tendency toward hypercapnia).

Pulmonary vs. extrapulmonary classification

Pulmonary (parenchymal): pneumonia, granulomatous disease (sarcoidosis, TB), pneumoconiosis, ILD, atelectasis, vascular disease (PE, edema), ARDS. Extrapulmonary: chest wall disease (kyphoscoliosis, ankylosing spondylitis, obesity), neuromuscular disease, pleural disease, and other causes (abdominal distention, surgery, pregnancy).

Age-related vulnerability, in brief: infants have cartilaginous ribs, poorly developed accessory muscles, and fewer alveoli/collateral pathways (Pores of Kohn, channels of Lambert) — restriction relies heavily on diaphragm function. Elderly patients have ↓ ciliary activity, ↓ chest wall compliance, ↓ cough reflex, and impaired immunity — all independently raising infection/restriction risk.
Idiopathic Pulmonary Fibrosis

Etiology

Disease of the elderly (predominantly >60). Incidence: 0.9–9.3/100,000 (Europe/N. America), 3.5–13/100,000 (Asia/S. America). SNPs linked to telomere length, surfactant production, mitogenesis, immunity — alongside familial predisposition.

The three-hit pathogenesis

Chronic epithelial injury (inhaled toxins) + aging + genetic predisposition → poor wound healing (alveolar stem cells fail to regenerate) → profibrotic/antifibrotic imbalance → overstimulated wound-healing cells → excess collagen/ECM. Self-reinforcing: ↓ compliance itself drives further fibrosis.

Clinical picture

Progressive dyspnea + nonproductive cough; clubbing (40-80%); bibasilar "Velcro" end-expiratory crackles; late cyanosis; anorexia/weight loss.

Diagnosis & treatment

HRCT: UIP pattern (honeycombing ± traction bronchiectasis). PFTs: ↓ VC, ↓ TLC, ↓ diffusing capacity. First-line: antifibrotics (pirfenidone, nintedanib) — slow decline AND improve survival. O2 therapy target SpO2 >88%. Prognosis remains poor overall (irreversible, progressive).

Sarcoidosis

Epidemiology

Bimodal age (peaks 30 and 50). US incidence 60/100,000 (90% with lung/thoracic node involvement). Highest in Black individuals (18/100,000) vs. White (8), Hispanic (4), Asian (3). First-degree relative → 5x risk. HLA-DRB1/DQB1 variants linked to progressive disease.

Pathogenesis

Noncaseating epithelioid granulomas (CD4+ T cell-rich, surrounded by Tregs/B cells/fibroblasts) from innate + adaptive immune activation — trigger antigen unknown, though silica/metal inhalation and certain bacteria (Propionibacterium, Mycobacterium) are associated.

Clinical features

Malaise, fatigue, weight loss, fever, insidious dyspnea, dry cough. Also: erythema nodosum, skin lesions, hepatosplenomegaly, lymphadenopathy. Acute: enlarged nodes + arthritis. Ocular: uveitis (65%), iritis, conjunctivitis.

Diagnosis — the Scadding staging system

Stage 0: normal. I: hilar adenopathy alone. II: hilar adenopathy + bilateral infiltrates. III: infiltrates without adenopathy. IV: advanced fibrosis/honeycombing. Labs: leukopenia, anemia, ↑ eosinophils, ↑ ESR, ↑ calcium (11%), ↑ ACE (40-80%), anergy to common antigens (70%). Definitive diagnosis: transbronchial biopsy (60-90% yield).

Pneumoconiosis

Macrophage-driven pathogenesis

Alveolar macrophages engulf inorganic dust and secrete lysozymes (H2O2) to control it — this same process damages alveolar walls, depositing fibrous material. Redox-active metals (iron, copper in coal) generate reactive O2/N species → lipid peroxidation → ↑ TNF-α → chronic inflammation → fibrosis.

The silent-latency trap

Workers remain SYMPTOM-FREE for 10-20 years despite ongoing chronic exposure — meaning accurate exposure history/timeline matters more than current symptoms for staging. Atmospheric pollutants (sulfur oxides, tobacco smoke) worsen disease by paralyzing the mucociliary clearance system that would otherwise help clear dust.

ARDS

Scale & the classic triad

Acute hypoxemia (refractory to supplemental O2) + noncardiogenic pulmonary edema + ↓ lung compliance. >190,000 US cases/year; ~38.5% hospital mortality. Causes: sepsis (>40%, 90% mortality when combined), aspiration (>30%), trauma (20%), fat emboli, shock of any cause. Black/Hispanic patients and males have higher mortality.

Pathogenesis

Direct/indirect alveolar injury → TLR activation → chemokines/ROS/neutrophil extracellular traps → endothelial + epithelial dysfunction → fluid/protein/immune cell flooding into airways. ↓ Compliance from surfactant loss/inactivation + proteinaceous alveolar fluid → high ventilatory pressures needed. ↓ PaO2 from shunt (unventilated) + low V/Q (poorly ventilated) alveoli.

Two phenotypes

Hyperinflammatory: ↑ IL-6, IL-8, sTNFR-1, ICAM-1 — more organ dysfunction, shock, ↓ protein C, higher mortality/ventilator days. Hypoinflammatory: less severe. Driver of phenotype selection is unclear.

Clinical course

Onset within a week of the precipitating event. Progression: ↑ pulse, dyspnea, ↓ PaO2 → tachycardia, tachypnea, hypotension, restlessness, frothy secretions, crackles/rhonchi, accessory muscle use; cyanosis is LATE. Diagnosis: 2012 Berlin criteria (+ Kigali modification) — bilateral patchy/diffuse opacities without an alternative hypoxia cause.

Survivors of the acute injury generally return to near-normal lung function, though follow-up studies (9 months-4 years) show mild residual restrictive changes, cough, dyspnea, and excess sputum in some.
Neonatal Respiratory Distress Syndrome

Incidence & risk factors

60% in infants <30 weeks without antenatal steroids (35% with steroids); 5% in infants >34 weeks. Risk factors: birth <25 weeks, poorly controlled maternal diabetes, antepartum hemorrhage, C-section without labor, perinatal asphyxia, multiples, prior affected infant, Rh incompatibility. Mechanism link: labor normally triggers catecholamine release + surfactant release + fetal lung fluid clearance — bypassing labor removes this protective trigger.

Core pathophysiology

Surfactant deficiency (type II cells mature after ~32 weeks) → ↑ alveolar surface tension → ↓ compliance (to 1/5–1/10 of normal) → neonate must generate 25–30 mmHg intrathoracic pressure to keep alveoli open, but has a soft, compliant chest wall that resists this. Result: progressive atelectasis, ↑ pulmonary vascular resistance, hypoxemia, acidosis. Leakage of proteinaceous fluid + fibrin/cellular debris → hyaline membrane formation.

Presentation

Shallow respirations, retractions, nasal flaring (↑ airway diameter to overcome resistance), hypotension, low temp, oliguria, tachypnea (60-120/min), bradycardia. Late: frothy sputum, central cyanosis, expiratory grunt (physiologic auto-PEEP against a partially closed glottis), "see-saw" paradoxical chest movement.

Diagnosis & treatment

L/S ratio ≥2:1 (≥3:1 if diabetic mother) = mature lungs; phosphatidylglycerol presence also indicates maturity. CXR: normal → diffuse "ground-glass"/whiteout. Treatment: antenatal steroids (↑ compliance ~23%), mechanical ventilation with PEEP/CPAP, exogenous surfactant (↓ mortality 50%), lowest effective FiO2 (high FiO2 risks retrolental fibroplasia/blindness), high-frequency ventilation for severe cases.

Pneumothorax

Three types

Primary spontaneous: tall, thin males 20-40, no underlying disease; smoking ↑ risk; ~20,000 US cases/year, 6x more common in males. From ruptured apical subpleural blebs (negative pressure zone in the upright lung). Secondary: complication of existing lung disease (asthma, emphysema, CF, TB, ILD). Tension: traumatic, medical emergency.

Catamenial pneumothorax

A distinct secondary subtype linked to menstruation and endometriosis, mechanism unknown, occurs primarily in the RIGHT hemothorax — a genuinely odd, specific pattern worth remembering as an exception.

Tension pneumothorax mechanism

Air enters on inspiration, can't escape on expiration → progressive pressure buildup → ipsilateral lung collapse + mediastinal shift to the CONTRALATERAL side → ↓ venous return/CO. Presentation: severe tachycardia, hypotension, tracheal deviation, JVD, hyperresonance, subcutaneous emphysema.

Treatment thresholds

<15-25% collapse: may not require hospitalization, monitor for spontaneous resolution over weeks. >15-25%: chest tube + 100% O2 (quadruples reabsorption rate by lowering pleural nitrogen partial pressure). Recurrent SP: chemical pleurodesis or thoracotomy with stapling/laser. Recurrence rate: 25-50% within 1 year for primary SP.

Pleural Effusion

Normal physiology

~17 mL normal pleural fluid; constant turnover from parietal capillaries → pleural space → parietal lymphatics.

Transudate vs. exudate — the defining lab cutoffs

Transudate: pleural/serum protein <0.5, pleural/serum LDH <0.6, specific gravity <1.016 — from ↑ hydrostatic or ↓ oncotic pressure (heart failure, cirrhosis/ascites, nephrotic syndrome, myxedema). Exudate: protein >0.5, LDH ratio >0.6 — from malignancy, infection/pneumonia, PE, sarcoidosis, post-MI syndrome, pancreatic disease.

Five types: transudate, exudate, empyema (infected, high-protein exudate), hemothorax (fluid hematocrit >50% of peripheral blood — usually from trauma), and chylothorax (lymphatic leak from thoracic duct trauma, rheumatoid effusion, or TB pleuritis).

Chest Wall Deformities

Kyphoscoliosis

80-90% idiopathic (adolescent onset, 4-5:1 female); remainder congenital (Pott disease) or NMD-related. Higher vertebral deformity = greater respiratory compromise. Treatment thresholds: <20° monitor; moderate → brace; >40° → surgical stabilization (Harrington rod); >60° correlates with poor later pulmonary function.

Ankylosing spondylitis

3:1 male, ages 15-35. 90% HLA-B27 positive. TNF-driven inflammation → ligament/costovertebral/sacroiliac joint fibrosis → calcification/ossification → chest wall immobility. Breathing shifts to diaphragm-dependent as ribcage fixes in place. ESR elevated in 85%. Treatment: exercise/breathing programs + NSAIDs.

Flail chest: ribs fractured at TWO distant sites → free-floating segment moves PARADOXICALLY (inward on inspiration, outward on expiration) — opposite of normal chest wall movement. Classic mechanism: steering wheel chest impact in an MVA. Often accompanied by pulmonary contusion, pneumothorax, hemothorax, subcutaneous emphysema. PaO2 often drops BEFORE clinical symptoms become obvious — a dangerous lag.
Pneumonia

Three entry routes

(1) Aspiration of oropharyngeal secretions/gastric contents (20-35% of all pneumonias), (2) inhalation of contaminants (virus, mycoplasma), (3) hematogenous spread from systemic circulation.

Bacterial vs. viral exudate — a key pathogenesis distinction

Bacterial: alveolar exudate consolidates, hard to expectorate, causes significant V/Q mismatch/hypoxemia. Viral: does NOT produce exudative fluid — a fundamentally different pathologic picture even though both cause "pneumonia."

Pulmonary Tuberculosis

Scale & risk groups

1/3 of world population infected; ~2 million deaths/year. US: 3/100,000, >90% from REACTIVATION of prior infection. Risk groups: malnourished, overcrowded living, immunosuppressed, incarcerated, immigrants (36% of new US cases), elderly; 2/3 of new US cases in racial/ethnic minorities.

Primary vs. reactivation TB

Primary: usually clinically/radiographically silent, can lie dormant for decades. Reactivation: occurs when immunity is impaired — HIV, corticosteroids, silicosis, diabetes are known triggers. Miliary TB: disseminated hematogenous spread.

Mind Map — From Inhalation to Ghon Tubercle

Ghon tubercle anatomy

Parenchymal component: well-circumscribed necrotic nodule → later fibrotic/calcified. Lymph component: in the draining lymph nodes. M. tuberculosis prefers HIGH-oxygen areas — this is why reactivation TB localizes to apical posterior segments of the upper lobes and superior segments of the lower lobes.

Clinical presentation

Low-grade fever, chronic cough (most common symptom), hemoptysis, night sweats, fatigue, weight loss, malaise, anorexia. Apical crackles on exam. Extrapulmonary sites: peritoneum, GI tract, liver, spleen, bone/joints, lymph nodes, CNS, GU system.
