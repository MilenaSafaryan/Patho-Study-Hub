Key Terms & Definitions

Start here
ECV imbalance (saline imbalance)
A change in the AMOUNT of extracellular fluid with normal concentration — too much or too little isotonic fluid.
Concentration imbalance (water imbalance)
A change in the osmolality/serum sodium concentration of extracellular fluid, independent of total volume.
Third spacing
Sodium-containing fluid sequestered outside the accessible extracellular compartment (e.g., ascites) — causes ECV deficit signs without external fluid loss.
Hyponatremia
Serum sodium below 135 mEq/L — extracellular fluid too dilute.
Dilutional hyponatremia
Hyponatremia from gaining relatively more water than salt (e.g., SIADH, psychogenic polydipsia).
Depletional hyponatremia
Hyponatremia from losing relatively more salt than water (e.g., thiazide diuretics).
SIADH
Syndrome of inappropriate ADH secretion — excessive water retention causing dilutional hyponatremia.
Hypokalemia
Serum potassium <3.5 mEq/L — causes hyperpolarization of muscle cells.
Hyperkalemia
Serum potassium >5.0 mEq/L — causes hypopolarization of muscle cells.
Ionized calcium
The physiologically active, unbound fraction of serum calcium (~half of total serum calcium).
Hypocalcemia
Serum calcium below 9 mg/dL — increased neuromuscular excitability (Trousseau/Chvostek signs).
Hypercalcemia
Serum calcium above 11 mg/dL — decreased neuromuscular excitability.
Trousseau sign
Carpal spasm after ~3 minutes of arterial occlusion to the hand — a hypocalcemia indicator.
Chvostek sign
Facial muscle spasm from tapping the facial nerve near the ear — a hypocalcemia indicator (can be positive in normal neonates).
Fluid Compartments & Homeostasis

The 2/3 - 1/3 split

In all age groups except infants, ~2/3 of body fluid is intracellular, 1/3 extracellular. Infants START with MORE extracellular than intracellular fluid — this reverses within a few months of life.

Compartment composition — opposite profiles

Intracellular: rich in potassium, magnesium, phosphates, proteins; low sodium/chloride. Extracellular (vascular + interstitial): rich in sodium, chloride, bicarbonate; low potassium/magnesium/phosphate. Vascular fluid has abundant protein; interstitial/transcellular fluid has very little.

Total body water by age/body composition

Full-term newborn: ~75% water (preterm even higher). Lean adult male: ~60%. Adult female: ~50% (more body fat). Older male: ~50%; older female: ~45%. More body fat = less body water, since fat holds relatively little water.

Thirst triggers

↑ Extracellular osmolality (osmoreceptor-mediated), ↓ circulating volume (baroreceptor- and angiotensin II-mediated), dry oral mucous membranes. Older adults have DIMINISHED osmoreceptor-mediated thirst — a key reason dehydration risk rises with age independent of access to water.

Starling Forces & Fluid Distribution

Mind Map — The Four Starling Forces at the Capillary

Vascular ↔ interstitial: filtration

Net result of the four opposing Starling forces above — "strongest push wins" at any given point along the capillary, like a swinging door pushed from both sides.

Interstitial ↔ intracellular: osmosis, not filtration

Water crosses cell membranes via aquaporins — a fundamentally different transport mechanism than the vascular/interstitial exchange, which matters for understanding why sodium (extracellular) and potassium (intracellular) imbalances affect fluid shifts differently.

ECV Deficit

Definition — concentration stays normal

Removal of sodium-containing (isotonic) fluid from the extracellular compartment — the AMOUNT decreases but the CONCENTRATION stays normal. Causes: GI loss (emesis, diarrhea, suction, fistula), renal loss (adrenal insufficiency, salt-wasting disease, diuretics, bed rest), other loss (hemorrhage, diaphoresis, third-spacing, paracentesis, burns).

Third-spacing — an easy-to-miss cause

Fluid like ascites or bowel-obstruction fluid REMAINS in the body but becomes inaccessible to the extracellular compartment — producing full ECV deficit signs/symptoms WITHOUT any fluid leaving the body and often without measurable weight loss.

Sudden weight loss — the most sensitive marker

1 L saline = 1 kg. Losing 1 kg in 24 hours = 1 L of fluid lost — it's physiologically IMPOSSIBLE to lose a kilogram of fat overnight, so rapid weight loss this size is essentially diagnostic of fluid loss (accurate scale required).

Other manifestations

Postural BP drop + reflex ↑ HR (orthostatic testing), flat neck veins supine, prolonged capillary/vein refill, oliguria with concentrated urine, dizziness/syncope. Slower-developing deficits also show: ↓ skin turgor, dry oral mucosa, hard stools, sunken eyes, tongue furrows, absent tears/sweat. Infants: sunken fontanel (neck veins unreliable in infants).

Severe ECV deficit can progress to hypovolemic shock, which is fatal without effective treatment. Treatment: isotonic sodium-containing fluid replacement.
ECV Excess

Etiology

Addition/retention of isotonic saline. Causes: excessive IV isotonic infusion (normal saline, Ringer/lactated Ringer), or renal Na+/water retention (hyperaldosteronism, chronic HF, cirrhosis, acute glomerulonephritis, end-stage kidney disease, Cushing disease, corticosteroid therapy).

Clinical manifestations

Sudden weight gain (same 1kg=1L logic as deficit, in reverse), edema, and circulatory overload signs: bounding pulse, upright neck vein distention, dependent crackles, dyspnea, orthopnea, frothy sputum if severe pulmonary edema. Infants: bulging fontanel.

Why low-sodium diets are prescribed in compensated heart failure: sodium holds water in the extracellular compartment — even a salty meal alone can cause measurable next-day weight gain from the isotonic saline formed by the salt + the water someone drinks in response.
Hyponatremia

Dilutional hyponatremia (gain of water > salt)

SIADH, excessive D5W infusion, hypotonic irrigation, tap water enemas, psychogenic polydipsia, forced water ingestion, beer potomania, freshwater near-drowning, SSRIs. Ectopic ADH: small cell lung cancer commonly synthesizes/releases ADH — NOT subject to normal feedback inhibition, so inappropriate amounts keep being released regardless of actual osmolality.

Depletional hyponatremia (loss of salt > water)

Thiazide diuretics (especially in older females), salt-wasting renal disease, replacing GI/sweat/burn losses with water but not salt.

Marathon/triathlon hyponatremia is BOTH mechanisms at once: salt loss through heavy sweating PLUS water gain from excessive intake PLUS inappropriate ADH-driven water reabsorption — a genuinely combined-mechanism example worth remembering as the exception to the either/or framing.
Clinical manifestations = nonspecific CNS dysfunction from neuron/glial cell swelling (water moves INTO cells by osmosis when extracellular fluid becomes relatively more dilute than intracellular fluid). Progression: malaise, anorexia, nausea, headache → confusion, lethargy, seizures, coma. Profound hyponatremia can cause fatal cerebral herniation. Rate matters: a rapid drop in osmolality produces more severe symptoms than an equally low value reached slowly.

The four-process balance

Plasma electrolyte concentration = net result of intake, absorption, distribution, and excretion — a dynamic system where changes in one process trigger compensatory changes in another (e.g., ↑ intake → ↑ excretion to normalize).

Distribution — the hormone-controlled shift

Electrolytes move between extracellular fluid and "electrolyte pools" (cells + bone) via epinephrine (K+), insulin (K+ and phosphate), and PTH (calcium) — these shifts can happen within MINUTES, independent of any actual intake/excretion change.

Electrolyte loss through abnormal routes (emesis, NG suction, paracentesis, hemodialysis, wound/fistula drainage) is a distinct category from normal urine/fecal/sweat excretion — clinically important because it often requires deliberate intake/excretion adjustment to compensate, unlike routine losses the body already regulates automatically.
Hypokalemia

Etiology categories

↓ Intake (anorexia, NPO, fasting, poor diet access). Shift INTO cells (alkalosis, excess insulin, β-adrenergic stimulation, hypokalemic periodic paralysis). ↑ Renal excretion (potassium-wasting diuretics, corticosteroids, Cushing disease, hyperaldosteronism, hypomagnesemia). ↑ Fecal/skin loss (diarrhea, diaphoresis). Abnormal-route loss (emesis, suction, fistula).

The black licorice mechanism — a genuinely specific fact

Glycyrrhizin in black licorice inactivates the enzyme that normally degrades cortisol → excess cortisol stimulates the SAME mineralocorticoid receptors aldosterone acts on → increased renal potassium excretion. Found in traditional medicines across many cultures (China, Korea, India, Egypt, Middle East, Mexico) — a real-world source easy to overlook.

Mechanism: hyperpolarization → both smooth and skeletal muscle become LESS reactive to stimuli. Manifestations: abdominal distention, ↓ bowel sounds, paralytic ileus, postural hypotension, skeletal weakness/flaccid paralysis (ASCENDING, starts in lower extremities, can reach respiratory muscles — MORE commonly causes respiratory paralysis than hyperkalemia does). Cardiac: ectopic beats, ↓ AV conduction velocity, prolonged action potentials, shortened absolute/prolonged relative refractory period. Also: polyuria (interferes with ADH action at renal tubules). Chronic hypokalemia: rhabdomyolysis, myocardial necrosis, nephropathy.
Hyperkalemia

Etiology categories

↑ Intake (rapid/excessive IV potassium, massive stored-blood transfusion — stored RBCs leak K+, high-dose penicillin G potassium). Shift OUT of cells (metabolic acidosis from nonorganic acids, insulin deficiency, crushing injury, tumor lysis syndrome, hyperkalemic periodic paralysis). ↓ Excretion (oliguria/AKI/CKD, potassium-sparing diuretics, adrenal insufficiency, ACE inhibitors/ARBs/aldosterone blockers, nephrotoxic ATN).

Potassium adaptation in CKD

Chronic end-stage kidney disease patients often develop tolerance — relatively mild symptoms at potassium levels that would be disabling in others. Mechanism: ↑ aldosterone increases COLONIC potassium excretion (compensating for lost renal excretion) and shifts K+ into cells, helping normalize resting membrane potentials.

Mechanism: hypopolarization — opposite direction from hypokalemia, but BOTH cause muscle weakness (via different mechanisms). Mild: intestinal cramping/diarrhea. Worse: resting membrane potential rises above threshold → cells discharge once, then can't re-contract → skeletal weakness/flaccid paralysis (also ascending, starts in lower extremities). Cardiac: ↓ duration/rate of rise of action potentials, ↓ conduction velocity → dysrhythmias; severe hyperkalemia → cardiac arrest.
Hypocalcemia

Total vs. ionized calcium — a critical distinction

Only UNBOUND ionized calcium is physiologically active. Total serum calcium can be NORMAL while ionized calcium is LOW if more calcium is bound to proteins/citrate — "ionized hypocalcemia" with a normal total calcium lab value. Classic cause: massive transfusion of citrated blood/FFP (citrate binds calcium).

Etiology categories

↓ Intake/absorption (poor diet, CKD's deficient active vitamin D, excess phytates/oxalates, steatorrhea, pancreatitis, malabsorption). ↓ Physiologic availability (hypoparathyroidism, excess phosphate, tumor lysis syndrome, hypomagnesemia, alkalosis, citrated blood transfusion, elevated free fatty acids, CKD). ↑ Excretion (steatorrhea, pancreatitis).

Pancreatitis's dual mechanism

Lack of pancreatic lipase → impaired fat digestion → BOTH dietary calcium AND calcium secreted into the gut bind to undigested fat and are excreted — decreased absorption AND increased excretion simultaneously, plus calcium can bind to necrotic pancreatic tissue, reducing physiologic availability further.

Mechanism: increased neuromuscular excitability

↓ Threshold potential → action potentials generate more easily. Trousseau sign: carpal spasm after ~3 min arterial occlusion. Chvostek sign: facial spasm from tapping the facial nerve (can be positive in normal neonates — interpret cautiously). Also: paresthesias, muscle twitching/cramping, hyperactive reflexes, tetany, laryngospasm, seizures. Cardiac: prolonged action potential plateau, impaired AV/intraventricular conduction, impaired contractility → can cause heart failure.

Hypercalcemia

Etiology categories

↑ Intake/absorption (milk-alkali syndrome, vitamin D overdose including shark cartilage supplements). Shift from BONE to ECF (hyperparathyroidism, immobilization, Paget disease, bone tumors, multiple myeloma, leukemia, nonosseous malignancies producing bone-resorbing factors). ↓ Excretion (thiazide diuretics, familial hypocalciuric hypercalcemia).

Malignancy's dual mechanism

Tumors can produce PTH-related protein and prostaglandins that shift calcium OUT of bone AND separately decrease renal calcium excretion — two simultaneous pathways converging on hypercalcemia, similar in structure to pancreatitis's dual hypocalcemia mechanism above.

Mechanism: DECREASED neuromuscular excitability — the exact opposite direction from hypocalcemia. ↑ Threshold potential → harder to generate action potentials. Manifestations: anorexia, nausea, emesis, constipation, fatigue, polyuria, muscle weakness, diminished reflexes, headache, confusion, lethargy, personality change. Cardiac: shortened action potential plateau, ↑ sinus node diastolic depolarization rate, delayed AV conduction. Can cause nephrocalcinosis (kidney tissue calcium deposits) or renal calculi.
