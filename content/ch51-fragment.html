Key Terms & Definitions

Start here
Osteoarthritis (OA)
A progressive, noninflammatory disease of diarthrodial (freely movable) joints, especially weight-bearing ones — the most common form of arthritis worldwide.
Diarthrodial joint
A freely movable joint (e.g., knee, hip) — the type of joint OA specifically targets.
Articular cartilage
The smooth tissue covering joint surfaces; its progressive loss is the defining feature of OA. Contains no nociceptors, so cartilage itself cannot directly cause pain.
Chondrocytes
Cartilage cells that release proteolytic and collagenolytic enzymes after joint injury, starting the matrix breakdown process in OA.
Osteophytes
Bone spurs that form at joint margins from new bone formation — a hallmark radiologic and structural finding in OA.
Subchondral bone sclerosis
Thickening/hardening of the bone just beneath the cartilage, occurring as cartilage loss progresses in OA.
Heberden nodes
Bone spurs at the distal interphalangeal (DIP) joints — the most common hand deformity in OA.
Bouchard nodes
Bone spurs at the proximal interphalangeal (PIP) joints — analogous to Heberden nodes but one joint level up.
Genu varus
Outward bowing of the knees ("bow-legged") — can result from cartilage loss in the medial knee compartment.
Genu valgus
Inward angling of the knees ("knock-kneed") — can result from cartilage loss in the lateral knee compartment.
Crepitus
A grating/crackling sensation or sound with joint movement — a classic OA physical exam finding.
Erosive OA
An aggressive hand-OA subset with central subchondral bone erosion, more joint swelling, and severe mobility loss; tends to affect younger patients (females 40–60).
Loose bodies
Fragments of cartilage that break off into the joint space in advanced OA.
Rheumatoid arthritis (RA)
A systemic autoimmune inflammatory disease causing symmetric polyarthritis, distinct from OA's localized, mechanical process.
Rheumatoid factor (RF)
An autoantibody against the Fc region of the body's own IgG; positive in 60–80% of RA patients but doesn't track with disease severity.
Anti-CCP antibody
An antibody targeting citrullinated peptides; supports an RA diagnosis.
Shared epitope
A common 5-amino-acid sequence in certain HLA-DRB1 alleles, associated with higher RA susceptibility.
Pannus
Hypertrophied, vascularized synovial tissue in RA that invades and destroys cartilage, ligaments, and tendons.
Swan neck deformity
PIP hyperextension with MCP/DIP flexion — a classic RA hand deformity.
Boutonnière deformity
PIP flexion with DIP hyperextension, from extensor tendon rupture/laxity — the mirror pattern of swan neck.
Enteropathic arthritis
Joint disease associated with inflammatory bowel disease (ulcerative colitis or Crohn disease).
Neuropathic osteoarthropathy (Charcot joint)
Joint destruction from loss of proprioception/pain sensation, most often from diabetes, tabes dorsalis, or syringomyelia.
Hemophilic arthropathy
Progressive joint destruction from recurrent hemarthrosis in hemophilia patients.
Gout
A disorder of uric acid metabolism causing monosodium urate crystal deposition in joints and tissue.
Hyperuricemia
Elevated serum uric acid — the biochemical precursor to gout, from either overproduction or underexcretion.
Tophi
Crystalline urate deposits in bone and connective tissue — the hallmark of chronic tophaceous gout.
Intercritical gout
The symptom-free interval between acute gout attacks, during which urate deposition continues silently.
Overview & Epidemiology

Scale of the problem

Arthritis is the most common disabling musculoskeletal condition in the US — ~46 million people affected, rising as the population ages. Over 150 distinct rheumatologic diseases exist; OA is just one, but the most common overall.

Who's affected

Prevalence rises with age — highest incidence in those over 70. In postmenopausal women specifically, the knees and hands are most frequently affected.

Exact prevalence is hard to pin down: diagnosis is inconsistent, longitudinal data is limited, and x-ray evidence of degeneration doesn't always correlate with symptoms — someone can have radiologic OA with no pain, or vice versa.
Etiology & Pathogenesis

Risk factors — "wear and tear" drivers

Obesity, joint trauma, congenital disorders (hip dysplasia, joint laxity, leg length discrepancy).

Other predisposing factors

Occupational/lifestyle joint stress, genetic predisposition, and hormonal status (postmenopausal).

Mind Map — The Cascade From Injury to Joint Failure

Why aging cartilage is more vulnerable

Decreased hydration of cartilage with age increases susceptibility to wear and damage — this is separate from, but compounds, the mechanical injury cascade above.

Multifactorial mechanism

Biomechanical, biochemical, inflammatory, and immunologic factors can all contribute — OA isn't purely "mechanical wear," even though that's the traditional framing.

Structural Changes in the Joint

Cartilage-level changes

Fissuring, pitting, and erosion — in severe cases, erosion denudes the full thickness of cartilage down to bone.

Bone-level changes

Osteophyte formation, subchondral bone sclerosis, and cyst formation.

Loose bodies

Cartilage fragments can break off entirely into the joint space.

Joint effusion

Common in advanced disease — the synovium becomes inflamed and secretes excess synovial fluid, causing the joint to distend.

Even though OA is classified as "noninflammatory," the synovium can still become secondarily inflamed as structural damage progresses — inflammation here is a downstream consequence, not the primary driver like it is in rheumatoid arthritis.
Clinical Manifestations

Core signs/symptoms

Bony enlargement of joints, crepitus with movement, morning stiffness lasting less than 30 minutes (improves with mobility), and pain with function.

Most commonly affected joints

Weight-bearing joints (hips, knees), cervical and lumbosacral spine, and the interphalangeal joints of the hands/feet. Can affect one isolated joint or multiple joints (especially in the hand).

Radiologic findings

Bony proliferation at joint margins (osteophytes/bone spurs), asymmetric joint space narrowing, subchondral bone sclerosis. Later: joint malalignment, subchondral cysts.

Synovial fluid analysis

When present, fluid is translucent and noninflammatory — <2000 WBC/mm³. This lab distinction is a useful way to differentiate OA from inflammatory arthritides on joint aspiration.

Pain pattern

Initially relieved by rest; stiffness can follow prolonged sitting. As disease progresses, even light activity triggers discomfort, and night pain becomes common.

Why cartilage itself isn't the pain source

Cartilage contains no nociceptors — pain actually originates from intraarticular and periarticular structures (synovium, bone, ligaments), not the cartilage breakdown itself.

Acute inflammatory flares (synovitis) can occur after a specific traumatic incident, but acute inflammation is not typically a prominent feature of OA overall — this is one of the key features distinguishing it from inflammatory arthritides.

Hand OA — Heberden & Bouchard Nodes

Heberden nodes — DIP joints

The most common hand deformity in OA; bone spurs on the dorsolateral and medial aspects of the distal interphalangeal joints.

Bouchard nodes — PIP joints

Same mechanism, one joint level up — at the proximal interphalangeal joints.

Knee changes

Loss of cartilage in the medial or lateral knee compartment can produce structural malalignment — genu varus (bow-legged) or genu valgus (knock-kneed).

Other common findings

Local pain over joint margins, tenderness, crepitus, and muscle atrophy around affected joints.

Quick memory hook: Heberden = distal (both start with vowel-adjacent sounds you can pair as "H before B, D before P" — Heberden→DIP, Bouchard→PIP).
Erosive OA — A More Aggressive Subset

What makes it different

Aggressive joint space narrowing, sclerosis, osteophyte formation, AND central subchondral bone erosion at the small joints — the central erosion is the distinguishing structural feature versus typical hand OA.

Joint involvement pattern

PIP joints most frequently involved, though DIP joints can be affected too.

How it differs clinically from typical hand OA

More joint swelling, more severe loss of joint mobility (from the central erosions), and tends to affect younger patients — especially females age 40–60.

Treatment

Mainly symptomatic: occupational therapy, acetaminophen, oral/topical anti-inflammatories. Antimalarials (hydroxychloroquine) and low-dose methotrexate have been tried for symptom relief, but neither has been shown to slow disease progression.

Treatment

Nonpharmacologic — first line

Weight reduction (for weight-bearing joints), exercise/physical therapy for range of motion, muscle strength, and conditioning.

Orthotics & assistive devices

Shoe insoles (lateral wedge inserts), heel lifts, patellar taping, joint braces/splints for stabilization; canes/walkers to offload weight-bearing stress and stabilize gait.

First-line pharmacologic — acetaminophen

The initial analgesic typically recommended for mild OA symptom management.

NSAIDs

Decrease swelling and pain. Most are nonselective; COX-2-selective agents have equivalent clinical benefit with potentially fewer GI side effects than traditional nonselective NSAIDs.

The only disease-modifying treatments so far are surgical (e.g., joint replacement) — no pharmacologic agent has been shown to reverse OA's structural progression; all current drug therapy is symptomatic.

Rheumatoid Arthritis

Systemic autoimmune disease

Where OA is a localized, mechanical problem, RA is the opposite: a systemic autoimmune disease that happens to show up most visibly in the joints. This contrast is the organizing idea for the whole RA section.

Epidemiology

Scale

~0.24–1% of the US population. Females 2x more likely than males, peak incidence 4th–5th decades — this sex difference disappears after menopause, a strong hint that hormones play a role.

Genetic risk

Monozygotic twin of an RA patient: ~15% risk (vs. ~1% general population risk) — a real but incomplete genetic component. Family history raises risk 2–3x. Affects all races; not climate-dependent.

Etiology & Pathogenesis

The working theory

An abnormal autoimmune response — possibly triggered by a bacterial/viral antigen — in a genetically susceptible person. No specific reproducible infectious cause has ever been confirmed despite extensive searching.

The genetic piece — HLA-DR4 and the "shared epitope"

Class II HLA genes (antigen-presenting cell structure) drive susceptibility. Certain HLA-DR4 alleles associate with higher RA rates in White populations; a subgroup of HLA-DRB1 alleles share a 5-amino-acid sequence called the shared epitope, suspected to affect autoreactive T-cell development. Different HLA-DR4 alleles link to RA risk differently across ethnicities.

Possible triggers

Infectious agents, environmental exposures, tobacco use. Higher onset in premenopausal females or post-pregnancy suggests hormonal influence. Physical/psychological stress is associated with triggering acute exacerbations.

RF and anti-CCP antibodies

Activated B cells produce rheumatoid factor (RF) — an antibody against the Fc region of the body's own IgG (an antibody against an antibody) — and anti-CCP antibodies, which target citrullinated peptides (arginine converted to citrulline). RF is positive in 60–80% of RA patients, but its titer does NOT track with disease activity/severity.

Mind Map — From Synovial Trigger to Pannus Destruction

Key cytokines (know a few by name)

TNF-α, IL-1β, IL-6, IL-8, IL-15, IL-17, IL-18, IL-23 — perpetuate and escalate joint inflammation. Newer biologic drugs are designed specifically to target these cytokines and activated inflammatory cells.

Pannus — the direct destroyer

Hypertrophied, vascularized synovial tissue (lymphocytes, macrophages, histiocytes, fibroblasts, mast cells) that expands "tumor-like" and invades cartilage, ligaments, joint capsules, and tendons — causing bone erosion, cysts, fissures, tendon rupture, and joint deformity.

Systemic symptoms

Fatigue, malaise, diffuse musculoskeletal aching often precede or accompany flares. Stiffness/swelling after prolonged sitting; progressive difficulty with walking, stairs, opening jars, fine finger movements. Weight loss, depression, low-grade fever are common.

Timeline

Usually develops over weeks to months, though it can progress over a few years — early diagnosis and intervention is emphasized because pannus-driven joint destruction is progressive.

Classic Deformities

Swan neck deformity

PIP hyperextension + MCP/DIP flexion — from intrinsic muscle/tendon contractures.

Boutonnière deformity

PIP flexion + DIP hyperextension — from rupture/laxity of the extensor tendons. (Note this is the mirror-image pattern from swan neck — worth drilling the contrast directly.)

Both deformities plus ulnar deviation reflect the same underlying process — pannus-driven ligament/tendon laxity and mechanical dysfunction — just manifesting differently depending on which specific tendons/ligaments are affected at each joint.
Extraarticular Manifestations

Subcutaneous nodules

15–20% of patients — dense perivascular inflammatory cell collections surrounded by palisading fibroblasts.

Cardiac

Pericarditis (often asymptomatic), rare myocarditis, mitral valve disease, conduction disease/complete heart block. Long-standing RA also carries higher risk of atherosclerotic CAD than age-matched controls.

Pulmonary

Pleural effusions (~20%), interstitial lung disease, pulmonary fibrosis, pulmonary nodules.

Skin & eyes

Palpable purpura or nail fold infarcts (from leukocytoclastic vasculitis of skin arterioles); episcleritis, scleritis, or secondary Sjögren syndrome (dry eyes/mouth).

Why this matters clinically: RA is genuinely a whole-body disease, not "just" a joint disease — this is the single biggest conceptual difference from OA, and the reason RA management involves monitoring far beyond the joints themselves.
Enteropathic Arthritis

What it is

Joint disease associated with the two inflammatory bowel diseases — ulcerative colitis and Crohn disease. Occurs in up to 25% of IBD patients; can sometimes PRECEDE GI symptoms.

Proposed mechanism

Gut inflammation lets bacteria cross into lymphoid tissue/bloodstream from the bowel lumen → cross-reactive immune response also targets joints (immune cross-reactivity with bacterial antigens).

Presentation

Peripheral arthritis: usually asymmetric, pauciarticular (few joints), commonly knees/ankles — can be mild/transient-but-relapsing, or chronic/erosive in severe cases. Spinal involvement in 10–20% (sacroiliac/spinal ligament inflammation, HLA-B27/B44 associated).

Extra-articular signs

Erythema nodosum, pyoderma gangrenosum, episcleritis, bilateral anterior uveitis (5–8%). Up to 5% develop primary sclerosing cholangitis (progressive hepatic fibrosis/failure). Amyloidosis possible in Crohn disease.

Treatment: manage the underlying GI disease with immunosuppressants; arthritis itself managed with NSAIDs (limited by GI toxicity), COX-2 inhibitors, corticosteroids, or sulfasalazine/azathioprine. TNF-α antagonists (adalimumab, infliximab, certolizumab) help both joint and bowel symptoms — notably, some newer bowel-targeted biologics (vedolizumab) have limited effect on the joint disease specifically, showing the two aren't treated identically despite being linked.
Neuropathic Osteoarthropathy (Charcot Joint)

Mechanism

Loss of normal proprioception/pain sensation → repeated unnoticed joint damage — likely a combination of neurovascular and neurotraumatic processes. Exact mechanism unclear.

Causes

Diabetes mellitus, tabes dorsalis, and syringomyelia are the three most common causes; also peripheral nerve injury, pernicious anemia, alcoholism, multiple sclerosis.

Presentation: swollen, deformed, unstable joint with advanced destruction/pathologic fractures on imaging. Treatment: immobilization + reduced weight bearing — surgery has poor outcomes (nonunion, dislocation, infection risk) precisely because the underlying sensory deficit keeps causing reinjury.
Hemophilic Arthropathy

Mechanism

Joint bleeding (from Chapter 14's hemophilia) stimulates synovial proliferation, chronic inflammation with degradative proteinase release, and cartilage composition changes with less stress resistance — chronic synovitis eventually destroys the joint.

Three stages

1 (acute): bleeding as the child starts walking; flexed joint position, muscle atrophy predisposes to further hemarthrosis. 2 (chronic synovitis): repetitive hemorrhage → edematous, warm, but PAINLESS joint. 3 (destruction): joint integrity destroyed.

Larger joints (elbows, hips, knees) affected far more than the wrist. Also watch for muscle hemorrhage (iliopsoas, forearm, gastrocnemius), muscle cysts, and pseudotumors from osseous hemorrhage. Treatment: enhance clotting medically (see Chapter 14) plus education/prevention to protect joints before deformity sets in.
Gout — Etiology & Pathogenesis

The core mechanism

Humans lack the enzyme uricase, so uric acid (a purine metabolism waste product) can't be oxidized to a more soluble compound — it must be cleared by the kidneys instead. When production exceeds removal, hyperuricemia results, and crystalline sodium urate deposits in joints/tissue.

Epidemiology

~4% prevalence in the US; highest in males and those >60. Risk rises with age and with serum urate level. Familial tendency common.

Dietary/lifestyle triggers

Purine-rich foods (organ meats, red meat, seafood) raise risk — but high-purine vegetables do NOT increase risk, an important exception. High-fructose sodas/colas raise risk. Alcohol (especially beer/ale) is strongly linked; dairy may actually lower hyperuricemia somewhat.

Other hyperuricemia causes

Congenital enzyme deficiencies (Lesch-Nyhan syndrome); high cell-turnover states (hemolytic anemia, polycythemia vera, myelodysplastic syndromes, tumor lysis syndrome after cancer treatment); chronic kidney disease (impaired excretion).

Acute attacks are often triggered by: trauma, surgery, acute illness, alcohol, or certain medications — worth knowing as a "why did this flare happen now" checklist.
The Four Phases of Gout

Mind Map — The Four Phases, in Order

1 — Asymptomatic hyperuricemia

Elevated serum urate, NO clinical signs. Males: can start at puberty. Females: usually doesn't appear before menopause (estrogen is protective, similar to its cardiovascular effect). No treatment needed at this stage.

2 — Acute gouty arthritis

The most common early clinical sign. Joint is warm, red, exquisitely tender (even light touch hurts). MTP joint of the great toe is classically first, though ankle/tarsal/knee/hand/wrist/elbow can also be affected. Sudden onset, often waking the patient from sleep, with intense pain + diffuse periarticular erythema. Diagnosis: clinical picture + hyperuricemia + urate crystals demonstrated in synovial fluid.

Attack duration & pattern

Initial attacks: subside in 1–2 days, or last up to 1–2 weeks, then symptom-free until the next episode. Over time, attacks become more frequent, with mild arthralgia even between episodes.

3 — Intercritical gout

The symptom-free interval between attacks — but NOT truly quiescent: urate crystals can still be aspirated from joints, and soft tissue deposition continues silently even while the patient feels fine.
