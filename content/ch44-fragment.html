Key Terms & Definitions

Primary injury
Brain damage occurring immediately at the moment of injury — largely irreversible once it has happened.
Secondary injury
Further neurologic damage developing after the primary injury, progressing over days to years; often the target of clinical intervention.
Ischemia
Oxygenated blood delivery below the level needed to meet tissue metabolic demand.
Hypoxia
Cellular oxygen deficiency, from either decreased blood flow (ischemia) or decreased blood oxygenation (hypoxemia).
Necrosis
Uncontrolled cell death with membrane rupture and release of intracellular contents; follows severe ischemic injury (profound ATP loss).
Apoptosis
Programmed, energy-requiring cell death in which cells shrink and die without releasing contents; associated with moderate injury (partially maintained ATP).
Excitotoxin
A substance (like excess glutamate) that overstimulates neurons to the point of causing cell injury.
NMDA / AMPA receptors
Glutamate-activated ion channels; AMPA activation causes Na+ entry/depolarization, which removes the Mg2+ block on NMDA channels, allowing damaging Ca2+ influx.
Reperfusion injury
Secondary damage from oxygen free radical formation and inflammatory cell invasion when blood flow is restored after ischemia.
Level of consciousness (LOC)
A sensitive indicator of brain function, ranging from full alertness to coma.
Glasgow Coma Scale (GCS)
A standardized 3–15 point tool scoring eye opening, verbal response, and motor response to assess LOC with high interrater reliability.
Decorticate posturing
Abnormal flexion of the arms/wrists with leg extension (GCS motor score 3) — indicates a lesion above the brainstem.
Decerebrate posturing
Arm and leg extension with wrist/leg internal rotation (GCS motor score 2) — indicates a lower, more severe brainstem lesion.
Oculovestibular reflex
Brainstem reflex adjusting eye position in response to head movement; tested via doll's eyes or cold calorics in unconscious patients.
Traumatic brain injury (TBI)
Brain tissue injury resulting from trauma; not synonymous with head injury (cranial injury doesn't always mean brain injury).
Coup / contrecoup injury
Focal injury at the impact site (coup) or, with polar injury, additional damage at the opposite pole from brain movement within the skull (contrecoup).
Diffuse axonal injury
Widespread stretching/shearing of axonal white matter from brain rotation/shifting; often causes coma from time of injury.
Concussion (mild TBI)
Brief LOC alteration (<30 min) with no CT evidence of brain damage.
Second impact syndrome
Malignant cerebral edema in an athlete who returns to play before fully recovering from a prior concussion.
Epidural hematoma
Arterial bleeding (often middle meningeal artery) between the skull and dura; classic "lucid interval" then rapid deterioration.
Subdural hematoma
Venous bleeding from torn bridging veins between the dura and arachnoid membrane; slower-forming than epidural.
Subarachnoid hemorrhage
Bleeding into the CSF-filled space between arachnoid and pia mater; from trauma (bridging veins) or, more often, aneurysm/AVM rupture (arterial).
Battle sign / Raccoon eyes
Bruising behind the ear / bilateral periorbital hematomas — signs of basilar skull fracture.
Stroke (brain attack)
A cerebrovascular event causing localized CNS infarction; previously "cerebrovascular accident."
Transient ischemic attack (TIA)
Stroke-like symptoms without evidence of infarction on imaging — the definition is now imaging-based, not time-based.
Penumbra
Ischemic but still-viable brain tissue surrounding an infarct core; the target of early reperfusion therapy.
Thrombotic stroke
Ischemic stroke from local atherosclerosis/clot formation in cerebral vasculature.
Embolic stroke
Ischemic stroke from a clot (often cardiac-origin, e.g. atrial fibrillation) traveling to and lodging in a cerebral artery.
Lacunar infarct
Small infarct from occlusion of penetrating arterioles, strongly associated with chronic hypertension and diabetes.
tPA (alteplase)
The thrombolytic approved for acute ischemic stroke, given within 3 (up to 4.5 in select patients) hours of symptom onset.
Mechanical thrombectomy
Endovascular clot retrieval for large-vessel occlusion strokes, effective up to 24 hours from onset in select patients.
Cerebral aneurysm
A dilated, ballooned, structurally weakened segment of a cerebral artery — the most common cause of nontraumatic subarachnoid hemorrhage.
Saccular (berry) aneurysm
The most common aneurysm shape — round, typically at arterial bifurcations in the circle of Willis.
Hunt and Hess scale
A grading scale (I–V) for aneurysmal subarachnoid hemorrhage severity, correlating with mortality (15% at grade I up to 95% at grade V).
Cerebral vasospasm
Pathologic narrowing of vessels near a ruptured aneurysm, typically day 4–14 post-rupture, causing ischemia/infarction.
Arteriovenous malformation (AVM)
A congenital fistulous connection between artery and vein without a capillary bed, causing progressively enlarging, hemorrhage-prone vessels.
Vascular steal syndrome
Progressive neurologic dysfunction from blood being shunted into an AVM and away from normal brain tissue.
Meningitis
Inflammation of the meninges, most commonly bacterial, invading the subarachnoid space.
Kernig / Brudzinski sign
Classic (but not sensitive) bedside signs of meningeal irritation in bacterial meningitis.
Encephalitis
Inflammation of the brain parenchyma itself, usually viral (most commonly HSV-1).
Brain abscess
A localized, capsule-forming collection of pus within brain parenchyma, from penetrating injury, contiguous spread, or hematogenous spread.

Mechanisms of Brain Injury

Foundations

Whatever the initial cause — trauma, stroke, infection — acute brain injuries share common cellular mechanisms. Understanding this shared pathway is the foundation for every disorder that follows in this chapter.

Primary vs. Secondary Injury

Primary injury

Occurs immediately at the onset — mechanical shearing/tearing/stretching in trauma, or immediate irreversible ischemia in stroke. Little can be done to reverse it once it happens.

Secondary injury

Develops afterward, progressing over days to years; this is where the majority of clinical intervention is targeted, though effective prevention remains elusive and mortality/morbidity stays high.

Mind Map — ATP Depletion Determines Cell Fate

Key distinction: Necrosis follows severe ischemic injury; apoptosis is associated with moderate injury. This single spectrum — how much ATP is lost — determines whether a cell recovers, dies "tidily" (apoptosis), or ruptures and spills its contents (necrosis, which itself triggers further local damage).
Ischemia, Hypoxia & Cellular Energy Failure

Why the brain is so vulnerable

The brain is ~2% of body weight but receives ~15% of cardiac output and 20% of total body oxygen consumption (~750 mL/min). Neurons need huge amounts of ATP but store almost no glycogen — so when oxygen delivery drops, there's no backup fuel reserve.

The critical time window

Brain cells tolerate ATP loss for only minutes. Roughly 5–10 minutes of complete occlusion causes irreversible brain damage in humans. Complete occlusion is rare, but even partial occlusion sustained long enough can cause the same irreversible damage.

What glycolysis does under ischemia

Anaerobic glycolysis continues briefly, producing pyruvate → lactate. This releases H+, causing cellular acidosis — itself a damaging byproduct that degrades neuronal integrity.

Where most neuronal ATP actually goes

The Na+-K+ pump consumes three-fourths of a neuron's ATP, maintaining ion gradients. When energy fails, potassium leaks out while sodium, chloride, and calcium flood in (anoxic depolarization) — setting up the calcium overload cascade.

Calcium overload is the central event: Ischemic cells can't pump calcium out (that's energy-dependent too), and mitochondria — normally able to sequester excess calcium — lose that ability when their own energy fails. The resulting calcium overload activates phospholipases that damage mitochondrial and cell membranes (lipid peroxidation), and disrupts countless calcium-regulated enzyme systems. This is the single mechanistic thread connecting ischemia, energy failure, and irreversible cell damage.
Excitatory Amino Acids (Glutamate Excitotoxicity)

Glutamate's normal role

The principal excitatory neurotransmitter in the adult CNS — important for rapid neuron-to-neuron signaling and brain development. In excess, it becomes an excitotoxin.

Why excess builds up during injury

Impaired membrane integrity releases excess glutamate into synapses, while ischemia disables the energy-dependent reuptake mechanisms that would normally clear it.

Mind Map — Glutamate-Mediated Calcium Influx

The two receptor types

AMPA receptors open first, causing Na+ entry and depolarization. That depolarization is what removes the Mg2+ ion normally blocking the NMDA channel — only then can glutamate binding open NMDA and let calcium flood in.

Vulnerable regions

Small neurons in the cerebral cortex and hippocampus are particularly prone to glutamate excitotoxicity — selective damage in these areas can occur.

Reperfusion Injury

The paradox

Restoring blood flow is urgent to salvage tissue — but previously ischemic cells face new danger: returning oxygen reacts erratically with accumulated substrates (AMP, xanthine, hypoxanthine breakdown products), producing free radicals (hydroxyl radicals, superoxide, peroxide) that cause lipid peroxidation and membrane damage.

Immune/inflammatory component

Ischemia compromises the blood-brain barrier (injured capillary endothelial cells), allowing systemic immune cells to enter and inflammatory cytokines (IL-1, IL-6, TNF) to accumulate. Microglia also activate, and platelet aggregation in cerebral vessels can further reduce perfusion.

Neuroinflammation is now understood to contribute to both acute damage and longer-term, ongoing brain changes after injury — not just a short-term reperfusion problem.

Manifestations of Brain Injury

Assessment

LOC, cranial nerve reflexes, and brain hemodynamics are the core windows into neurologic status — and changes in any of them can evolve rapidly, which is why frequent reassessment matters so much.

Level of Consciousness (LOC)

LOC is one of the most sensitive indicators of altered brain function. Decline is usually progressive — cortical neurons are most sensitive, so cognitive/memory impairment (confusion) tends to show up early.

Severity by total score

Mild >12, moderate 9–12, severe <8. A score of 3 (the lowest possible) suggests likely fatal damage, especially with fixed/nonreactive pupils and absent oculovestibular responses.

Scoring rules

Score the best response; test each limb separately for motor response (paralysis on one side shouldn't be mistaken for a lower motor score); apply consistent, vigorous painful stimuli for accuracy.

Motor response is the strongest single predictor of outcome — higher initial GCS scores tend to predict better recovery, though imaging and other factors refine the prognosis further.
GCS can be confounded by sedation, hypotension, and paralytics — always factor these in before interpreting a score.
Cranial Nerve Reflexes

Pupil reflex — CN II & III

A sluggish/dilating pupil can be the first sign of brain compression from rising ICP.

Pupil patterns

Bilaterally fixed and dilated: inadequate cerebral perfusion. Afferent pupillary defect: optic nerve damage on the affected side.

Doll's eyes

Rotate the head (after cervical spine cleared): intact brainstem → eyes move opposite to head rotation. Absent response → severe brainstem impairment.

Cold calorics

Cold water in the ear canal: intact brainstem → tonic eye deviation toward the irrigated side. Used in brain death evaluation.

Corneal reflex: a wisp of cotton touched to the cornea should elicit a blink — absence is another marker of severely impaired brain function.

Traumatic Brain Injury (TBI)

Trauma

TBI ≠ head injury — cranial injury doesn't always mean brain injury. It's the leading cause of death and disability among young adults and children in the US.

Epidemiology & Severity

Scale of the problem

~2.87 million TBIs annually in the US; over 50,000 deaths. Males are 3x more likely than females to die from TBI.

Who's affected, and how

Falls: leading cause in ages 0–4 and 65+. Motor vehicle trauma: highest hospitalization rates in ages 14–24.

Severity classification (by GCS)

Severe: GCS ≤8. Moderate: 9–12. Mild: >12.

Confounders

GCS is affected by sedation, hypotension, and paralytics — factor these in before interpreting a score. A more reliable outcome estimate is possible 48–72 hours post-injury.

Types of Primary TBI (by Location)

Mind Map — Focal vs. Polar vs. Diffuse Injury

Focal injuries can still be dangerous beyond the local symptoms: localized hemorrhage or edema can act as a space-occupying lesion, raising ICP and causing brain shifting/herniation — watch for decreased LOC, cranial nerve dysfunction, and contralateral weakness even from a seemingly "focal" injury.
Concussion & Second Impact Syndrome

Definition

Mild TBI: LOC alteration <30 minutes, no CT evidence of brain damage. The most common injury in military personnel and athletes.

Symptoms

Headache, nausea, vomiting, dizziness, fatigue, blurred vision; cognitive/emotional disturbances (irritability). May resolve quickly or persist much longer. Effects can be cumulative with repeated trauma.

Second impact syndrome: a young athlete returning to play before fully recovering from a concussion can develop malignant cerebral edema — associated with poor neurologic outcomes. This is the entire rationale behind graduated return-to-play protocols.

Return-to-play principle

Progress through stages (rest → light aerobic → sport-specific drills → noncontact training → full contact → return to play), staying at least 24 hours at each stage, advancing only if asymptomatic. Returning symptoms send the athlete back to the previous level. Medical clearance is required before full-contact practice.

Intracranial Hematomas

Mind Map — Three Hematoma Types by Location & Source

Epidural prognosis

Often minimal primary injury — with prompt CT diagnosis and surgical evacuation, prognosis is usually excellent.

Subdural prognosis

Rapid deterioration usually means severe primary injury — worse prognosis than epidural bleeds.

Chronic subdural

Common in the elderly (brain atrophy → more mobile brain) and with alcohol/anticoagulant use. Symptoms can be subtle and misattributed to dementia.

Subarachnoid hemorrhage

Causes meningeal irritation and a bloody spinal tap. Risks secondary vasospasm and hydrocephalus.

Secondary Injury & Treatment in TBI

Sources of secondary injury

Hypotension/hypoxia from concomitant trauma, brain swelling (can worsen for 48–72 hrs post-injury), rebleeding/vasospasm, CNS infection risk, and seizures/fever raising metabolic demand.

Watch for concurrent injuries

Attention to other life-threatening trauma can delay head imaging — TBI patients need simultaneous evaluation for skull fractures, hematomas, and injuries elsewhere.

Initial treatment priorities

After cardiopulmonary stabilization: noncontrast head CT + cervical spine CT. Surgically correctable lesions get prompt neurosurgical evaluation.

Supportive targets

Maintain normal temperature, PaCO2, glucose, and volume. HOB ~30°. Elevated ICP: mannitol, hypertonic saline, sedation.

Basilar skull fracture signs: clear CSF drainage from ears/nose, bilateral periorbital hematomas ("raccoon eyes"), and bruising under the ear (Battle sign) — raises CNS infection risk.

Cerebrovascular Disease & Stroke

"Brain attack"

Classic symptoms: sudden numbness/weakness (especially one-sided), confusion or speech trouble, visual disturbance, dizziness/imbalance, or severe headache. Any of these — even if temporary — warrants immediate care.

Stroke Epidemiology & Risk Factors

Scale

~795,000 new US strokes/year, ~130,000 deaths. 5th leading cause of death in the US; leading cause of disability; 2nd leading cause of death worldwide (6.3 million deaths/year).

Who's affected

Two-thirds occur after age 65; more common in males and in African Americans than White Americans. Incidence has declined recently due to better hypertension treatment and smoking reduction.

Risk factors

Hypertension, diabetes, hyperlipidemia, smoking, long-term alcohol abuse, advancing age, hypercoagulable states, family history. Prior stroke significantly raises subsequent stroke risk.

Atrial fibrillation — a key embolic risk factor

AFib causes blood stagnation in the left atrium, raising mural thrombus/left atrial appendage thrombus risk — this is why AFib patients commonly receive anticoagulation for stroke prevention.

Ischemic strokes make up 85% of all strokes (thrombotic + embolic); the remainder are hemorrhagic.
Ischemic Stroke & TIA

Thrombotic stroke

From atherosclerosis in extra/intracranial vessels and hypercoagulable states. Carotid bruits can signal significant plaque — screening in patients over 50 can help identify at-risk individuals; carotid duplex confirms stenosis severity.

Embolic stroke

Usually cardiac-source emboli (mural thrombi, valve vegetations) or carotid plaque disruption. AFib is a classic cause via left atrial thrombus formation.

The timeline of ischemic damage

Neurologic deficits appear after just 1 minute of insufficient oxygen. Several minutes of continued ischemia → irreversible cellular damage. Within hours, the local area becomes infarcted/necrotic.

The penumbra — the treatment target

Ischemic-but-viable tissue surrounding the infarct core, sustained by partial/collateral flow. Salvaging the penumbra via early thrombolysis/reperfusion is the entire goal of acute stroke treatment — but it's time-sensitive; without reperfusion, the penumbra can progress to infarction too.

TIA redefined: No longer defined by symptom duration (though usually <24 hrs, often <20 min) — now defined by the absence of infarction on imaging. If diffusion-weighted MRI shows damage, it's classified as a stroke regardless of how quickly symptoms resolved. About 30% of stroke patients had a prior TIA — TIAs are an important warning sign.

Lacunar infarcts

From penetrating arteriole occlusion — basal ganglia, pons, cerebellum, internal capsule are common sites. Strongly linked to chronic hypertension and diabetes. Often not visible on CT acutely (diffusion-weighted MRI needed); prognosis is usually good with more circumscribed (pure motor or sensory) deficits.

Acute Ischemic Stroke Treatment

Initial workup

Stabilize first, then NIHSS + GCS assessment. STAT noncontrast head CT ± CTA — exam alone can't distinguish ischemic from hemorrhagic stroke.

Why timing is everything

Symptom onset time determines thrombolytic/thrombectomy eligibility. "Last known normal" is the reference point, not when symptoms were noticed.

Thrombolytic therapy

Alteplase (tPA): the only FDA-approved thrombolytic; within 3 hours of onset (up to 4.5 in select patients).

Mechanical thrombectomy

For large-vessel occlusion, up to 24 hours from onset, at comprehensive stroke centers.

Blood pressure goals — don't overcorrect:

ScenarioBP Goal
Not a thrombolysis candidate<220/120
Thrombolysis candidate (pre-treatment)≤185/110
After receiving thrombolytics≤180/105
After mechanical thrombectomySystolic <160
Overcorrecting hypertension can worsen cerebral ischemia and increase infarct size — this is a common point of confusion since aggressive BP lowering feels intuitively "safe" but is actually harmful here.

Fluids & glucose

Isotonic IV fluids only (avoid dextrose — worsens cerebral edema). Keep glucose <180 mg/dL; avoid hyperthermia.

Swallow screening & anticoagulation

Bedside swallow screen before any oral intake (CN V, VII, IX, X, XII injury → aspiration risk). Anticoagulation considered for progressive stroke or embolic etiology (AFib, mural thrombus, dissection) — NOT within 24 hours of thrombolytics, due to bleeding risk.

Cerebral Aneurysm

Vascular structural disease
Etiology, Pathogenesis, Manifestations & Treatment

What it is

A dilated, ballooned, structurally weakened arterial segment — the most common cause of nontraumatic subarachnoid hemorrhage (AVM is second).

Epidemiology

Rupture: ~10 per 10,000; more common in females; usually ruptures age 30–60.

Rupture risk factors

Larger size, certain locations, multiplicity, smoking, heavy alcohol intake, uncontrolled hypertension. Annual rupture risk overall: 1–2%.

Structure & location

95% located in the circle of Willis, often at bifurcations. Saccular (berry) aneurysms are most common.

Classic presentation: sudden "worst headache of my life" (thunderclap headache), plus photophobia, nausea/vomiting, nuchal rigidity, focal deficits, seizure, or coma.

Hunt and Hess scale (severity → mortality):

GradePresentationMortality
IHeadache only15%
VComatose95%

Vasospasm

Typically day 4–14 post-rupture; reduces cerebral blood flow → ischemia/infarction. Monitored daily with transcranial Doppler.

Rebleeding

Highest risk in the first 24 hours after rupture; re-rupture carries a 70% mortality rate.

Emergency management

Airway/hemodynamic stabilization → immediate head CT → CTA. SBP goal ≤140 with IV labetalol/nicardipine.

Definitive treatment

Endovascular coiling is used in the majority of cases; craniotomy/clipping is common for MCA aneurysms. Securing the aneurysm ASAP reduces rebleeding risk.

Arteriovenous Malformation (AVM)

What it is

A congenital fistulous connection between artery and vein with no capillary bed — high-pressure arterial blood exposes the venous system directly.

Epidemiology

2nd most common cause of spontaneous SAH. Most diagnosed before age 40; more frequent in males.

Rupture risk

2–4% per year; rebleeding risk after first rupture is as high as 44%, highest in year one.

How AVMs present

Hemorrhage (50%), seizures (30%), or vascular steal syndrome (blood shunted away from normal tissue).

Treatment options

Surgical resection, radiosurgery (gamma knife — slow, takes years), or embolization (usually combined with surgery).

Central Nervous System Infections

Meningitis · Encephalitis · Abscess

Organisms reach the CNS hematogenously, by contiguous spread, along nerves, or via maternal-fetal exchange. Meningitis and abscess are usually bacterial; encephalitis is usually viral.

Meningitis

Leading cause

Streptococcus pneumoniae causes >60% of adult bacterial meningitis in the US.

Mortality

3–7% for most bacterial causes, up to 20–37% for S. pneumoniae. Neurologic deficits in 30–50% of survivors.

The classic triad (headache, fever, neck stiffness) is present in fewer than 50% of cases — don't rely on it. Kernig/Brudzinski signs may be present but aren't sensitive.

Don't delay treatment for workup

Blood cultures before antibiotics; head CT before LP only if new seizure, decreased LOC, focal deficit, or papilledema — treatment should never wait on CT/LP if suspicion is high.

Treatment

Empirical IV antibiotics + supportive care. Corticosteroids (dexamethasone) are often added — much of the CNS damage is from the immune response itself, and steroids may improve mortality, especially in pneumococcal meningitis.

Encephalitis

Leading cause

Usually viral; HSV-1 is the classic culprit. ~50% of HSV encephalitis cases die or have lasting impairment.

HSV-1 mechanism

Reactivates from the trigeminal nerve, causing hemorrhagic necrosis classically in the temporal lobes.

Presentation

Evolves over several days: fever, headache, seizure, confusion, progressing toward stupor/coma; hallucinations and personality changes may occur.

CSF findings

Normal glucose (unlike bacterial meningitis), elevated WBC with mononuclear predominance.

Diagnosis & treatment

MRI is the study of choice. Empirical acyclovir should start immediately and continue until HSV/VZV is ruled out — never wait on imaging/CSF results.

SIADH risk: encephalitis patients frequently show excessive ADH secretion and water retention — careful fluid management is essential.
Brain Abscess

How organisms arrive

Penetrating wounds/surgery, contiguous spread (sinusitis, otitis), or hematogenous spread from a distant infection.

Risk factors

Chronic sinusitis/otitis, endocarditis, congenital heart disease, IV drug use, immunocompromise.

Deceptively quiet presentation: like meningitis, the classic triad (headache/fever/focal deficits) occurs in fewer than half of cases. Headache is the most consistent symptom.

Imaging

CT/MRI shows a characteristic ring-enhancing lesion.

Treatment by size

<2.5 cm: antibiotics ± aspiration may suffice. ≥2.5 cm: usually requires surgical excision. Empirical broad-spectrum IV antibiotics, narrowed by culture, for 6–8 weeks.
