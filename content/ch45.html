Key Terms & Definitions

Start here

Type in the box to filter.

Seizure
A transient neurologic event from excessive/abnormal neuronal electrical discharge — a singular event, not a diagnosis.
Epilepsy
A chronic, recurrent, enduring predisposition to seizures requiring long-term treatment — distinct from a single seizure.
Epileptogenic focus
The area of hyperexcitable, autonomously firing neurons from which a seizure originates.
Dementia
A syndrome (not a disease) of progressive cognitive decline — memory, personality, language, judgment — from various underlying pathologies.
Delirium
An acute, fluctuating confusional state with disturbed consciousness and attention; can mimic dementia but has abrupt onset, often worse at night.
β-amyloid plaques
Extracellular aggregates of neurotoxic Aβ42 peptide fragments (from APP cleavage) — a cardinal pathologic feature of Alzheimer disease.
Neurofibrillary tangles (NFTs)
Helical filaments of hyperphosphorylated tau protein inside neurons — the other cardinal AD feature, more closely tied to actual cognitive decline than plaques.
APOe4
The strongest known genetic risk factor for sporadic late-onset Alzheimer disease; linked to inefficient clearance of Aβ42.
Substantia nigra
The dopamine-producing brain region whose degeneration causes Parkinson disease.
Lewy bodies
Intraneuronal inclusions composed of α-synuclein; pathognomonic for sporadic Parkinson disease.
Bradykinesia
Slowness of movement — a core Parkinson disease feature.
Cogwheel rigidity
Ratchet-like resistance felt when passively moving a Parkinson patient's extremity.
Cerebral palsy
Permanent, nonprogressive damage to the developing brain's upper motor neurons, occurring pre/peri/postnatally.
Hydrocephalus
Abnormal CSF accumulation in the ventricular system from obstruction or impaired resorption.
Normal-pressure hydrocephalus
Increased CSF volume without increased ICP (brain tissue loss offsets it) — classic triad: gait instability, urinary incontinence, dementia.
Multiple sclerosis (MS)
A chronic autoimmune demyelinating disease of the CNS causing inflammation and scarring of myelin sheaths.
Lhermitte sign
An electric shock-like sensation radiating down the spine into the extremities — a classic MS finding.
Oligoclonal bands
Discrete IgG bands in CSF supporting an MS diagnosis.
Spina bifida
Defective closure of the neural tube; ranges from occulta (hidden) to cystica (externally protruding sac).
Myelomeningocele
The most severe form of spina bifida cystica — meninges, CSF, AND spinal cord protrude through the defect.
Amyotrophic lateral sclerosis (ALS)
Progressive degeneration of both upper and lower motor neurons causing muscle wasting, paralysis, and eventually respiratory failure.
Spinal shock
Temporary (hours to weeks) complete loss of function below a spinal cord injury level, including flaccid paralysis and absent reflexes.
Neurogenic shock
Distributive shock from loss of sympathetic outflow after high (cervical/upper thoracic) SCI — hypotension + bradycardia.
Autonomic dysreflexia
A life-threatening reflex hypertensive crisis in SCI at/above T6, triggered by a stimulus (often bladder/bowel) below the injury level.
Guillain-Barré syndrome (GBS)
An autoimmune, inflammatory demyelinating disease of the peripheral nervous system causing ascending weakness/paralysis.
Albuminocytologic dissociation
High CSF protein with normal WBC count — the classic GBS CSF finding.
Ataxia
Muscle incoordination — the hallmark of cerebellar dysfunction.
Bell palsy
Idiopathic facial nerve (CN VII) paralysis, now understood to often be viral (HSV/herpes zoster) in origin.

Brain & Cerebellar Disorders

Chronic conditions

Unlike the acute injuries in the previous chapter, these conditions involve long-term neurologic dysfunction requiring ongoing management rather than emergency intervention.

Seizures & Epilepsy

Seizure vs. epilepsy

A seizure is a singular transient event; epilepsy is the chronic, recurrent predisposition to seizures. ~10% of the global population will have a seizure; only 1–2% develop epilepsy (~70 million people worldwide).

Why epilepsy incidence is rising

More people surviving serious head trauma, stroke, intracranial infection, and brain tumors — all of which can leave a seizure-prone focus behind.

Common causes

Idiopathic, acute stroke, intracranial lesions, metabolic encephalopathy, drugs/alcohol/toxins, head trauma, metabolic disturbances, CNS infections, hyperpyrexia.

Common individual triggers

Flashing lights, loud noises, rhythmic music, fever, exhaustion, sleep deprivation, poor nutrition, menstrual hormonal changes, hyperventilation, injury, drugs, and psychosocial stress.

Mind Map — Seizure Pathogenesis

ILAE 2017 seizure classification

Three onset types: focal (localized to one hemisphere), generalized (whole brain, involves thalamus/RAS → loss of consciousness), and unknown onset. Focal seizures are further split by awareness level (aware vs. impaired) then motor/nonmotor onset.

Terminology update

Old terms now retired: complex partial, simple partial, partial, psychic, dyscognitive, secondarily generalized tonic-clonic — replaced for clearer worldwide communication and to support therapy-based patient clustering.

Nonmotor (absence) seizures: almost always children (rarely before age 4 or after puberty); brief (2–10 sec) staring spells with abrupt onset/termination; person is unaware but may continue routine motor tasks; sometimes first noticed as poor academic performance. Atypical forms add myoclonic jerks and automatisms (lip smacking, repetitive movements).
Dementia — Overview & Classification

Dementia is a syndrome, not a disease

Progressive decline in memory PLUS other cognitive domains (personality, language, judgment, abstract thinking, complex tasks). Often insidious onset; agnosia (lack of insight into one's own deficits) is common.

Delirium vs. dementia — don't confuse them

Delirium: acute onset, fluctuating, often worse at night, disturbed consciousness/attention, from causes like polypharmacy, metabolic abnormality, nutritional deficiency, infection. Can occur more easily on top of existing dementia.

Neurodegenerative dementias

Alzheimer disease, dementia with Lewy bodies, Parkinson disease dementia, multiple system atrophy, chronic traumatic encephalopathy, prion disease (Creutzfeldt-Jakob), ALS.

Nonneurodegenerative dementias

Vascular dementia, normal-pressure hydrocephalus, metabolic (hypothyroidism, Cushing syndrome), depression, bipolar disorder, neoplasia, toxin exposure, infections (syphilis, HIV), vitamin B12 deficiency — many of these are reversible, which is why full workup matters.

AD accounts for 60–80% of all dementias; vascular dementia is second most common. Prevalence rises sharply with age: 3% (ages 65–74) → 17% (75–84) → 32% (85+).
Always rule out reversible causes first — full workup includes CBC, chemistry panel, thyroid function, B12, syphilis serology, and a review of anticholinergic medications (a common, overlooked cause of cognitive changes in the elderly).
Alzheimer Disease

Mind Map — Alzheimer Disease Pathogenesis

The two cardinal traits

β-amyloid plaques: extracellular, present years before cognitive decline; correlate with disease severity. Neurofibrillary tangles (NFTs): intracellular, from hyperphosphorylated tau; NOT AD-specific but more closely tied to actual neuronal death/cognitive decline than plaques are.

Brain changes

Diffuse neuronal damage and atrophy — advanced AD brains can weigh up to 20% less than normal. Temporoparietal and anterior frontal regions chiefly affected (enlarged sulci/ventricles, atrophic gyri).

Neurotransmitter changes

↓ Acetylcholine synthesis (β-amyloid may be responsible) is the key deficit; glutamate, dopamine, and serotonin imbalances also contribute.

Genetics

Early-onset (rare): presenilin-1/2 and APP mutations — all increase Aβ42. Late-onset (common): APOe4 is the strongest known risk factor — one allele = 2–3x risk.

Other risk factors: family history, head trauma, diabetes, lower education, inactive lifestyle.

Clinical progression: early (short-term memory loss) → middle (declining judgment/communication, needs ADL help) → late (swallowing difficulty, incontinence, loss of ambulation).

Disease-modifying treatments (new)

Lecanemab, donanemab — monoclonal antibodies clearing amyloid plaques. First drug class to target AD pathophysiology directly.

Symptomatic treatment

Acetylcholinesterase inhibitors (donepezil) for mild-moderate AD — boost available acetylcholine.

Vascular Dementia

Cause

A single cerebrovascular insult, multiple lacunar infarcts, or microvascular pathology. 2nd most common dementia type.

Overlap with AD

Growing evidence the two often coexist in elderly patients, though usually treated as separate entities.

Risk factors mirror stroke risk factors: hypertension and diabetes.
Parkinson Disease (PD)

Scale

2nd most common neurodegenerative disorder; >1 million affected in the US, ~90,000 new cases/year (50% increase since 2014). Age >65 is the single greatest risk factor.

Core features

Bradykinesia, rigidity, tremor, postural instability — a movement disorder from basal ganglia dysfunction.

Mind Map — Dopamine-Glutamate Balance in the Basal Ganglia

The 70-80% rule

70–80% of the substantia nigra is already damaged before motor symptoms appear. Non-motor changes (sleep, mood, GI) can precede motor symptoms.

Pathognomonic finding

α-synuclein and Lewy bodies — present in sporadic PD.

Demographics & risk factors

Male:female ratio ~3:2; age >65 is the single greatest risk factor. TBI and pesticide exposure are associated risks.

Clinical manifestations, in typical order of recognition:

FeatureDetail
Early/overlooked signsLoss of flexibility, aching, fatigue — often attributed to normal aging
Tremor (often first noticed)Resting, unilateral initially; "pill-rolling"; becomes bilateral over time
RigidityCogwheel rigidity on passive movement
Bradykinesia effectsMicrographia, hypophonic speech, delayed swallowing, difficulty initiating movement
Gait/posturalShuffling gait, absent arm swing, impaired postural reflexes → falls
AutonomicOrthostatic hypotension
Non-motorDepression, daytime sleepiness, restless legs, dementia
Paradoxical kinesia: despite profound bradykinesia, many PD patients can move quickly in an emergency (e.g., fire) — an interesting and clinically notable phenomenon.

Levodopa/carbidopa — the mainstay

Dopamine itself can't cross the BBB; levodopa (its precursor) can. Carbidopa blocks peripheral conversion of levodopa to dopamine, reducing side effects (peripheral dopamine → adrenaline/noradrenaline → BP changes) and preserving more levodopa to cross into the CNS.

Long-term levodopa complications

"On-off" phenomena (sudden symptom return) and dyskinesias (abnormal movements) with prolonged use.

Other drug classes

Dopamine agonists (pramipexole, ropinirole); MAO-B inhibitors slowing dopamine metabolism (selegiline, rasagiline, safinamide); amantadine (helps akinesia/dyskinesia); anticholinergics (help tremor, rigidity, drooling).

Surgical option

Deep brain stimulation: thalamic electrical stimulator interrupts tremor-causing impulses, can reduce medication needs. Embryonic stem cell transplantation remains controversial with inconsistent results.

Cerebral Palsy

Definition

Permanent, nonprogressive damage to the developing brain's upper motor neurons — occurs prenatally, perinatally, or in early infancy. Occurs in ~2 per 1000 live births.

"Nonprogressive" doesn't mean "unchanging"

Though the brain damage itself doesn't progress, affected individuals face evolving challenges into adulthood: chronic pain, scoliosis, respiratory dysfunction, and functional decline.

Etiologic factors

Maternal prenatal infection, birth trauma, reduced oxygen supply, kernicterus, prematurity. Often multifactorial with no single identifiable cause.

Associated complications

Seizures, intellectual disability, visual/hearing impairment, respiratory problems, orthopedic disabilities.

TypeMechanismManifestations
Spastic (most common)Upper motor neuron damageHypertonia, exaggerated DTRs, contractures; "scissors" gait

Most children have a mixed presentation rather than a single pure type.

Treatment

No cure — goal is maximizing function: muscle relaxants for spasticity, Botox, orthopedic surgery/braces, comprehensive rehab.

Hydrocephalus

Mind Map — Three Types of Hydrocephalus

Congenital causes

Neural tube defects, aqueduct stenosis, prenatal infection.

Acquired causes

Infection, neoplasm, vascular causes; in premature infants, intraventricular hemorrhage can cause it.

In neonates: unfused skull bones mean rising pressure causes the whole head to swell — developmental delays may follow.

Treatment

Ventriculoperitoneal (VP) shunt is the most effective treatment. Endoscopic third ventriculostomy is an alternative for obstructive hydrocephalus.

Cerebellar Disorders

The cerebellum's three jobs

(1) Coordinates muscle groups (with the cerebral cortex) for skilled movement, (2) works below consciousness to keep movement smooth/steady, (3) maintains balance.

How it works

Compares the cerebrum's intended movement (motor commands via corticospinal tract) against actual movement (muscle receptor feedback), then sends correcting impulses back to both cerebrum and muscle.

Causes

Abscess, hemorrhage, tumors, trauma, viral infection, chronic alcoholism.

Clinical manifestations

Ataxia (incoordination), hypotonia, intention tremors, gait/balance disturbance — classic staggering/lurching gait with foot raised too high, brought down with a "clap."

Key distinguishing point: loss of cerebellar function does NOT cause paralysis — this is purely a coordination problem, not a motor strength problem.

Spinal Cord & Peripheral Nerve Disorders

Below the brain
Multiple Sclerosis (MS)

Epidemiology

>2.3 million affected worldwide; onset age 20–40; 2–3x more common in females. Higher rates at northern latitudes.

Genetic risk

Monozygotic twins: 30–50% concordance — a real but incomplete genetic component.

Immune mechanism

Autoimmune demyelination, likely triggered by viral infection or environmental exposure in a genetically susceptible person. Both antibody- and T-cell mediated injury occur.

Commonly affected structures

Optic nerves, corticospinal/cerebellar/posterior column systems — no predictable lesion pattern.

Symptoms by system:

SystemSymptoms
Cranial nerveOptic neuritis (blurred vision), diplopia, facial weakness/numbness
MotorWeakness, paralysis, spasticity, abnormal gait
SensoryParesthesias, Lhermitte sign (electric shock down the spine)
CerebellarDysarthria, tremor, ataxia, vertigo
Bowel/bladderUrgency, incontinence, retention
CognitiveShort-term memory loss, poor concentration, mood changes
FatigueOverwhelming, not relieved by rest
Symptoms are worsened by heat, infection, trauma, stress.

Four clinical course patterns:

Course% of patientsPattern
Relapsing-remitting~85%Clear relapses with acute decline, then partial/complete recovery
Primary-progressive~10%Slow, continuous decline from the start; no relapses/remissions
Secondary-progressiveCommon eventual conversion from RRStarts relapsing-remitting, then steady decline

Diagnostic criteria

"Dissemination in time and space" — 2+ episodes at different times AND 2+ signs in different CNS areas. MRI plaques and CSF oligoclonal bands support diagnosis.

Treatment

Acute attacks: corticosteroids hasten recovery. Disease-modifying therapy: reduces long-term autoimmune activity. Plus symptom management (antispasmodics, etc.).

Spina Bifida

Mind Map — Spina Bifida Spectrum, Least to Most Severe

Etiology

Occurs ~4 weeks gestation. Risk factors: folate deficiency, anticonvulsant drugs, maternal obesity/diabetes.

Prevention

Folic acid supplementation before conception and during pregnancy significantly decreases risk.

Spina bifida occulta

Most common in the lumbosacral region. Signs: hair tuft, midline dimple, port-wine angioma. Often no problems at all.

Myelomeningocele specifics

Sac may leak CSF → infection risk. Delivered via C-section. Permanent damage: motor/sensory loss below the defect, bowel/bladder dysfunction, hydrocephalus. Cord tethering can worsen symptoms with growth.

Time-sensitive treatment: myelomeningocele is covered with a sterile dressing immediately at birth; surgical repair generally required within 72 hours.
Amyotrophic Lateral Sclerosis (ALS)

Definition

Progressive degeneration of BOTH upper and lower motor neurons — muscle wasting, progressive paralysis, death usually within a few years. Also called Lou Gehrig disease.

Epidemiology

Onset typically age 40–75, more common in males. 5–10% familial, rest sporadic.

Pathology

Misfolded protein aggregates in motor neurons/glia. Neuroinflammation is prominent (shared with AD, PD, MS).

Clinical course

Muscle twitching/cramping first, often in hands. Progresses to speech, swallowing, breathing muscles. Hyperreflexia in a weak, atrophied extremity is highly suggestive of ALS. Most die of respiratory failure within 3–5 years.

What's spared: sensory function, cognition, and bowel/bladder sphincter control are usually preserved.

Treatment (no cure)

Riluzole — extends life 2–3 months. Edaravone — slows functional decline. Multidisciplinary care is essential.

Spinal Cord Injury (SCI)

Epidemiology

Primarily affects the young, though incidence is rising in older adults too. Males 3–4x more likely; more common weekends/summer. Leading causes: land transportation, violence (gunshot wounds), falls, recreational accidents. Alcohol/risk-taking behavior often involved.

Mechanisms of injury

Flexion (tears posterior ligaments) — most unstable, often severe deficits. Hyperextension — most common, tears anterior ligaments. Compression — crushes vertebrae, drives bone fragments into the canal.

Primary vs. secondary SCI injury

Primary: direct axonal disruption, neuronal death, disrupted blood flow → ischemia. Secondary: cytotoxic edema, granulocyte invasion, ionic imbalance, excitotoxic neurotransmitter release, free radicals, macrophage/lymphocyte infiltration → progressive neurodegeneration beyond the original injury site.

Systemic effects worsen the injury

Lost autoregulation → profound systemic BP drop → worsens cord ischemia. Concurrent trauma often adds hypoxia, hypotension, temperature dysregulation, and glucose derangement.

Mind Map — Spinal Shock vs. Neurogenic Shock vs. Autonomic Dysreflexia

Autonomic dysreflexia mechanism: pain/stimulus below the injury triggers sympathetic vasoconstriction and severe hypertension; the resulting baroreceptor response tries to compensate (bradycardia, vasodilation above the injury → flushing), but descending inhibitory signals can't get past the cord lesion to calm the sympathetic response below it. BP can become dangerously high. Most common trigger: full bladder or constipation.

Functional expectations by injury level (selected):

LevelFunctional expectation
C4Total dependence for transfers/ADLs; adaptive devices (mouth stick) for phone/computer
C5Self-feeding with devices; short-distance wheelchair mobility (electric preferred); needs assistance with ADLs
C6Independent grooming/feeding; weak hand grasp; can roll over, drive with adaptations
C7Independent wheelchair transfer; most ADLs independent; excellent bed mobility
T1Bed/wheelchair independent; self-catheterization
T10May stand with braces for exercise; still wheelchair-dependent for mobility
L2–L5Can ambulate indoors with braces/canes

Acute management

Spinal stabilization; surgical decompression within 24 hours if cord compression present. High-dose methylprednisolone is NOT currently recommended.

Autonomic dysreflexia treatment

Remove/alleviate the painful stimulus first; adrenergic blockers for the hypertensive crisis if needed.

Guillain-Barré Syndrome (GBS)

What it is

An autoimmune, inflammatory demyelinating disease of the peripheral nervous system.

Epidemiology

One of the most common causes of nontraumatic paralysis in the Western world.

Triggers

Often follows an infection 1–4 weeks prior. Campylobacter jejuni enteritis is the most strongly associated trigger.

Mechanism

Molecular mimicry — the antecedent infection triggers cross-reactive antibodies that mistakenly target peripheral nerve myelin.

Classic presentation: progressive, ascending weakness/paralysis — legs → arms → face; respiratory muscles can be affected. Peak disability ~3–4 weeks.

Diagnosis

Classic CSF finding: albuminocytologic dissociation — high protein with a NORMAL WBC count.

Treatment & prognosis

Most recover spontaneously; ~10% left with lasting disability. Plasmapheresis or IVIG for severe cases. Steroids are contraindicated.

Bell Palsy (Facial Nerve Palsy)

Definition

Bell palsy = idiopathic facial nerve paralysis specifically; almost half of all facial palsy cases are idiopathic.

Other causes to rule out

Herpes zoster, otitis media, Lyme disease, GBS, tumor, stroke.

Updated understanding of cause

Now understood to often be caused by HSV or herpes zoster reactivation.

Risk factors

Peaks age 15–45. Diabetes, hypothyroidism, pregnancy.

Clinical presentation

Rapid onset (24–48 hrs); unilateral facial droop, diminished blink, hyperacusis, decreased taste. Facial sensation is generally intact (motor nerve problem).

Workup

Must rule out otitis media, Lyme disease, tumor, stroke.

Eye protection is a priority: inability to fully close the eye risks corneal damage — lubricating drops and nighttime patching are essential.

Treatment

Corticosteroids improve recovery odds. Antivirals only if herpetic vesicles are present.

Prognosis

Most recover spontaneously (~3 weeks); about 30% have residual disability.
