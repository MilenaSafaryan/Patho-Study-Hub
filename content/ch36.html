Key Terms & Definitions

Start here

Learn these first — nearly everything else in the chapter is built from this vocabulary. Type in the box to filter.

Dysphagia
Subjective difficulty swallowing; can involve trouble initiating a swallow or a "sticking" sensation.
Odynophagia
Pain during swallowing; may accompany dysphagia.
Peristalsis
The coordinated, wave-like muscle contractions that move contents through the GI tract.
Aperistalsis
Complete absence of peristaltic activity in the esophagus.
Achalasia
A disorder of esophageal smooth muscle function that impairs peristalsis and LES relaxation.
Lower Esophageal Sphincter (LES)
The muscular ring between the esophagus and stomach that normally prevents reflux.
Pyrosis
Medical term for heartburn — a substernal burning sensation from acid reflux.
Diffuse Esophageal Spasm (DES)
High-amplitude, uncoordinated esophageal contractions that alternate with normal peristalsis, causing chest pain that can mimic angina.
Visceral Pain
Diffuse, poorly localized pain from stretching/inflammation of an internal organ.
Somatic Pain
Sharp, well-localized pain from injury to the abdominal wall or peritoneum.
Referred Pain
Pain felt at a site distant from its source, but within the same dermatome/nerve segment.
Aerophagia
Subconscious swallowing of air — a normal source of intestinal gas.
Eructation
Belching; expulsion of swallowed air from the stomach.
Constipation
Small, infrequent (fewer than 3/week), or difficult bowel movements.
Fecal Impaction
A firm, immovable mass of stool obstructing the lower GI tract, common after chronic constipation.
Diarrhea
Increased frequency and fluidity of bowel movements; >200 g stool/24 hr is the objective definition.
Osmotic Diarrhea
Diarrhea from poorly-absorbed solutes pulling water into the bowel lumen.
Secretory Diarrhea
Diarrhea from toxins that actively stimulate intestinal fluid secretion (e.g., cholera toxin).
Exudative Diarrhea
Diarrhea from mucus, blood, and protein leaking from inflamed bowel (e.g., IBD).
Stomatitis
Ulcerative inflammation of the oral mucosa (lips, cheeks, palate).
GERD
Gastroesophageal reflux disease — backflow of gastric contents into the esophagus through the LES.
Barrett Esophagus
Replacement of normal esophageal squamous epithelium with columnar tissue from chronic acid exposure; precancerous.
Hiatal Hernia
Protrusion of part of the stomach through the diaphragm into the thorax.
Mallory-Weiss Syndrome
A mucosal tear at the gastroesophageal junction from forceful vomiting, causing bleeding.
Esophageal Varices
Dilated esophageal veins from portal hypertension, usually due to cirrhosis; risk of severe hemorrhage.
Gastritis
Inflammation of the stomach lining; acute (toxins) or chronic (often H. pylori).
Helicobacter pylori
A bacterium that colonizes the gastric mucosa and is the major cause of chronic gastritis and peptic ulcer disease.
Gastroenteritis
Inflammation of the stomach and small intestine, often infectious ("stomach flu").
Peptic Ulcer Disease (PUD)
Mucosal injury of the esophagus, stomach, or duodenum from acid/pepsin exceeding mucosal defenses.
Irritable Bowel Syndrome (IBS)
A functional motility disorder with alternating diarrhea/constipation and cramping, without identifiable structural pathology.
Ileus
A functional (non-mechanical) obstruction from loss of bowel propulsive activity.
Strangulation
Impaired blood supply to a bowel segment, risking ischemia, gangrene, and perforation.
Volvulus
Twisting of the bowel on itself, causing obstruction and vascular compression.
Intussusception
Telescoping of one bowel segment into an adjacent (usually distal) segment.
Megacolon
Massive dilation of the colon, congenital or acquired.
Hirschsprung Disease
Congenital absence of autonomic ganglia in a colon segment, preventing normal relaxation and causing megacolon.
Malabsorption
Failure of the GI tract to properly absorb or digest one or more dietary nutrients.
Celiac Disease
Immune-mediated intolerance of gluten causing villus atrophy and malabsorption.
Steatorrhea
Excess fat in the stool, a sign of fat malabsorption.
Dumping Syndrome
Rapid emptying of stomach contents into the small intestine after gastric surgery, causing osmotic fluid shift and rebound hypoglycemia.
Short Bowel Syndrome
Severe malabsorption/diarrhea following surgical removal of large portions of small intestine.
Adenomatous Polyp
A benign colon growth that is the major precursor lesion for colon cancer.
Sessile vs. Pedunculated Polyp
Sessile = broad, flat base; pedunculated = attached by a narrow stalk.
Dukes Classification
A staging system for colorectal cancer based on depth of invasion and nodal/metastatic spread.

Manifestations of GI Tract Disorders

Symptoms

GI symptoms depend on where the problem is located. This section covers the core symptom vocabulary — dysphagia, pain, vomiting, gas, and bowel-pattern changes — that every later disorder in this guide will reference.

Dysphagia — difficulty swallowing

Dysphagia falls into three categories based on where the swallowing process breaks down.

Mind Map — Three Categories of Dysphagia

Definition

Subjective difficulty swallowing — trouble initiating a swallow, or the sensation that food "sticks" in the esophagus. Odynophagia (painful swallowing) may accompany it.

Red flag

Type 1 dysphagia (delivery problem) carries a major aspiration risk — food/fluid can be coughed back out the nose or mouth during attempted swallowing.

Remember it this way

  • Category 1 (delivery): the "starting the swallow" step fails → worse with liquids, aspiration risk.
  • Category 2 (transport): the esophagus body doesn't push properly → "stuck behind the sternum," solids before liquids.
  • Category 3 (entry): the exit door (LES) is blocked or dysfunctional → substernal tightness during swallowing.
Esophageal Pain

Heartburn (pyrosis)

Reflux of acidic gastric contents irritates esophageal sensory nerves and can trigger esophageal muscle spasm — substernal burning that may radiate to the neck/throat.

Non-heartburn chest pain

From esophageal distention or powerful contractions (e.g., diffuse esophageal spasm). Can be indistinguishable from angina, including radiation to neck/shoulder/arm/jaw.

Infectious esophagitis

HSV, CMV, or Candida — common in immunocompromised patients (AIDS, chemo). Produces dull, aching pain worse with swallowing.

Key point

Because esophageal pain can mimic cardiac pain so closely, cardiac causes must always be ruled out first in the workup of chest pain.

Abdominal Pain

Abdominal pain has three patterns — knowing which one you're dealing with narrows down the cause fast.

Mind Map — Three Types of Abdominal Pain

Onset matters: Sudden/instantaneous pain suggests perforation or organ rupture. Gradual pain over time suggests chronic states like diverticulitis or ulcerative colitis. Pain is rarely the only symptom — it's usually paired with vomiting or a bowel-pattern change.
Vomiting

Definition

Forceful expulsion of gastric contents via coordinated abdominal contractions plus reverse esophageal peristalsis. Almost always paired with nausea.

Not just GI

Also caused by metabolic, endocrine, vestibular (inner ear), and cardiac disease, infection, electrolyte imbalance, drugs, surgery, and early pregnancy.

Intestinal Gas

Normal sources

Swallowed air (aerophagia), normal digestion, acid neutralization by bicarbonate, and diffusion from blood.

Excess belching

Usually just swallowed air, but persistent excess can signal a motility disorder or gastric outlet obstruction.

Lactose intolerance

Missing lactase → undigested lactose is fermented by bacteria → gas. A classic example of a digestive-enzyme cause of gas.

Flatulence

Usually from bacterial action on gas-producing foods (beans, certain vegetables); can also be from IBD or enteric infection.

Constipation

Definition

Fewer than 3 stools/week, or small/difficult/hard bowel movements.

Contributing factors

  • Low-fiber, high-processed diet (fiber/cellulose bulks the stool)
  • Sedentary lifestyle
  • Pathologic causes (obstruction, diverticulitis)
  • IBS (constipation-predominant)
  • "Functional" constipation — no identifiable cause, often psychological

In elderly patients

Poor diet + inactivity + slowed peristalsis with age → chronic constipation → risk of fecal impaction (a firm, immovable stool mass obstructing the lower GI tract).

Diarrhea

Increased frequency and fluidity of bowel movements (>200 g/24 hr). Acute vs. chronic (>4 weeks). There are four classic pathophysiologic mechanisms.

Mind Map — Four Mechanisms of Diarrhea

Causes to know

  • Acute: IBD flare, acute infection, emotional stress, leakage around impacted feces
  • Chronic (>4 weeks): chronic infection (often immune-compromised), altered motility/integrity, malabsorption, endocrine disorders
  • Episodic: food allergy, GI irritants like caffeine

Disorders of the Mouth & Esophagus

Entry portals

The mouth and esophagus are the entry points for nutrients. Problems here aren't usually emergencies, but they can seriously affect long-term nutrition and quality of life.

Stomatitis

Definition

Ulcerative inflammation of the oral mucosa (buccal mucosa, lips, palate).

Causes

Mechanical trauma; irritants (alcohol, tobacco, chemicals); pathogens (bacteria, viruses); chemo drugs; radiation; autoimmune disease (reactive arthritis, Behçet syndrome); vitamin deficiency; sometimes idiopathic.

Cold sores (HSV-1)

Most common type — acute herpetic gingivostomatitis. Prodromal tingling/itching, fever, pharyngitis, then vesicles on an erythematous base that rupture into painful ulcers. HSV stays latent in dorsal ganglia and can reactivate with stress.

Treatment

Antivirals (acyclovir, valacyclovir, famciclovir) for HSV; steroids/immunomodulators for autoimmune causes; topical anesthetics/mucosal barriers (Orabase, lidocaine) for comfort and nutrition. Severe idiopathic cases may need anti-TNF agents.

Gastroesophageal Reflux Disease (GERD)

GERD = backflow of gastric contents into the esophagus through the LES. Multifactorial — think of it as a tug-of-war between things that weaken the LES / raise abdominal pressure, and the esophagus's ability to clear and tolerate that reflux.

Mind Map — GERD: Causes → Complications

H. pylori & GERD

Controversial relationship — actually an inverse association. Eradicating H. pylori for PUD can unmask/worsen reflux.

Treatment ladder

Diet/behavior changes → antacids/H2-blockers for occasional symptoms → PPIs (mainstay for chronic GERD, typically 8-week course) → surgery (laparoscopic Nissen fundoplication) for intractable cases.

Long-term PPI risks

Malabsorption of B12, iron, magnesium, calcium; increased hip fracture risk (osteoporosis); increased risk of microscopic colitis and C. diff colitis. Causality still debated.

Barrett esophagus

Columnar tissue replaces normal squamous epithelium from chronic acid exposure. ~30x increased esophageal cancer risk (0.1–0.4%/year). Requires regular endoscopic screening; dysplasia may be treated with radiofrequency ablation.

Risk factors

Anything raising intra-abdominal pressure: ascites, pregnancy, obesity, chronic straining/coughing. Linked to a Western diet and Western toileting posture.

Who's affected

~60% of people over 60 (mostly asymptomatic); more common in females.

Complications

Predisposes to GERD (heartburn, chest pain, dysphagia); Cameron ulcers (mucosal ulcers where the stomach slides through the diaphragm — can cause chronic GI blood loss); rare life-threatening incarceration.

Treatment

Same medical therapy as GERD. Surgery (Nissen fundoplication) for acute incarceration or intractable reflux.

Mallory-Weiss Syndrome

Definition

Longitudinal mucosal/submucosal tear at the cardia or lower esophagus from forceful/prolonged vomiting, where the upper esophageal sphincter fails to relax.

Typical patient

~75% male, history of excessive alcohol or salicylate use. Other contributors: coughing, straining, trauma, esophagitis, gastritis, colonoscopy prep (polyethylene glycol). Hiatal hernia predisposes.

Manifestations

Hematemesis (vomiting blood), blood per rectum after forceful vomiting, epigastric/back pain. Can range mild to massive; profuse bleeding near the cardia can → life-threatening shock.

Treatment

Diagnosed via endoscopy during active bleeding. Most need at least one transfusion, but bleeding often stops on its own. Active bleeding: endoscopic coagulation, epinephrine injection, or interventional radiology; surgery in select cases.

Esophageal Varices

Cause

Complication of portal hypertension — in the West, usually cirrhosis from alcoholism or viral hepatitis. In tropical regions, Schistosoma infection and hepatitis B are major causes.

Risk

Affects >50% of cirrhotic patients; ~30% of those with varices experience a hemorrhage within 2 years of diagnosis. Detailed management is covered in the liver chapter.

Alterations in the Integrity of the GI Wall

Inflammation & ulceration

Roughly 30 feet of GI tract can be damaged by infection, inflammation, or wall weakness. When the wall's integrity is compromised, digestion and absorption suffer along with it.

Gastritis

Acute gastritis

Triggered by toxins (alcohol, aspirin, irritants) or viral/bacterial/autoimmune illness. In the West, NSAID overuse and alcohol/tobacco are the leading causes.

Chronic gastritis

Driven mainly by H. pylori, discovered in 1983. Likely person-to-person (possibly fecal-oral) transmission. Causes persistent, superficial inflammation in nearly everyone infected.

Consequences of H. pylori

PUD, atrophic gastritis, gastric adenocarcinoma, MALT lymphoma. Possible (unconfirmed) links to colon, pancreatic, and hepatobiliary cancer.

Manifestations & treatment

May be asymptomatic; acute cases show anorexia, nausea, vomiting, postprandial discomfort, occasionally hematemesis. Resolves once the trigger is removed and the epithelium renews.

Gastroenteritis

Definition

Inflammation of the stomach and small intestine. Chronic form usually stems from a primary disorder like Crohn disease. Acute form ("stomach flu") is typically infectious.

Causes

Direct infection (e.g., Norwalk/norovirus), preformed bacterial toxins (S. aureus, B. cereus), toxin-producing bacteria (C. perfringens), or disrupted normal flora (e.g., during travel).

Who's at risk

Usually self-limited in adults, but fluid loss from diarrhea/vomiting can be life-threatening in children and the elderly, especially in resource-poor settings.

Treatment

Supportive — fluid and electrolyte replacement, especially oral rehydration solutions (Oralyte, Pedialyte).

Peptic Ulcer Disease (PUD)

PUD happens when injury factors (acid, pepsin, NSAIDs, H. pylori) overwhelm the mucosa's protective mechanisms.

Mind Map — PUD Pathogenesis

Gastric vs. Duodenal ulcer

Gastric: epithelial barrier breakdown is primary; only ~37% have H. pylori — all GUs need endoscopy + biopsy to rule out malignancy.
Duodenal: excess acid secretion (↑vagal tone) is primary; up to 95% have H. pylori; testing usually not needed since probability is so high.

Pain pattern

GU: pain on an empty stomach, sometimes soon after eating.
DU: pain 2–3 hours after a meal, relieved by more food.

H. pylori mechanics

Thrives in acid; slows healing and raises recurrence. Genes like CagA and dupA damage cells and block epithelial repair.

Treatment

PPIs to block acid secretion; sucralfate as a protective coating; eradicate H. pylori when present. Smoking worsens healing and raises recurrence — cessation matters.

Watch for: A significant number of ulcers are asymptomatic — GI bleeding or perforation can be the first sign, with no prior warning.

Alterations in Motility & Malabsorption

Movement problems

Too-fast transit means not enough time to absorb nutrients. Too-slow or blocked transit causes distention, obstruction, and potentially ischemia. Same GI tract, opposite failure modes.

Irritable Bowel Syndrome (IBS)

Definition

Alternating diarrhea and constipation with abdominal cramping — with no identifiable structural pathology. This is the key distinction from IBD. Affects up to 20% of the US population.

Pathogenesis

Primarily a motility disorder — colonic slow-wave activity is markedly increased, and sensory response to distention is heightened. Genetic vs. environmental (infection, stress, diet) triggers still unclear.

Treatment — diarrhea type

Dietary modification (avoid lactose, legumes, gas-forming foods) → antispasmodics (loperamide, eluxadoline) → bile-acid sequestrant if needed. Alosetron helps but has restricted use due to side effects; eluxadoline carries pancreatitis risk.

Treatment — constipation type

Osmotic laxative (polyethylene glycol) first-line; lubiprostone, linaclotide, plecanatide, tenapanor for refractory cases. High-fiber diet helps normalize myoelectric activity.

Intestinal Obstruction

Blockage can be mechanical (a physical blocker) or functional (loss of propulsion, called ileus) — the distinction drives everything about workup and treatment.

Mind Map — Mechanical vs. Functional Obstruction

Untreated small-bowel obstruction carries a high risk of death from shock and vascular collapse — this is a surgical emergency in progression.

Treatment

Surgery to correct/remove the mechanical cause; supportive care with bowel decompression (endoscopy/tubes) and fluid/electrolyte replacement.

Volvulus

Definition

The bowel twists on itself, obstructing the lumen and compressing blood vessels. Most common sites: cecum and sigmoid colon.

Cause & risk

Rotation anomaly, ingested foreign body, or adhesion (cause not always identifiable). More common in elderly patients with other medical conditions.

Danger

Twisting cuts blood flow → gangrene, necrosis, perforation. If both ends of a segment are twisted, a closed-loop obstruction results.

Treatment

Endoscopic manipulation is usually tried first for colonic volvulus; surgery is reserved for failure of that approach.

Intussusception

Definition

Telescoping of a bowel segment into the adjacent (usually distal) segment.

Who's affected

Most common in infants, 3x more often in males. Cause in infants usually unknown, though linked to viral infections and (historically) some rotavirus vaccines. In older children: Meckel diverticulum. In adults: usually a tumor.

Progression

Peristalsis pulls in more bowel → edema, venous engorgement, hemorrhage → obstruction → gangrene, shock, perforation if surgery is delayed.

Megacolon

Most common Western cause

Prolonged constipation/obstipation — including in young children navigating toilet training, and elderly patients relying long-term on enemas/laxatives.

Important consideration

A small number of children with encopresis have experienced sexual abuse — this should be considered, even though most affected children are psychologically normal.

Other causes

Hirschsprung disease (congenital); Chagas disease (Trypanosoma cruzi — common in Central/South America); C. difficile colitis (acute megacolon = surgical emergency); Ogilvie syndrome (rare).

Hirschsprung Disease

Definition

Congenital absence/reduction of autonomic nerve ganglia in the smooth muscle of the large intestine — usually the rectosigmoid area (90% of cases).

Mechanism

The aganglionic segment contracts but can't relax to propel contents forward → stool stasis → proximal colon dilation (megacolon).

Epidemiology

~1 in 5,000 live births; can be familial; ~3.8:1 male predominance; often coexists with Down syndrome.

Danger in infants

Fecal stagnation can cause enterocolitis with bacterial overgrowth, profuse diarrhea, hypovolemic shock, and perforation. Colonic lavage stabilizes the infant until surgery (the definitive treatment).

Mucosal Malabsorption Disorders

Nutrient failure

Malabsorption = failure to absorb or digest dietary nutrients — typically shows up as diarrhea. Here it's grouped by cause: mucosal disease of the small intestine, or surgical loss of stomach/bowel.

Mind Map — Malabsorption Disorders Overview

Celiac Disease

Cause

Immune reaction to gliadin (a gluten protein in wheat) in genetically predisposed people → villus atrophy and loss of surface enzyme activity.

Epidemiology

2x more common in females; prevalence ~1 in 300, mostly northwestern European ancestry. Onset can be in infancy or, more commonly, the 4th–5th decade.

Manifestations

Abdominal pain, weight loss, flatulence, diarrhea; fat-soluble vitamin deficiency, osteoporosis; the skin condition dermatitis herpetiformis; increased risk of GI lymphoma.

Diagnosis & treatment

Blood tests (tTG-IgA, EMA-IgA, DGP-IgA) plus biopsy confirmation. Treatment = strict gluten-free diet + supplement iron/folate/B12/fat-soluble vitamins. Refractory cases may need steroids; rule out lymphoma if non-responsive.

Tropical Sprue

Cause

Malabsorptive syndrome of unclear cause, likely from bacterial overgrowth producing fermentation products that damage small-intestine mucosa.

Manifestations

Severe diarrhea with blood-tinged stools, abdominal distention, steatorrhea. Can cause B12/folate deficiency.

Treatment

Caribbean-origin cases respond well to broad-spectrum antibiotics (linked to enterotoxin-producing coliforms); other regions (e.g., India) respond less predictably. Add antidiarrheals and folic acid replacement.

Dumping Syndrome

Cause

Impaired gastric emptying after gastrectomy → large volume of hyperosmolar chyme "dumped" rapidly into the small intestine. Notably occurs with Roux-en-Y gastric bypass.

Mechanism

Hyperosmolar contents draw water into the lumen → diarrhea + abdominal pain. Rapid glucose absorption → insulin spike → rebound hypoglycemia 1–3 hours after eating.

Manifestations

Early (15–30 min): nausea, vomiting, abdominal pain, diarrhea, palpitations, flushing. Late (hours later): rebound hypoglycemia symptoms.

Treatment

Small, frequent meals (6–8x/day) instead of 3 large ones; restrict carbohydrates; medications to reduce motility or octreotide; rare cases need surgical rerouting.

Short Bowel Syndrome

Cause

Severe diarrhea/malabsorption after surgical removal of large portions of small intestine. Severity depends on how much and which part was removed.

High-risk resection

Removing the distal ileum + ileocecal valve is worst — loses the valve's role in controlling transit time, causing too-rapid transit, plus loses the site of vitamin B12/bile acid absorption (B12 needs IM replacement).

Manifestations

Protein/calorie malnutrition, vitamin deficiencies, weight loss, failure to thrive.

Treatment

The bowel can adapt — remaining villi enlarge if oral nutrients are gradually reintroduced. Total parenteral nutrition (IV) may be needed temporarily or indefinitely. Intestinal transplant is an option in select cases.

Neoplasms of the GI Tract

Tumors & cancer

GI cancers can develop anywhere along the tract, each with distinct risk factors, symptom patterns, and prognosis. A useful pattern: right-sided colon tumors bleed silently; left-sided tumors obstruct early.

Mind Map — GI Neoplasms Overview

Esophageal Cancer

Epidemiology

1–2% of all cancers (~17,000 US cases/yr); 3x more common in males, typically age 60+. Overall 5-year survival only ~18%.

Risk factors

Smoking, alcohol, and diets low in fruit/vegetables cause ~90% of squamous cell cases. Chronic severe reflux (especially with achalasia) is a major risk for adenocarcinoma. H. pylori may actually be protective here.

Trend

Adenocarcinoma of the gastroesophageal junction is now more common in the US than squamous cell cancer, reflecting rising Barrett esophagus rates.

Manifestations

Insidious spread via esophageal lymphatics; can form esophagobronchial/esophagopleural fistulas (→ pneumonia/abscess). Luminal obstruction causes dysphagia, odynophagia, weight loss.

Treatment

Stenting, ablation for luminal patency; surgical resection ± chemo/radiation for selected patients (up to 30% curative-intent survival), but metastasis at diagnosis (liver, lungs) is common and prognosis is generally poor.

Gastric Carcinoma

Epidemiology

~25,000 US cases/yr, males >30 at higher risk (2:1 ratio). Japan's prevalence is ~10x the US. Overall 5-yr survival ~28%, better if caught early (before muscle-layer invasion).

Cause

H. pylori doubles risk via multifocal atrophic gastritis. Epstein-Barr virus is another contributor. Aspirin appears protective; alcohol is not a major risk factor here (unlike esophageal cancer).

Manifestations

Early disease is usually silent. Advanced disease: anorexia, weight loss, GI bleeding; spreads rapidly via lymphatics, bloodstream, and direct extension.

Treatment

Surgical resection with margins is the only truly effective treatment. Neoadjuvant chemo can help resectable cases; immunotherapy is an active research area.

Small Intestinal Neoplasms

Overview

Uncommon — under 5% of GI tumors. Usually adenocarcinoma, in people over 50; also carcinoid tumors, lymphoma, sarcoma.

Manifestations

Partial/complete small bowel obstruction; if near the ampulla of Vater, can obstruct the common bile duct → jaundice. Bleeding, ulceration, and occasionally intussusception can occur.

Treatment

Surgical removal of the tumor and affected bowel; chemotherapy for lymphoma/carcinoid.

Colon Polyps

Definition

Any protrusion into the GI lumen. Sessile = broad flat base. Pedunculated = attached by a narrow stalk.

Cancer risk

Adenomatous polyps are the major precursor of colon cancer via dysplasia — some already contain carcinoma in situ. Hyperplastic polyps are benign and carry no cancer risk.

Management

Usually asymptomatic (occasionally occult/gross bleeding or obstruction-related pain). Removed via biopsy during sigmoidoscopy/colonoscopy.

Colon Cancer

Epidemiology

~145,000 US cases/yr, equal male/female distribution. #2 cause of cancer death (after lung).

Risk factors

Advancing age (risk doubles per decade after 40, up to 80); high-fat/low-fiber diet; UC/CD; family history (>15% risk with a first-degree relative vs. 5% general population); hereditary syndromes (familial adenomatous polyposis / Gardner syndrome, hereditary nonpolyposis colorectal cancer).

Screening

Colonoscopy every 10 years starting at age 45 for average risk; more aggressive screening for those with family history or IBD.
