Key Terms & Definitions

Type in the box to filter.

Hyperfunction / Hypofunction
Excessively high or depressed hormone levels (or conditions mimicking them) — the two basic categories of endocrine disease.
Primary endocrine disorder
Intrinsic malfunction of the hormone-producing target gland itself.
Secondary endocrine disorder
Malfunction of the hypothalamus/pituitary cells that control the target gland.
Tertiary endocrine disorder
Malfunction originating in the hypothalamus (upstream of the pituitary).
Ectopic hormone production
Hormone secretion from an abnormal tissue site, usually a malignant tumor (e.g., lung tumors secreting ADH).
Target tissue resistance
Normal or elevated hormone levels without normal hormonal action, due to receptor or postreceptor defects.
Growth hormone (GH)
Anterior pituitary hormone that increases lean body mass, reduces fat, and raises blood glucose; many effects mediated by IGF-1.
IGF-1 (somatomedin)
Insulin-like growth factor-1, released from the liver in response to GH; mediates most GH effects and is a stable marker of GH secretion.
Craniopharyngioma
A slow-growing tumor at the junction of the anterior/posterior pituitary, believed present at birth; a common cause of GH deficiency.
Pituitary giantism
GH excess occurring before epiphyseal closure, causing markedly accelerated linear growth in children.
Acromegaly
GH excess in adults (after epiphyseal closure) causing bony/soft tissue overgrowth without increased height.
TRH / TSH
Thyroid-releasing hormone (hypothalamus) stimulates TSH (pituitary), which stimulates T3/T4 release from the thyroid.
Hashimoto (lymphocytic) thyroiditis
Autoimmune thyroiditis; the most common cause of acquired hypothyroidism.
Cretinism
Significant mental/physical developmental defects from untreated congenital hypothyroidism.
Myxedema
Generalized nonpitting edema from glycosaminoglycan accumulation, seen in severe/prolonged hypothyroidism.
Myxedema coma
A life-threatening medical emergency from severe untreated hypothyroidism, with altered mental state and thermoregulation problems.
Graves disease
Autoimmune hyperthyroidism from autoantibodies that stimulate TSH receptors, causing diffuse toxic goiter and often exophthalmos.
Exophthalmos
Protrusion of the eyes from retroorbital tissue swelling in Graves disease, caused by autoimmune injury (not by thyroid hormone levels directly).
Thyroid storm
Life-threatening acute release of excessive thyroid hormone, with fever, severe tachycardia, and possible heart failure.
Cortisol
The primary glucocorticoid; provides negative feedback on CRH/ACTH release.
Addison disease
Primary adrenocortical insufficiency from destruction of the adrenal cortex.
Addisonian crisis
Acute, life-threatening adrenal insufficiency with vascular collapse.
Congenital adrenal hyperplasia (CAH)
An enzymatic defect in cortisol synthesis causing adrenal hyperplasia, cortisol deficiency, and androgen excess.
Cushing syndrome
The clinical features of hypercortisolism, regardless of cause.
Cushing disease
Hypercortisolism specifically caused by a pituitary ACTH-secreting adenoma.
Pheochromocytoma
A usually-benign chromaffin tissue tumor (usually in the adrenal medulla) causing excess catecholamine secretion and hypertension.
Parathyroid hormone (PTH)
Increases serum calcium via effects on bone, kidney, and (with vitamin D) intestine; secretion is regulated directly by serum calcium, not the pituitary.
CaSR
Calcium-sensing receptor — a G protein-coupled receptor that lets parathyroid cells detect serum calcium and regulate PTH gene expression.
Calcitonin
Produced by thyroid parafollicular (C) cells; tends to decrease blood calcium by promoting bone formation and inhibiting resorption.
Chvostek sign
Facial muscle contraction from tapping the facial nerve anterior to the ear — indicates neuromuscular hyperexcitability from hypocalcemia.
Trousseau sign
Carpal spasm from BP-cuff-induced ischemia — indicates neuromuscular hyperexcitability from hypocalcemia.
ADH (vasopressin)
Posterior pituitary hormone that increases renal collecting duct/distal tubule water permeability and reabsorption.
Diabetes insipidus (DI)
Insufficient ADH activity, causing excessive dilute urine loss and hypernatremia.
Central DI
DI from failure to produce/release ADH from the hypothalamus/posterior pituitary.
Nephrogenic DI
DI from renal unresponsiveness to ADH (kidney disease, receptor defects, electrolyte problems, drugs like lithium).
SIADH
Syndrome of inappropriate ADH secretion — excess ADH causes water retention and dilutional hyponatremia.

Basic Concepts of Endocrine Disorders

Foundations

Almost every endocrine disorder in this chapter can be sorted using the same two questions: is it too much hormone or too little, and is the problem in the gland itself or in the system telling the gland what to do?

Etiology & Classification of Endocrine Disorders

Where dysfunction originates

The hypothalamus/pituitary, the hormone-producing gland itself, or the target tissue. Etiology may be congenital, infectious, autoimmune, neoplastic, idiopathic, or iatrogenic.

Genetic causes

Inborn defects can cause excess hormone precursor production (enzymatic block) or enzyme deficiencies impairing synthesis — e.g., congenital adrenal hyperplasia (enlarged adrenals, deficient cortisol).

Autoimmune causes

More common in females; genetic predisposition + environmental trigger. Antibodies against self-tissue can cause hyperfunction (Graves disease) or gland destruction/hypofunction (Addison disease).

Ectopic hormone production

Usually from a malignant tumor — e.g., some lung tumors secrete ADH, causing water intoxication and hyponatremia.

Target tissue resistance

Normal/elevated hormone levels with no normal hormonal action — shown by diminished response to exogenous hormone. Mechanisms: receptor defects, antireceptor antibodies, defective postreceptor action.

Iatrogenic causes

Chemotherapy, radiation, or surgical gland removal — often results in chronic hypofunction needing long-term hormone replacement.

Mind Map — Primary vs. Secondary Endocrine Disorders (Thyroid Example)

The rule to memorize

  • The target gland hormone (T4, cortisol, etc.) is what causes symptoms — always the starting point for interpreting labs.
  • Primary disorder: target gland hormone is abnormal, trophic hormone (TSH, ACTH) moves in the opposite direction trying to compensate.
  • Secondary disorder: both the trophic hormone and target gland hormone move in the same direction (both low, or both high).

Growth Hormone Disorders

Anterior pituitary

GH is released under hypothalamic control (GH-releasing hormone stimulates, somatostatin inhibits) and acts mostly through liver-derived IGF-1. Its effects — and its disorders — look completely different depending on whether the bone epiphyses are still open.

Mind Map — GH Imbalance by Age

Growth Hormone Deficiency

Three categories

(1) Decreased GH secretion, (2) defective GH action (abnormal GH or receptor), (3) defective IGF-1 generation.

Associated birth history

Prolonged labor or breech delivery is common. May also occur with midline craniocerebral defects — consider GH deficiency in any child with nystagmus, retinal abnormalities, or midline/midfacial defects like cleft lip/palate (pituitary develops alongside these structures).

Common causative tumors

Midline brain tumors — optic nerve gliomas and craniopharyngiomas (arise at the anterior/posterior pituitary junction, present at birth, slow-growing; may grow large without typical increased-ICP signs — delayed growth may be the first clue in older children).

Other causes

Radiation therapy (for brain tumors/leukemia) damaging hypothalamic/pituitary function; traumatic skull/sella turcica injury interrupting vascular connections.

Infants

Usually normal birth length/weight. Risk of hypoglycemia (GH + cortisol needed for euglycemia) — can occur after fasting as brief as 3 hours; recurrent hypoglycemia risks seizures/permanent cerebral damage. In utero deficiency in males → micropenis, undescended testicles.

Children

Fall below 3rd percentile growth; delayed dental eruption/irregular permanent teeth; thin hair, poor nail growth. Older children: more fat mass, less muscle mass, delayed bone formation, delayed puberty if other pituitary hormones are affected.

Diagnosis

Annual growth evaluation; if abnormal velocity, endocrinologic workup with GH-stimulating agents (insulin, arginine, levodopa, clonidine) or timed nighttime GH sampling.

Treatment

GH replacement — stimulates linear growth in children with open epiphyses, and depletes the excess fat stores typical of GH-deficient children.

Adults: Can become GH deficient after pituitary tumor resection or head trauma. Manifestations debated, but may include diminished lean body mass, hypercholesterolemia, and decreased bone density.
Growth Hormone Excess (Giantism & Acromegaly)

Cause

Nearly always a benign pituitary somatotropic adenoma producing uncontrolled GH. GH + liver-derived IGF-1 act together to drive tissue growth.

Best diagnostic marker

Serum IGF-1 — more stable than GH (which fluctuates through the day); an elevated IGF-1 indicates GH hypersecretion. High IGF-1 plus GH not suppressed by oral glucose confirms the diagnosis.

Giantism (childhood, epiphyses open)

Markedly accelerated growth velocity, quickly exceeding the 95th percentile; untreated may reach 8+ feet, with risk of early cardiovascular death from cardiomegaly/heart failure.

Acromegaly (adults, epiphyses closed)

Equal male/female frequency, 4th–5th decades; can be clinically subtle. Bone density and short-bone thickness (hands, feet) increase instead of height.

Progressive changes

Increased ring/shoe size; prominent brow (frontal sinus growth); progressive underbite/prognathism (mandible growth); coarsened facial features, skin tags; goiter and cardiomegaly (organ enlargement); deepened voice (vocal cord thickening); enlarged tongue → sleep apnea; increased colonic polyps (malignant potential).

Diagnostic delay

Average patient has an active pituitary tumor for 7+ years before evaluation — changes are usually attributed to "just growing older." Bone/soft tissue changes are mostly irreversible.

Watch for: Headache and visual disturbances if the tumor enlarges (↑ ICP); glucose intolerance/hyperglycemia is common — GH is a "diabetogenic hormone."

Treatment

Surgical removal (transsphenoidal approach, though tumor may be too large to fully resect); octreotide (synthetic somatostatin) suppresses GH production; pegvisomant (GH receptor antagonist) blocks GH action at target tissues; postoperative radiation for incompletely resected tumors.

Thyroid Hormone Disorders

T3/T4/TSH axis

TRH (hypothalamus) → TSH (pituitary) → T3/T4 (thyroid). Thyroid hormone governs the body's metabolic rate, so hypo- and hyperthyroidism produce nearly mirror-image symptom sets.

Hypothyroidism

Congenital causes

Thyroid dysgenesis (most common), abnormal TSH receptors, defective thyroid hormone synthesis. Significant untreated developmental defects = "cretinism."

Most common acquired cause

Hashimoto (lymphocytic/autoimmune) thyroiditis — lymphocytic infiltration enlarges the thyroid; hormone production falls, TSH rises, and symptoms progress as the gland fibroses.

Other acquired causes

Thyroid irradiation, surgical removal, iodine deficiency (prevents T3/T4 formation but not thyroglobulin — unchecked TSH drives goiter formation), goitrogenic foods (turnips/cabbage — minimal clinical significance), lithium (hypothyroidism in up to 20% of users).

Secondary hypothyroidism

Uncommon; from defects in TSH production — severe head trauma, cranial neoplasms, brain infections, cranial irradiation, neurosurgery.

Congenital hypothyroidism is a true emergency for the brain: thyroid hormone is essential for CNS development — irreversible intellectual disability occurs without early replacement therapy. Newborn screening has made this rare.

Untreated infant signs

Dull appearance, thick protuberant tongue, thick lips (feeding difficulty), prolonged neonatal jaundice, poor muscle tone, bradycardia, mottled extremities, umbilical hernia, hoarse cry.

Adult/older child manifestations

↓ basal metabolic rate underlies most symptoms: weakness, lethargy, cold intolerance, ↓ appetite, bradycardia, narrow pulse pressure, weight gain, ↑ cholesterol/triglycerides, ↑ atherosclerosis, goiter, cool/dry skin, constipation, depression, concentration/memory problems. Females: menstrual irregularities with increased flow/clotting.

Myxedema & myxedema coma: Severe/prolonged hypothyroidism causes nonpitting edema from glycosaminoglycan accumulation. Myxedema coma is a life-threatening emergency — altered mental state, thermoregulation problems, usually triggered by sepsis, trauma, or certain medications.

Diagnosis

Elevated TSH is the most sensitive early indicator in primary hypothyroidism (detectable before symptoms) — T4/T3 decline comes later. In rare hypothalamic-pituitary dysfunction, BOTH TSH and T4 are inappropriately low.

Treatment

Oral levothyroxine. Symptoms resolve gradually over weeks; TSH should normalize as euthyroid state returns. Overtreatment (low TSH) should be avoided — contributes to osteoporosis. Too-rapid replacement can cause insomnia, anxiety, mood lability.

Hyperthyroidism & Graves Disease

Three general mechanisms

(1) Follicular cell hyperfunction (Graves disease), (2) follicular cell destruction releasing preformed hormone (Hashimoto thyroiditis, subacute thyroiditis), (3) exogenous thyroid hormone ingestion.

Primary vs. secondary hyperfunction

Primary (autonomous): adenomas, rarely carcinoma. Secondary: TSH receptor stimulation.

Interesting twist: Hashimoto thyroiditis (usually the classic cause of hypothyroidism) often presents FIRST with hyperthyroid symptoms — the injured gland releases its stored hormone — before progressing to hypothyroidism as the autoimmune process destroys the gland.

Graves disease

Most common cause of hyperthyroidism — autoantibodies bind and stimulate TSH receptors, causing diffuse toxic goiter. Autoimmune; linked to HLA B8/DR3; peak incidence in the 2nd–3rd decades. High T3/T4 suppresses pituitary TSH → very low serum TSH.

Symptoms — mechanism

From increased metabolic rate + enhanced sympathetic activity: insomnia, restlessness, tremor, irritability, palpitations, tachycardia, heat intolerance, diaphoresis, poor concentration. Weight loss despite increased appetite. Amenorrhea/scant menses in females.

Exophthalmos

Autoimmune injury to retroocular structures (NOT caused by thyroid hormone levels) — autoantibodies bind TSH and IGF-1 receptors on orbital fibroblasts, triggering immune infiltration, cytokine release, and excess glycosaminoglycan secretion — tissue swells, pushing the eye forward. Reducing thyroid hormone doesn't necessarily stop progression. Newer drug: teprotumumab-trbw (blocks IGF-1 receptor).

Diagnosis

Undetectable TSH is the best indicator of primary hyperthyroidism; T4/T3 elevated. 24-hr radioactive iodine uptake with diffuse homogeneous uptake confirms Graves and excludes neoplasm.

Thyroid storm: Life-threatening acute release of excess thyroid hormone, triggered by physical/psychological stress or gland manipulation (surgery, procedures). Presents with fever, marked tachycardia, dysrhythmias, heart failure, extreme agitation/psychosis — requires prompt treatment.

Treatment

β-blockers for acute symptoms; drugs inhibiting thyroid hormone production; radioactive iodine to destroy part of the gland; surgery reserved for tumor-associated hyperthyroidism (risk: acute hypocalcemia if parathyroids inadvertently removed). Rare pituitary-adenoma-driven cases: surgical/laser pituitary destruction.

Adrenocortical Hormone Disorders

Cortisol, aldosterone, androgens

The adrenal cortex makes three hormone classes. Cortisol is under CRH/ACTH feedback control; aldosterone answers to angiotensin II, not the pituitary; adrenal androgens have no real feedback control at all — which explains why they run wild in congenital adrenal hyperplasia.

Adrenocortical Insufficiency (Addison Disease)

Three levels

Primary (Addison disease): adrenal cortex disease itself. Secondary: inadequate pituitary ACTH. Tertiary: inadequate hypothalamic CRH.

Primary causes

Idiopathic/autoimmune destruction, tuberculosis, adrenal trauma/hemorrhage (often with anticoagulant therapy), fungal disease (histoplasmosis), neoplasia. High functional reserve — symptoms may not appear until 90% of cortical tissue is nonfunctional.

Secondary causes

Usually iatrogenic — prolonged exogenous corticosteroid use suppresses CRH/ACTH via negative feedback, causing adrenal cortex atrophy. Sudden steroid withdrawal or acute stress can't be met by the atrophied gland → acute insufficiency. Also: pituitary/hypothalamic tumors, infection, radiation, postpartum necrosis, trauma, surgery.

Addisonian crisis

A true medical emergency — inadequate glucocorticoids AND mineralocorticoids. Diminished vascular tone, reduced cardiac output, inadequate blood volume → potentially lethal vascular collapse (hypotension, tachycardia, shock).

Early manifestations

Anorexia, weight loss, weakness, malaise, apathy, electrolyte imbalance, and hyperpigmentation (unsuppressed ACTH stimulates melanocyte receptors). Salt craving from sodium deficit.

If untreated

GI symptoms (nausea, vomiting, diarrhea, dehydration), hypotension, tachycardia — sudden onset suggests acute (emergency) adrenal insufficiency.

Diagnosis

Decreased plasma cortisol; therapy is often started presumptively since decompensation progresses fast. ACTH (cosyntropin) provocation test for chronic cases — cortisol should rise after synthetic ACTH if the adrenal cortex works normally; failure indicates primary insufficiency. Imaging: small glands = autoimmune destruction; large/calcified = TB; large/smooth = hemorrhage.

Treatment

Hormone replacement mimicking natural rhythm (~2/3 dose morning, 1/3 evening). Crisis: intermittent IV glucocorticoids until symptoms resolve, then taper; plus volume replacement for mineralocorticoid-deficiency-related urine losses. Stress dosing: illness, injury, or major psychological stress can double/triple the needed daily dose; use parenteral route if oral intake is restricted.

Congenital Adrenal Hyperplasia (Adrenogenital Syndrome)

Mind Map — CAH Pathophysiology

Female newborns

Virilization of genitalia — enlarged clitoris, fused labia resembling a scrotal sac.

Male newborns

Enlarged penis and hyperpigmented scrotum — often subtle enough to be missed.

Adult female (late-onset)

Virile characteristics: beard, deeper voice, baldness, masculine pubic hair distribution, clitoral growth resembling a penis, masculine protein deposition in skin/muscle.

Adult male / prepubertal male

Adult males: virilization less obvious (masculine traits are normal anyway) → harder to diagnose. Prepubertal males: usually causes precocious puberty.

Treatment

Glucocorticoid therapy — prevents adrenal insufficiency complications AND suppresses ACTH, letting the adrenal cortex atrophy and androgen overproduction stop.

Hypercortisolism (Cushing Syndrome)

Levels of origin

Primary: adrenal cortex disease (adenoma). Secondary: pituitary ACTH hyperfunction. Tertiary: hypothalamic dysfunction/injury.

Terminology

Cushing syndrome = the clinical picture of hypercortisolism, any cause. Cushing disease = specifically the pituitary-dependent form.

Common causes

Pediatric/adult: excess pituitary ACTH from microadenomas/adenomas; ectopic ACTH from nonpituitary tumors. In the US, exogenous steroids (for allergic/autoimmune disease) are a very common cause.

Classic appearance

Round "moon face" with flushed cheeks, increased total body fat (especially abdominal), dorsocervical fat pad, capillary fragility, thin skin with purple striae/ecchymoses over abdomen/arms/thighs.

Metabolic effects

Decreased muscle mass/weakness; cortisol increases insulin resistance → glucose intolerance/hyperglycemia; hypertension (from salt-retaining activity + increased blood volume); low serum potassium.

Adrenal Medulla Disorder: Pheochromocytoma

Catecholamines
Pheochromocytoma

Normal medulla function

Secretes catecholamines under sympathetic stimulation: epinephrine (~80%) and norepinephrine (~20%, also the postganglionic sympathetic neurotransmitter). Both bind adrenergic receptors on effectors (heart, smooth muscle, glands) to enhance/prolong sympathetic effects.

What it is

A tumor of chromaffin tissue causing excess catecholamine production/release. Usually benign (malignant in ~10%); usually in the adrenal medulla but can arise anywhere chromaffin tissue exists (e.g., sympathetic ganglia).

Mind Map — Pheochromocytoma: Classic Triad

Triggers for hypertensive episodes

Stress, excitement, physical activity, certain drugs, tobacco smoking.

Other symptoms

Tremor, nervousness, emotional lability, pallor, fatigue, GI complaints, orthostatic hypotension; hypermetabolic signs like fever and weight loss.

Diagnosis

CT and MRI are commonly used to locate adrenal tumors.

Why urgency matters

Uncontrolled hypertension can cause end-organ damage and stroke — prompt diagnosis and aggressive therapy are necessary.

Treatment

Surgical removal is the usual (and only curative) treatment. Presurgical sympathetic blocking medications manage BP/symptoms. If surgery is contraindicated, drugs blocking catecholamine production are an option. After a stabilization period post-surgery, the sympathetic nervous system compensates and hormone replacement isn't needed. Annual follow-up recommended due to recurrence risk.

Parathyroid Gland Disorders

Calcium regulation

There are usually four parathyroid glands, and unlike everything covered so far, PTH answers directly to serum calcium — not the hypothalamic-pituitary system at all.

How PTH secretion is controlled

The CaSR (calcium-sensing receptor, a G protein-coupled receptor) detects serum calcium. Low calcium → PTH release. High calcium → PTH suppression. NOT under hypothalamic-pituitary control.

PTH's three targets

Bone: ↑ osteoclastic activity, releasing calcium/phosphate. Kidney: ↑ renal calcium reabsorption (↓ urinary loss). Intestine: ↑ absorption, but this requires vitamin D.

Calcitonin

From thyroid C cells; opposes PTH — increases osteoblastic bone formation and inhibits osteoclastic breakdown, tending to lower blood calcium. Role in overall calcium homeostasis isn't fully clear.

Hyperparathyroidism

Cause

Often unclear etiology; PTH keeps secreting despite elevated calcium. Some cases from CaSR gene mutations. Single adenoma causes the majority of cases; hyperplasia accounts for the rest.

An important distinction

Primary hyperparathyroidism rarely causes hypercalcemic crisis. Malignant tumors secreting PTH-like hormone elsewhere in the body are actually the more frequent cause of extreme hypercalcemia/hypercalcemic crisis.

Pregnancy complication

Maternal hypercalcemia suppresses fetal/newborn PTH → neonatal hypocalcemia and tetany. This may be the first clue that the mother has undiagnosed hyperparathyroidism.

Renal failure link

Chronic renal failure causes hyperparathyroidism via reduced active vitamin D (impairs calcium absorption) and impaired glomerular filtration (limits phosphate excretion).

Drug-induced pseudo-hyperparathyroidism

Lithium and thiazides can raise serum calcium, risking misdiagnosis.

Manifestations

May be asymptomatic hypercalcemia. Kidney stones and bone demineralization (osteoporosis) are common. Severe hypercalcemia: polyuria, dehydration, anorexia, nausea, vomiting, constipation; cardiac issues (bradycardia, heart block, cardiac arrest).

Labs (primary, non-renal)

↑ serum calcium, low-to-low-normal serum phosphorus, ↑ urinary calcium/phosphate excretion, ↑ serum PTH.

Treatment

Surgical removal of the abnormal gland(s) is the treatment of choice. Asymptomatic cases may defer surgery, managed with hydration (prevents kidney stones) and ambulation (maintains bone density). Hypercalcemic crisis: rapid volume expansion with normal saline (improves GFR, ↑ calcium excretion); non-thiazide diuretics can further increase calcium excretion.

Hypoparathyroidism

Most common cause

Parathyroid or thyroid surgery/radiation — damage to parathyroid blood supply, postsurgical swelling, or fibrosis after thyroidectomy.

After single-adenoma removal

The other parathyroids had been suppressed by the hyperfunctioning gland — removing it can cause a temporary deficiency state until the remaining glands resume normal function.

Other causes

Congenital absence of parathyroid tissue, idiopathic hypoparathyroidism (infants/children), autoimmune destruction.

Acute hypocalcemia manifestations

Circumoral numbness, distal extremity paresthesias, muscle cramps, fatigue, neuromuscular irritability, anxiety, nonspecific EEG changes, prolonged QT interval.

Severe manifestations

Carpopedal spasm, laryngospasm, seizures.

Bedside signs of neuromuscular irritability

Chvostek sign: facial muscle contraction from tapping the facial nerve anterior to the ear. Trousseau sign: carpal spasm from BP-cuff-induced ischemia (inflated above systolic pressure for 3–5 min).

Labs

Low serum calcium, elevated phosphorus; parathyroid antibodies present if autoimmune.

Treatment

Emergency: IV calcium for acute hypocalcemic crisis (tetany, laryngospasm, convulsions); calcitriol (activated vitamin D) may help. Long-term: oral calcium + vitamin D supplementation (not PTH replacement).

Antidiuretic Hormone (ADH) Disorders

Posterior pituitary

ADH (vasopressin) controls renal water reabsorption in response to blood osmolality. Its two disorders are near-perfect opposites: too little ADH (or unresponsive kidneys) causes water loss and hypernatremia; too much causes water retention and hyponatremia.

Mind Map — DI vs. SIADH

Diabetes Insipidus (DI)

Definition

Insufficient ADH activity causing excessive dilute urine loss. ADH normally increases collecting duct/distal tubule permeability to water.

Central DI causes

Damage to hypothalamic ADH-producing cells: closed head trauma, intracranial tumors, neurosurgery. Adult DI breakdown: 30% idiopathic, 20% surgical brain tumor treatment, 16% nonsurgical brain trauma, 25% secondary to brain tumors, 9% post-hypophysectomy.

Nephrogenic DI causes

Kidney unable to respond to ADH — chronic renal disease, receptor defects, electrolyte abnormalities, drugs (e.g., lithium).

Drug effects on ADH

Suppress release: alcohol, phenytoin, α-adrenergic agents. Enhance release: β-adrenergic agents, barbiturates, carbamazepine, nicotine, morphine, and others.

Manifestations

Polyuria (up to 15 L/day) and polydipsia, decreased urine specific gravity. Nocturia disrupts sleep. Hypernatremia (>145 mEq/L) from water loss without sodium loss: thirst, dry mucous membranes, poor skin turgor, decreased saliva/sweat, disorientation, lethargy, seizures.

When it becomes life-threatening

If the hypothalamic thirst center is also damaged, water losses aren't compensated by increased intake — DI becomes life-threatening.

Diagnosis

Rule out diabetes mellitus/kidney disease (glucose, electrolytes, creatinine, BUN). Compare serum/urine osmolality + urine specific gravity. Dilute urine + water deficit + hypernatremia + low serum ADH = diagnostic of central DI.

Water deprivation test

Restrict water, monitor urine osmolality hourly; at plateau, give vasopressin. Central DI: urine concentrates and polyuria/polydipsia resolve. Nephrogenic DI: little/no response.

Treatment

DDAVP (synthetic ADH analog) daily replacement; free fluid access; home urine specific gravity testing can help patients self-adjust dosing.

Syndrome of Inappropriate ADH Secretion (SIADH)

Cause

Inappropriately excessive ADH — commonly ectopic production from tumors, especially primary lung malignancies. Nonmalignant lung disease (especially pulmonary TB) can also stimulate ADH. Drug-induced: carbamazepine, morphine, barbiturates, and others.

Must rule out first

Adrenal insufficiency and hypothyroidism can also increase ADH secretion and cause hyponatremia — both must be excluded before diagnosing SIADH.

Manifestations

Weakness, muscle cramps, nausea/vomiting, postural BP changes, poor skin turgor, fatigue, anorexia, lethargy. Severe: confusion, hemiparesis, seizures, coma.

Labs

Hyponatremia (<135 mEq/L); urine osmolality inappropriately HIGH (water reabsorbed); serum osmolality LOW (diluted); also low hematocrit and BUN from extracellular fluid dilution.

Treatment

Free water restriction — should produce a slow steady rise in serum sodium. Severe symptoms: IV saline + diuretic to promote free water loss. Correct hyponatremia slowly to avoid rapid shifts in brain cell volume. Persistent cases: lithium can block ADH's effects.
