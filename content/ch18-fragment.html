Key Terms & Definitions

Start here
CHD / CAD / ischemic heart disease
Overlapping terms for insufficient oxygenated blood delivery to the myocardium due to atherosclerotic coronary arteries.
Foam cells
Lipid-filled macrophages within the arterial wall — the hallmark cell of early atherosclerotic plaque.
Lipid core
The pool of accumulated lipid and cellular debris within an atherosclerotic plaque; large cores predispose to rupture.
MINOCA
Myocardial infarction with nonobstructive coronary artery disease — MI criteria met without significant (<50%) angiographic stenosis.
Stable angina
Predictable, exertion-triggered chest pain from fixed atherosclerotic stenosis; relieved by rest/nitroglycerin.
Vasospastic (Prinzmetal/variant) angina
Unpredictable angina at rest from coronary vasospasm, unrelated to exertion or oxygen demand.
Acute coronary syndrome (ACS)
Umbrella term for unstable angina and MI — abrupt onset from acute plaque disruption and thrombosis.
STEMI / NSTE-ACS
ST-elevation vs. non-ST-elevation presentations of ACS, distinguished by ECG and confirmed with cardiac biomarkers.
Type 1 MI (T1MI)
MI from atherosclerotic plaque rupture/thrombosis — the most common MI mechanism.
Type 2 MI (T2MI)
MI from myocardial oxygen supply-demand mismatch without plaque rupture (e.g., shock, severe hypotension).
Valve stenosis
Failure of a valve to open completely, creating a pressure gradient and extra pressure work for the heart.
Valve regurgitation
Failure of a valve to close completely, allowing backflow and creating extra volume work for the heart.
Myocarditis
Inflammatory disorder of heart muscle with necrosis/degeneration of cardiac cells, most often postviral.
Cardiomyopathy
Noninflammatory heart muscle disease — dilated, hypertrophic, or restrictive — genetic or acquired.
Cardiac tamponade
Life-threatening external compression of heart chambers by accumulated pericardial fluid, impairing filling.
Beck triad
Hypotension + distended neck veins + muffled heart sounds — the classic tamponade triad.
Pulsus paradoxus
An exaggerated (≥10 mmHg) fall in systolic BP during inspiration — a tamponade finding.
Foramen ovale
The fetal opening between the right and left atria, allowing right-to-left shunting to bypass the lungs.
Ductus arteriosus
The fetal channel connecting the pulmonary artery to the aorta, bypassing the collapsed fetal lungs.
CHD Overview & Epidemiology

Scale

CVD accounts for ~41.2% of all US deaths; ~41% of those are from CHD. >19.1 million people globally have CHD (an 8% rise since 2018), though age-adjusted death rate fell 14% over the same period — incidence and mortality are moving in opposite directions. Annual US economic cost of CVD/stroke: >$407 billion, projected to exceed $1 trillion by 2035.

Sex differences

Males and females are affected in roughly equal numbers overall, but females tend to be older at presentation — male sex is a risk factor for earlier onset (~10 years earlier on average).

CHD causes ~1 in 6 US deaths and includes angina pectoris and MI under the American Heart Association's statistical umbrella. Other CHD sequelae: dysrhythmias, sudden cardiac arrest, heart failure.

The lipoprotein transport system

LDL (high cholesterol) transports cholesterol TO peripheral tissues via LDL receptors — highest atherosclerosis risk. VLDL (high triglycerides) also raises risk. HDL transports cholesterol FROM peripheral tissues back to the liver, removing atheromatous plaque — protective. The liver normally clears ~75% of circulating LDL cholesterol via receptor-mediated endocytosis.

Familial hypercholesterolemia

The most common genetic hyperlipidemia — a defect in the LDL receptor on liver cells impairs cholesterol clearance. Even with normal lipid metabolism genetically, a high-fat diet alone can overwhelm the liver's LDL-clearing capacity.

Nearly 50% of MIs occur in patients WITHOUT hyperlipidemia — a critical reminder that lipid levels are one risk factor among many, not a gatekeeping diagnostic requirement.
Atherosclerosis Pathogenesis

Mind Map — From Endothelial Injury to Vulnerable Plaque

Why endothelial injury happens where it does

Chronic hemodynamic wall stress (explains plaque localization at arterial branch points and hypertension's role as a risk factor), cigarette smoke toxins, circulating inflammatory cytokines, and hyperlipidemia.

Stable vs. vulnerable plaque

Older plaques with substantial collagen/fibrin form a stabilizing cap. Vulnerable plaques have: (1) active inflammation, (2) large lipid core + thin cap, (3) endothelial erosion with platelet adherence, (4) fissured/ruptured cap, (5) severe stenosis → high shear stress.

The clinical trap: the MOST vulnerable plaques often don't significantly obstruct the lumen before they rupture — meaning ACS frequently strikes people whose disease was completely asymptomatic. Extent/severity of atherosclerotic lesions overall are NOT good predictors of ischemia severity.

Statin therapy — current standard

Statins are first-line; fibrates/niacin are no longer first-line. Dosing is based on cardiovascular risk category, NOT a lipid-level target — lipid lowering is only one of statins' benefit mechanisms. Major statin-benefit groups: known ASCVD (secondary prevention); LDL >190; diabetics age 40–75 with LDL 70–189; nondiabetics age 40–75 with LDL 70–189 and ≥7.5% estimated risk.

MINOCA

5–8% of ACS cases meet MI criteria without significant angiographic stenosis (<50%). Five subtypes: epicardial/microvascular spasm, spontaneous coronary dissection, coronary embolism, Takotsubo cardiomyopathy, myocarditis. Requires imaging (coronary imaging, echo, cardiac MRI) to sort out which.

Pathophysiology of Ischemia

Why the heart is uniquely vulnerable

Myocardial cells store minimal ATP and can't slow activity when supplies run low — they need continuous oxygen delivery for aerobic ATP synthesis, unlike tissues that can better tolerate transient hypoxia.

Two critical factors

(1) Rate of coronary perfusion, (2) myocardial workload (determined by HR, preload, afterload, contractility). Even very high oxygen consumption rarely causes ischemia unless perfusion is already impaired — demand alone isn't usually sufficient.

Five ways coronary perfusion is impaired

  • Large, stable atherosclerotic plaque
  • Acute platelet aggregation/thrombosis
  • Vasospasm
  • Failure of microcirculation autoregulation
  • Poor perfusion pressure

Collateral circulation

Because fibrous plaque progresses slowly over years, the heart can develop alternative blood-flow pathways — preserving flow despite near-total occlusion. This is why stable plaque may be silent until oxygen demand suddenly rises (exercise/stress) — the classic stable angina pattern.

Coronary blood flow occurs primarily during diastole and depends on aortic driving pressure — a fall in BP (shock, hemorrhage, anesthesia) can reduce coronary perfusion even with completely normal coronary arteries, producing a type 2 MI. The far more common mechanism, atherosclerotic plaque disruption, is a type 1 MI.
Angina Pectoris

Referred pain mechanism

Ischemic pain signals travel via C8 and T1–T4 dorsal root ganglia — the same pathways carrying sensory input from the jaw, neck, and arm, which is why cardiac pain is often perceived as originating from those areas instead of (or in addition to) the chest.

Presentation nuances

Described as burning, crushing, squeezing, choking ("an elephant sitting on my chest," or the Levene sign — a clenched fist over the chest). Often mistaken for indigestion or dental pain. Atypical presentations (back pain, fatigue, weakness without chest pain) occur, especially in certain populations.

Stable angina

Most common form. Stenotic vessels dilate poorly under increased demand → predictable, reproducible exertional/emotional-strain pain. Relieved by rest and nitroglycerin (coronary + peripheral vasodilation, ↓ preload, ↓ workload).

Vasospastic (Prinzmetal) angina

Unpredictable pain AT REST, unrelated to exertion/HR/oxygen demand. Mechanism: vascular smooth muscle hypercontractility (↑ intracellular Ca2+ via MLC-kinase/phosphatase dysregulation), abnormal vasospastic chemical secretion by local mast cells, reactive oxygen species. Responds well to calcium channel blockers. Biomarkers elevated: CRP, MCP-1, ICAM-1, IL-6 — a genuine chronic low-grade inflammatory component.

Acute Coronary Syndrome & MI

Unstable angina vs. MI — the key distinction

Unstable angina: occlusion is partial or clot dissolves before myocardial cell death. MI: occlusion is complete and persists long enough to cause irreversible necrosis. Historically differentiated by biomarkers (CK-MB, troponin I/T elevated = MI); now grouped together in ACS treatment protocols since acute management is similar.

Diagnostic pathway

ECG first: ST elevation → candidate for reperfusion/fibrinolytic therapy → STEMI. No ST elevation → check biomarkers → elevated = NSTE-ACS (formerly NSTEMI); normal = unstable angina. Patients with unstable angina/NSTE-ACS don't benefit from thrombolytics but may get PCI (catheter-based thrombus removal, plaque disruption, or stenting).

Clot formation cascade in ACS

Plaque rupture exposes thrombogenic collagen → platelets adhere → release chemicals attracting more platelets → platelet plug → coagulation cascade activates → platelet-fibrin clot (may occlude vessel or embolize downstream). Activated platelets also release vasoactive products (serotonin, thromboxane) that can cause additional coronary spasm.

MI epidemiology

>365,914 US deaths in 2017. Frequency rises with age. Females <45 have a 6-fold LOWER MI risk than same-age males — but risk approaches male levels after menopause and becomes essentially equal by age 80 (an estrogen-protection pattern, similar to what's seen with atherosclerosis generally).

Why aspirin works prophylactically: thrombus formation is now understood as central to coronary obstruction — long-term low-dose aspirin (antiplatelet) has been shown to reduce mortality from ischemic heart disease.
Stenosis vs. Regurgitation

Stenosis

Failure to open completely → pressure gradient across the valve → extra PRESSURE work for the affected chamber. Significant hemodynamic consequences begin around 50% orifice narrowing. Progresses slowly (years-decades), allowing time for compensatory hypertrophy.

Regurgitation

Failure to close completely → backflow → extra VOLUME work. Can develop suddenly (infection, papillary muscle rupture) — sudden regurgitation is poorly tolerated since little compensation is possible, unlike the slow-onset compensатed pattern typical of stenosis.

Mitral Valve Disease

Mitral stenosis — thresholds worth knowing

Normal orifice: 4–6 cm². Symptoms begin around 2 cm². Critical stenosis: 1 cm², with a pressure gradient ≥20 mmHg typically developing. Consequence chain: ↑ LA pressure → LA hypertrophy → ↑ pulmonary vascular pressure → chronic pulmonary hypertension → RV hypertrophy → right heart failure if uncorrected.

Mitral stenosis presentation

Exertional dyspnea (most common complaint), orthopnea, cough, paroxysmal nocturnal dyspnea, fatigue/weakness from reduced LV filling. Atrial fibrillation is common (excessive atrial volume) and predisposes to atrial clot → embolization → stroke. Low-pitched rumbling diastolic murmur at apex ± opening snap.

Mitral regurgitation mechanism

Backflow during systole → giant V waves on atrial pressure monitor. Severity depends partly on afterload (high afterload → more regurgitant flow). LV must pump extra volume to maintain effective forward stroke volume — both LA and LV dilate/hypertrophy to compensate.

Compensation window

Compensation is often maintained for MANY YEARS before symptoms appear — but severe, uncorrected regurgitation eventually leads to left-sided heart failure.

Myocarditis

Cause categories

Viral (most common in the Northern Hemisphere — adenovirus, coxsackievirus B, CMV, EBV, hepatitis C, HSV, HIV, influenza, SARS-CoV-2, and more), bacterial, cardiotoxins (anthracyclines, cocaine, heavy metals, Chagas disease), hypersensitivity reactions (drugs, insect bites), and systemic disorders (Kawasaki, sarcoidosis, Wegener granulomatosis).

Mechanism debate

Cardiac involvement typically appears days-weeks after a viral infection elsewhere. Direct viral cytotoxicity plays some role, but an immune response directed against the heart is thought to be the MORE important mechanism. Chagas disease (from Trypanosoma cruzi, endemic to Central/South America) is a notable nonviral cause.

Pathology & presentation

LV dysfunction or dilation of all 4 chambers; "flabby" myocardium with patchy/diffuse necrosis, WBC infiltrate; endocardial structures usually normal. Course ranges from asymptomatic to rapidly evolving heart failure. Common symptoms: fatigue, exertional dyspnea, dysrhythmia/palpitations.

Course & treatment

Many recover completely; others progress (sometimes years later) to dilated cardiomyopathy — myocarditis and DCM genuinely overlap and are hard to separate. Treatment: supportive heart failure therapy; immunosuppression considered for autoimmune/hypersensitivity-associated myocarditis.

Cardiomyopathy

Mind Map — Three Morphologic Types

Primary vs. secondary classification

Primary: unknown cause. Secondary: known cause (ischemic, valvular, hypertensive, inflammatory, metabolic, muscular dystrophy-related, toxic, peripartum, etc.) — classification continues evolving as genetic understanding (the "phenome-genome" system) advances.

Dilated cardiomyopathy (DCM)

Causes: alcohol toxicity (+ thiamine deficiency), genetic (20–35% have an affected first-degree relative; sarcomere/cytoskeleton protein defects), pregnancy (peripartum CM — high lymphocytic activation, recurrence risk in future pregnancies), postviral myocarditis. Clinical picture: slowly progressive biventricular failure with low EF.

Hypertrophic cardiomyopathy (HCM)

Autosomal dominant; sarcomere protein mutations (myosin heavy chain, myosin-binding protein C, tropomyosin, troponin T) account for 70–80% of cases. ~25% have septal-predominant hypertrophy causing dynamic aortic outflow obstruction. Strenuous activity can precipitate outflow obstruction → negligible SV → sudden death — a genuine risk in young athletes.

HCM treatment logic

Drugs that ↑ contractility or HR are AVOIDED (worsen diastolic filling/outflow obstruction) — opposite of typical heart failure management. β-blockers and calcium channel blockers dampen hypercontractility instead. Myectomy surgery is now rarely needed given effective drug therapy.

Restrictive cardiomyopathy (RCM): the rarest form — stiff, fibrotic ventricle with impaired diastolic filling but preserved systolic function; idiopathic or associated with infiltrative disease (amyloidosis, endomyocardial disease).
Pericardial Effusion & Cardiac Tamponade

Normal vs. pathologic volume

Normal pericardial fluid: 30–50 mL, thin and clear. Pathologic accumulation: up to 500 mL. Four effusion types: serous (transudate — HF, hypoproteinemia), serosanguineous (trauma, surgery, CPR), blood/hemopericardium (penetrating trauma), purulent (infected, WBC-laden).

Cardiac tamponade — the danger

External compression of heart chambers impairs filling. Occurs when accumulation is large OR sudden (a slow accumulation can stretch the pericardium and delay tamponade even at larger volumes).

Beck triad: hypotension + distended neck veins + muffled heart sounds. Pulsus paradoxus: systolic BP falls ≥10 mmHg with inspiration, from exaggerated respiratory effects on impaired cardiac filling. Additional findings: rising intrachamber filling pressures, dull chest pain, diminished ECG amplitude, compressed cardiac silhouette on x-ray. Treatment: pericardiocentesis — untreated tamponade progresses to cardiovascular collapse and death.
Asymptomatic effusions are sometimes found incidentally on chest x-ray and may simply be monitored/aspirated as needed — not every effusion requires emergency intervention; the key variable is rate of accumulation and resulting hemodynamic compromise.
Acute & Chronic Pericarditis

Acute pericarditis

Most cases uncertain etiology, presumed viral. Typically self-resolves within 2 weeks; NSAIDs + colchicine are often the only therapy needed. Presents with fever, leukocytosis, malaise, tachycardia, and characteristic chest pain (may radiate to back, mimic angina, involve esophageal discomfort/dysphagia). Pericardial friction rub — transient, squeaky/scratchy sound. ECG: diffuse ST elevation from epicardial injury.

Post-MI pericarditis

Has become less common with early reperfusion therapy. Early form occurs days after MI (epicardial-surface infarction extending inflammation to adjacent pericardium — severity tracks with infarct size). Delayed form = Dressler syndrome.

Chronic pericarditis — two forms

Adhesive mediastinopericarditis: from suppurative/caseous pericarditis, prior cardiac surgery, or chest irradiation — pericardial sac destroyed, heart adheres to surrounding mediastinal structures, ↑ workload from opposed contraction. Constrictive pericarditis: often post-tuberculous (or idiopathic) — dense, nonelastic, fibrous, scarred sac encases the heart like a stiff cage, impairing diastolic filling.

Constrictive pericarditis treatment

Symptoms (exercise intolerance, weakness, fatigue, venous congestion) often advanced by diagnosis since the process is slow. Treatment: pericardiectomy (surgical removal) + inotropic support to improve contractility.

Embryologic Development

Four structures behind most congenital defects

(1) Atrial septum development, (2) ventricular septum development, (3) division of the truncus arteriosus into pulmonary/aortic arteries, (4) valve development. Nearly all common congenital heart defects trace back to abnormalities in one of these four processes.

The primitive heart tube

Three layers: inner endothelial layer (becomes chamber/valve lining), outer myoepicardial mantle (becomes epicardium/muscle), and cardiac jelly between them (precursor to endocardial cushion tissue — critical for septa formation). Begins beating by day 23; folds into an asymmetric loop forming the primitive atrium, future LV, and future RV/truncus arteriosus.

Atrial septation

Septum primum forms passively from an indentation caused by the overlying truncus arteriosus. Superior/inferior endocardial cushions grow toward each other but don't fuse — leaving the ostium secundum (later called the foramen ovale), which stays open throughout fetal life.

Ventricular septation

Lower septum forms by circular growth/fusion of muscular ventricular walls, then proliferates upward toward the atria; inferior endocardial cushion tissue grows downward to meet it. Truncus arteriosus divides into pulmonary/aortic channels via fusion of endocardial cushion mounds, which also form the semilunar valves.

The two fetal bypass structures — know both mechanisms: the foramen ovale lets blood flow right-to-left between atria (bypassing the RV) because LA pressure is low and high pulmonary resistance keeps RV pressure high, impeding RV filling. The ductus arteriosus connects the pulmonary artery to the aorta, letting blood flow pulmonary-artery-to-aorta because of high resistance in the collapsed fetal lungs. Both close after birth once the lungs inflate (↓ right-side resistance) and umbilical cord clamping raises systemic vascular resistance — reversing the pressure gradients that kept them open.
