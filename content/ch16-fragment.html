Key Terms & Definitions

Start here
Cardiac output (CO)
Stroke volume × heart rate; the volume of blood the heart ejects per minute.
Stroke volume (SV)
The volume of blood ejected per heartbeat; determined by preload and myocardial contractility.
Preload
The end-diastolic volume — how much blood fills the heart before it contracts, determined by venous return.
Systemic vascular resistance (SVR)
The resistance to blood ejection into the arterial circulation, synonymous with cardiac afterload; determined mainly by arteriolar radius.
Systolic blood pressure
The peak arterial pressure during ventricular contraction; primarily determined by stroke volume.
Diastolic blood pressure
The minimum arterial pressure during ventricular relaxation; primarily determined by SVR.
Pulse pressure
Systolic pressure minus diastolic pressure.
Mean arterial pressure (MAP)
The time-weighted average pressure across the cardiac cycle — not a simple average, since more time is spent in diastole.
Baroreceptors
Pressure-sensitive receptors in the carotid sinuses and aortic arch that provide rapid, moment-to-moment blood pressure regulation.
Vasomotor center
The medullary brainstem region that integrates baroreceptor/chemoreceptor input and directs SNS/PSNS output to control blood pressure.
Renin
An enzyme released by juxtaglomerular cells in response to decreased renal arterial pressure; cleaves angiotensinogen to angiotensin I.
Angiotensin-converting enzyme (ACE)
Produced by vascular endothelium (especially in the lungs); converts angiotensin I to angiotensin II.
Angiotensin II
A potent arterial vasoconstrictor that also stimulates aldosterone release — the central effector of the RAAS.
Aldosterone
An adrenal cortex hormone that increases renal sodium (and secondarily water) reabsorption, raising blood volume and pressure.
Atrial natriuretic peptide (ANP)
Released by stretched atrial muscle fibers; promotes sodium/water excretion, lowering blood volume and pressure — opposes the RAAS.
Endothelin-1 (ET-1)
A renal medullary peptide involved in renal perfusion, electrolyte movement, and renin release; levels are abnormal in hypertension.
Primary (essential) hypertension
Hypertension with no clearly identifiable single cause — 90–95% of hypertension cases.
Secondary hypertension
Hypertension with an identifiable underlying cause.
Isolated systolic hypertension (ISH)
Systolic ≥140 mmHg with diastolic <90 mmHg — the dominant hypertension subtype after age 55.
Isolated diastolic hypertension (IDH)
Diastolic ≥90 mmHg with systolic <140 mmHg — more common in younger adults.
White coat effect
Situational blood pressure elevation specifically in clinical settings, not reflective of the patient's usual pressure.
Orthostatic hypotension (OH)
A drop in systolic BP ≥20 mmHg or diastolic ≥10 mmHg (or excessive HR rise) within 3 minutes of standing.
Vasovagal response
A paradoxical ↑ parasympathetic / ↓ sympathetic response causing bradycardia and vasodilation instead of the expected compensatory response.
Microalbuminuria
Small amounts of protein in urine — an early indicator of hypertensive glomerular/renal injury.
Determinants of Blood Pressure

Mind Map — BP = CO × SVR, Broken Down

Systolic pressure ← stroke volume

An increase or decrease in SV produces a corresponding change in systolic pressure — SV is the primary determinant of the systolic number.

Diastolic pressure ← SVR

SVR is the major determinant of diastolic pressure — a change in arterial resistance directly changes the diastolic number.

Poiseuille's law applied to SVR

Resistance depends on tube radius, tube length, and fluid viscosity. Since blood viscosity and total arterial length are relatively constant, arteriolar radius becomes the major determinant of SVR in practice.

Systolic vs. diastolic as blood moves distally

As blood moves from large vessels (aorta) into smaller vessels, systolic pressure increases while diastolic pressure decreases — an important basic concept underlying many blood pressure disorders and treatments.

Average adult values: peak (systolic) pressure ~110 mmHg, minimum (diastolic) pressure ~70 mmHg — giving a pulse pressure of ~40 mmHg in a "textbook normal" adult.
Measuring Blood Pressure

MAP — not a simple average

Because more time is spent in diastole than systole, MAP is weighted toward the diastolic value, not a straight arithmetic mean of systolic and diastolic. Clinically used for cardiovascular assessment and titrating IV vasoactive drugs.

Direct vs. indirect measurement

Direct: intraarterial catheter (usually radial artery) + transducer — most accurate, but used only in controlled settings (OR, ICU). Indirect: brachial artery via sphygmomanometer/auscultation or automated oscillometry — the standard, preferred method since references are based on it and it's least stressful to patients.

Which arm to use

No consistent pattern of right-vs-left arm differences in healthy people — best practice is to check both arms initially and record the higher reading.

When auscultation fails

In shock states, systolic pressure alone may be obtained by palpation or Doppler ultrasound amplification when sounds can't be auscultated.

Sources of Measurement Error

Cuff and position errors (know the directions)

Cuff too small → falsely elevated. Cuff too large → falsely decreased. Arm above heart → falsely decreased. Arm below heart → falsely elevated. Arm unsupported → falsely elevated. Supine position → ~5 mmHg lower diastolic. Seated without back support → ~6 mmHg higher diastolic. Crossed legs → systolic ↑ 2–8 mmHg.

Technique & behavioral factors

Excessive stethoscope pressure → falsely low diastolic. Less than 1 minute between repeat measurements → falsely elevated. Deflation faster than 3 mmHg/sec → falsely low systolic, falsely high diastolic. Exercise, eating, smoking, or caffeine within 30 minutes → falsely elevated. Talking during measurement → falsely elevated.

The "white coat effect" — first described by Scipione Riva-Rocci in 1897 — refers to situational BP elevation specifically in clinical settings. Pickering and colleagues found this accounts for the entire elevated reading in approximately 15–20% of stage 1 hypertension patients — meaning a meaningful fraction of "hypertensive" diagnoses based on a single office visit may be reflecting anxiety, not true sustained hypertension. Most common in older adults but can occur at any age.
Normal reference values assume the patient is seated, back supported, arm at heart level — deviating from this standardized position is itself a source of error, not just equipment issues.
Short-Term Regulation

Speed & purpose

Accommodates position changes, emotional changes, and physiologic changes (fever, volume depletion) within seconds — BP can double within 5–10 seconds. Mediated by the sympathetic nervous system (SNS), directed by the medullary vasomotor center.

Receptor-level mechanism

SNS release of epinephrine/norepinephrine → binds α1 receptors on arterial smooth muscle → vasoconstriction → ↑ SVR. Binds β1 receptors in the heart → ↑ SA node firing rate → ↑ HR. PSNS has almost no effect on most systemic vessels (exception: facial venodilation = blushing) but slows HR at rest. Central α2 receptors provide negative feedback, dampening further catecholamine release.

Mind Map — The Baroreceptor Reflex

Anatomy & signal pathway

Baroreceptors concentrated in carotid sinuses and the aortic arch. Aortic signals travel via CN X (vagus); carotid signals via CN IX (glossopharyngeal) — both terminate in the medullary vasomotor center.

Limits and quirks worth knowing

The reflex reduces minute-to-minute BP fluctuation by ~33% in animal studies. Responsiveness declines with age (arterial stiffening, hypertension, diabetes). Baroreceptors reset to a new (elevated) baseline within 1–2 days of chronic BP elevation — meaning they stop effectively opposing chronic hypertension, which is why they're thought to contribute to long-term BP dysregulation via renal SNS stimulation.

Chemoreceptors in the carotid/aortic bodies respond to hypoxia (↑H+, ↑CO2) and stimulate the vasomotor center — but this mechanism only kicks in significantly once systolic pressure drops below ~80 mmHg, acting as a backup floor rather than routine regulation.
Long-Term Regulation — The RAAS

The core long-term principle

Long-term BP regulation is fundamentally about the balance between water/sodium intake and renal excretion. ↑ ECF volume (intake > excretion) → ↑ CO, and combined with volume-induced ↑ SVR (local autoregulation constricting arterioles to prevent high-flow damage) → ↑ arterial pressure.

Why sodium matters more than water alone

Sodium isn't eliminated as rapidly as water, so elevated sodium intake more reliably raises BP. Excess sodium raises ECF osmolality → activates thirst → also triggers hypothalamic/posterior pituitary ADH release → ADH binds collecting duct receptors → enhanced water reabsorption to dilute the excess sodium back down — but this reabsorbed water adds to blood volume.

Mind Map — The RAAS Cascade

Timing details worth knowing

Renin keeps producing angiotensin I for ~30–60 minutes until it's cleared. Angiotensin II is inactivated within minutes by angiotensinases but is continuously regenerated. Full vasoconstrictive effect takes ~20 minutes to peak but can raise pressure to 50% of normal after severe hemorrhage.

The dual mechanism of angiotensin II

(1) Direct arterial vasoconstriction → ↑ SVR immediately. (2) Stimulates aldosterone → sodium/water retention → ↑ venous return → ↑ myocardial stretch → ↑ contractility and SV (a slower, volume-based effect). Both pathways converge on raising BP, just on different timescales.

Local (intrarenal) RAAS effects, distinct from the systemic cascade: renin receptors within the kidney (mesangium, below renal artery endothelium) locally enhance angiotensinogen → angiotensin I conversion — angiotensinogen and ACE are BOTH found directly in tubular/mesangial cells and epithelium. This means the RAAS isn't purely a systemic, circulating hormone system — it has a genuinely local, intrarenal arm too.
Other Long-Term Factors & Circadian Rhythm

SNS effects on the kidney specifically

SNS neurotransmitters bind renal β1 receptors → ↑ renin release. Also: renal arteriolar constriction → ↓ GFR, and ↑ tubular sodium/water reabsorption (via ↑ angiotensin II/aldosterone) — this is the mechanism behind BP elevation during severe prolonged stress.

Atrial natriuretic peptide (ANP) — the RAAS's counterbalance

Released by stretched atrial muscle fibers when heart volume rises. ↑ GFR + ↓ sodium reabsorption → natriuresis/diuresis → ↓ circulating volume → ↓ BP. A direct physiologic opponent to the RAAS.

Endothelin-1 (ET-1)

Produced in the renal medulla; binds renal receptors triggering an autocrine vasodilatory response affecting renal perfusion, electrolyte movement, and renin release. Levels are abnormal in hypertension — a genuinely essential (if less famous) participant in BP control.

miRNA and endothelial injury (a newer research angle)

Shear stress on endothelial cells → microdehiscence (junction separation) → ↑ permeability to lipoproteins + ↓ vascular smooth muscle compliance. Some miRNAs promote vessel-wall inflammation, ↑ VCAM-1, ↓ ACE expression, and impair nitric oxide — an evolving mechanistic layer beneath the classical hormonal story.

Circadian rhythm: governed by the suprachiasmatic nuclei (the body's internal clock). BP rises before waking (morning surge), peaks mid-morning, then declines to its lowest point at night (nocturnal dip). Circadian BP elevations are linked to diabetic nephropathy, sudden cardiac death, MI, and stroke — timing of cardiovascular events isn't random.
Hypertension — Epidemiology & Classification

Scale

~48.1% (119.9 million) of US adults have hypertension. Highest prevalence: non-Hispanic Black adults (57.5% males, 58.4% females) — notably higher than White (48.9%/42.6%) or Hispanic American (50.3%/35.3%) adults. Globally: >40% of adults ≥25, >1.28 billion people; 7.8 million deaths/year worldwide.

The awareness/control gap

~46% of adults with hypertension are UNAWARE they have it; only ~21% have it controlled — this gap, not just prevalence, is a major part of why hypertension is called the "silent killer."

JNC 8 treatment goals: age <60 (all health states) and age ≥60 with CKD/diabetes → <140/90. Age ≥60 WITHOUT CKD/diabetes → more lenient goal of <150/90. Treatment targets are age- and comorbidity-adjusted, not a single universal number.
Primary Hypertension & Subtypes

Primary vs. secondary

Primary (essential): no single identifiable cause — idiopathic, 90–95% of cases. Secondary: identifiable underlying cause. In children specifically, most preadolescent hypertension is SECONDARY, but by adolescence 85–95% is primary — the age-related shift is a useful clinical pattern.

Pediatric prevalence

~3.5% of children/adolescents have hypertension; primary hypertension is rare before age 10 and linked to family history, obesity, and lifestyle when it occurs.

This isn't just trivia — it explains why clinical focus shifts from diastolic to systolic control as patients age, and why ISH becomes the dominant, most consequential subtype in older adults.
In adolescents/young adults, ISH is attributed to increased arterial elasticity during rapid growth (↑ brachial systolic pressure with unchanged aortic pressure) or increased SV — a different mechanism than the arterial stiffening that drives ISH in older adults.
Risk Factors

Nonmodifiable

Increasing age, family history, ethnicity, metabolic syndrome (borderline — has both genetic and lifestyle roots).

Modifiable

Obesity/sedentary lifestyle, dietary factors (↑ fat, ↑ sodium, ↓ potassium, ↓ calcium), tobacco use, elevated glucose/cholesterol/triglycerides, ↓ HDL/↑ LDL.

Obesity's outsized role

Accounts for 65–75% of overall hypertension risk. Metabolic syndrome prevalence rises steeply with weight: ~7% in at-risk-for-overweight adolescents, 29% in overweight adolescents, 50% in severely obese adolescents.

Ethnicity

African American adults have the highest hypertension risk — mechanism unclear, but increased salt sensitivity is the leading hypothesis. Genetics likely affects renin levels, salt sensitivity, angiotensin II responsiveness, and endothelin/nitric oxide activity, but no single gene explains a meaningful share of population-level hypertension.

Early-life/intrauterine risk factors — an easily overlooked category: maternal smoking, pregnancy-induced hypertension, poor maternal diet (including inadequate calcium), low birth weight followed by rapid catch-up growth, and lower maternal socioeconomic status all predict later hypertension. Breastfeeding is protective. This reflects a genuinely developmental-origins model of hypertension risk, not just adult lifestyle factors.
End-Organ Damage

Why it's called the "silent killer"

Early hypertension causes no overt symptoms — substantial end-organ damage can occur before diagnosis. Risk of cardiovascular disease DOUBLES with each 20/10 mmHg incremental BP increase.

Cardiac pathway

↑ SVR → ↑ afterload → ↑ myocardial O2 demand/workload → compensatory left ventricular hypertrophy (seen even in children/adolescents with hypertension). Concurrent CAD limits O2 supply — the combination of ↑ demand + ↓ supply predisposes to ischemia (angina, MI), and both LVH and MI raise heart failure risk.

Vascular/atherosclerotic pathway

↑ Wall tension → ↑ collagen accumulation + elastin fragmentation/breakage. Chronic low-grade arterial inflammation + dyslipidemia together accelerate atherosclerotic plaque formation. Atherosclerosis has been documented even in adolescents and young children with hypertension.

Renal pathway

Chronic hypertension eventually overwhelms the kidney's normal autoregulation of glomerular blood flow → glomerular damage → large molecules leak into urine. Microalbuminuria (proteinuria) is the earliest detectable sign of hypertensive renal injury — and it's typically asymptomatic at that stage, making it a critical early-detection target.

For patients over 50, systolic hypertension poses a far greater cardiovascular risk than diastolic elevation — reinforcing the age-related shift in which number matters most clinically (see the Primary Hypertension section above).
Orthostatic Hypotension

Diagnostic criteria

↓ Systolic BP ≥20 mmHg OR ↓ diastolic ≥10 mmHg within 3 minutes of standing — OR an excessive HR rise of 20–30 bpm. In the elderly, the drop can be delayed up to 10+ minutes, so a single quick check may miss it.

Normal mechanism being defeated

Standing pools 500–1000 mL of blood in the lower-extremity venous system → baroreceptors sense the drop → SNS activation → ↑ HR + ↑ arterial tone restores upper-body (especially cerebral) perfusion. OH = this compensatory response is too slow or inadequate.

OH is not just a fall risk — it's a systemic warning sign: associated with cardiovascular disease and predictive of stroke, cognitive impairment, and death, not merely dizziness/syncope. Reported in 6–30% of healthy elderly with otherwise normal blood pressure.

Causes — nervous system/mechanical

Damage to the vasomotor center or CNS/peripheral nervous system, diabetic nerve damage, multiple sclerosis (autoimmune), spinal cord injury, altered baroreceptor sensitivity, arterial stiffness, prolonged bed rest, normal aging. Parkinson disease: 37–58% prevalence of OH.

Causes — volume/drug/reflex

Volume depletion (hemorrhage, burns, severe diarrhea) — compensatory mechanisms fail after ~30% volume loss. Drugs: antihypertensives, tricyclic antidepressants, pain medications. Alcohol and heat exposure (vasodilation). Cardiac dysrhythmias impairing CO.

The vasovagal response — a genuine physiologic paradox: instead of the expected ↑ SNS/↓ PSNS compensatory response, some triggers (stress, pain, coughing, ↑ intraabdominal/intrathoracic pressure) cause the OPPOSITE: ↑ parasympathetic + ↓ sympathetic activity → bradycardia + vasodilation → worsened hypotension. This is a fundamentally different mechanism from simple "compensation failure," and worth keeping distinct from ordinary OH causes.

Nonpharmacologic management

Slow position changes; avoid hot baths/saunas (vasodilation); avoid large/carbohydrate-heavy meals (postprandial splanchnic pooling); at symptom onset — squat, bend forward, or cross legs while tightening leg/buttock muscles; compression stockings, abdominal binders, elevated head of bed; liberal salt/fluid intake unless contraindicated.

Workup

Careful medication history review, thorough history/physical, chemistry panel, CBC, 12-lead ECG. Assistive devices (cane with fold-out seat, walker with seat) can help reduce elderly fall risk specifically from OH episodes.
