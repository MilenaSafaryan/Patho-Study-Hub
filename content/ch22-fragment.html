Key Terms & Definitions

Start here
Asthma
Reversible airway obstruction + airway inflammation + increased airway reactivity to stimuli.
Extrinsic (allergic) asthma
IgE-mediated asthma, often with atopy/family history; ~1/3 to 1/2 of cases.
Intrinsic (nonallergic) asthma
Asthma with no allergy history, adult-onset, less favorable prognosis, poor response to immunotherapy.
Status asthmaticus
Severe asthma unresponsive to typical inhaled bronchodilators — life-threatening.
Charcot-Leyden crystals
Crystallized eosinophil membrane enzymes found in asthmatic sputum.
Curschmann spirals
Mucous casts of bronchioles found in asthmatic sputum.
Peak expiratory flow (PEF)
The maximal expired airflow during forced vital capacity — the best indicator of airflow reduction in asthma.
Chronic bronchitis (Type B COPD)
Productive cough >3 months/year for ≥2 consecutive years, other causes excluded.
Reid index
(b−c)/(a−d) — the ratio measuring bronchial mucous gland thickness; normal <0.4, severe bronchitis ~0.7.
Blue bloater
Classic chronic bronchitis presentation — cyanosis + edema from right-sided heart failure.
Emphysema (Type A COPD)
Destructive alveolar wall changes + abnormal enlargement of distal air sacs.
α1-Antitrypsin
A protective enzyme inhibiting proteolytic alveolar breakdown; deficiency causes early-onset emphysema.
Radial traction
The normal outward pull of surrounding alveolar tissue that holds small airways open; lost in emphysema.
Pink puffer
Classic emphysema presentation — near-normal oxygenation maintained via sustained high respiratory effort.
Bronchiectasis
Permanent dilation of bronchi from recurrent infection/inflammation, obstructive and suppurative.
Digital clubbing
Fibrous tissue hyperplasia at the nail bed from prolonged hypoxemia; severity parallels disease severity.
Bronchiolitis
Widespread bronchiolar inflammation, most often from RSV, primarily affecting infants/young children.
Cystic fibrosis
Autosomal recessive exocrine gland disorder causing thick, obstructive mucus — the most common genetic lung disease in the US.

Mind Map — Where Along the Airway Obstruction Happens

These categories are mostly a convenience — most real respiratory diseases involve multiple mechanisms at once. The major obstructive airway diseases (asthma, bronchitis, emphysema) rarely present in "pure" isolated form; most COPD patients have a mix of chronic bronchitis and emphysema simultaneously.
Asthma — Etiology & Triggers

Scale

~262 million cases globally in 2019, projected +100 million by 2025. Most common chronic childhood disease; disproportionately high in non-Hispanic Black individuals, household poverty, and premature/low-birth-weight children. US cost: $82 billion (2013).

Five phenotypes

Early-onset, late-onset eosinophilic, exercise-induced, obesity-related, neutrophilic — clinical features are broadly similar across all types despite different underlying triggers.

Extrinsic (allergic) vs. intrinsic (nonallergic)

Extrinsic: IgE-mediated, ~1/3–1/2 of cases, linked to atopy/hay fever/eczema/family history/positive skin tests; responds to immunotherapy/environmental control. Intrinsic: no allergy history, adult-onset, respiratory infections/psychological factors implicated, LESS favorable prognosis, variable/poor response to therapy, immunotherapy NOT helpful.

Exercise-induced asthma

Bronchospasm within 3 minutes after exercise ends, resolves in ~60 min. Mechanism: heat/water loss + increased mucosal osmolarity → mediator release from basophils/mast cells. Running/jogging/tennis are common triggers; bicycling/swimming much less so.

Occupational asthma

Worsens progressively with repeated exposure; symptoms clear on weekends/vacation, recur on return to work — this pattern alone often establishes the diagnosis. Many occupational allergens (formaldehyde, isocyanates, metals) do NOT provoke skin test reactions, and hyposensitization is usually ineffective.

Aspirin-induced asthma

Occurs in adults with nasal polyps/sinusitis/asthma. NOT immunologically mediated (skin testing not useful) — aspirin/NSAIDs block arachidonic acid→prostaglandin conversion, shunting it toward the leukotriene pathway instead, and leukotrienes are powerful bronchoconstrictors.

Other triggers: food additives (tartrazine/Yellow No. 5, MSG, sodium/potassium metabisulfite), hops in beer (non-IgE), and GERD (presumed aspiration of refluxed acid → bronchoconstriction).
Asthma — Pathogenesis

Mind Map — The Allergic (IgE) Asthma Cascade

The immune signaling detail

DCs + epithelial cytokines TSLP, IL-25, IL-33 stimulate innate lymphoid cells (ILC2s) — these plus TH2 cells secrete IL-4, IL-5, IL-9, IL-13, which stimulate eosinophils, mucus production, epithelial activation, mast cell proliferation. IL-4/IL-13 specifically drive TH2/ILC2/eosinophil migration AND IgE class-switching in B cells.

Two-phase mediator response

Immediate: histamine, tryptase, leukotrienes, prostaglandins, bradykinins, eosinophil chemotactic factor, serotonin → smooth muscle contraction, mucus, ↑ vascular permeability. Secondary response 6-12 hours later (thought driven by neutrophil chemotactic factor) — more REFRACTORY to treatment than the initial reaction.

Airway remodeling — the long-term structural change: basement membrane thickens from 7 μm (healthy) to 17.5 μm (asthmatic) over time (classic Hogg 1982 finding). This pathologic remodeling, plus declining pulmonary function over time, can progress to COPD if uncontrolled. Acetylcholine (via muscarinic receptors) drives ongoing mucus secretion/bronchoconstriction/inflammation, while reduced secretion of the bronchodilator neuropeptide VIP removes a normal counterbalance.
Genetics: locus 17q21 (childhood asthma), ST2/IL-1RL1 polymorphisms (exacerbation risk despite inhaled corticosteroids), PKN2/PTK2/ALPP (development risk), ADAM33 (strongly linked to bronchial hyperresponsiveness).
Asthma — Clinical Manifestations & Diagnosis

Wheeze mechanics

Vibration in narrowed airways, like a wind-instrument reed. Because airways naturally widen on inspiration, INSPIRATORY wheeze reflects greater constriction than expiratory wheeze. Location of loudest wheeze indicates obstruction site (loudest at mouth = large airway obstruction).

The intensity trap

Wheeze INTENSITY is NOT a reliable severity indicator — when obstruction is most severe, the patient may not move enough air to generate any wheeze at all ("silent chest" = ominous, not reassuring). PEF is the best objective indicator of airflow reduction; PEF <50-60 L/min = severe exacerbation.

Severity progression

Mild: monophonic expiratory wheeze, tachycardia, tachypnea (tachycardia is an EARLY distress sign). Severe: accessory muscle use, intercostal retractions, distant breath sounds, orthopnea, agitation, cyanosis, confusion. Status asthmaticus: severe asthma failing to respond to typical inhaled bronchodilators — life-threatening emergency.

Diagnostic findings

Sputum: Charcot-Leyden crystals, eosinophils, Curschmann spirals. FEV1/FVC <75% indicates airflow obstruction. Radiograph: normal or hyperinflation with flattened diaphragm in progressive disease.

Acute Bronchitis

Etiology

~80% of 12 million annual US cases are viral (influenza A/B, parainfluenza, RSV, coronavirus, rhinovirus, coxsackievirus, adenovirus). Nonviral: S. pneumoniae, H. influenzae, mycoplasma, Moraxella, Chlamydia pneumoniae. Highest incidence: smokers, young children, elderly; winter peak.

Distinguishing from bronchiolitis and pneumonia

Acute bronchitis affects trachea/bronchi (larger airways) vs. bronchiolitis affecting small bronchioles. Chest x-ray: normal in bronchitis, pulmonary infiltrates in pneumonia — the key imaging distinguisher.

Diagnosis hinges on recent-onset cough as the hallmark — neither purulent sputum NOR elevated WBC reliably indicates bacterial cause. Treatment: mostly supportive (viral cases don't respond to antibiotics); antitussives, β2-agonists selectively, codeine for sleep-disrupting cough, increased fluids, avoid smoke, humidifier.
Chronic Bronchitis (Type B COPD)

Definition & etiology

90% caused by cigarette smoking. Diagnosed SYMPTOMATICALLY: productive cough >3 months/year for ≥2 consecutive years, other causes excluded. 3:1 ratio of chronic bronchitis to emphysema cases nationally.

Reid index

(b−c)/(a−d) — measures mucous gland layer thickness in the bronchial wall. Normal <0.4; severe bronchitis ~0.7. Inflammation is predominantly NEUTROPHIL-driven (↑ IL-8 recruits neutrophils; CD8 T-lymphocytes also elevated; 30-fold eosinophil increase during acute exacerbations).

Mind Map — Chronic Bronchitis to Cor Pulmonale

The "blue bloater" mechanism

V/Q mismatch from narrowed airways/mucus plugging → hypoxemia + hypercarbia → compensatory pulmonary vasoconstriction → pulmonary hypertension → RV dilation (cor pulmonale) → right-sided heart failure (JVD, hepatomegaly, dependent edema). Bronchial wall destruction can also progress to bronchiectasis.

Clinical picture

Typical patient: overweight, 30s-40s+, 1:2 male:female ratio, exertional SOB, excess sputum (worst mornings), history of smoking, chills/malaise/muscle aches/fatigue/insomnia/loss of libido. End-stage: distended neck veins, RV heave/gallop, peripheral edema; cyanosis is a LATE sign.

Diagnosis: normal TLC, ↑ RV, ↓ FEV1, ↑ PaCO2/↓ PaO2 (often <65 mmHg) — abnormal ABGs appear EARLY. Secondary polycythemia from chronic hypoxemia is common. Bacterial colonization with H. influenzae/S. pneumoniae is typical.

Emphysema (Type A COPD)

Scale & etiology

15 million Americans have COPD (~10M chronic bronchitis, ~5M emphysema); >120,000 deaths/year. Causes: smoking (>70 pack-years highly predictive), air pollution, occupational exposure (welding, mining, asbestos), α1-antitrypsin deficiency (~1% of cases). Normal aging from ~age 30 mimics emphysema changes at a slow rate (4% gas-exchange surface loss per decade) — emphysema is an accelerated version of a universal process.

α1-antitrypsin deficiency

Consider when emphysema develops before age 50 in a smoker. Hereditary; low serum levels (25-50 mg/dL). α1-antitrypsin normally inhibits neutrophil-derived elastase from breaking down alveolar tissue — deficiency removes this protection.

Mind Map — Smoking's Two-Pronged Attack

The mechanical cascade after wall loss

↓ Capillary bed (impairs gas exchange) + loss of elastic tissue → ↓ radial traction (the outward pull that normally holds small bronchioles open) → airway collapse + ↑ resistance → air trapping in distal alveoli → distended air sacs → further collapsing pressure on proximal bronchi → progressive obstruction → bullae formation (large thin-walled cysts, further robbing gas transport).

Three classifications

Centriacinar (centrilobular): linked to smoking + chronic bronchitis, destroys respiratory bronchioles. Panacinar (panlobular): destroys alveoli — the classic α1-antitrypsin deficiency pattern. Paraseptal: affects peripheral lobules.

The "pink puffer" mechanism

Sustained high respiratory effort + relatively preserved V/Q matching → near-normal arterial O2 until LATE in disease — unlike blue bloaters, who desaturate earlier. Patients become thin from ↑ caloric expenditure (work of breathing) + ↓ intake capacity. Bullae formation creates pneumothorax risk (sudden chest pain + dyspnea on the affected side).

Diagnostic findings

↑ FRC, ↑ RV, ↑ TLC, ↓ FEV1, ↓ FVC, FEV1/FVC below normal limit. X-ray: hyperinflation, flattened diaphragm, blebs/bullae, narrow mediastinum, small "vertical" heart. ECG: normal or tall P waves; sinus tachycardia often the first hypoxemia sign; supraventricular dysrhythmias possible. PaO2 mildly ↓ (60–80 mmHg); PaCO2 low/normal until late stages.

Bronchiectasis

Etiology & classification

Acquired or congenital; obstructive AND suppurative. 50% of cases associated with cystic fibrosis; also linked to COPD, asthma, immunodeficiency syndromes. Shapes: saccular (cavity-like), cylindrical (uniform widening), fusiform (combination) — little clinical difference between the three.

Pathogenesis

Recurrent infection/inflammation → destruction of central bronchial walls + obliteration of peripheral bronchi/bronchioles. Pseudomonas aeruginosa is the most common causative organism (~33% of cultures) and is linked to higher mortality when chronic. Ciliated epithelium → squamous transformation → impaired mucociliary clearance → obstruction + recurrent infection cycle.

Classic presentation

Chronic productive cough with copious purulent, foul-smelling sputum that separates into THREE LAYERS in a collection cup. Also: hemoptysis, fever, night sweats, moist crackles/rhonchi, halitosis. Digital clubbing parallels disease severity — assess via nail angle (normal 160°, clubbed >180°) or the Schamroth sign (obliterated diamond space when nail beds are opposed).

Diagnosis & treatment

High-resolution CT: airway-to-artery diameter ratio ≥1, or airways tracking to lung periphery. Treatment: antibiotics + bronchodilators + chest percussion/postural drainage; hydration/nutrition for secretion clearance; bronchoscopy/lavage or surgical resection in severe localized cases. Complications: malnutrition, recurrent pneumonia, RV failure, visceral abscess (can spread via lymphatics/venous drainage to the brain).

Bronchiolitis

Etiology

RSV causes ~50% of cases; also influenza (A/B/C), H. influenzae, pneumococci, hemolytic strep, mycoplasma, chlamydia, Pneumocystis jiroveci. RSV: yearly winter-spring epidemics, mainly children <2 years, ~5-day incubation, entry via nose/eyes. In adults: linked to smoking, toxic fumes, immunosuppression.

Three obstruction mechanisms

(1) Inflammatory exudate displaces surfactant. (2) Chemical mediators cause bronchiolar constriction. (3) Inflammation induces fibrosis/narrowing. Plus goblet cell metaplasia and increased bronchial muscle mass compound the narrowing.

Presentation: wheeze, crackles, ↓ breath sounds, retractions, tachypnea, low-grade fever; otitis media is a common complication. Diagnosis: RSV antigen from nasal washings (ELISA/immunofluorescence). Treatment: humidified O2, bronchodilators, sometimes corticosteroids, hydration; smoking avoidance strongly encouraged. RSV vaccine now available/recommended for pregnant people, young children, and older adults.
Cystic Fibrosis

Genetics & epidemiology

Autosomal recessive exocrine gland disorder — the most common genetic lung disease in the US. Incidence: 1 in 2000-3000 White births (1 in 26 White people is a heterozygous carrier); 1 in 10,000 Black births; 1 in 80,000 Native American births; almost never seen in Asian populations.

Classification & core defect

Classified as both an airflow-obstructive AND a suppurative disorder. Core finding: hypersecretion of abnormal, thick mucus that obstructs exocrine glands/ducts.

Prognosis has improved dramatically: once described as fatal in infancy, median survival now approaches 50 years, with ~35% of the 30,000 US cases now being adults. This is directly attributed to advances in therapy and earlier recognition/management of complications.
