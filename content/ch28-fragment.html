Key Terms & Definitions

Start here
Acute kidney injury (AKI)
Sudden reduction in kidney function — fluid/electrolyte/acid-base disruption, retained nitrogenous waste, ↑ creatinine, ↓ GFR.
RIFLE classification
Risk, Injury, Failure, Loss, End-stage — a five-tier AKI staging system.
Prerenal AKI
Reduced kidney function from impaired renal perfusion — the cause lies upstream of the kidney.
Postrenal AKI
Reduced kidney function from obstruction of urine outflow distal to the kidney.
Intrinsic (intrarenal) AKI
Kidney injury from damage within the kidney itself — vascular, glomerular, tubular, or interstitial.
Acute tubular necrosis (ATN)
Tubular cell injury from ischemia or nephrotoxins — the most common form of intrinsic AKI.
Contrast-induced AKI (CI-AKI)
Nephropathy from radiographic contrast media, historically the most common nephrotoxic AKI cause.
Chronic kidney disease (CKD)
Decreased kidney function or damage present ≥3 months, or GFR <60 mL/min/1.73m² for 3 months.
End-stage renal/kidney disease (ESRD/ESKD)
The final outcome of CKD, requiring dialysis or transplant for survival.
Uremic syndrome
Multi-system toxicity from retained metabolic waste products as renal function declines.
Secondary hyperparathyroidism
Compensatory PTH elevation from CKD-related hypocalcemia, driving CKD bone/mineral disease.
Cardiorenal anemia syndrome
The interlinked, escalating combination of worsening CKD, anemia, and heart failure.
Protein-energy wasting (PEW)
Loss of muscle/visceral protein stores in advanced CKD — distinct from general malnutrition.
Acute-on-chronic kidney disease
AKI developing in a patient with preexisting CKD.
AKI Definition & RIFLE Staging

KDIGO 2012 diagnostic criteria (any ONE qualifies)

↑ Serum creatinine ≥0.3 mg/dL within 48 hours, OR ↑ creatinine ≥1.5x baseline within 7 days, OR urine output <0.5 mL/kg/h for 6 hours.

Aging kidney vulnerability

Renal blood flow ↓ ~10%/decade starting age 40; GFR ↓ ~0.8-1.0 mL/min/1.73m² per year. Aging kidney is less able to concentrate/dilute urine, conserve sodium, produce prostaglandin, or maintain renin/aldosterone — all of which raise AKI susceptibility independent of comorbidities.

Prerenal AKI

Absolute vs. relative volume decrease

Absolute: hemorrhage, dehydration, burns. Relative: distributive shock, third-spacing/edema, or decreased cardiac output (cardiogenic shock, dysrhythmias, tamponade, HF, MI) — even though the kidney normally gets 20-25% of CO, that volume can be inadequate when CO drops significantly.

The normotensive exception

Most prerenal AKI follows an episode of hypotension — but NSAIDs, ACE inhibitors, and ARBs can precipitate prerenal AKI in susceptible people WITHOUT BP dropping below normal, by directly interfering with renal autoregulation. NSAIDs constrict afferent arterioles; ACEIs/ARBs dilate efferent arterioles — either action lowers glomerular perfusion pressure. Highest risk: age >60 with atherosclerotic CVD, preexisting renal insufficiency, HF, advanced liver disease, or nephrotic syndrome.

Prerenal oliguria is a normal, initially harmless physiologic response — autoregulation + RAAS activation try to preserve GFR/circulating volume, and the kidney tolerates perfusion reduction well as long as it doesn't exceed 20-25% of normal. If hypoperfusion is identified and corrected promptly, function fully restores. But if hypoxia/ischemia persists more than a few hours, prerenal AKI PROGRESSES to intrinsic AKI (ATN) — the transition point between a reversible functional problem and actual structural damage.
Postrenal AKI

Causes & who's affected

BPH, kinked/obstructed catheters, intraabdominal tumors, strictures, calculi. More common in the ELDERLY, and the MOST amenable to intervention among all AKI types — a single obstructed kidney is compensated for by the other; only bilateral (or bladder outlet/urethral) obstruction causes clinically apparent postrenal AKI.

Time-dependent reversibility

Normalization depends entirely on how long the obstruction persists. If it continues, increasing retrograde pressure causes ATN (intrinsic AKI); if obstruction persists days-to-weeks, damage can become irreversible. This is exactly why postrenal AKI is described as the type where prompt intervention matters most.

Intrinsic AKI & ATN

Four anatomic subtypes

Vascular: inflammation/obstruction/damage to small vessels. Glomerular: acute glomerulonephritis (immune complex deposition in the basement membrane). Interstitial: pyelonephritis, allergic drug reaction, autoimmune disease. Tubular: ATN. COVID-19 is a notable exception that can affect ALL FOUR areas simultaneously.

ATN — the most common intrinsic AKI

Accounts for nearly HALF of all hospitalized-patient AKI cases; the most prevalent intrinsic AKI type in the elderly specifically. Two mechanisms: ischemic (sepsis is the most common cause — vasodilation causes intrarenal hypoperfusion; develops in ~50% of critically ill patients) and nephrotoxic (aminoglycosides, NSAIDs, amphotericin B, cisplatin, tetracycline, and historically — contrast media, the single most common nephrotoxic offender).

Contrast-induced AKI: can develop within 12-24 hours of administration; defined as ↑ creatinine 0.3-1.0 mg/dL or 25-100% from baseline at 48 hours. Rates have fallen to <20% since low-osmolality contrast became standard — a genuine practice-driven improvement over the past two decades.
CKD Overview & Risk Factors

Scale

>800 million people worldwide (>10% of the global population); projected to be the 5th leading cause of years-of-life-lost globally by 2040. Diabetes + hypertension together account for >60% of US ESRD cases, followed by glomerulonephritis and cystic kidney disease.

Definition

Decreased kidney function OR kidney damage present ≥3 months (by blood tests, urinalysis, imaging), OR GFR <60 mL/min/1.73m² for 3 months regardless of other damage evidence.

Diabetes vs. hypertension progression patterns

Type 1 diabetic nephropathy progresses to ESRD MORE often than type 2 — but many type 2 patients die of CVD before reaching ESRD, an important nuance about competing risks. 60-80% of CKD patients have hypertension, rising to 80% on dialysis — hypertension is BOTH a cause and a consequence of CKD.

Nonmodifiable vs. contested risk factors

Family history, toxin exposure history, age >65 are genuinely nonmodifiable. Race/ethnicity (Black patients develop CKD at higher rates) has traditionally been framed as nonmodifiable/genetic, but current research increasingly questions how much of this reflects social determinants of health rather than biology — an evolving area, not settled science.

Progression & Staging

The 75% threshold

More than 75% of total nephrons must be lost before clinical manifestations appear — remaining nephrons compensate by enlarging/increasing clearance capacity. This compensation itself may cause further injury over years (theorized mechanism: increased workload → glomerular/arteriolar sclerosis → further decline → ESRD) — a self-perpetuating cycle regardless of the original cause.

Monitoring progression

Serial GFR, degree of proteinuria, urinary sediment (WBC/RBC), serum creatinine, and renal ultrasound (kidney size) together track disease trajectory over time.

Complications of CKD

Cardiovascular disease — the real cause of death

92% of patients with eGFR <30 have BP ≥140/90. Most CKD patients die of CVD, not kidney failure itself — CVD is BOTH a risk factor for AND a complication of CKD, present at every stage (not just ESRD). Contributing factors: dyslipidemia, anemia, electrolyte-driven dysrhythmias, endothelial dysfunction, proteinuria, premature atherosclerosis, volume overload.

Metabolic acidosis mechanism

Normal daily metabolism produces 50-80 mmol MORE acid than base — kidneys normally handle this via H+ secretion/bicarbonate production. As this capacity is lost, pH can fall precipitously (coma/death below pH 6.8). Also worsened by hyperkalemia (K+ exchanges for intracellular H+, directly lowering blood pH). Respiratory system attempts compensation via ↑ rate/depth.

Mind Map — CKD Bone/Mineral Disorder Cascade

PTH's three normal calcium-raising actions

(1) ↑ Renal calcium reabsorption, (2) ↑ dietary calcium absorption (requires kidney-activated vitamin D), (3) mobilize calcium from bone. In CKD, chronically elevated PTH drives bone pain/deformity/fractures AND vascular/soft tissue calcification — and abnormal PTH/calcium/phosphorus levels are independently linked to cardiac mortality during dialysis.

Anemia mechanism

↓ Erythropoietin (kidney-produced) → ↓ RBC production. Compounded by iron/folate/B12 deficiency, chronic inflammation, PTH-driven marrow suppression, and a uremic environment that shortens RBC lifespan below the normal 120 days. ~76% of stage 5/dialysis-initiation patients have Hb <11 g/dL. Chronic compensatory cardiac changes (dilation, LV hypertrophy) create cardiorenal anemia syndrome — CKD, anemia, and heart failure worsening each other in a loop.

Protein-energy wasting (PEW): distinct from general malnutrition — driven by uremic anorexia, altered taste, chronic illness depression, chronic inflammation, acidosis, and endocrine disruption, producing negative nitrogen balance from ↑ catabolism + ↓ synthesis. Albumin/prealbumin are the best serum markers, though inflammation can confound interpretation. Hypoalbuminemia on dialysis is strongly linked to mortality.
AKI Prevention

The core message: AKI is often entirely preventable

Identify at-risk patients + prophylactic intervention. Address hypotension/hypovolemia, reduce/stop contributing meds (antihypertensives, opioids), support nutrition, remove invasive lines/catheters early (infection risk), monitor/treat infection promptly.

Contrast-induced AKI prevention

IV saline volume expansion before AND after contrast; N-acetylcysteine for at-risk patients (though evidence for its effectiveness is genuinely MIXED — worth noting rather than treating as settled); check baseline creatinine in suspected renal dysfunction; use low- or iso-osmolar contrast in renal insufficiency.

Postrenal AKI is the LEAST common AKI type but the MOST readily identifiable and treatable — prompt correction of the obstruction prevents progression to ATN.
CKD Management

Blood pressure targets — an important nuance

Newest KDIGO guidance recommends systolic BP <120 mmHg for CKD patients not on dialysis (intensive management), even using the term "high BP" for pressures below standard hypertension thresholds but still too high for someone with CKD. ACEI/ARB is preferred (also reduces proteinuria) — but lowering BP does not necessarily improve survival, and intensive BP management is NOT specifically renoprotective — a genuinely counterintuitive point worth remembering.

Statins — clear benefit, with a caveat

Robust evidence for statins in CKD patients NOT on dialysis and in transplant recipients (↓ cholesterol, ↓ cardiovascular events). But NO evidence of improved GFR or slowed CKD progression, and improved outcomes have NOT been demonstrated in patients already on RRT — benefit is real but stage-dependent.

Potassium management thresholds

Mild hyperkalemia (<6 mmol/L): dietary restriction + correct acidosis. >6 mmol/L: urgent treatment — IV calcium gluconate, D5W+insulin, or potassium-binding agents.

Phosphate binders — a real trade-off

Calcium carbonate/acetate: effective, inexpensive, but risk hypercalcemia/vascular calcification. Non-calcium-based binders avoid that risk but cost more — clinical OUTCOMES are equivalent between binder classes, so the choice is really a cost-vs-hypercalcemia-risk trade-off, not an efficacy question.

Dialysis & Transplant

Three RRT modalities

Hemodialysis: artificial semipermeable membrane; blood passes through capillary tubules bathed in dialysate; solutes diffuse out, water leaves via osmosis. AV fistula is preferred access. Typically 3x/week, ~4 hours each. Peritoneal dialysis: peritoneum as the membrane; CAPD (manual exchanges, 4-5+ hour dwell, no machine) vs. CCPD (cycler machine, overnight exchanges, ~1.5h each). CRRT: continuous, hospital-only, for hemodynamically UNSTABLE AKI patients — gentler fluid removal avoids the hypotension risk of intermittent large-volume removal.

Historical context

First human hemodialysis: 1924. 1972: US national ESRD program signed into law — ESRD remains the ONLY diagnosis granted automatic Medicare eligibility regardless of age, a unique policy status.

Despite dialysis being lifesaving, CVD remains the most common cause of death in ESRD patients — dialysis addresses the uremia/fluid/electrolyte crisis but doesn't reverse the cardiovascular risk burden that accumulated alongside the kidney disease.

Kidney transplant

5-year survival just under 80% (higher for living-donor). Common post-transplant complications: CVD, obesity, hypertension, dyslipidemia, diabetes, cerebrovascular disease, anemia, gout, depression, bone disease, malignancy, infection. Standard immunosuppression: tacrolimus or cyclosporine + mycophenolate + prednisone. 2020 US legislation now ensures immunosuppressant coverage — historically, transplant failure was sometimes attributable to patients discontinuing therapy after losing insurance coverage.

CKD in older adults

Majority of CKD patients are older adults; age >65 makes up 50% of the dialysis population. Genuinely unclear whether CKD in aging reflects the aging kidney itself or accumulated CVD risk factor exposure — but most older CKD patients die of CVD rather than progressing to ESRD, reinforcing the same cardiovascular-dominance pattern seen throughout CKD generally.
