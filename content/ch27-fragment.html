Key Terms & Definitions

Start here
Nephralgia
Renal/kidney pain — the kidney itself has few pain receptors, but the capsule is richly innervated.
CVA tenderness
Costovertebral angle tenderness — the classic exam finding for intrarenal capsular pain (T10-L1 dermatomes).
Renal agenesis
Failure of a kidney to embryonically develop; bilateral is incompatible with life.
ARPKD / ADPKD
Autosomal recessive (neonatal-onset) vs. autosomal dominant (adult-onset) polycystic kidney disease.
Renal cell carcinoma (RCC)
The most common kidney cancer; often asymptomatic until advanced/metastatic.
Nephroblastoma (Wilms tumor)
The most common pediatric kidney cancer, from nephrogenic blastemal cells.
Pyelonephritis
Infection of the kidney (renal tubules, pelvis, calices) — an upper urinary tract infection.
Urosepsis
Bloodstream infection originating from a urinary tract infection — causes 25% of all sepsis cases.
Hydronephrosis
Kidney/renal pelvis enlargement from obstructed urine outflow.
Nephrolithiasis
Kidney stone formation and passage anywhere in the urinary tract.
Struvite (staghorn) calculi
Magnesium-ammonium-phosphate stones forming around urea-splitting bacterial infections, sometimes filling the renal pelvis.
Podocytes
Specialized glomerular epithelial cells with foot processes forming slit pores — part of the filtration barrier.
Nephrotic syndrome
Heavy proteinuria (3-3.5 g/24h) from glomerular basement membrane damage — edema, hyperlipidemia.
Nephritic syndrome
Glomerular inflammation/leakiness causing gross hematuria and RBC casts.
Glomerulonephritis
Immune-mediated glomerular inflammation, primary or secondary to systemic disease.
Renal Pain

Why extensive kidney damage can be painless

Most of the kidney parenchyma lacks pain receptors — but the renal CAPSULE is richly innervated by nociceptors. Pain occurs when the capsule is distended, inflamed, or punctured (fluid accumulation, infected/bleeding cysts, hemorrhage, neoplastic expansion, biopsy/trauma).

Renal pelvis pain is different

The renal pelvis and rest of the urinary tract have MANY pain receptors — obstruction causes pain if it distends the pelvis/capsule. But large calculi can develop insidiously and painlessly in the pelvis/calices until they start moving toward the ureteral junction.

CVA tenderness reflects T10-L1 dermatomal referral — visceral (renal/ureteral) and cutaneous afferent fibers converge on the same spinal neurons, which is why the pain can be felt across a broad dermatomal region rather than precisely localized to the kidney itself.

Renal Agenesis & Hypoplasia

Bilateral vs. unilateral agenesis

Bilateral: metanephros fails to develop at all → incompatible with extrauterine life → stillbirth/early death. Unilateral (URA): ~1 in 200 births, compatible with life, often associated with other anomalies; remaining kidney compensates via enlargement; lifelong monitoring still recommended.

Renal hypoplasia

Some development occurs but kidneys are smaller than normal — likely gene-mutation driven. Bilateral severe cases may be incompatible with life; milder/unilateral cases may not affect function until later in life. ↑ Risk of eventual CKD either way.

Cystic Kidney Disease (ARPKD/ADPKD)

Shared mechanism, different genes

Both ARPKD and ADPKD genes code for proteins on the PRIMARY CILIUM of tubule cells — a mechanoreceptor sensing urine flow, linked to a Ca2+ channel. Mutations cause growth dysregulation/abnormal tissue remodeling — pathophysiology is similar despite genetically distinct causes.

Renal cysts generally

More common in males, increasing with age — found in ≥1/3 of patients over 50. Significant cysts can cause flank pain and hemorrhage.

ADPKD progression mechanism

Cyst expansion compacts/distorts renal vasculature → local ischemia → activates the intrarenal RAAS. ↑ Kidney/cyst volume correlates directly with progressive GFR decline. HTN develops in 60% BEFORE renal impairment even begins — aggressive BP control slows progression of function loss, proteinuria, and hematuria.

Treatment

Primarily supportive (BP control). Tolvaptan (vasopressin V2 antagonist) is the only disease-modifying therapy — slows kidney volume increase and function decline, but can cause significant liver enzyme elevation, limiting use to specific patient subsets. ESRD requires dialysis/transplant.

Benign Renal Neoplasms

Worth knowing by name

Oncocytoma: hardest to distinguish from RCC. Angiomyolipoma: adipose + smooth muscle + vessels — hormone-dependent, mostly postpubescent females. Mixed epithelial stromal neoplasm: perimenopausal females on estrogen replacement. Nephroma: can't be reliably distinguished from RCC (adults) or Wilms tumor (children) on imaging alone.

Detection & management

Usually found incidentally on imaging for other reasons; may cause a palpable mass, flank pain, or hematuria if large. Management mirrors other renal tumors: surveillance, biopsy, thermal ablation, partial/radical nephrectomy depending on size/imaging characteristics/patient factors.

Renal Cell Carcinoma

Scale & genetics

~73,500 US cases/year (3% of all cancers); higher in males/older adults. Clear cell RCC: from proximal tubule cells, VHL tumor suppressor gene LOST (loses its growth-restraining function) — frequently metastatic. Papillary RCC (10-15%): MET proto-oncogene overactive (codes for a growth factor receptor).

Modifiable risk factors

Smoking, obesity, and hypertension are the important MODIFIABLE ones. Also: diabetes, asbestos exposure, multiparity, long-term dialysis, kidney transplant.

RCC's clinical trap: commonly ASYMPTOMATIC until advanced — often already metastasized at diagnosis. When symptoms appear: CVA tenderness, hematuria, palpable abdominal mass (the "classic triad," though not always all present together); dyspnea/cough/bone pain if metastatic. Despite this, overall 5-year survival is up to 75% — reflecting how much stage at diagnosis drives outcome.
Nephroblastoma (Wilms Tumor)

Epidemiology

5% of all pediatric malignancies, up to 90% of childhood kidney cancers; most common <5 years old; more common in females; usually unilateral/sporadic (only 1-2% familial).

Pathology

Triphasic appearance: undifferentiated blastemal + stromal + epithelial cells (varying differentiation). WT1 gene (chromosome 11 short arm) was the first identified association; epigenetic 11p15 abnormalities also implicated. Favorable vs. unfavorable (diffuse anaplasia) histology affects prognosis. Lung is the most common metastasis site.

Presentation: palpable abdominal mass (most common), abdominal pain, hematuria; can produce an IVC tumor thrombus → decreased venous return → lower extremity edema. Treatment: nephrectomy + radiation + chemotherapy → 5-year survival now >90%, a dramatic improvement from historically lethal outcomes.
Host Defenses & UTI Risk

Normal protective mechanisms

Acidic urine pH + urea = hostile bacterial environment. Males: bacteriostatic prostatic secretions. Females: distal urethral gland mucus traps bacteria. Both sexes: micturition washes out small bacterial numbers; unidirectional flow (vesicoureteral junction contraction on filling) prevents reflux; epithelial physical barrier + indigenous anti-adhesion proteins.

Most common organisms

E. coli — by far the most common overall. Enterobacter, Enterococcus, Proteus mirabilis, Klebsiella — usually linked to calculi or anatomic abnormality. S. saprophyticus, group B strep in certain populations. Uropathogenic bacteria have adhesins that let them bind urinary epithelium directly.

Key risk factors: increasing age, vesicoureteral reflux, congenital anomalies, female sex, pregnancy, spermicide-diaphragm use, neurogenic bladder, catheterization/instrumentation, obstruction (calculi, BPH), immunodeficiency, diabetes, obesity, prior UTI. Early catheter removal is one of the most impactful preventive interventions available.
Acute & Chronic Pyelonephritis

Acute pyelonephritis

Requires obstruction or reflux to actually reach the kidney (bladder infections alone are common but rarely ascend without one of these). Risk factors: pregnancy (physiologic urinary tract changes), diabetes, anatomic abnormalities (especially vesicoureteral reflux in children), obstruction. Highest incidence: young females, infants, elderly. Sudden onset: fever, chills, CVA tenderness + lower UTI symptoms (dysuria, urgency, frequency), nausea/vomiting/anorexia worsening dehydration.

Complications

Abscess, sepsis, ARDS, recurrent/chronic pyelonephritis, CKD from scarring (more likely with preexisting anatomic/functional abnormalities). Urosepsis = UTI-originating bloodstream infection, responsible for 25% of ALL sepsis cases — a striking statistic given how common and often minor UTIs otherwise seem.

Diagnosis & treatment

Bacteria + WBC casts on urinalysis (casts specifically distinguish from lower UTI). Outpatient antimicrobials 7-10 days typically; IV antimicrobials/fluids + hospitalization for complicated cases (e.g., septicemia). Overall risk of lasting renal function decline after a single episode is LOW when treated promptly.

Chronic pyelonephritis

Small, atrophied, scarred kidneys with blunted calices from persistent/recurrent infection — typically from chronic reflux of infected urine. Risk factors: bacteriuria + obstructive disorders (calculi, neurogenic bladder, VUR, underlying intrarenal disease). Symptoms vague/inconsistent: flank/abdominal pain, fever, malaise, anorexia. A genuine potential cause of CKD. Treatment: correct underlying cause + antimicrobials + support renal function.

Obstruction Mechanism

The location-severity relationship

The MORE DISTAL the obstruction, the LESS dilation occurs (pressure distributes over a greater surface area). Complete ureteral obstruction → hydroureter. Continued kidney dilation → hydronephrosis, flattened renal papillae, declining GFR/perfusion, eventual ischemia. Untreated over weeks: tubular atrophy, medullary destruction, scarring, nonfunctioning glomeruli.

Partial vs. complete, unilateral vs. bilateral

Partial obstruction is FAR more common — renal pelvis can dilate significantly with minimal structural/functional disruption. If BILATERAL, fluid retention manifestations appear (weight gain, nausea, anorexia, malaise, headaches, ↑ abdominal girth, ankle edema). Functional partial obstruction: modest ↓ blood flow/GFR, impaired urine concentration, impaired K+/H+ secretion. Compensatory hypertrophy occurs in the unaffected kidney.

Nephrolithiasis

Scale & pathogenesis basics

~11% lifetime US risk; lower risk in Black/Hispanic populations than White; uncommon before 20, peak 40-60; male:female ratio has fallen to <2:1 over the past 25 years. Core mechanism: urine SUPERSATURATION with a specific solute — crystals form in concentrating areas of the nephron/pelvis. Dehydration (↓ solvent) and high solute excretion both promote it; free-flowing urine and adequate fluid intake INHIBIT crystal aggregation.

The matrix

Every stone type has a noncrystalline organic "matrix" component (protein, sugar, glucosamine, bound water, organic ash) — a small fraction of total weight but present in ALL stone types regardless of crystal composition.

Struvite/staghorn stones — a genuinely distinct mechanism: bacteria that split urea into ammonium + bicarbonate ALKALINIZE the urine, which both supports further bacterial growth AND drives stone formation simultaneously — a self-reinforcing infection-stone cycle. These stones form around a bacterial nucleus that creates an antimicrobial barrier (making antibiotics less effective at clearing the infection) and can grow into a jagged "staghorn" shape filling the renal pelvis.

Clinical presentation

Kidney/pelvis stones: dull localized flank pain. Ureteral movement: spasmodic, intermittent, sharp renal colic, radiating to ipsilateral groin/testicle/labia, with nausea/vomiting/diaphoresis/tachycardia/tachypnea. Pain often noticeably decreases once the stone reaches the bladder. Urethral partial obstruction: dysuria/urgency/frequency. Hematuria (micro or macro) from lining injury as stones pass.

Size determines management

<5 mm: high spontaneous passage chance. 5-7 mm: ~50% chance. >7 mm: almost always needs urologic intervention (shockwave lithotripsy, ureteroscopy/basket retrieval, percutaneous approaches). NSAIDs are preferred first-line pain management (highly effective for renal colic specifically), opioids also used. Recurrence: 30-50% within 5-10 years — dietary modification tailored to stone type reduces this risk.

Glomerular Structure & Classification

The three-layer filtration barrier

Endothelium → basement membrane → podocytes (specialized epithelial cells with foot processes forming slit pores). Filtrate passes through the slit pores into Bowman capsule → proximal tubule. Glomerulopathies account for the great majority of ESRD cases.

Primary vs. secondary

Primary: kidney is the only/predominant organ involved. Secondary: results from systemic disease — autoimmune/immune-complex diseases (Goodpasture, SLE), diabetic nephropathy, hypertensive renal damage. Diabetic nephropathy is the most common SECONDARY glomerulopathy overall.

Descriptive classification vocabulary

Diffuse (all glomeruli) vs. focal (some glomeruli). Global (whole glomerulus) vs. segmental (patches within a glomerulus). Membranous = capillary wall thickening from IgG/complement (C5) deposition. Sclerotic = scarring from persistent/recurrent injury. Deposit location: mesangial, subendothelial, or subepithelial.

Why classification is genuinely difficult

Disparate underlying causes can produce SIMILAR injury patterns on imaging/light microscopy — this is exactly why etiology-based classification (with biopsy/histology when needed) is recommended over purely descriptive/pattern-based classification.

Mind Map — Postinfectious GN Pathogenesis

Postinfectious GN timing

Classic streptococcal etiology (group A β-hemolytic strep, skin/throat infection) — now less common due to early antibiotic treatment. Glomerular damage begins 1-6 WEEKS after the initial infection — a delayed, immune-complex-mediated process, not a direct infection of the kidney itself. Viruses also commonly implicated.

Vasoactive amplification

Locally-acting angiotensin II and leukotrienes contract mesangial cells → reduce glomerular capillary perfusion → further ↓ GFR. In severe cases, the coagulation cascade activates and fibrin deposits in capillaries, compounding the perfusion/filtration impairment.

Classic presentation: smoky or coffee-colored urine (hematuria + RBC casts = nephritic syndrome pattern). Proteinuria is variable and can occasionally reach nephrotic-range. ↓ GFR → ↑ circulating volume → edema + hypertension; oliguria with rising creatinine/BUN. In children especially, resolution can occur within ~2 weeks with supportive care alone — a notably good short-term prognosis for an acute inflammatory kidney process.
