Concepts of Pathophysiology

Key Terms & Definitions

Start here
Pathophysiology
The study of abnormalities in the physiologic functioning of living beings — the intersection of pathology (disease study) and physiology (normal function study).
Etiology
The study of the causes or reasons for a disease or phenomenon.
Idiopathic
A condition whose cause is unknown.
Iatrogenic
A condition resulting from an unintended or unwanted effect of medical treatment.
Risk factor
A factor that increases the probability of disease but where the causal link is less than certain.
Causative agent
A factor closely and definitively linked to a disease's development (e.g., a specific pathogen for an infectious disease).
Pathogenesis
The development or evolution of a disease, from initial stimulus to the full expression of its manifestations.
Sign
An objective manifestation of disease, observable/measurable by an examiner (e.g., vomiting).
Symptom
A subjective abnormality reported only by the affected individual (e.g., nausea).
Syndrome
A constellation of signs and symptoms whose underlying etiology has not yet been determined.
Latent/incubation period
The interval between exposure to an injurious agent and the first appearance of signs/symptoms.
Prodrome
The appearance of the first, often nonspecific, signs/symptoms indicating disease onset (e.g., headache, malaise).
Acute phase (manifest illness)
The stage at which a disease reaches its full intensity, with signs/symptoms at their greatest severity.
Subclinical stage
A stage during which disease processes are established but the patient still functions normally — no symptoms yet perceived.
Exacerbation
A relatively sudden increase in the severity of a disease or its signs/symptoms.
Remission
A decline/abatement in the severity of signs and symptoms; if permanent (often >5 years), the person is considered cured.
Convalescence
The stage of recovery after a disease, injury, or surgery.
Sequela
A pathologic condition resulting from a prior disease (e.g., cardiac valve scarring after rheumatic fever).
Complication
A new, separate disease process arising secondarily because of changes produced by the original problem (e.g., bacterial pneumonia after a viral infection).
Validity (accuracy)
The degree to which a measurement reflects the true value of what it's meant to measure.
Reliability (precision)
A test's ability to give the same result on repeated measurement — can be reliable yet still inaccurate.
Sensitivity
The probability a test will be positive in someone who has the condition; low sensitivity = more false negatives.
Specificity
The probability a test will be negative in someone who does NOT have the condition; low specificity = more false positives.
Positive predictive value (PPV)
The probability disease is actually present given a positive test result.
Negative predictive value (NPV)
The probability disease is actually absent given a negative test result.
Endemic disease
A disease native to, and consistently present in, a specific local region.
Epidemic
A disease disseminated to many individuals at the same time within a region/population.
Pandemic
An epidemic that has spread worldwide across multiple countries/continents.
Acclimatization
A normal adaptive physiologic response to an environmental change (e.g., increased RBC count at high altitude).
Primary prevention
Preventing disease onset entirely, by reducing susceptibility or exposure (e.g., vaccination).
Secondary prevention
Early detection and management of disease in its preclinical/early clinical stages (e.g., screening).
Tertiary prevention
Rehabilitative/supportive care in advanced disease or disability, aimed at restoring function.
What Is Pathophysiology?

Where the word comes from

Pathology (pathos = suffering) studies disease via organs/tissues/cells/fluids. Physiology (physis = nature) studies mechanical, physical, and biochemical function. Pathophysiology combines both — the study of abnormal physiologic function.

Why "classic presentations" are still useful

No two people are structurally/functionally identical, but discovering common, expected responses allows prediction of clinical progression, identification of likely causes, and selection of the most helpful interventions — even though individuals often vary from the textbook picture.

The conceptual, not just descriptive, angle: pathophysiology studies processes and relationships common across many different diseases (e.g., inflammation, hypotension, fluid volume deficit, hypoxia, ischemia are each relevant to numerous unrelated pathologies) — this is different from a pure pathology textbook, which focuses on distinguishing similar diseases from each other.
Advances in genomics/epigenomics have transformed the field — deeper insight into immune mechanisms has driven progress against cancer, COVID-19, and HIV/AIDS, while also raising new ethical questions (e.g., gene therapy).
The Four-Part Framework — Etiology

Pathophysiology is organized around four interrelated topics used throughout the whole course: etiology, pathogenesis, clinical manifestations, and treatment implications.

Mind Map — The Four-Part Framework

Etiology in a nutshell

Identifying the causal factors that, acting together, provoke a disease or injury.

When cause is unclear or treatment-caused

Idiopathic: cause unknown. Iatrogenic: caused by medical treatment itself.

Risk factor vs. causative agent

A risk factor increases probability but isn't certain to cause disease (e.g., smoking and coronary heart disease). A causative agent has a much tighter link — e.g., a specific virus causing a specific infectious disease.

Most diseases are multifactorial

Coronary heart disease results from genetic predisposition + diet + smoking + blood pressure + other factors acting in concert — no single factor "causes" it alone.

Even strong causative links aren't deterministic: heavy alcohol use is considered the cause of alcoholic liver cirrhosis, yet only a portion of heavy drinkers actually develop it — diet, lifestyle, mental health, viral infection, genetics, and biological sex all modify individual risk.
Categories used to classify disease etiology: congenital, degenerative, iatrogenic, idiopathic, immunologic, infectious, inherited, metabolic, neoplastic, nutritional deficiency, physical agent–induced, and psychogenic. No system is fully comprehensive — some diseases span multiple categories.
Pathogenesis

Definition

The dynamic sequence of physiologic events — cell, tissue, organ, and systemic changes — that occurs in response to an etiologic agent, from initial stimulus to full disease expression.

Why the immune system is a key example

It communicates with essentially every other cell in the body; disruption of the balance between immune tolerance (of normal cells) and immune surveillance (for abnormal/foreign cells) underlies a large share of degenerative, immunologic, and inflammatory diseases.

The systems-approach caveat: pathophysiology texts (and clinical exams) are typically organized by body system for teaching convenience, but the body functions as an integrated whole — intercellular communication networks aren't confined to single systems, so don't think of organ systems as truly separate in disease processes.
Clinical Manifestations — Signs, Symptoms & Syndromes

Sign vs. symptom

Sign: objective, observable by an examiner (vomiting). Symptom: subjective, reportable only by the patient (nausea). Classic paired example worth memorizing exactly.

Nonspecific findings

Fever and headache signal something is wrong but don't point to a specific cause — further exam/labs are needed to narrow the differential.

Syndrome: used when a particular constellation of signs/symptoms is recognized, but the underlying etiology hasn't yet been determined. Example: AIDS was originally identified purely as a "helper T-cell deficiency syndrome" of unknown cause — now understood as a late stage of HIV infection specifically.
Stages & Clinical Course

Mind Map — Typical Disease Timeline

Latent/incubation vs. subclinical stage

Latent/incubation: exposure has happened, some detectable biological changes may occur, but no patient-recognized symptoms yet. Subclinical stage: disease is well-established, but patient still functions normally — large organ reserve capacity can mask early damage (e.g., chronic renal disease can destroy one kidney and partially damage the other before symptoms appear).

Silent/latent periods mid-illness

Some diseases have signs/symptoms disappear temporarily during the course of illness. Example: total-body irradiation syndrome has a latent period between prodrome and manifest illness. Syphilis can have two separate latent periods (between primary/secondary and between secondary/tertiary stages).

Acute vs. chronic

Acute: relatively severe, short course (hours to a few weeks). Chronic: lasts months to years — may begin acutely and become prolonged if the body's defenses can't overcome the cause, or may develop insidiously with no acute phase at all.

Exacerbation vs. remission

Exacerbation: sudden worsening. Remission: decline in severity — if permanent (commonly defined as >5 years), the person is considered cured. Relevant especially in autoimmune diseases with alternating courses.

Sequela vs. complication — a commonly confused pair: a sequela is a pathologic condition that results directly from the original disease process (e.g., cardiac valve scarring after rheumatic fever). A complication is a NEW, separate disease process that arises secondarily (e.g., bacterial pneumonia complicating a viral respiratory infection). The distinction is whether it's a direct consequence of the same process, or a genuinely separate problem triggered by it.
Treatment Implications

The closing link in the framework

Understanding a disorder's etiology, pathogenesis, and clinical consequences directly suggests which treatments are likely to help — treatment isn't a separate topic tacked on, it's the logical output of correctly understanding the first three parts of the framework.

Concepts of Normality in Health & Disease

Interpreting data

Before any finding can be called "abnormal," you need a working definition of "normal" — and that's much harder to pin down than it sounds.

Statistical Normality

The bell curve approach

Most diagnostic variables follow a normal distribution. The "normal range" is typically defined as the mean ± 2 standard deviations — capturing about 95% of the population's values, with 2.5% above and 2.5% below.

The reference population matters enormously

Bone density example: an elderly woman's bone density might fall in the "normal range" compared to same-age peers, but be >2 SD below the mean compared to younger women. Which comparison group is correct? This is genuinely controversial — it's hard to separate "disease" from "normal aging."

Change from baseline can matter more than the absolute value: a BP of 90/70 might be totally normal for one person but a significant, worrying drop for someone whose usual BP is 120/80. This is why patients are typically measured 2–3 times to establish their own usual baseline before interpreting a "deviation."
Reliability, Validity & Predictive Value

Validity (accuracy)

How closely a measurement reflects the true value — e.g., how well a pulse oximeter reading matches a direct arterial blood gas measurement.

Reliability (precision)

Whether a test gives the same result on repeat measurement. Key distinction: a test can be reliable (consistent) but still invalid (inaccurate) — consistently wrong is still wrong.

The single most counterintuitive point in this section: even a test with 99% sensitivity AND 99% specificity has only a 67% positive predictive value if used to screen a low-prevalence population (2% prevalence in the textbook example) — meaning 33% of positive results would be false positives. Who you test matters as much as how good the test is. This is exactly why indiscriminate screening of low-risk populations produces so many false alarms.

Real-world reliability problem: PT and INR

Prothrombin time (PT) varies by the reagent/lab method used — the same blood sample sent to different labs could give meaningfully different results. The INR (international normalized ratio) is a correction that standardizes PT values across labs, giving it higher reliability than raw PT.

Individual Factors Influencing Normality

Age differences

Most organs shrink with age — exceptions are the male prostate and the heart, which enlarge. Elderly patients have diminished temperature sensation (burn risk from heating pads/bath water), fewer sweat glands, and less thirst perception. A resting HR of 120 bpm is normal for an infant but abnormal for an adult.

Sex differences (know these ranges)

Hemoglobin: females 12–16 g/dL, males 13–18 g/dL. ESR: males <13 mm/h (slightly higher in females). Creatinine: females 0.4–1.3 mg/dL, males 0.6–1.5 mg/dL.

Situational differences — acclimatization

Increased RBC count at high altitude is a normal adaptive response to lower oxygen availability (acclimatization) — the same increase at sea level would be abnormal. Context determines whether a deviation is pathologic.

Time variations — circadian/diurnal rhythm

Body temperature, growth hormone, and cortisol all show diurnal variation. Urinary 17-ketosteroid excretion peaks 8–10 AM and is 2–3x higher than its lowest point (midnight–2 AM) — the time a sample is drawn can change how it should be interpreted.

Cultural considerations: culture shapes which signs/symptoms/behaviors are even perceived as abnormal, when and from whom people seek care, and how illness/pain are experienced and expressed — "normal" isn't a purely biological concept.
The single-measurement caution repeated as a key point: one elevated glucose reading ≠ diabetes; one high BP reading ≠ hypertension; a temporary feeling of hopelessness ≠ clinical depression. Trends across repeated measurements are far more reliable than any single data point.

Epidemiology

Patterns across populations

Epidemiology studies disease patterns across groups rather than individuals — occurrence, incidence, prevalence, transmission, and distribution.

Endemic, Epidemic & Pandemic

The three terms, precisely

Endemic: native to / consistently present in a local region. Epidemic: disseminated to many people at once. Pandemic: an epidemic that has spread worldwide.

Real example — Ebola 2014–15

Started in Guinea (March 2014), spread to Liberia and Sierra Leone, then produced travel-associated cases in Nigeria, Mali, Senegal, and beyond Africa (including the US) — over a year, caused more than 10x the combined case count of all previous Ebola outbreaks.

Pandemics are more common now than historically because of the speed and volume of global human travel — a new flu strain can spread continent to continent almost every season.
Aggregate Factors Affecting Disease Patterns

Five principal factors: age, ethnic group, sex/gender, socioeconomic/lifestyle factors, and geographic location.

Age

Gestation is physiologically distinct (differentiation/growth-focused, few homeostatic mechanisms of its own). Postuterine neonatal risks: immaturity, respiratory failure, birth injury, congenital malformation. Developmental processes occur in early life; aging processes occur from 60+ (studied by gerontology).

Ethnic group

Hard to fully separate from socioeconomic/cultural/geographic confounders, but some patterns are clearly genetic: sickle cell anemia is far more common in African populations; pernicious anemia is more common in Scandinavians and rare in Black populations worldwide.

Sex vs. gender — the distinction

Sex = biological. Gender = psychosocial/cultural expression on a continuum. Both matter clinically, especially when self-reported gender differs from biological sex.

Sex-linked disease patterns (know a few cold)

More common in females: Alzheimer dementia, systemic lupus erythematosus, toxic goiter/hypothyroidism, long COVID, rheumatoid arthritis. Equal in both sexes: osteoarthritis (unlike RA). More common in males: thromboangiitis obliterans. Also: pyelonephritis is more common in young females (shorter urethra, easier bacterial ascent); atherosclerosis onset is delayed ~20 years in females (estrogen's protective effect).

Socioeconomic factors & lifestyle

Poverty, malnutrition, overcrowding, and environmental extremes drive many diseases. Parasitic disease examples: schistosomiasis is tied to irrigation-ditch use (snail host); trichinosis comes from undercooked infected pork.

Antibiotic resistance — a modern epidemiologic crisis

~2 million hospital-acquired bacterial infections/year in the US, ~90,000 deaths. >70% of hospital-acquired infections are resistant to at least one common drug. Staphylococcus (leading cause of hospital infections) is resistant to 95% of first-choice and 30% of second-choice antibiotics.

Geographic location

Developed nations: heart disease, cancer, COVID-19, liver/kidney/lung disease, and violent accidents dominate mortality. Developing nations: undernutrition, diarrhea, and infectious disease dominate.

Geography examples worth remembering

Malaria is limited to regions where the Anopheles mosquito can survive (expanding with climate change). Bartonellosis (Carrión disease) is found only in Peru, Ecuador, Chile, and Colombia, for reasons that remain unclear despite seemingly favorable conditions elsewhere.

Levels of Prevention

Mind Map — Primary, Secondary & Tertiary Prevention

Primary prevention — reducing susceptibility/exposure

Vaccination is the flagship example — childhood immunization has driven down measles, poliomyelitis, pertussis, and neonatal tetanus. Global childhood immunization coverage for six major diseases was ~80% as of 2023. Also includes improved nutrition, sanitation, housing, and economic conditions.

Where it fits in the disease timeline

Secondary prevention applies in the preclinical and early clinical stages (screening catches disease before/just as symptoms emerge). Tertiary prevention applies in advanced disease/disability — it doesn't cure, it restores function and reduces disability.

WHO's definition of health, worth knowing verbatim in spirit: complete physical, mental, and social well-being — not merely the absence of disease. This framing is why "levels of prevention" go beyond just stopping illness; the goal is positive health/functional wholeness, including for people managing chronic disease.
