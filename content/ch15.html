Alterations in Blood Flow

The physics of how blood actually moves through vessels, how the body controls that flow, and what happens when arteries, veins, or lymphatics are obstructed or structurally altered. Built from your Chapter 15 material.

Key Terms & Definitions

Start here
Hemodynamics
The principles governing blood flow — pressure, resistance, velocity, laminar/turbulent flow, wall tension, compliance.
Poiseuille's law
The formula describing how vessel length, radius, and blood viscosity determine resistance to flow.
Ohm's law (hemodynamic)
Q = P/R — blood flow equals the pressure difference divided by resistance.
Systemic vascular resistance (SVR)
Resistance peripheral to the heart and lungs; primarily determined by arteriolar tone.
Velocity
Distance traveled per unit time (cm/sec); varies inversely with total cross-sectional area.
Laminar flow
Streamlined blood flow in layers, with central blood moving fastest (parabolic profile).
Turbulent flow
Disrupted, crosswise flow — audible as a bruit, palpable as a thrill; promotes thrombus formation.
Law of Laplace
Wall tension = distending pressure × vessel radius; explains why large-radius, high-pressure vessels (aneurysms) are prone to rupture.
Critical closing pressure
The pressure (~20 mmHg) below which a vessel can no longer be held open and flow ceases.
Vasomotor tone
The continuous baseline level of vascular smooth muscle contraction from basal vasomotor center discharge.
Autoregulation
An organ/tissue's intrinsic ability to maintain relatively constant blood flow despite changes in arterial pressure.
Nitric oxide (NO)
The primary endothelium-derived vasodilating gas; deficiency implicated in hypertension and heart disease.
Active (functional) hyperemia
Increased local blood flow in response to increased tissue metabolic demand.
Reactive hyperemia
The flushing/increased flow seen after a temporary flow reduction is reversed (e.g., after a tourniquet is removed).
Thrombus
A stationary blood clot formed within a vessel or heart chamber.
Embolus
Material (often a dislodged thrombus) that travels through the bloodstream and lodges elsewhere, obstructing flow.
Vasospasm
Sudden arterial smooth muscle constriction causing flow obstruction.
Vasculitis / phlebitis / arteritis
Inflammation of an artery's intima / a vein's lining / an autoimmune arterial inflammatory process, respectively.
Compartment syndrome
Increased pressure within a fascial compartment compromising circulation — from swelling, bleeding, or external compression.
Arteriosclerosis
General term for arterial thickening/hardening; encompasses Mönckeberg sclerosis, arteriolar sclerosis, and atherosclerosis.
Atherosclerosis
Plaque buildup (lipid, smooth muscle, inflammatory debris) in the intima of large/medium arteries — the dominant, most clinically significant arteriosclerotic process.
Aneurysm
A localized dilation of an arterial wall — a weakened area at risk of rupture.
Arteriovenous fistula (AVF)
An abnormal direct communication between an artery and a vein.
Valvular incompetence
Failure of venous valves to close properly, allowing backward blood flow.
Varicose veins
Superficial vein valvular incompetence causing dilated, tortuous, visible veins.
Chronic venous insufficiency
Valvular incompetence involving the deep veins of the legs.
Deep vein thrombosis (DVT)
A thrombus in a deep vein, most often the legs/pelvis — the most frequent source of pulmonary emboli.
Lymphedema
Lymphatic fluid accumulation in the interstitium from obstructed/altered lymph flow — primary (congenital) or secondary (acquired/iatrogenic).
Pressure, Resistance & Poiseuille's Law

The basic principle

Blood flows from high pressure (arterial/arteriolar walls) to low pressure (venous system) — the greater the pressure difference, the greater the flow rate. Resistance opposes this flow; flow and resistance are inversely related.

Poiseuille's law — three determinants of resistance

Vessel length, vessel radius, and blood viscosity. Resistance changes directly with length; inversely with the 4th power of the radius; and directly with viscosity.

Mind Map — Radius Change Has an Exponential Effect

Length vs. radius vs. viscosity — which matters most clinically

Vessels can't change their own length, but they CAN change diameter substantially — this makes diameter change the single most important determinant of resistance in real physiology (disease processes and drug therapies act mainly here).

Viscosity — the hematocrit connection

↑ RBCs (polycythemia) or ↓ plasma (dehydration) → ↑ hematocrit → ↑ viscosity → ↑ resistance → slower flow. Directly links back to Chapter 13.

Ohm's law application

Q (flow) = P (pressure difference) / R (resistance). Arterioles are the major site of resistance in the vascular system.

SVR — clinical stakes both directions

↑ SVR (e.g., hypertension) → heart must work harder to eject blood → ↑ myocardial O2/nutrient demand. ↓ SVR pathologically → blood distributed over too large an area, flow slows dramatically → organs (kidney, brain) may not get adequate flow — this is exactly what happens in distributive shock.

Velocity, Laminar & Turbulent Flow

Velocity vs. flow — not the same thing

Velocity varies inversely with total cross-sectional area. Aorta/vena cava: small total cross-sectional area → fastest velocity. Capillary beds: largest total cross-sectional area (from massive branching) → slowest velocity — which is exactly what allows time for gas/nutrient exchange.

Laminar flow — the parabolic profile

Blood flows in layers; central blood moves fastest (only in contact with other blood), outer layer moves slowest (friction against vessel intima, cells stick here).

Turbulent flow

Crosswise flow disrupting the normal forward current. Increases with ↑ velocity and ↑ vessel radius — predictable at the aortic root and major artery branch points. Clinically: audible as a bruit, palpable as a thrill.

Why turbulence matters pathologically

Turbulence slows velocity right around its source, causing blood components to adhere to each other and to the vessel wall — directly promoting thrombus formation. This is the mechanistic link between turbulent flow (from branching, obstruction, or a roughened intimal surface) and clot formation.

Wall Tension & the Law of Laplace

The law of Laplace

Distending pressure (P) = wall tension (T) ÷ radius (r) — rearranged, T = P × r. Increasing EITHER radius or distending pressure increases wall tension.

Why this explains aneurysm rupture risk

High wall tension can rupture a vessel. This is a particular danger when high blood pressure AND a large vessel radius coexist — exactly the situation in an arterial aneurysm, where both factors driving tension upward are present simultaneously.

Critical closing pressure

As distending pressure falls, wall tension forces exceed it, radius decreases, resistance rises, flow declines — at ~20 mmHg, the vessel can no longer be held open and flow stops entirely.

Wall tension scales with vessel size

Wall tension drops dramatically from ~170,000 dynes/cm in the aorta to just 16 dynes/cm in capillaries, then rises to ~21,000 dynes/cm in the vena cava — smaller-radius vessels need far less wall tension to balance the same distending pressure.

Central Control Mechanisms

The vasomotor center

Located in the medulla; a basal discharge rate produces continuous baseline vascular smooth muscle contraction (vasomotor tone). Responds to both excitatory and inhibitory afferent stimuli.

SNS dominates — parasympathetic barely matters here

All vessels except small venules/capillaries have adrenergic SNS innervation. Parasympathetic innervation matters for the heart, but NOT for peripheral resistance regulation.

ReceptorEffect when stimulatedClinical relevance
α1 (arterial smooth muscle)VasoconstrictionNorepinephrine's main peripheral effect; α1 agonists ↑ BP, α1 antagonists ↓ BP
β2 (skeletal muscle vessels)VasodilationMainly responds to epinephrine (adrenal medulla), not SNS norepinephrine — boosts O2/nutrient delivery to muscle under demand, not baseline tone

Why arteries respond more to SNS than veins

Arteries have the most smooth muscle → most affected by SNS stimulation. Veins have little neural innervation — venoconstriction plays only a minor role except in skin and the splanchnic (gut) circulation.

Venous & thoracic pumps

Venous pump: skeletal muscle contraction compresses veins, forcing valves open and propelling blood toward the heart — lost with bed rest/immobilization, reducing right heart preload and increasing cardiac workload. Thoracic pump: inspiration increases negative intrathoracic pressure, boosting venous return.

Intrinsic Control (Autoregulation)

What autoregulation means

An organ's own ability to maintain relatively constant blood flow despite changes in arterial pressure — has limits, and the range varies slightly by organ.

The stretch/myogenic contribution

As arterial pressure rises, vessel walls stretch → vascular smooth muscle contracts (stretched muscle contracts) → vasoconstriction; precapillary sphincters close early too, raising resistance. A real contributor, but NOT the primary autoregulatory mechanism.

Mind Map — The Endothelium's Vasodilating vs. Vasoconstricting Signals

Nitric oxide — the star player

A gas produced by vascular endothelial cells; diffuses to smooth muscle, binds intracellular receptors, alters cytoplasmic Ca2+ → vasodilation. Almost all vasodilation is NO-mediated. NO deficits/decreased responsiveness are a major research focus in hypertension and heart disease.

Metabolite-driven local control

CO2, lactic acid, histamine, prostaglandins directly alter flow to an area. Acetylcholine, bradykinin, histamine, substance P work partly by boosting NO formation.

Hyperemia — two types

Active/functional: ↑ local flow matching ↑ metabolic demand. Reactive: flow surges after a temporary reduction is reversed (classic tourniquet-removal flushing).

Exercise effects

Physical activity enhances vascular relaxation and NO release, improving vascular remodeling in hypertension — plus favorable changes in prostacyclin, thromboxane, endothelin, and several microRNAs.

Control of Lymphatic Flow

Lymphatic pumps

Lymphatic vessels have their own valves (like veins) preventing backflow. Lymph is propelled by: contraction when stretched (the primary factor — contraction rate increases as lymph volume increases), compression by adjacent skeletal/smooth muscle, arterial pulsation, and intrathoracic pressure changes from breathing.

What enhances lymphatic flow

Increased physical activity, increased blood pressure, increased respiratory rate — all mechanically boost lymph movement, similar to how they boost venous return.

Thrombus

What distinguishes thrombosis from normal clotting

Thrombosis = pathologic clot formation (vs. the homeostatic clotting process from Chapter 14). Composed of aggregated platelets, clotting factors, and fibrin adhering to vessel walls. Can form in heart chambers (abnormal rhythms, post-MI, valve damage/replacement) or peripheral vessels.

What promotes thrombosis

Hypercoagulable states, certain drugs (oral contraceptives), dramatically slowed or turbulent flow, and damaged/roughened intimal walls.

Arterial thrombosis

Reduces distal flow → ischemia (acute arterial occlusion, MI, stroke). Manifests as intermittent claudication (pain with activity, relieved by rest), a cool/cyanotic limb, and — as a late sign — a painful arterial ulcer near a toe.

Venous thrombosis

Impairs venous return → edema. Thrombophlebitis = vein inflammation (phlebitis) + thrombus together — most commonly from IV catheter/needle-related inflammation. Signs: calf/groin tenderness, swelling, increased skin temperature; Homan sign (calf pain with foot dorsiflexion) present in only ~10% of thrombophlebitis cases.

Risk factors common to both arterial and venous thrombosis: hypercoagulable conditions, polycythemia, dehydration, platelet aggregation, pump failure (heart failure, shock, dysrhythmias), aging, trauma/surgery, anesthetic agents, oral contraceptives, tobacco. Arterial-specific: arteriosclerosis/atherosclerosis. Venous-specific: immobilization/sedentary lifestyle.
Treatment principle: anticoagulants PREVENT thrombus enlargement/new thrombi — they do NOT dissolve existing clots. Existing clots resolve via the body's own fibrinolysis, surgical removal, or IV thrombolytics. Prophylaxis: antiembolic stockings, sequential compression devices, early ambulation.
Embolus

Most common type

Thromboembolus — a dislodged thrombus (from vessel intima, heart valve leaflets, or formed within a heart chamber). Left-heart thromboemboli → typically lodge in a cerebral artery → stroke. Most thromboemboli actually originate in deep pelvic/leg veins → travel to the right heart → lodge in pulmonary arteries → pulmonary embolism (PE).

PE presentation is nonspecific

May be asymptomatic, or present with sudden dyspnea, ↑ respiratory rate, chest pain — can be a cause of sudden death. This nonspecificity is a real diagnostic challenge, especially in pregnancy (dyspnea occurs in up to 70% of normal pregnancies too).

Embolus typeSource / mechanism
Fat embolusBone trauma/surgery (long bones especially) — inflammatory pressure forces marrow fat into the bloodstream
Tumor embolusMalignant cells breaking off and traveling via blood
Bacterial embolusInfected material breaking free (e.g., endocarditis valve leaflets) — seeds a new infection site
Air embolusAir entering via IV catheter; obstructs small vessels; the volume needed to cause harm is inconsistent/unpredictable
Amniotic fluid embolusLabor/delivery abdominal pressure forces amniotic fluid into circulation; doesn't obstruct flow directly — its proteins/cells act as antigens, triggering an immune response instead

Treatment

Embolectomy (surgical removal) for accessible thromboemboli. For recurrent emboli from the venous system: a surgically implanted vena cava filter (e.g., Greenfield filter) traps emboli, which the body's own plasmin then destroys in place.

Vasospasm, Inflammation & Mechanical Compression

Vasospasm

Sudden arterial smooth muscle constriction; cause is often unknown. Examples: variant (Prinzmetal) angina, cerebral vasospasm after hemorrhagic stroke, migraine (cerebral vasospasm followed by vasodilation). Can be triggered by hormonal sensitivity, food additives, cold exposure, or emotional stress.

Inflammation — three related terms

Vasculitis: intimal artery inflammation. Phlebitis: venous lining inflammation. Arteritis (angiitis): specifically autoimmune arterial inflammation, often triggered by viral/bacterial (especially streptococcal) infection or drug reactions (sulfonamides, phenothiazines). All three can serve as a focus for thrombosis.

Compartment syndrome: external compression (trauma, tight cast/dressing) or internal swelling (bleeding, edema within a fascial compartment) compromises circulation. Neurovascular changes are assessable BEFORE ischemia becomes irreversible — this window is the whole reason for frequent neurovascular checks. Untreated: prolonged hypoxia, ischemia, tissue necrosis.
Types of Structural Alterations

Four categories

Valvular incompetence (venous valve damage — varicose veins if superficial, chronic venous insufficiency if deep). Arteriosclerosis/atherosclerosis (arterial wall thickening/plaque). Aneurysms (localized arterial dilation, rupture risk). AVFs (abnormal artery-vein connection, usually congenital).

Three types of arteriosclerosis

Mönckeberg sclerosis: noninflammatory, medial calcification of small/medium arteries — vessel stays patent (intima uninvolved) but stiff. Arteriolar sclerosis: small artery thickening/narrowing with hypertension. Atherosclerosis: the dominant, most clinically significant type — affects intermediate/large arteries.

Atherosclerosis

Scale & significance

The leading cause of death in the US/western Europe, rising in developing nations. Most mortality: coronary artery occlusion (CAD) → MI. Remainder: thrombotic/hemorrhagic events, primarily brain (stroke) and extremities.

Where it strikes

Large/medium arteries: coronary, cerebral, carotid, femoral, and the aorta. Peripheral involvement (usually lower extremities) = peripheral arterial disease (PAD) — affects 10–15% of the population, ~50% asymptomatic (underdiagnosed/undertreated), yet symptomatic PAD carries a WORSE prognosis than CAD or cerebrovascular disease alone.

Mind Map — Atherosclerosis Pathogenesis Cascade

The full sequence, worth memorizing

(1) Endothelial injury → inflammatory response, ↑ permeability. (2) LDL breaches the intima. (3) Leukocytes drawn in, join endothelial cells oxidizing lipids → further damage. (4) Platelets aggregate/activate → release platelet-derived growth factor. (5) Smooth muscle cells migrate to intima, proliferate. (6) Result: atherosclerotic plaque (smooth muscle + lipoproteins + inflammatory debris).

Two ways plaque causes trouble

Slow: plaque gradually enlarges, narrows the arterial orifice, reduces perfusion over time. Acute: plaque can rupture, triggering thrombus formation and sudden, complete loss of perfusion (the mechanism behind most heart attacks).

Atherosclerosis Risk Factors

Modifiable

Smoking, elevated BP, glucose intolerance, elevated cholesterol/LDL, decreased physical activity, obesity, weight fluctuations, ineffective stress management.

Nonmodifiable

Age, sex, ethnicity, heredity.

Smoking mechanism (worth knowing in full): injures endothelium directly, generates superoxide anions, ↓ NO production/bioavailability, ↑ endothelin. Nicotine also raises LDL/triglycerides and lowers HDL, causes vasospasm and ↑ platelet aggregation, and triggers catecholamine release (↑ BP/HR → ↑ myocardial O2 demand). Net effect: 2–4x coronary heart disease risk — and this risk is synergistic (multiplied, not just added) with hypertension, hypercholesterolemia, or diabetes. Good news: quitting cuts risk 50% within the first year, and risk equals a nonsmoker's by 10 years.
The Framingham Study (started 1948, still ongoing across generations) is the source of most of what's known about atherosclerotic risk factors. LDL receptors on artery muscle cells absorb cholesterol for cellular use — excess unremoved cholesterol becomes a cellular irritant that drives endothelial injury and plaque formation.
Valvular Incompetence & Varicose Veins

Mechanism

Veins are low-pressure by design — flow depends on skeletal muscle/intraabdominal/intrathoracic pumps, not vessel-wall contraction. Prolonged elevated pressure against these pumps overstretches the vein walls until valve cusps can no longer meet → backflow → further engorgement.

Who's at risk

Prolonged standing occupations, obesity, pregnancy (both raise venous pressure). Affects up to 10% of the North American/European population.

Varicose veins specifically

Superficial vein involvement (greater saphenous vein primarily). Superficial, darkened, raised, tortuous — best seen standing (gravity increases visible pressure/distention). Aching/heavy discomfort; venous ulcers possible.

Treatment

Conservative: leg elevation, avoid prolonged standing, elastic stockings, avoid crossing knees/ankles, exercise, weight reduction. Aggressive: sclerotherapy (chemical injection + compression → vein obliterates, collateral circulation compensates), vein stripping/ligation.

Chronic Venous Insufficiency & Deep Vein Thrombosis

Chronic venous insufficiency

Deep vein valvular incompetence (superficial femoral, anterior/posterior tibial, peroneal veins). Communicating/perforating veins let elevated superficial pressure eventually damage the deep system too. Risk factors: heart disease, prior extremity trauma/phlebitis, prior DVT.

Venous stasis ulcers

Superficial veins rupture under activity-driven pressure → RBCs leak, break down → brown skin pigmentation. Ulcers located at the medial malleolus, pink-red base, irregular edges, moderately painful (contrast with the arterial ulcer table above).

CVI diagnosis & treatment

Primarily clinical; ultrasound is best objective method if needed. Treatment centers on compression. Ulcers are prone to secondary infection (S. aureus, P. aeruginosa, β-hemolytic strep) requiring systemic antibiotics.

Deep vein thrombosis

Most often lower extremities (upper extremity DVT less common). May be asymptomatic; if symptomatic: edema, local inflammation signs, dilated superficial veins, pain from nerve pressure/inflammation.

DVT is treated aggressively because lower extremity/pelvic DVT is the most frequent source of pulmonary emboli. Hospitalization for IV anticoagulation is common; outpatient oral anticoagulation is an alternative. Patients with a prior DVT face ongoing hypercoagulation risk and may need long-term prophylactic antiplatelet/anticoagulant therapy, including during any future hospitalization for unrelated reasons.
Lymphedema

Primary vs. secondary

Primary: congenital anomaly/dysfunction of the lymphatic system. Secondary: acquired from disease or iatrogenic causes. Worldwide, the leading secondary cause is infection by the filarial worm Wuchereria bancrofti (affects >90 million people globally). In the US, the leading cause is surgical lymph node removal or radiation damage during cancer treatment.

Cancer-related lymphedema — the numbers

Upper extremity: 15–28% of breast cancer survivors, especially after axillary node dissection; can appear days to 6–8 weeks post-treatment. Lower extremity: up to 80% after groin/pelvic lymph node dissection. Also seen with gynecologic cancer (20%), melanoma (16%), GU cancer (10%), head/neck cancer (4%) — can occur even after minimally invasive procedures like sentinel node biopsy.

Pathophysiology & progression

Lymph fluid collects in the interstitium → inflammatory response → subcutaneous adipose hypertrophy → fibrotic changes. Starts as soft, pitting edema; untreated, progresses to brawny edema (thick, roughened skin) and permanent limb deformity.

Diagnosis & treatment

Diagnosis of exclusion (rule out cardiac/renal/PVD causes first); lymphoscintigram is the primary functional test. No cure exists. Decongestive lymphatic therapy (skin care + massage + compression + exercise) achieves 40–60% edema reduction for many patients. No FDA-approved medication exists; diuretics are NOT recommended. Surgery (debulking, lymphatic-venous bypass, liposuction) is reserved for very select cases since it can be harmful.
