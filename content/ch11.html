Key Terms & Definitions

Start here
Leukemia
A "circulating tumor" disseminated from the start, primarily involving blood and bone marrow.
Lymphoma
A malignancy that localizes in lymph tissue, though often disseminated by diagnosis.
Multiple myeloma
Malignant transformation of B-lymphocyte plasma cells, with a predilection for forming bone tumors.
Lymphadenopathy
Enlarged, nontender lymph nodes — a common finding in lymphoma and some leukemias.
Myeloproliferative disease
Overproduction of one or more functional, normal-appearing blood cell types from a multipotent stem cell (e.g., CML, PV, ET).
Myelodysplastic syndrome (MDS)
Neoplastic cells that are morphologically/functionally abnormal (unlike myeloproliferative disease) — poor prognosis.
Philadelphia chromosome (Ph+)
The balanced 9;22 translocation forming the BCR/ABL fusion gene — characteristic of CML.
BCR/ABL
The fusion gene/oncoprotein from the Philadelphia chromosome; a functional enzyme driving proliferation and blocking apoptosis.
Tyrosine kinase inhibitor (TKI)
Drug class binding the BCR/ABL domain, dramatically improving CML survival.
Blast
An immature precursor cell; blast percentage in marrow/blood defines leukemia phase/type (e.g., ≥20% blasts = AML).
Acute promyelocytic leukemia (APL)
An AML subtype with t(15;17)/PML-RARα fusion; most curable AML subtype, especially with ATRA.
ATRA (all-trans-retinoic acid)
A drug that releases PML-RARα's inhibitory block on myeloid differentiation — used specifically in APL.
CLL/SLL
Chronic lymphocytic leukemia / small lymphocytic lymphoma — the same disease process, distinguished only by location (blood/marrow vs. nodes/spleen).
Richter transformation
Malignant transformation of CLL/SLL into a more aggressive B-cell lymphoma — poor prognosis.
Lymphoblast
An immature lymphoid precursor cell characteristic of ALL.
Bence Jones protein
Light-chain antibody fragments found in urine in multiple myeloma; can cause kidney damage.
Reed-Sternberg cell
The malignant B-cell-derived diagnostic cell of Hodgkin lymphoma — often a minority of the actual tumor cellularity.
Ann Arbor / Lugano staging
The staging system for Hodgkin lymphoma based on node location/number and presence of systemic ("B") symptoms.
Leukemia, Lymphoma & Myeloma — The Conceptual Distinction

Mind Map — Three Related but Distinct Presentations

Nonspecific warning symptoms

Malaise, weakness, unexplained fever, night sweats, unintentional weight loss, recurrent infections. Lymphadenopathy is common in lymphoma and some leukemias. Often discovered incidentally on routine CBC — a very high WBC count or abnormal cell types should trigger further workup.

The WHO's key conceptual insight

Leukemias and lymphomas are NOT separate disorders — they represent different stages/presentations of the same underlying biologic disease, classified primarily by cell type rather than body location.

Earlier detection is generally associated with better prognosis/cure across these malignancies — the same principle as solid tumor cancers.
Classification Systems

Two lineages

Myeloid lineage: RBCs, platelets, monocytes, granulocytes. Lymphoid lineage: B cells, T cells, NK cells. Modern classification (ICC, WHO 5th edition) is organized by cell lineage/type rather than anatomic location.

Why classification keeps changing

Genetic/molecular characterization (gene fusions, rearrangements, mutations) has made classification increasingly specific and complex — with emphasis on therapeutically/prognostically actionable biomarkers, not just morphology.

"Non-Hodgkin lymphoma" is a still-used clinical term NOT included in the WHO classification — it covers a large, diverse group of B-cell/T-cell/NK-cell lymphomas whose grouping has little actual relevance to prognosis or treatment (it's a catch-all, not a biologically meaningful category).
Myeloproliferative Diseases (Overview)

The three classics

CML = excess granulocytes. Polycythemia vera (PV) = excess RBCs. Essential thrombocythemia (ET) = excess platelets. All arise from a multipotent hematopoietic progenitor.

Key shared feature — functional cells

Unlike MDS/AML, myeloproliferative disease cells are usually functional and morphologically normal — this is why these disorders are often insidious/indolent with vague symptoms (fatigue, rash, itching) and are frequently found incidentally on routine CBC.

Common features: multipotent progenitor involvement, marrow hypercellularity, overproduction of one or more functional cell lines. Associated mutations include BCR/ABL and JAK2 among others. These disorders can transform/progress into more aggressive malignancy over time.
Chronic Myeloid Leukemia (CML)

Epidemiology

~15% of US leukemia cases; median age 57–60; slightly more common in males; rare in childhood.

The Philadelphia chromosome

A balanced translocation between chromosomes 9 and 22 → juxtaposes the BCR and ABL genes → BCR/ABL fusion gene. The fusion protein is a functional enzyme that drives proliferation and reduces apoptosis. CML is unusual among cancers in that a single oncogene can confer the malignant state.

Mind Map — Three Phases of CML

Chronic phase presentation

Usually diagnosed here — relatively asymptomatic; high granulocyte count + splenomegaly; retains normal marrow function. Symptoms if present: fatigue, early satiety, weight loss, diaphoresis, bleeding, bone pain, abdominal discomfort (splenomegaly).

Progression to blast crisis

Behaves like acute leukemia with poorer prognosis; driven by accumulating additional molecular abnormalities (insertions, deletions, point mutations) and loss of clone differentiation. The bone marrow microenvironment contributes to leukemic stem cell persistence and treatment resistance.

Treatment revolution: survival rose from 22% (1970) to as high as 90% with anti-BCR/ABL tyrosine kinase inhibitor (TKI) therapy — TKIs bind the BCR/ABL domain directly, driving leukemic cell counts to zero or near-zero. Some patients in deep, sustained molecular remission (>3 years) can be carefully taken off TKI therapy, though relapse can occur (thought to be from residual CML stem cells TKIs don't eliminate). 5–10% don't respond to first-gen TKIs; 20–30% develop resistance over time (from additional BCR/ABL mutations) — 2nd/3rd generation TKIs target these. Only known curative treatment: allogeneic hematopoietic stem cell transplant.
Acute Myeloid Leukemia (AML)

Epidemiology

Primarily a disease of adults (80% of adult acute leukemia, only 15–20% of childhood acute leukemia). Average age 64–71; incidence rising as population ages.

Diagnostic threshold

Bone marrow aspirate must show >20% blasts to diagnose AML. Myeloblastic cells: large, nonsegmented nucleus, fine chromatin.

Genetic complexity

AML has both driver and passenger mutations — one of the first cancers sequenced at the whole-genome level. Molecular profiling (cytogenetics + molecular testing) is now standard for risk stratification and treatment guidance. TP53 mutations = poorer prognosis.

AML vs. ALL — hard to distinguish clinically

Both present with bone pain, anemia, thrombocytopenia, and infection susceptibility (skin, GU, GI, respiratory sites). Abrupt onset, care sought within weeks. Overall 5-year survival: 28% (adults), 60–70% (children). Remission rates: 80–85% (children) vs. 40–80% (adults, depends on disease characteristics).

Acute promyelocytic leukemia (APL) — the special exception: only 10–15% of AML cases, but the MOST curable AML subtype (80–90% 5-year disease-free survival). Caused by t(15;17) → PML/RARα fusion gene, which binds a nuclear repressor complex and blocks myeloid differentiation. ATRA (all-trans-retinoic acid) releases RARα's inhibitory hold on differentiation — adding it to chemotherapy dramatically improves disease-free survival, potentially curative.

Two-phase treatment

Remission induction: goal = complete remission (CR), using cytotoxic chemo + targeted therapy/monoclonal antibodies as appropriate. Consolidation/postremission: eliminate any undetected residual leukemic cells. Patients completing only 1–2 chemo cycles due to toxicity almost always relapse even if CR was achieved.

Targeted mutations & transplant

FLT3 and IDH1/IDH2 mutations have specific targeted drugs. Stem cell transplant may be used at first CR (based on age/comorbidities/cytogenetic risk) or reserved for relapse.

Chronic Lymphocytic Leukemia (CLL/SLL)

Epidemiology & behavior

Most common adult leukemia in the Western world; average age 70; rare in Asian/Pacific Islander populations. Malignant B-cell precursor; indolent course, often asymptomatic, found incidentally.

Mechanism

Leukemic B cells don't produce antibodies normally → ↑ infection susceptibility. Marrow infiltration reduces other cell lines (RBCs, platelets). Cells are characterized by defective apoptosis → extended lifespan (derived from mature peripheral B cells).

CLL and SLL are the SAME disease — just different manifestations depending on location. CLL = blood/marrow involvement. SLL = same monoclonal B cells but in lymph nodes/spleen WITHOUT overt marrow/blood involvement.

Prognostic genetics

IGHV mutation (immunoglobulin variable region) = better prognosis, better chemo response. TP53 aberrations = shorter survival, treatment resistance.

Watch-and-wait is often appropriate

Given average age ~70, indolent disease often isn't treated — no survival benefit shown for treating asymptomatic disease, even high-risk features. Patients monitored for blood count changes, growing lymphadenopathy, frequent infections, and "B symptoms" (weight loss, fatigue, night sweats).

Richter transformation: ~5% of CLL/SLL patients transform into a more aggressive B-cell lymphoma, from additional mutations (TP53, CDKN2A, MYC). Signs: sudden dramatic lymph node growth, dramatic weight loss/fevers/night sweats, severe cytopenias. Poor treatment response, poor prognosis.
Only currently curative option: stem cell transplant. Newer targeted B-cell inhibitor and BCL2-inhibitor therapies have extended survival significantly, even in TP53-mutant or relapsed disease.
Acute Lymphoblastic Leukemia (ALL)

Cell origin & diagnostic threshold

85% B-cell origin, remainder T-cell. Lymphoblasts (immature) accumulate. ≥20% leukemic lymphoblasts in marrow required for diagnosis. Can also present as lymphoblastic LYMPHOMA — same disease, treated the same way.

Epidemiology — the pediatric disease

Most common malignancy diagnosed in patients <15 years old. Peak incidence age 2–5; more common in young males.

Presentation

Abrupt onset: bone pain, bruising, fever, infection. Young children may refuse to walk; parents report fatigue, appetite loss, abdominal pain. Spleen/liver/nodes may enlarge. 3% present with CNS signs from leukemic brain infiltration. Mediastinal masses, lymphatic obstruction, testicular infiltration possible.

Prognosis by age/subtype

5-year survival: 89% (children), 61% (adolescents/young adults), 20–40% (older adults). Pre-B-cell type in children: 90% cure rate. Mature B-cell or immature T-cell leukemia: poorer prognosis.

CNS prophylaxis is essential: without specific meningeal-directed therapy, CNS disease develops in ~50% of adult patients. This is why treatment protocols specifically include CNS-targeted treatment, not just systemic chemo.
Multidrug chemo for induction; postremission maintenance (± stem cell transplant) for 2–3 years to prolong remission. Adults generally need more intensive therapy than children to achieve CR.
Hairy Cell Leukemia

Basics

Rare (~2% of adult leukemias), B-cell phenotype, named for hairlike surface projections. Median age ~55, 4:1 male predominance. Cause unknown but associated with herbicide exposure and family history.

Genetics

Virtually all patients have BRAF V600E activating mutations (RAS-RAF-MAPK pathway) → increased proliferation/survival. The same mutation appears in malignant melanoma, non-small cell lung cancer, and colon cancer — a shared oncogenic pathway across very different cancer types.

Excellent treatability despite being incurable by standard means: chemotherapy produces >90% complete remission rates, with remissions commonly lasting >10 years. Targeted BRAF inhibitors and BTK inhibitors (also used in melanoma) are used for relapsed/refractory disease. No cure except allogeneic stem cell transplant.
Multiple Myeloma

What it is

Malignancy of mature, antibody-secreting plasma cells (mature B lymphocytes) — 2nd most frequently diagnosed hematologic malignancy. Predilection for invading bone, forming multiple tumor sites; can also target lymph nodes, liver, spleen, kidneys.

Epidemiology

Exclusively adult, usually >40 years old, median age 69. More common in males; 2x prevalence in African American vs. European ancestry populations. Rising incidence in the developed world.

Monoclonal antibody spike

All malignant plasma cells are a single clone → identical monoclonal antibody produced in excess → detectable as a sharp "spike" on serum protein electrophoresis (vs. the normal broad, polyclonal distribution).

Bence Jones protein

Excess light-chain antibody fragments accumulate in blood and urine (urine form = Bence Jones protein) — helps confirm diagnosis AND directly contributes to pathogenesis, since it can accumulate in the kidneys and cause damage.

Hodgkin Lymphoma

Epidemiology

~30% of malignant lymphoma, ~8500 new US cases/year. Bimodal age distribution: peak 15–30 years, then again after 50. More common in males, who also have worse prognosis. Overall 5-year survival ~89% (all stages treated).

Reed-Sternberg cells

Malignant, B-cell-derived (from germinal centers), but constitute only ~2% of the actual tumor cellularity — most of the mass is reactive inflammatory cells. EBV found in ~40% of transformed Reed-Sternberg cells in developed countries (higher in developing countries) — thought to contribute to pathogenesis.

The defining clinical feature: predictable, orderly spread. Unlike other lymphomas, Hodgkin lymphoma metastasizes along contiguous lymphatic pathways in a step-wise, predictable pattern — only late in disease (after vascular invasion) does widespread hematogenous spread occur. This predictability is exactly why staging (Ann Arbor/Lugano) works so well for guiding treatment.

Two main types

Nodular lymphocyte-predominant (rare, 5% of cases). Classical type (95% of cases, further divided into 4 subtypes by reactive cell composition).

Typical presentation

Painless lymphadenopathy ± fever, night sweats, pruritus, weight loss, malaise. Usually above the diaphragm — cervical nodes most common, then supraclavicular/axillary/mediastinal. Subdiaphragmatic (inguinal nodes most common there) is less typical.

Staging & Treatment

Ann Arbor / Lugano staging

Based on clinical symptoms + affected node locations. "A" = no symptoms. "B" = symptoms present (>10% body weight loss, unexplained fever, night sweats — the classic "B symptoms"). Clinical stage (CS): history/exam/CT/PET. Pathologic stage: invasive procedures (laparotomy, biopsy).

Treatment by extent

Localized tumors → radiation therapy works well (often detected while still localized). Disseminated disease → systemic chemotherapy. Nonbulky stage IA/IIA previously treated with radiation alone, but relapse rates were high — now combined chemo + limited-field radiation is standard. Bulky disease, B symptoms, or stage III/IV require chemo ± radiation.

Early-stage disease with appropriate treatment: >90% 10-year disease-free survival. Relapsed disease: high-dose chemo/radiation + autologous stem cell rescue, OR newer checkpoint inhibitor immunotherapies targeting PD-L1 (same mechanism discussed in Chapter 7 cancer immune evasion).
Long-term survivorship risks — important for younger cured patients: higher risk of second malignancy (AML from chemo, breast cancer from chest radiation), heart disease, thyroid dysfunction, infertility/early menopause, and other metabolic abnormalities. These patients need lifelong careful screening and follow-up as they age — cure isn't the end of the clinical relationship.
