Amenorrhea

Definitions

Primary: failure to begin menses by age 16. Secondary: cessation of established regular menstruation for ≥6 months. Normal (nonpathologic) causes: before menarche, after menopause, during pregnancy/lactation.

Core mechanism

Almost always reflects disrupted hormonal sequencing — anything preventing normal estrogen production OR blocking estrogen's action on the endometrium interrupts menstrual flow. Causes: physical/emotional stress (disrupts gonadotropin production) or ovarian/adrenal/pituitary tumors (excess or deficient hormone production).

Treatment targets the underlying cause — hormonal supplementation to restore normal cycling, or surgery if a neoplasm is responsible.

The shared mechanism at menarche and perimenopause: in BOTH cases, absent/diminished progesterone leaves the endometrium unopposed by estrogen — producing a thick, fragile, extremely vascular lining that bleeds spontaneously and unpredictably because its blood vessels also fail to constrict properly. In adolescents this reflects immature pituitary-ovarian coordination; in perimenopausal women it reflects declining/erratic follicle function — different root causes converging on the identical endometrial mechanism.
Dysmenorrhea

Primary

No identifiable pathology. Sharp suprapubic cramping, radiating to inner thighs/lower sacrum ± nausea/diarrhea/headache. Develops 1-2 years after menarche (once ovulatory cycles establish). Mechanism: progesterone → ↑ prostaglandins from the endometrium → uterine contraction + endometrial capillary ischemia = the cramping pain.

Secondary

Related to underlying pathology (endometriosis, leiomyomas, pelvic adhesions). Duller pain, tends to INCREASE with age — opposite of primary dysmenorrhea's typical pattern.

Treatment: NSAIDs (prostaglandin inhibitors) are first-line for primary. Progestins/high-progestin-low-estrogen contraceptives work by limiting the high-estrogen-without-progesterone state that drives excess prostaglandin production. Secondary dysmenorrhea requires treating the underlying condition (may include diagnostic laparoscopy).

Etiology by age/parity

Infants/nulliparous women: congenital defects in pelvic support integrity. Women who've given birth: ligament trauma during childbirth, especially with multiple deliveries.

Treatment options

Kegel exercises, weight loss, avoiding constipation/coughing for mild cases. Pessary for poor surgical candidates. Hysterectomy (often vaginal approach) is a common definitive treatment, though newer trials support uterus-preserving procedures as an alternative.

Retrodisplacement: detectable in 20-30% of ALL women — often a normal lifelong variant, not necessarily pathologic. Five positions: anteverted, midposition, anteflexed, retroflexed, retroverted. Most women are asymptomatic; when symptomatic — pelvic pain/pressure, dysmenorrhea, dyspareunia (rarely infertility). Pessary or laparoscopic surgery if treatment is needed.
Cystocele & Rectocele

Cystocele

Bladder protrudes into the ANTERIOR vaginal wall. Causes: childbirth injury, surgery, aging, inherent weakness; risk factors include obesity and heavy lifting. Symptoms (moderate-severe): vaginal pressure, dysuria, incontinence, back pain, palpable anterior bulge.

Rectocele

Rectum protrudes into the POSTERIOR vaginal wall. Same general causes as cystocele, plus multiparity and postmenopausal status specifically. Symptoms: difficult bowel evacuation, chronic constipation/laxative dependency, pressure, dyspareunia, posterior bulge.

Treatment for both: mild → Kegels/pessary. Moderate-severe → surgical repair restoring the anterior or posterior vaginal wall support (mesh may reinforce cystocele repair; rectocele repair realigns the levator ani muscles).
Pelvic Inflammatory Disease

Scale & long-term consequences

4.4% lifetime self-reported prevalence in sexually experienced reproductive-age women — >2 million prevalent US cases. Among women with PID history: infertility in 8%, ectopic pregnancy in 9%, chronic pelvic pain in 18% — genuinely significant downstream reproductive consequences from what's often treated as "just an infection."

How the normal defense fails

Cervical mucus is normally bacteriostatic, blocking ascending infection. PID follows anything that alters/destroys this barrier: IUD insertion, pelvic surgery, abortion procedures, peripartum infection. Bacteria can also arrive hematogenously or from a nearby infection focus (pelvic abscess, ruptured appendix, sigmoid diverticulitis).

Organisms

N. gonorrhoeae and C. trachomatis are most common (they penetrate cervical mucus especially well); staphylococci, streptococci, diphtheroids, and coliforms (Pseudomonas, E. coli) also contribute.

Presentation & treatment

Abdominal tenderness, cervical/adnexal pain on palpation, temp >38°C, WBC >10,000/mm³, possible pelvic abscess/mass, purulent discharge. Treatment: early aggressive antibiotics covering BOTH gonorrhea and chlamydia. Ruptured pelvic abscess is life-threatening and may require total abdominal hysterectomy + bilateral salpingo-oophorectomy.

Vulvovaginitis & Bartholinitis

Candida — the dominant cause

~75% of women affected at least once; 40-45% have ≥2 episodes. C. albicans needs glucose — grows more in high-glycogen states: secretory phase of the cycle, diabetes, pregnancy, oral contraceptives, estrogen supplementation. Antibiotic use also promotes it (kills protective glucose-consuming bacteria → Candida overgrowth).

Presentation by cause

Candidiasis: thick white discharge, red/edematous mucosa with adherent white flecks, intense pruritus, NORMAL vaginal pH (<4.5). Other infectious agents (Trichomonas, Haemophilus vaginalis, gonorrhea, HPV, HSV-2): malodorous purulent discharge, red/swollen labia, dysuria, dyspareunia.

Bartholinitis: infection of the Bartholin glands (vaginal orifice lubrication) — usually N. gonorrhoeae or C. trachomatis. Can progress to abscess (Bartholin cyst) with tenderness/swelling/pus; treated with antibiotics ± incision and drainage.
Uterine Leiomyomas

Epidemiology

Highest prevalence age 50-54 (15.9%). NEVER found before puberty, rarely grow after menopause. More common in Black women than White women.

Estrogen-driven mechanism

Not fully understood, but estrogen + human growth hormone appear to stimulate susceptible fibromuscular tissue — supported by growth acceleration with high-dose estrogen and late pregnancy (when both hormones peak), and shrinkage/disappearance after menopause when estrogen falls.

Symptoms scale with size/location: abdominal pain/pressure, abnormal bleeding/discharge, backache, constipation, urinary frequency if large enough to compress nearby organs; can impair fertility and pregnancy maintenance. Small asymptomatic myomas are monitored; large/multiple/symptomatic ones are surgically removed (up to hysterectomy).

Endometriosis

Scale

~10-15% of reproductive-age women; 5-50% of infertile women; up to 5-21% of women hospitalized for pelvic pain. Most active age 30-40, especially in nulliparous women; rare before 20 or after menopause. ~30% infertility rate among those diagnosed.

Three etiology theories

Transportation: retrograde menstrual flow through the oviducts implants tissue elsewhere. Metaplasia: inflammation/hormonal change converts coelomic epithelium directly into endometrial-type tissue. Induction (currently favored): combination — regurgitated endometrium chemically induces mesenchyme to become endometrial tissue.

Common sites

Ovary, cul-de-sac/pouch of Douglas peritoneum, uterosacral/round ligaments, oviduct, uterine peritoneal surface; less often bladder or large intestine. Despite being benign, endometriosis shares malignant-like traits: it can grow, infiltrate, and spread.

The bleeding-inflammation cycle

Implants remain hormonally responsive — proliferate and bleed cyclically just like normal endometrium. When they rupture (often just before/after menses), brown blood debris spills onto the peritoneum → chemical peritonitis → fibrous tissue formation → dense pelvic adhesions from repeated cyclic irritation.

Distinguishing endometriosis pain from primary dysmenorrhea: endometriosis pain begins 5-7 days BEFORE peak menses and lasts 2-3 days, and is more diffuse (lower abdomen, vagina, posterior pelvis, back) — compared to primary dysmenorrhea's more cramp-like, abdominal-midline-concentrated pain. Severity of pain does NOT correlate with disease extent.
Treatment: NSAIDs + hormonal agents (progestins, androgenic agents, GnRH) to interrupt the cyclic hormonal stimulation driving implant activity → atrophy. Surgical removal/destruction of implants for more severe cases; total hysterectomy + oophorectomy for widespread rapidly progressing disease.
Cervical Cancer

Scale & screening

~11,500 new US cases, ~4,200 deaths (2020). Nearly ALL caused by persistent HPV infection; other factors: early-age intercourse, multiple partners, multiple pregnancies. Squamous cell carcinoma = 95% of invasive cases.

Updated screening guidance

2020 ACS update: begin screening at age 25 with HPV testing alone, repeated every 5 years through age 65 (superseding the older 21-start/Pap-every-3-years approach, though that's still considered acceptable). Stop after hysterectomy unrelated to cervical neoplasia, or after age 65 with a normal 10-year screening history.

Preinvasive changes can be present for up to 10 years before invasive cancer develops — and are entirely asymptomatic, detectable only by screening. Once symptoms appear (abnormal bleeding, discharge, postcoital pain/bleeding), the cancer has usually already progressed beyond early stages. 5-year survival: 92% localized vs. 17% distant spread — an enormous stage-dependent gap that underscores why screening matters so much here specifically.
HPV vaccination: Gardasil (quadrivalent — types 6, 11, 16, 18) covers 70% of cervical cancers and 90% of genital warts; Cervarix (bivalent — 16, 18); Gardasil 9 (nine-valent, most current). Recommended ages 11-12 (as young as 9), catch-up through 26; shared decision-making for ages 26-45; not licensed beyond 45. Ideally given before sexual activity begins, though sexually active people can still benefit.
Endometrial, Ovarian, Vaginal & Vulvar Cancer

Endometrial cancer

Related factors: infertility, late menopause (>55), obesity, diabetes, hypertension, unopposed estrogen stimulation. Most common initial symptom: intermenstrual or postmenopausal bleeding — a genuinely useful early warning sign. 5-year survival: 96% local vs. 20% distant.

Ovarian cancer — the silent one

Now the LEADING cause of death from genital cancer (surpassing cervical). Peak incidence age 60-80. No symptoms until LATE disease → higher mortality. When symptoms appear: ↑ abdominal girth, weight loss, abdominal pain, dysuria/frequency, constipation — all from intraabdominal metastasis, not early local disease. 5-year survival at distant stage: only 31%. BRCA1/BRCA2 carriers are increasingly offered prophylactic oophorectomy/salpingo-oophorectomy.

Vaginal cancer

Uncommon, typically ≥50 years. Thin-walled vagina + rich lymphatics → metastasis to bladder, rectum, vulva, pubic bone. Symptoms: spotting/discharge, pain, groin masses, urinary changes.

Vulvar cancer

Rare (0.6% of cancers in women); peak mid-60s but can occur at any age including infancy. Risk factors: STIs, chronic vulvar pruritus/swelling/dryness, obesity, hypertension, diabetes, nulliparity. Leukoplakic (whitish plaque/ulcer) changes often PRECEDE carcinoma — an important early warning finding. Local relapse is common regardless of surgical approach.

Pregnancy-Induced Hypertension

Scale & disparity

>70,000 maternal deaths worldwide annually; complicates 10% of ALL pregnancies. US Black women have a 60% HIGHER preeclampsia rate than White women — a significant, well-documented health equity gap. Risk groups: teenagers, women in late 30s/early 40s, multiple fetuses, preexisting hypertension/renal/cardiovascular disease/diabetes.

The paradoxical renal pattern

PIH causes ↓ renal blood flow AND ↓ GFR — the EXACT OPPOSITE of the normal renal changes expected in pregnancy. Mechanism: thickened glomerular tufts with fibrinoid basement membrane deposits. Also: salt/water retention, weight gain, edema, widespread arterial spasm (especially kidneys, brain, liver).

The self-worsening cycle: rising arterial pressure promotes more arterial spasm, which promotes further pressure rise — a vicious cycle. Severe PIH: extreme vascular spasticity, clonic convulsions → coma, renal failure, liver malfunction, extreme hypertension, usually near term. Untreated mortality is high, but rapid vasodilators + seizure prophylaxis + corticosteroids (fetal lung maturation) + rapid delivery have brought mortality down to <1% — a genuine modern treatment success story.
Hyperemesis, Placental Disorders & Spontaneous Abortion

Hyperemesis gravidarum

Occurs in 0.3-2% of pregnancies (vs. ~50% having transient first-trimester nausea) — can be life-threatening (severe dehydration, electrolyte imbalance, hepatic/renal damage, encephalopathy, death if uncontrolled). Suspected mechanism: abnormal response to placental hCG production. Treatment: IV fluids/nutrition correction, antiemetics, hospital supportive care.

Placenta previa vs. abruptio placentae

Previa: placenta over the internal cervical os; ~1 in 200 deliveries; linked to multiple pregnancies/prior C-section. Abruptio: premature separation after 20 weeks; ~1% of deliveries; causes include trauma, short umbilical cord, IVC occlusion, PIH, abnormal uterine anatomy. Both can cause overt/concealed hemorrhage and fetal oxygen compromise — C-section is the typical intervention for either.

Spontaneous abortion ("miscarriage"): estimated 50% of ALL conceptions, but only 10-20% among KNOWN pregnancies (many occur before pregnancy is recognized). ~61% of first-trimester abortuses show chromosomal abnormalities. Other maternal factors: infection (CMV, HSV, rubella), reproductive tract abnormalities, immune/endocrine disorders, physical/psychic trauma. Incomplete abortion (retained products) requires intervention to control bleeding and remove remaining tissue.
Reactive-Inflammatory Breast Disorders

Mammary duct ectasia

Chronic periductal inflammation, mostly in POSTMENOPAUSAL women. Ducts distend with debris → irritation → inflammation → granulation tissue around a debris-filled cavity → palpable central/subareolar mass + reactive fibrosis causing dimpling, distortion, and NIPPLE INVERSION. Critical point: these findings mimic malignancy — biopsy is routinely needed to rule out cancer before diagnosing ectasia.

Breast abscess (nonlactational)

Recurring problem, more common with diabetes, steroid therapy, other skin lesions, smoking. Unlike lactational abscesses (usually S. aureus), nonlactational abscesses usually grow MULTIPLE organisms on culture. Treatment: antibiotics (typically erythromycin) + ultrasound-guided aspiration.

Fat necrosis — the great mimicker

Death of fat tissue after trauma, more common after breast procedures and in larger/pendulous breasts. Presents as a painless, firm, ill-defined, poorly mobile mass with possible skin thickening/retraction — clinically and mammographically it can be indistinguishable from carcinoma, and patients often can't recall the triggering trauma. Excisional biopsy is required when it can't be reliably distinguished otherwise.

Implant reactions

Silicone implants: gel encased in polyurethane/other materials — controversy exists over immune-type reactions; US regulation now favors saline-filled implants for routine cosmetic use. Blunt chest trauma risks silicone rupture/leakage — documenting implant presence matters for trauma care planning.

Benign Breast Disorders

Fibrocystic breast changes — not actually a "disease"

Present to SOME degree in essentially all female breasts — a classification applied to cyclic, hormone-related palpable masses/tenderness, not a distinct pathology. Ages 30-50 most common; tenderness peaks right before menses; cysts are firm, regular, MOBILE, usually upper-outer quadrant, fluctuating with the cycle.

The cancer-risk correction

Older belief that ALL fibrocystic change raises breast cancer risk has been DISPROVEN — only specific tissue change subtypes carry increased risk; the vast majority of women with fibrocystic changes are NOT at meaningfully elevated risk. This is a genuinely important reassurance point for patient counseling.

Treatment: oral contraceptives (lower cycle-related hormone levels), NSAIDs for discomfort, local heat, supportive bra, avoiding methylxanthines (tea, coffee, cola, chocolate — thought to raise cAMP and breast metabolic activity), low-fat/high-carb diet. Specific benign neoplasms (fibroadenomas, adenomas, papillomas) are freely movable, encapsulated, well-demarcated masses that don't invade/metastasize — but still require biopsy to differentiate from carcinoma.
Breast Cancer

Scale

The most common cancer in women aged 25-75; second-leading cause of cancer death in US women. ~297,790 new 2023 US cases, ~43,170 deaths. More common in White women, but incidence is RISING in Black and Asian women. Occurs in males too, but ~100x less commonly.
