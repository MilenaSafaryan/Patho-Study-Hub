Key Terms & Definitions

Start here
Heart failure (HF)
A complex syndrome from structural/functional impairment of ventricular filling or ejection, unable to meet metabolic demand.
Ejection fraction (EF)
Stroke volume ÷ end-diastolic volume; normal is 55–70%.
HFrEF
HF with reduced EF (≤40%) — systolic dysfunction, impaired contractility.
HFpEF
HF with preserved EF (≥50%) with evidence of elevated LV filling pressures — diastolic dysfunction.
HFmrEF
HF with mildly reduced EF (41–49%) plus evidence of elevated filling pressures.
dP/dt
A measure of inotropy — how quickly the ventricle develops a forceful contraction.
Lusitropy
The energy-requiring active process of myocardial relaxation (calcium removal from cytoplasm).
Frank-Starling mechanism
Greater diastolic muscle fiber stretch (preload) produces greater force of contraction, up to a point.
Eccentric hypertrophy
Muscle fiber elongation from excess preload/volume overload.
Concentric hypertrophy
Muscle fiber thickening from high afterload/pressure overload.
Forward failure
Poor cardiac output causing inadequate perfusion of tissues/organs.
Backward failure
Congestion of blood behind the failing pumping chamber.
Cor pulmonale
Right ventricular hypertrophy from pulmonary disease-driven increased pulmonary vascular resistance.
S3 gallop
A low-frequency early-diastolic sound from rapid ventricular filling under increased volume load.
GDMT
Guideline-directed medical therapy — the four pillars for HFrEF: ARNI, MRA, β-blockers, SGLT2 inhibitors.
Dysrhythmia (arrhythmia)
A cardiac rhythm abnormality of impulse generation or conduction.
Automaticity
A dysrhythmia mechanism from abnormal spontaneous depolarization during phase 4 of the action potential.
Early afterdepolarization (EAD)
A triggered impulse during phase 3 repolarization from reopened calcium channels.
Delayed afterdepolarization (DAD)
A triggered impulse after repolarization is complete, from spontaneous SR calcium release.
Reentry
A cardiac impulse that continues to depolarize part of the heart after the main impulse finishes, restimulating recovered tissue — the mechanism behind most tachydysrhythmias.
Escape rhythm
Takeover by a slower, lower pacemaker after SA node failure.
Atrial fibrillation (AF)
Disorganized, irregular atrial rhythm with irregular ventricular response — the most common sustained dysrhythmia.
Classification & Diagnosis

Scale

Incidence: 27/1000 population after age 65. US HF deaths rose from 275,000 (2009) to 457,212 (2022). Direct cause of 1.2 million admissions/year, contributing to 2–3 million more.

Diagnostic criteria (2022 AHA/ACC/HFSA)

Requires evidence of structural change (LV hypertrophy, atrial enlargement, ventricular dilation) PLUS signs of congestion (pulmonary edema on x-ray, elevated LVEDP, elevated PCWP, elevated E/e' ratio) PLUS elevated NT-proBNP or BNP.

Mortality pattern worth knowing: patients with low EF but NO congestive symptoms have survival similar to those with preserved EF WITH symptoms. Highest mortality: low EF + congestive symptoms together. Overall, ~50% of HF patients die within 5 years of diagnosis — this is a genuinely serious diagnosis, not just a chronic nuisance condition.
Common causes: ischemic heart disease/MI, hypertension, and valvular disease are the top three in the US. Other causes: genetic cardiomyopathy, amyloidosis, chemo cardiotoxicity, substance abuse, chronic tachycardia, autoimmune disease, sarcoidosis, peripartum cardiomyopathy, myocarditis, iron overload, thyroid disease.
HFrEF — Systolic Dysfunction

Four contributing mechanisms

(1) Myocyte loss (esp. from MI — pump failure severity tracks with muscle lost); (2) accelerated apoptosis (triggered by excess neurohormone stimulation and ischemia); (3) ATP deficiency (fewer myosin cross-bridge cycles per contraction AND impaired diastolic calcium removal); (4) β1-receptor down-regulation from chronic sympathetic overstimulation.

Why β-blockers help despite being negative inotropes

Chronic β1 overstimulation by norepinephrine paradoxically REDUCES receptor responsiveness over time. β-blockers interrupt this chronic overexcitation — clinical trials show improved EF and reduced mortality despite the seemingly counterintuitive use of a negative inotrope in a low-CO state.

RAAS and cardiac remodeling: RAAS activation is initially adaptive (maintains BP via vasoconstriction) but chronic aldosterone exposure has direct TOXIC effects on the myocardium — fibrosis and decreased contractility. This fibrotic scarring + chamber enlargement is called cardiac remodeling, and GDMT targeting SNS + RAAS pathways can produce measurable REVERSE remodeling.
HFpEF — Diastolic Dysfunction

Two impaired processes

(1) Lusitropy (active) — energy-requiring calcium removal from cytoplasm back into SR and extracellular fluid; impaired by ischemia/ATP deficiency. (2) Passive compliance — ventricular wall stretch to accommodate filling; reduced by fibrin/collagen scarring or hypertrophic wall thickening. Often impossible to distinguish clinically which process dominates.

Who gets HFpEF

More common in females, the elderly, and those WITHOUT a history of MI — despite CHD/hypertension being risk factors for both systolic and diastolic failure, something about individual physiology determines which pathway predominates.

Aging's specific contribution

Impaired G-protein coupling to adenylyl cyclase → ↓ myocardial cAMP response to β-stimulation → declining max HR with age. Impaired SR calcium release/reuptake → prolonged contraction, impaired relaxation. Collagen/amyloid/lipofuscin deposition → hypertrophy + ↓ compliance.

Pressure-volume loop signature

Systolic failure: HIGHER-than-normal diastolic volume, LOW EF. Diastolic failure: poor compliance, LOWER diastolic volume at HIGHER-than-normal pressure. Both show elevated end-diastolic pressure — but the volume side of the picture is opposite.

EF 40–50% doesn't fit cleanly into either category — treatment must be individualized to clinical presentation rather than following a strict EF-based protocol.
Compensatory Mechanisms & Remodeling

Mind Map — Three Compensatory Mechanisms, All Ultimately Harmful

SNS activation

Baroreceptor-triggered: ↑ HR/contractility (though failing heart is less responsive), venoconstriction (↑ preload), arteriolar constriction (maintains BP), and juxtaglomerular cell renin release (starts RAAS). Problem: chronically elevated afterload increases cardiac workload and can further ↓ SV — a self-worsening cycle.

Increased preload — the Frank-Starling trade-off

↓ CO → ↓ renal perfusion → fluid retention + RAAS-driven Na+/water reabsorption → ↑ volume → ↑ fiber stretch → ↑ contraction force (Frank-Starling). But the curve flattens: HF patients often retain SO much volume they're on the flat part of the curve, gaining minimal contractile benefit while worsening congestion — this is exactly why diuretics (preload reduction) help despite lowering "preload," a variable that's normally beneficial.

Myocardial hypertrophy — law of Laplace

Tension = (transmural pressure × radius) ÷ wall thickness. Chronic wall tension elevation (from high preload OR high afterload) drives hypertrophy, which increases pumping force and brings wall tension back toward normal — a genuine compensatory adaptation, just a slow one (takes far longer than SNS/preload changes).

Eccentric vs. concentric — know which cause goes with which

Eccentric (fiber elongation): driven by excess PRELOAD/volume overload. Concentric (fiber thickening): driven by high AFTERLOAD/pressure overload (hypertension, aortic stenosis).

Left-Sided Heart Failure

Forward effects (both sides, same mechanism)

↓ Cerebral perfusion → restlessness, confusion, anxiety, impaired memory. ↓ Renal perfusion → oliguria, RAAS activation, potential kidney injury if severe. General fatigue and activity intolerance.

Left-sided backward effects — pulmonary

↑ Pulmonary venous/capillary hydrostatic pressure → transudative fluid into interstitium/alveoli → pulmonary edema. Cardinal early symptoms: nonproductive cough, dyspnea. Progresses to dyspnea on exertion → orthopnea → paroxysmal nocturnal dyspnea (PND).

Why orthopnea/PND happen specifically when lying down

Lying down redistributes blood volume from periphery to central circulation/heart — the failing LV can't handle the extra volume, worsening pulmonary congestion. Sitting/standing relieves it by letting blood pool peripherally again, reducing pulmonary hydrostatic pressure.

Physical findings

Cough (may become productive, blood-tinged from fragile capillary rupture, frothy from alveolar fluid), crackles/rales (location tracks severity — base only in mild, up to lower half in worse cases), hypoxemia, S3 gallop (positive likelihood ratio 4-11, specificity 95-99% for HF in the right clinical context), elevated LAP.

LAP thresholds: normal 4–12 mmHg. Acute rise to 25 mmHg → pulmonary edema. Patients with CHRONIC LAP elevation are more resistant and may not become symptomatic until pressures approach 40 mmHg — chronic adaptation raises the threshold for acute decompensation. Acute cardiogenic pulmonary edema is life-threatening: severe dyspnea, bolt-upright posture, bubbly crackles base-to-apex, pink frothy sputum, cyanosis — immediate treatment targets fluid volume reduction + oxygenation support.
Right-Sided & Biventricular Failure

Why left failure leads to right failure

LV and RV function in series — LV failure increases RV workload over time, producing biventricular failure (the most common pattern). TRUE isolated RV failure is rare, usually from RV infarction or pulmonary disease (cor pulmonale — RV hypertrophy from ↑ pulmonary vascular resistance).

RV's structural vulnerability

Thin RV musculature compensates well for GRADUAL resistance increases (via preload increase + hypertrophy) but has limited ability to handle ACUTE workload changes (RV infarction, large PE) — a structural asymmetry with the more muscular LV.

Backward effects — systemic venous congestion

Liver: enlarged, hepatocyte atrophy/necrosis from chronic passive congestion; impaired aldosterone metabolism perpetuates fluid retention. Portal system: ↑ pressure → ascites, congestive splenomegaly, GI symptoms (anorexia, discomfort). Kidney: congestion → ↓ GFR → further fluid retention. Peripheral: dependent edema (legs, sacrum if supine). Head/neck: impeded SVC drainage → jugular venous distention, impaired mental function.

Hepatojugular reflux test

Manual liver compression → sudden ↑ venous return to right heart. In right-sided HF, the compromised RV can't accommodate the surge → sudden jugular vein distention. Normally, the extra return would enter the heart unimpeded with no visible distention.

HF Treatment

The four GDMT pillars for HFrEF

ARNI (angiotensin receptor-neprilysin inhibitor), MRA (mineralocorticoid/aldosterone antagonist), β-blockers, and SGLT2 inhibitors. SGLT2i mechanism: blocks glucose cotransporter 2 in the proximal convoluted tubule → glucosuria + osmotic diuresis (↓ plasma volume, ↓ LV wall stress) + ketosis (heart uses ketones for more forceful contraction) — a genuinely multi-mechanism drug class, not just a diabetes drug repurposed.

Preload optimization — a balancing act

Elevated preload helps contraction (Frank-Starling) but worsens congestion and workload. Goal: minimize congestive symptoms WITHOUT compromising CO. Loop diuretics: ↑ renal blood flow, block Na+/Cl- reabsorption. RV is especially preload-sensitive — avoid excessive volume reduction.

Inotropes — why digitalis is the exception

Most positive inotropes (NE, isoproterenol, dobutamine, dopamine) are AVOIDED in chronic HF (↑ mortality) except short-term to avoid shock. Digitalis is the exception: inhibits Na+/K+ pump → ↑ intracellular Na+ → slows Na+-dependent Ca2+ removal → more Ca2+ available → stronger contraction. Also slows HR via parasympathetic activation. Doesn't INCREASE mortality but doesn't decrease it either — used for symptom control/readmission reduction in refractory HF, not as routine first-line therapy.

Digitalis safety window

Very narrow therapeutic window — target level slightly SUBtherapeutic (0.8–1.0 ng/mL) to avoid toxicity. Hypokalemia potentiates digitalis toxicity — an important drug-electrolyte interaction to watch.

Resynchronization therapy: indicated for wide QRS (>120 ms) + EF <35%. Biventricular pacing (atrium + both ventricles) coordinates depolarization, improving congestive symptoms/CO/activity tolerance. Often bundled with an AICD given the elevated sudden-cardiac-death risk in severe LV systolic dysfunction.
Conduction System & ANS Control

Normal pathway

SA node → intraatrial pathways + AV node (biatrial depolarization/contraction) → delay at AV node (allows atrial contraction to complete) → bundle of His → ventricles → Purkinje fibers (ventricular depolarization/contraction).

ANS control — intrinsic vs. extrinsic

Intrinsic cardiac ANS: interconnecting neurons/ganglia in epicardial fat near the atrial/pulmonary vein junction. Extrinsic: afferent (chemoreceptors, baroreceptors → brain) and efferent pathways — sympathetic via ventrolateral medulla → T1-L2 spinal segments; parasympathetic via vagal dorsal motor nucleus → SA node, AV node, ventricular epicardium. The two systems oppose each other to maintain homeostasis.

Dysrhythmias matter for two reasons: (1) they signal an underlying pathophysiologic problem, (2) they can directly impair CO. Three broad categories: abnormal sinus rate, ectopic impulse initiation, or conduction pathway disturbances.
Dysrhythmia Mechanisms

Automaticity

Failure to fully repolarize, or abnormal Na+/Ca2+ leakiness during phase 4, shifts resting potential toward threshold. Ischemia/ATP deficiency impairs electrolyte flux control; hypokalemia specifically contributes to abnormal automaticity.

Triggered activity — two subtypes

EAD (early afterdepolarization): during phase 3 relative refractory period — voltage-gated Ca2+ channels reopen, trigger another impulse (R-on-T pattern on ECG). DAD (delayed afterdepolarization): AFTER repolarization completes — spontaneous SR calcium release triggers Ca2+ influx → action potential if it reaches threshold. Digitalis toxicity and excess catecholamines are classic DAD triggers.

Mind Map — Reentry Mechanism

Reentry — the mechanism behind most tachydysrhythmias

Requires either abnormally SLOWED conduction (functional) or an unusually LONG pathway (anatomic). Gap junction density/structure (connexins) affects conduction rate between regions — genetic connexin mutations can predispose to dysrhythmia. Can form complex spiral "wavelets."

What predisposes to reentry

Myocardial ischemia, excessive catecholamines, electrolyte abnormalities — the same broad triggers that show up across most dysrhythmia mechanisms, reinforcing that ischemia/electrolytes/catecholamines are the recurring "usual suspects" throughout this whole topic.

ECG Basics & Normal Sinus Rhythm

ECG paper mechanics

Small box: 0.1 mV amplitude, 0.04 sec duration (at standard 25 mm/s paper speed). Large box (5 small boxes): 0.5 mV, 0.2 sec. These grid markings are how amplitude, duration, and heart rate are all measured directly off the strip.

Normal sinus rhythm criteria

Rate 60–100/min, originates in the SA node, follows the normal conduction pathway. On the strip: regular rhythm, one P wave per QRS complex, normal PR/QRS/QT intervals, no abnormal-looking beats.

Escape rhythms — the key identifying clue

Absent normal P waves/PR intervals. If a P wave IS present, it may be inverted and located before/during/after the QRS (retrograde conduction back to the atria). Usually poorly tolerated (slow, ↓ CO) — sinus node failure is managed with a pacemaker.

Atrial flutter subtypes

Typical: involves the cavotricuspid isthmus, may be congenital. Atypical: often acquired (prior surgery, lung disease). QRS is normal; ventricular rate depends on AV conduction ratio (2:1, 3:1 = regular; variable block = irregular).

Atrial Fibrillation

Origin & mechanism

95% of paroxysms originate near the pulmonary vein junction (highly electrically active "muscular sleeves" enabling rapid focal firing/reentry); remaining 5% from venae cavae, left atrial appendage, coronary sinus, or posterior LA wall. Chronic paroxysms → cellular inflammation → atrial fat thickening → fibrosis/remodeling → self-perpetuating substrate for sustained AF.

Two pathophysiologic roots

(1) Structural atrial changes (enlargement) — from uncontrolled HTN, post-MI, obstructive sleep apnea. (2) Cardiac ANS dysregulation.

Classification by duration

Paroxysmal: self-terminates <7 days. Persistent: 7 days–12 months. Long-standing persistent (chronic): >12 months. Also classified by ventricular rate (controlled <100 bpm vs. uncontrolled >100) and by valvular involvement (VAF vs. NVAF — affects anticoagulation choice).

Stroke risk — age-dependent, and dramatically so

Embolic stroke/TIA relative risk: 1.5% (age 50–59) → 23.5% (age 80–89). AF causes atrial "quivering" instead of contraction → blood stasis (especially left atrial appendage) → thrombus formation → embolization risk. Anticoagulation ranges from aspirin to full anticoagulation (warfarin, direct Xa inhibitors, direct thrombin inhibitors) based on risk stratification tools.

The "atrial kick" and HF interaction: normal atrial contraction adds 15–20% extra ventricular filling before systole. Losing this in AF can cause a 20–30% drop in CO — which is why HF patients often feel dramatically worse specifically when they go into AF, not just "a little worse." Treatment: cardioversion, or rate/rhythm control with calcium channel blockers, β-blockers, digitalis, or amiodarone.
Junctional & Ventricular Dysrhythmias

Junctional dysrhythmias

Originate proximal or distal to the AV node. P wave may precede, follow, or be buried in the QRS. Junctional tachycardia: 70–140/min — often indistinguishable from atrial tachycardia on ECG, so both may be called "supraventricular tachycardia."

Premature ventricular complexes (PVCs)

Depolarize ventricles WITHOUT activating atria/SA node — sinus rhythm continues undisturbed underneath. Classic identifying feature: compensatory pause (interval between the sinus beats bracketing the PVC = exactly 2x normal). QRS >0.10 sec, bizarre morphology, T wave opposite the main QRS deflection. Associated with CAD, drug overdose, hypokalemia, hypomagnesemia.

PVC patterns & significance

Bigeminy (every other beat), trigeminy (every third beat). High frequency can compromise CO. ≥3 consecutive PVCs at >100 bpm = ventricular tachycardia. Prophylactic antidysrhythmic use in ASYMPTOMATIC patients is NOT recommended — some agents actually increase mortality post-MI.

Ventricular tachycardia

Diverse mechanisms depending on underlying structural disease/channelopathy. Reentry is most common (unidirectional conduction around a scar → reexcitation), followed by triggered activity.

Dysrhythmia Treatment

The prodysrhythmic paradox

Most antidysrhythmic drugs are effective AND can themselves cause dysrhythmias — they work by altering ion movement across cardiac membranes, affecting automaticity and depolarization/repolarization timing. This dual-edged nature is why dysrhythmias are generally only treated when symptomatic or expected to progress.

Drug class logic (simplified)

Na+ channel blockers: depress conduction/excitability (Na+-dependent reentry). K+ channel blockers (amiodarone, sotalol): prolong refractory period. Ca2+ channel blockers: depress conduction (Ca2+-dependent reentry, e.g., AV nodal reentrant tachycardia). β-blockers: reduce phase 4 depolarization (automaticity-based dysrhythmias).

Ablation: an electrophysiologic study evokes/analyzes the dysrhythmia via intracardiac catheter electrodes, then interrupts (ablates, via radiofrequency or surgery) the generating focus. Also useful for assessing drug response and identifying sudden-cardiac-arrest risk. High-risk patients may receive an implantable defibrillator that detects lethal rhythms and delivers a corrective shock. Severely reduced CO (severe bradycardia, asystole, VT, VF) requires CPR until an effective rhythm is restored.
