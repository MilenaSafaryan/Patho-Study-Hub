Key Terms & Definitions

Start here
Reversible cell injury
Injury mild/short-lived enough that the cell returns to normal once the stress is removed.
Hydropic swelling (oncosis)
Cellular swelling from water accumulation due to Na+-K+ pump failure — the first manifestation of most reversible injury.
Atrophy
Decreased cell size/function in response to disuse, denervation, ischemia, starvation, lost trophic signals, or persistent injury.
Hypertrophy
Increased cell mass/functional capacity from a net increase in cellular protein content, without cell division.
Hyperplasia
Increased cell number via mitotic division, in cells capable of dividing.
Metaplasia
Replacement of one differentiated cell type with another, better able to tolerate ongoing injury; fully reversible.
Dysplasia
Disorganized cellular appearance (abnormal size/shape/arrangement) — an adaptive effort gone wrong; considered preneoplastic.
Carcinoma in situ
Severe dysplasia involving the entire thickness of the epithelium.
Necrosis
Pathologic, messy cell death from external injury — cell rupture, content spillage, and inflammation.
Apoptosis
Programmed, "tidy" cell death from intracellular signaling — no rupture, no inflammation; can be normal or pathologic.
Coagulative necrosis
The most common necrosis type; denatured proteins, tissue architecture preserved for weeks; typically from ischemia.
Liquefactive necrosis
Rapid dissolution of dead tissue into liquid debris; classic in the brain or with bacterial abscess formation.
Fat necrosis
Death of adipose tissue, classically from pancreatitis/trauma; forms calcium soaps (saponification), appears chalky white.
Caseous necrosis
Cheese-like, walled-off necrosis characteristic of tuberculosis-damaged lung tissue.
Gangrene
Necrosis of a large tissue area, usually from major blood supply interruption; dry, wet, or gas subtypes.
Ischemia
Interrupted blood flow to a tissue — injures cells faster than hypoxia alone because it also blocks nutrient delivery and waste removal.
Reperfusion injury
Cellular damage occurring after blood flow is restored to previously ischemic tissue — often causes more damage than the ischemia itself.
Free radicals (reactive oxygen species)
Molecules with an unpaired electron (superoxide, peroxide, hydroxyl radicals) that damage membranes, proteins, and DNA.
Telomere
Protective end-cap of a chromosome that shortens with each cell division, eventually triggering dormancy or death.
Telomerase
The enzyme that rebuilds telomeres — active in germ cells (near-immortality) and, notably, many cancer cells.
Hayflick limit
The finite number of divisions a cell line can undergo before becoming dormant or dying, tied to telomere shortening.
Sirtuins
A protein family linking caloric restriction to longevity — help regulate DNA repair and prevent apoptosis.
Dysbiosis
Derangement of the normal microbiome, implicated as a contributor to aging and disease.
Somatic death
Death of the entire organism — absence of respiration and heartbeat, with no inflammatory/immune response (unlike localized cell death).
Rigor mortis
Post-death muscle stiffening from perpetual actin-myosin cross-bridging as ATP depletes and calcium accumulates; appears within ~6 hours.
Lipofuscin
An age-related "wear and tear" pigment that accumulates in atrophied cells' residual lysosomal vesicles, giving a yellow-brown appearance.
Three Cellular Responses to Injury

Disease and injury are ultimately cellular and genomic phenomena — even complex multisystem disorders like cancer trace back to alterations in cell function.

Mind Map — How a Cell Responds to a Challenge

Necrosis vs. apoptosis — the key distinction to hold onto throughout this whole chapter: necrosis is caused by external injury and is a pathologic process with significant tissue damage. Apoptosis is triggered by intracellular signaling and may be a completely normal physiologic process (or pathologic, depending on context).
Hydropic Swelling

The mechanism

Na+-K+ pump failure (which requires ATP) → sodium accumulates intracellularly → creates an osmotic gradient → water rushes in. Any injury causing insufficient ATP production leads to this.

What it looks like

Large, pale cytoplasm; dilated endoplasmic reticulum; swollen mitochondria. Severe cases: ER ruptures, forming large water-filled vacuoles.

Generalized cellular swelling across an organ shows up clinically as organ enlargement — the "-megaly" suffix (hepatomegaly, splenomegaly) is literally describing this process at the organ level.
Intracellular Accumulations

Three categories: (1) excess normal substances (fat), (2) abnormal substances from faulty metabolism, (3) pigments/particles the cell can't degrade.

Lipids — fatty liver

Common site: liver. Alcohol causes fatty liver via direct toxicity plus preferential metabolism of alcohol over lipid. Fat-filled cells compress organelles to one side, tissue looks yellowish/greasy. Genetic lipid-metabolism disorders (Tay-Sachs, Gaucher) cause neurologic lipid accumulation.

Carbohydrates — glycogen & mucopolysaccharidoses

Diabetes mellitus is the common cause of excess glycogen storage — impaired glucose uptake → high serum/urine glucose → renal tubule cells reabsorb and store it as glycogen. Mucopolysaccharidoses: genetic enzyme defects prevent glycosaminoglycan degradation, causing intellectual disability and connective tissue disorders.

Proteins — two mechanisms

(1) Renal tubule cells recapture leaked plasma proteins via endocytosis when glomeruli become abnormally permeable. (2) Cellular stress denatures/aggregates proteins — chaperone (heat shock) proteins try to refold them; failures get tagged by ubiquitin and digested in the proteasome.

Pigments & particles

Endogenous: melanin (normal in tanned skin), hemosiderin and bilirubin (from hemoglobin breakdown — excess suggests RBC breakdown, iron overload, or hepatobiliary disease). Exogenous: mineral dusts (coal, silica, iron, lead, silver) inhaled into lung tissue → chronic inflammation → alveolar/capillary destruction → scarring/stiffening over years.

Calcium deposits

From altered calcium intake/excretion/metabolism — e.g., impaired renal phosphate excretion → calcium phosphate deposits in eye/heart/vessels. Heart valve calcification can obstruct flow or impair closure; vessel calcification narrows lumens.

Clinical marker: TB tubercles

Dead/dying tissue often calcifies and shows as dense areas on x-ray — calcified TB lung damage is specifically called a tubercle.

Reversibility rule for this whole section: with the exception of inorganic particles (mineral dusts, etc.), intracellular accumulations are generally reversible if the causative factor is removed.
Atrophy

Six general causes

Disuse, denervation, ischemia, nutrient starvation, interruption of endocrine/trophic signals, and persistent cell injury.

The logic behind it

Atrophy is the cell's effort to minimize energy/nutrient consumption by reducing the number of intracellular organelles and structures.

Worked examples by cause

Disuse: bed rest/casting → skeletal muscle shrinkage (reverses with activity). Denervation: loss of nerve stimulation → similar muscle shrinkage. Ischemia: chronic sublethal ischemia (common in heart, brain, kidney, lower leg) → atrophy (vs. complete interruption → cell death). Lost trophic signals: adrenal cortex/thyroid/gonads atrophy without their pituitary trophic hormones.

Two protein-degradation pathways

Ubiquitin-proteasome system: tags and digests targeted proteins. Autophagy: lysosomes fuse with cellular structures for hydrolytic degradation — this also happens normally, clearing damaged/senescent organelles. Lipofuscin (age-related pigment) accumulates in atrophied cells' residual vesicles as material resistant to full degradation.

Hypertrophy

Mechanism

Net increase in cellular protein content → increased cell mass AND functional capacity — no cell division involved.

Why it matters most for non-dividing cells

Especially important for cells unable to undergo mitosis (like differentiated cardiac/skeletal muscle cells) — hypertrophy is their only option to meet increased demand, since they can't achieve hyperplasia.

Physiologic examples

Exercise-induced skeletal muscle growth (primarily hypertrophy, though muscle satellite/stem cells can also divide). Trophic-hormone-driven hypertrophy in breast/uterine tissue.

Pathophysiologic examples

Liver enlargement from toxin exposure; cardiac muscle hypertrophy from chronic hypertension (increased workload).

Hypertrophy subsides when the demand is removed, but the cell may not fully return to baseline — persistent connective tissue changes can remain.
Hyperplasia

Mechanism & triggers

Increased cell number via mitosis — only possible in cells capable of dividing. Triggered by increased physiologic demand, hormonal stimulation, or persistent injury.

Demand-induced examples

↑ RBC production at high altitude; liver enlargement for drug detoxification.

Hormone-induced examples

Estrogen → increased endometrial/uterine stromal cell number. Dysregulated hormones/growth factors → pathologic hyperplasia (thyroid or prostate enlargement).

Chronic irritation examples

Calluses/corns (chronic skin friction); bladder epithelial hyperplasia from chronic cystitis inflammation.

Organ enlargement is often a combination of BOTH hypertrophy and hyperplasia together, not purely one or the other.
Metaplasia & Dysplasia

Metaplasia

Replacement of one differentiated cell type with another better suited to tolerate ongoing injury. Classic example: cigarette smoke irritation converts bronchial ciliated columnar epithelium → stratified squamous epithelium. Fully reversible; cells remain well-differentiated, but cancerous transformation CAN arise from metaplastic areas (lung, cervix, stomach, bladder).

Dysplasia

Disorganized cell appearance (size/shape/arrangement abnormalities) — an adaptive effort "gone astray." Most common in hyperplastic squamous epithelium, but also seen in intestinal mucosa. Significant potential to become cancerous — regarded as preneoplastic.

Carcinoma in situ = severe dysplasia involving the FULL thickness of the epithelium. Mild dysplasia may still be reversible if the cause is removed — but this reversibility window narrows as severity increases.
Quick memory anchor from the chapter's key points: hypertrophy/hyperplasia ← increased demand. Atrophy ← decreased demand or chronic ischemia. Metaplasia/dysplasia ← persistent injury.
Necrosis — Overview

Morphologic changes

Shrunken (pyknotic) nucleus → degrades (karyolysis); cell volume swells; ribosomes disperse; plasma and organelle membranes disrupt. Loss of plasma membrane permeability barrier is the critical event in cell death.

Systemic signs of tissue death

General inflammatory response: malaise, fever, ↑ heart rate, ↑ WBC count, ↓ appetite.

Using released proteins to localize damage — classic exam-relevant fact: elevated serum amylase → pancreatic damage. Elevated creatine kinase-MB or cardiac troponin → myocardial damage. Pain location can also help localize tissue destruction.

Apoptosis

Tidy vs. messy — the core contrast

Necrosis: messy, inflammatory, collateral damage. Apoptosis: tidy, no inflammation — apoptotic cells are quickly ingested by neighbors with minimal tissue disruption.

Apoptosis is common, not rare

More than half of fetal nerve cells undergo apoptosis during development. >95% of thymus-generated T lymphocytes are induced to undergo apoptosis. These are entirely normal, necessary physiologic processes.

Apoptosis in disease — a genuinely surprising fact: tissue death after a myocardial infarction is estimated to be only about 20% necrotic and 80% apoptotic. Cancer cell death from radiation/chemotherapy is believed to be primarily apoptotic as well.
Apoptotic cell counts are hard to measure directly in tissue because neighboring cells rapidly ingest apoptotic cells — so even though the process is common, you rarely "see" much evidence of it on a snapshot.
Ischemia & Hypoxic Injury

Why ischemia is worse than hypoxia alone

Ischemia doesn't just cut oxygen — it also lets metabolic wastes accumulate and cuts off nutrients needed for glycolysis. This is why ischemia is the most common cause of cell injury in clinical medicine and injures cells faster than hypoxia by itself.

The cascade

↓ O2 to mitochondria → ATP production stalls → Na+-K+ and Ca2+ pumps fail → Na+ accumulates → hydropic swelling; excess Ca2+ collects in mitochondria, worsening dysfunction. Anaerobic glycolysis provides a little ATP but produces pyruvate → lactate → cellular acidification (detectable as lactic acidosis on labs).

Ischemic injury is reversible up to a point — cell death occurs once plasma, mitochondrial, and lysosomal membranes are critically damaged. Death from ischemia typically takes minutes to hours to fully develop.
Reperfusion Injury
The counterintuitive core fact: most cellular damage from an ischemic event actually occurs AFTER blood flow is restored — not during the ischemia itself.

Three components of ischemia-reperfusion injury

(1) Calcium overload, (2) reactive oxygen species (free radical) formation, (3) subsequent inflammation.

Why calcium floods in on reperfusion

Restored blood is high in calcium; cells have depleted ATP and can't control ion flux — calcium accumulates in cytoplasm, triggering apoptosis or activating lipid-degrading enzymes (lipid peroxidation).

Why reperfusion generates free radicals

Ischemia lets ATP precursors (ADP, pyruvate) build up. When oxygen returns, a disorganized burst of high-energy electrons partially reduces oxygen, forming reactive oxygen species (superoxide, peroxide, hydroxyl radicals) — these damage membranes, denature proteins, and disrupt chromosomes.

The inflammatory tail

Ischemia-reperfusion is often followed by a generalized inflammatory state that can cause ongoing cellular/organ damage for days to weeks — recruited WBCs release enzymes that further damage the area.

Nutritional Injury

Common causes of malnutrition

Poverty, chronic alcoholism, acute/chronic illness, self-imposed dietary restriction, malabsorption syndromes.

Cell-type-specific vulnerability

Iron deficiency → primarily affects RBCs. Vitamin D deficiency → primarily affects bone. All cells need glucose + amino/fatty acid building blocks universally.

Aging vs. Disease

They're theoretically distinct

Maximum human lifespan (oldest documented: 122 years, 164 days) appears limited by the aging process itself, not by disease. The elderly are more vulnerable to disease, but aging and disease are generally viewed as separate phenomena — "pathologic aging" distinguishes abnormal from normal aging.

...but the line is genuinely blurry

Normal aging includes some bone loss — but too much becomes osteoporosis (a disease). Some arterial stiffening is normal aging — but at what point does it become pathologic? This ambiguity persists because we can't yet cleanly separate universal cellular aging processes from reversible disease effects.

Hallmarks of Aging

Cellular aging = cumulative decline in proliferative/reparative capacity + accumulated cellular/molecular damage from environmental exposure. Twelve current hallmarks provide the organizing framework:

The full list of 12

  • Genomic instability
  • Telomere attrition
  • Epigenetic alterations
  • Loss of proteostasis
  • Disabled autophagy
  • Deregulated nutrient sensing
  • Mitochondrial dysfunction
  • Cellular senescence
  • Stem cell exhaustion
  • Altered intercellular communication
  • Chronic inflammation
  • Dysbiosis

DNA damage & repair

DNA damage accumulates from UV radiation, normal metabolic oxidative stress, and replication errors. Aging repair systems fall behind → inhibited cell replication or triggered apoptosis. Support: premature aging syndromes are linked to defective DNA repair mechanisms.

Telomeres & the Hayflick experiments

Hayflick showed fibroblasts in culture divide only a finite number of times — cells from older donors divided fewer times than cells from younger donors. Telomeres (chromosome end-caps) shorten with each division until a critical point triggers dormancy/death. Telomerase rebuilds telomeres — active in germ cells (near-immortal) and stem cells (lower level); notably, many cancer cell types also produce telomerase, while most differentiated somatic cells don't.

Free radical theory & sirtuins

Larger animals (slower metabolic rate) tend to live longer — supporting the idea that metabolic rate drives oxygen radical production, and cumulative radical damage (especially to membranes) drives aging. Antioxidant defenses weaken over time, letting damage accumulate. Sirtuins link caloric restriction to extended lifespan — they help regulate DNA repair and prevent apoptosis in nutrient-deprived states.

Dysbiosis and aging — a genuinely striking finding: fecal microbiota transplant (FMT) from OLD mice to YOUNG mice up-regulated inflammatory cytokines in the young mice. FMT from YOUNG mice to OLD mice improved cognition and hippocampal structure in the old mice. The microbiome itself appears to be a modifiable target for anti-aging strategies.

Somatic Death

Definition

Death of the entire organism — no inflammatory/immune response occurs (unlike localized cell death). General features: absence of respiration and heartbeat — though this alone is insufficient, since resuscitation can sometimes restore both.

The postmortem timeline

Minutes after cardiopulmonary arrest: body temperature falls, skin pales, fluids pool in dependent areas. Within ~6 hours: calcium accumulation + ATP depletion → perpetual actin-myosin cross-bridging → rigor mortis (widespread muscle stiffening). Rigor mortis then gives way to flaccidity as tissue deteriorates. 24–48 hours: putrefaction becomes apparent (postmortem autolysis — widespread lytic enzyme release).

Brain death criteria (needed because mechanical ventilation can maintain heartbeat/breathing despite a nonfunctional brain): unresponsiveness, flaccidity, absent brainstem reflexes (swallowing, gagging, pupil/eye movements), absent respiratory effort off the ventilator, absent electrical brain activity, and absent cerebral blood flow. Criteria can vary somewhat by region, but this core set is consistent.
