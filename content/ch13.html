Red Blood Cell Disorders

Blood composition, how red cells are made and carry gases, and what happens when there are too few (anemia) or too many (polycythemia). Built from your Chapter 13 material.

Key Terms & Definitions

Start here
Erythrocyte (RBC)
The most numerous blood cell; biconcave, non-nucleated disk that transports O2 via hemoglobin; lifespan 80–120 days.
Hemoglobin
The oxygen-carrying protein comprising ~90% of RBC dry weight — 2 α-chains + 2 β-chains, each with an iron-containing heme group.
Reversible deformability
The RBC's ability to change shape (torpedo-like) to squeeze through capillaries narrower than its own resting diameter.
Hematopoiesis
The developmental process from pluripotential stem cells to mature blood cells; occurs in bone marrow in adults.
Reticulocyte
An immature RBC that has lost its nucleus but still contains organelle remnants; matures into an erythrocyte in 24–48 hours; normal count ~1% of total RBCs.
Erythropoietin
The kidney hormone that stimulates RBC production in response to tissue hypoxia.
Transferrin
The plasma protein that transports dietary iron to transferrin receptors on the RBC membrane.
Ferritin
The intracellular iron storage protein.
Hemoglobin F (fetal)
Two α + two γ chains; more efficient O2 carrier at low O2 tension; replaced by hemoglobin A within ~120 days after birth.
Hemoglobin A (adult)
Two α + two β chains; ~97% of adult hemoglobin.
Oxyhemoglobin
Hemoglobin reversibly bound to oxygen.
P50
The PO2 at which hemoglobin is 50% saturated — the standard reference point for assessing hemoglobin's oxygen-releasing ability.
Bohr effect
The shift in the oxyhemoglobin dissociation curve caused by changes in PCO2/H+ that enhances O2 uptake in the lungs and release in the tissues.
Haldane effect
Unloading of O2 in tissues facilitates the loading of CO2 onto hemoglobin.
Chloride shift
Chloride diffuses into the RBC to balance the charge as bicarbonate diffuses out, maintaining electrical neutrality during CO2 transport.
Carbaminohemoglobin
Hemoglobin directly bound to CO2 — an important acid-base buffer.
Anemia
A deficit of red cells causing reduced oxygen-carrying capacity and tissue hypoxia.
Polycythemia
An excess of red cells causing increased blood viscosity and volume.
Pancytopenia
Simultaneous deficiency of red cells, white cells, and platelets — hallmark of aplastic anemia.
Aplastic anemia
A stem cell disorder with reduced hematopoietic marrow tissue and fatty marrow replacement, causing pancytopenia.
Polycythemia vera
A myeloproliferative neoplasm from stem cell transformation, causing overproduction of RBCs, WBCs, and platelets together.
Composition of Blood

Mind Map — Blood Composition Breakdown

Scale

Total blood volume: ~75.5 mL/kg (males), ~66.5 mL/kg (females) — roughly 5–6 L, 7–8% of body weight. Normal arterial pH: 7.35–7.45.

The three plasma protein types

Albumin: maintains blood volume/pressure (colloid osmotic pressure). Globulin: α fraction (bilirubin/lipid/steroid transport), β fraction (iron/copper transport), γ fraction (antibodies). Fibrinogen: inactive precursor of fibrin (clot framework).

Plasma proteins are made mainly in the liver and normally can't leave the vascular space — this containment is exactly what allows them to exert colloid osmotic pressure and regulate fluid balance.

Why RBCs can't do certain things

Mature RBCs have NO nucleus, mitochondria, ribosomes, or other organelles — so they cannot synthesize protein or carry out oxidative reactions. Everything they need is already packed in before maturation.

Platelets aren't cells

They're cytoplasmic fragments of megakaryocytes — incapable of mitotic division. ~1/3 of the body's platelets are held in a splenic reserve pool, not actively circulating.

RBC Structure & Hematopoiesis

Where hematopoiesis happens across life

Yolk sac mesenchyme (early fetal) → liver/spleen (fetal, "extramedullary") → bone marrow only (adult, "medullary"). Bone marrow provides a reticular meshwork holding developing cells until mature enough to cross the endothelial barrier.

A stress signal to recognize

Early release of immature cells (reticulocytes, nucleated RBCs) into circulation in increased numbers signals the hematopoietic system is stressed or diseased — a useful clinical clue on a peripheral smear.

Two-stage process

Hematopoiesis = mitotic division (proliferation) + maturation (differentiation). Marrow output can be accelerated by: (1) increased daughter cell differentiation, (2) increased stem cell numbers, (3) conversion of fatty yellow marrow → productive red marrow (erythropoietin-stimulated).

Extramedullary hematopoiesis in adults = abnormal

Unlike the fetus, blood cell production outside bone marrow in an ADULT is usually a sign of disease — not a normal backup mechanism at that life stage.

The reticulocyte count is a useful clinical test of effective erythropoietic activity — because erythropoietin's job is specifically to stimulate stem cells to differentiate into proerythroblasts, a low reticulocyte count in the setting of anemia points toward a production problem rather than a destruction/loss problem.
Hemoglobin Synthesis

Structure

~300 hemoglobin molecules per mature RBC, comprising ~90% of dry weight. Each molecule: 2 pairs of polypeptide "globin" chains, each with an attached heme group (iron + protoporphyrin).

Iron trafficking

Dietary iron absorbed in duodenum/proximal jejunum → carried by transferrin → binds RBC membrane transferrin receptors → endocytosed → iron released, either stored as ferritin or used for heme synthesis.

Body iron distribution

~67% bound to heme (RBCs + muscle). ~30% stored (ferritin/hemosiderin in macrophages, liver). ~3% lost daily (urine, sweat, bile, shed intestinal cells).

Protoporphyrin synthesis

Made by mitochondria; the final heme molecule = 4 porphyrin units in a ring around a central iron atom.

Hemoglobin A22α + 2δ~2–3% of adult hemoglobin
Pediatric physiology — why infant hemoglobin drops after birth: at birth, erythropoietin disappears from plasma, arterial O2 saturation rises, and marrow is still immature — all slowing RBC production. Fetal hemoglobin (≈70% of infant total) also has a shorter RBC lifespan (70–90 days vs. 120 adult days), so turnover/destruction outpaces production. Rapid growth further dilutes hemoglobin via blood volume expansion. Levels bottom out around 2–3 months, then rise again from ~6 months as tissue O2 need finally triggers erythropoietin release and adult hemoglobin production resumes.
Several hundred hemoglobinopathies exist, mostly single amino acid substitutions in the α or β chains, classified by which chain is affected.
Nutritional Requirements for Erythropoiesis

Beyond iron

Requires adequate protein, vitamins (B12, folate, B6, riboflavin, pantothenic acid, niacin, ascorbic acid, vitamin E), and minerals. Folate/B12 deficiency → impaired DNA synthesis in erythroid cells (they're coenzymes in key metabolic reactions).

B12 absorption pathway

Requires intrinsic factor from gastric parietal cells → binds B12 → complex travels to the ileum → binds ileal mucosal receptors → absorbed → transported to tissues/liver. (Loss of intrinsic factor = pernicious anemia, covered under Anemia below.)

Oxygen Transport

The numbers to know

~97% of blood O2 travels bound to hemoglobin (oxyhemoglobin); ~3% dissolved in plasma. Each hemoglobin binds 4 O2 atoms. ~15 g hemoglobin/100 mL blood → ~20 mL O2/100 mL if fully saturated (20 vol%).

Normal gas values

PaO2: 80–100 mmHg. PvO2: 35–40 mmHg. SaO2: 95–100%. SvO2: 60–80%. Oxygen delivery (V̇O2): ~1000 mL O2/min. Oxygen consumption: ~200–250 mL O2/min.

O2 binds hemoglobin efficiently where PO2 is HIGH (pulmonary capillaries) and releases where PO2 is LOW (tissue capillaries) — the partial pressure gradient itself drives both loading and unloading.
The Oxygen-Hemoglobin Dissociation Curve

Why the curve shape matters

Upper (lung) portion: big PO2 changes → only small SO2 changes — protects O2 loading even with some variability. Lower (tissue/venous) portion: small PO2 drops → LARGE SO2 drops — this releases a big O2 reserve to tissues without needing much of a pressure change.

P50 — the reference point

The PO2 at which hemoglobin is 50% saturated. Used to assess hemoglobin's oxygen affinity: higher affinity → SO2 higher at any given PO2 (curve shifts left); lower affinity → SO2 lower at a given PO2 (curve shifts right).

The Bohr effect: the specific name for the PCO2/H+-driven shift that enhances O2 uptake in the lungs (where CO2/H+ are low) and O2 release in the tissues (where CO2/H+ are high) — a self-reinforcing system tuned to exactly where oxygen is needed most.
A right shift is exactly what the body needs during exercise or chronic disease states — more O2 delivery precisely when metabolic demand is elevated.
Carbon Dioxide Transport

Three transport forms

  • Dissolved gas
  • Bicarbonate ion (~90% of arterial CO2, ~60% of venous CO2)
  • Bound to hemoglobin as carbaminohemoglobin

The bicarbonate pathway mechanism

CO2 + H2O → carbonic acid (catalyzed by carbonic anhydrase, MUCH faster in RBCs than free plasma) → dissociates into H+ and HCO3−. HCO3− diffuses OUT of the RBC into plasma; H+ stays inside.

Chloride shift

As HCO3− leaves the RBC, chloride (Cl−) diffuses IN to maintain electrical neutrality — a direct consequence of the bicarbonate exodus.

The Haldane effect

Unloading O2 in the tissues actually FACILITATES CO2 loading onto hemoglobin — the two gas exchanges are mechanistically linked, not independent processes.

Carbamino compounds (hemoglobin + CO2) provide up to 50% of whole-blood buffering power — a bigger contribution to acid-base balance than people often expect from what's "just" a gas transport mechanism.
Classifying Anemia

Mind Map — Relative vs. Absolute Anemia

Relative anemia — a dilution problem

Normal total RBC mass, but expanded plasma volume "dilutes" the count. Example: pregnancy — plasma volume runs ~43% higher than nonpregnant, producing a dilutional anemia even with normal red cell mass.

Absolute anemia — two mechanisms

An actual decrease in red cell number, from either decreased production (marrow failure, nutritional deficiency, chronic disease) or increased destruction/loss (hemolysis, hemorrhage).

Classic lab pattern reminders: Iron deficiency → microcytic, hypochromic, ↓ MCV, ↑ TIBC. Pernicious anemia / folate deficiency → macrocytic (↑ MCV), oval macrocytes with hypersegmented neutrophils — pernicious anemia additionally has neurologic symptoms and a positive Schilling test (folate deficiency does not). Aplastic anemia → pancytopenia with normal MCV.
General Effects of Anemia

Compensatory mechanisms — increasing O2 supply

↑ Heart rate, ↑ cardiac output, ↑ circulatory rate, preferential blood flow to vital organs (selective shunting for short-term compensation).

Compensatory mechanisms — increasing O2 use

↑ 2,3-DPG in erythrocytes and ↓ hemoglobin O2 affinity in tissue (a right-shift of the dissociation curve) — releases more O2 per hemoglobin molecule. ↑ Erythropoietic activity is the long-term compensation.

What determines symptom severity

Severity of anemia, cardiopulmonary competency, individual O2 requirements (activity level), duration, underlying disease, and coexisting disease. Healthy young patients may be nearly asymptomatic even at Hgb 7–8 g/dL, while elderly/cardiopulmonary-disease patients can be symptomatic at Hgb 9–11 g/dL.

Symptom progression

Mild: often asymptomatic. Mild-moderate: fatigue, weakness, loss of stamina → tachycardia, exertional dyspnea. Moderate-severe: orthostatic hypotension, pallor, tachypnea, angina, heart failure, claudication, night cramps, headache, tinnitus, faintness.

Aplastic Anemia

Definition

Stem cell disorder: reduced hematopoietic marrow tissue, fatty marrow replacement, and pancytopenia — from toxic, radiation, or immunologic injury to marrow stem cells.

Acquired causes

Drugs (alkylating agents, antimetabolites, chloramphenicol — dose-dependent; phenylbutazone, hydantoin, gold salts — idiosyncratic), viral infections (hepatitis, EBV, HIV, dengue), pregnancy, and others. Most common form is actually iatrogenic — transient marrow failure after cytotoxic chemo/radiation.

Bimodal age pattern

Mostly presents ages 15–25, with a second peak at 60+. Insidious onset — often presents late, after pancytopenia manifestations are already evident.

Symptom clusters by cell line lost

RBC loss: weakness, fatigue, pallor, dyspnea, tachycardia. WBC/neutropenia: fever, chills, infections (mouth, perirectal common sites). Platelet loss: petechiae, bruising, nosebleeds, retinal hemorrhage, heavy menses.

Critical lab threshold: absolute granulocyte count <200/mm³ → immediate high susceptibility to infectious complications — this number specifically drives urgency of care. Diagnosis ultimately requires bone marrow biopsy.

Treatment

Identify/avoid toxin exposure; HLA/ABO typing of family for potential transplant donors; maintain minimal Hgb/platelet levels; infection prevention/management; bone marrow transplant evaluation; immunosuppressive therapy if not a transplant candidate.

Transplant outcomes

Only ~1/3 of patients have compatible donors. HLA-matched sibling transplant: 90% disease-free survival at 2 years. Overall cure rates: 80–85% (untransfused patients) vs. 55–60% (patients with multiple prior transfusions) — prior transfusion meaningfully worsens transplant outcomes. GVHD occurs in 20–30% of previously-transfused survivors.

Polycythemia Vera

Mechanism

A myeloproliferative neoplasm — single stem cell gains a selective growth advantage and gradually dominates marrow precursor production, causing overproduction of RBCs, WBCs, AND platelets together. Cause unknown; theorized mechanisms: unregulated neoplastic proliferation, an abnormal myeloproliferative factor, or increased stem cell sensitivity to erythropoietin/hematopoietins.

Key differentiating lab finding

Normal arterial O2 saturation — this is what separates PV from the far more common secondary (hypoxemic) polycythemia. Also: ↑ uric acid (from cell turnover), ↓ erythropoietin, ↑ vitamin B12, ↑ leukocyte alkaline phosphatase.

Mind Map — Clinical Evolution of Polycythemia Vera

Erythrocytic phase manifestations

Occlusive vascular events (TIA, stroke, MI, portal/superficial venous thrombosis) can be the FIRST sign of disease. Hyperviscosity symptoms: headache, dizziness, visual disturbance, intermittent claudication. Ruddy/florid face, telangiectasias, purplish cyanosis of lips/ears, distended retinal veins, HTN in ~half of patients.

Spent phase

Progressive hepatosplenomegaly, severe anemia, hemorrhage, weight loss/wasting — culminates in AML transformation, which is the final evolutionary phase of the disease.

Treatment targets specific numeric thresholds: phlebotomy 450–500 mL every 2–4 days until normal hematocrit (only 200–300 mL for elderly/cardiovascular disease patients). Maintain Hct at 42% (females) / 45% (males) — vascular complications and cognitive effects increase above 45%. Myelosuppressive therapy (hydroxyurea first-line, or radioactive phosphorus) is added once platelets exceed 800,000–1,000,000/μL. Hyperuricemia: allopurinol. Refractory pruritus/painful splenomegaly: splenectomy.
Unmanaged PV: survival <2 years. Treated: median survival 10–15 years — most deaths from thrombosis, hemorrhage, leukemia, or other myeloproliferative disease. The erythrocytotic phase specifically requires treatment, or thrombosis risk is extremely high.
Secondary & Relative Polycythemia

Secondary polycythemia

Absolute erythrocytosis from increased RBC production, driven by tissue hypoxia (high altitude, lung disease) → ↑ erythropoietin. Less commonly from renal/other tumors directly overproducing erythropoietin. Only the RBC line is increased — WBCs/platelets stay normal (unlike PV). Treatment: address the underlying cause; O2 for lung disease; phlebotomy can help reduce cardiovascular workload.

Relative (spurious) polycythemia

↑ Hematocrit with NORMAL or decreased total RBC mass — a concentration artifact, not a true cell excess. Two patient patterns: (1) obvious fluid balance disturbance (volume deficit, endocrine disorder), or (2) "stress polycythemia" — hypertension + ↑ Hct with NO fluid loss or true RBC mass increase, often in White middle-aged males or smokers.

Relative polycythemia prognosis is excellent IF the underlying cause (dehydration, smoking, chronic stress) is identified and resolved — but unresolved chronic anxiety or continued smoking can still produce the same hyperviscosity complications seen in true polycythemia vera, despite the fundamentally different mechanism.
