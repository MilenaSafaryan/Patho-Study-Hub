Key Terms & Definitions

GLUT-1 to GLUT-4
Tissue-specific glucose transporters. GLUT-1/2/3 are insulin-independent; GLUT-4 (muscle/adipose) is insulin-dependent.
Islets of Langerhans
Clusters of hormone-producing pancreatic cells: β cells (insulin), α cells (glucagon), δ cells (somatostatin), F cells (pancreatic polypeptide).
Proinsulin
The precursor molecule cleaved into insulin and C-peptide within secretory vesicles.
Amylin
A peptide hormone cosecreted with insulin by β cells; inhibits gastric emptying, induces satiety, and prevents postprandial glucose spikes.
Incretin effect
The enhanced insulin response to oral (vs. IV) glucose, mediated by gut hormones GIP and GLP-1.
GIP / GLP-1
Incretin hormones from intestinal cells; stimulate insulin production, promote β-cell proliferation, inhibit apoptosis. GLP-1 also delays gastric emptying and inhibits glucagon.
Glycogenesis
Production of glycogen from glucose, mainly in liver and muscle, in the fed state.
Glycogenolysis
Breakdown of stored glycogen into glucose, in the fasting state.
Gluconeogenesis
Production of new glucose from amino acids and other non-carbohydrate substrates, mainly in the liver.
Lipolysis
Breakdown of fat from adipose tissue into free fatty acids, stimulated by declining insulin levels.
Counterregulatory hormones
Corticosteroids, growth hormone, and catecholamines — all raise blood glucose and oppose insulin's effects.
Impaired fasting glucose (IFG)
Fasting plasma glucose 100–125 mg/dL — a prediabetes category.
Impaired glucose tolerance (IGT)
2-hour OGTT plasma glucose 140–199 mg/dL — a prediabetes category.
HbA1C
Glycosylated hemoglobin; reflects average blood glucose over ~2-3 months. Diabetes: ≥6.5%. Prediabetes: 5.7-6.4%.
Type 1A diabetes
Immune-mediated β-cell destruction; the more common form of type 1 diabetes.
Type 1B diabetes
Idiopathic β-cell destruction without autoimmune markers or HLA association; rare.
LADA
Latent autoimmune diabetes in adults — an autoimmune type 1 subtype typically diagnosed after age 35, often initially misdiagnosed as type 2.
GADA65
Antibody to glutamic acid decarboxylase 65 — an autoantibody marker of autoimmune (type 1A/LADA) diabetes.
Insulinopenia
Insufficient insulin production.
Polydipsia / Polyuria / Polyphagia
The classic triad of diabetes symptoms: excessive thirst, urination, and hunger.
Diabetic ketoacidosis (DKA)
Severe insulin deficiency causing lipolysis, ketoacid production, and metabolic acidosis; primarily a type 1 complication.
Kussmaul respirations
Deep, labored, "fruity"-smelling breathing — the respiratory compensation for the metabolic acidosis of DKA.
Nonketotic hyperglycemic hyperosmolar syndrome (NHHS)
Severe hyperglycemia with little/no ketosis and striking dehydration; primarily a type 2 complication, especially in older adults.
Dawn phenomenon
Early-morning blood glucose rise from increased growth hormone, cortisol, glucagon, and epinephrine release.
Macrovascular complications
Diabetes-accelerated damage to large vessels — cardiovascular disease, stroke, peripheral arterial disease.
Microvascular complications
Capillary basement membrane thickening leading to retinopathy and nephropathy.
Advanced glycation end products
Proteins altered by chronic high glucose exposure, implicated in microvascular complication pathogenesis.
Diabetic retinopathy
Progressive retinal capillary damage — background, preproliferative, and proliferative stages; the leading cause of new adult blindness in the US.
Diabetic nephropathy
Glomerulosclerosis from capillary basement membrane thickening; the leading cause of end-stage renal disease in the US.
Microalbuminuria
Small amounts of protein in the urine — the earliest detectable sign of diabetic nephropathy.
Diabetic neuropathy
Nerve damage from hyperglycemia, divided into autonomic and sensory dysfunction; linked to reduced myoinositol and the polyol pathway.
Polyol pathway
Degradation of glucose to sorbitol and fructose in nerves under hyperglycemia/insulinopenia, linked to reduced myoinositol and neuropathy.
Metformin
A biguanide; first-line oral agent for type 2 diabetes — suppresses hepatic gluconeogenesis and enhances peripheral glucose uptake without causing hypoglycemia.
Sulfonylureas
Insulin secretagogues used in type 2 diabetes for over 40 years (e.g., glyburide, glipizide, glimepiride).
SGLT2 inhibitors (gliflozins)
A newer diabetes drug class (canagliflozin, dapagliflozin, empagliflozin) that blocks renal glucose reabsorption.
Honeymoon period
A temporary return to normoglycemia without exogenous insulin after type 1 diagnosis, when insulin needs drop below residual production; rarely lasts more than a year.

Regulation of Glucose Metabolism

Foundations

Understanding diabetes starts with understanding how the body moves glucose around normally — via specific transporters, insulin/glucagon balance, and the fed/fasting cycle.

Glucose Transporters (GLUT-1 to GLUT-4)

Mind Map — Glucose Transporters by Tissue

GLUT-1, 2, 3 — insulin-independent

Remain in the plasma membrane whether or not insulin is present. GLUT-1: dominant at the blood-brain barrier. GLUT-2: primary liver transporter (also small amounts in β cells). GLUT-3: dominant transporter for neurons (also in β cells, alongside GLUT-1).

GLUT-4 — insulin-dependent

Found in muscle and adipose cells. Without insulin, sequestered in intracellular vesicles. Insulin binding triggers an intracellular signaling cascade that translocates GLUT-4 vesicles to the plasma membrane, allowing glucose entry. Without insulin binding, GLUT-4 is removed from the membrane again.

Hormonal Regulation: Insulin Synthesis & Action

Islet cell types

β cells → insulin (as proinsulin); α cells → glucagon; δ cells → somatostatin; F cells → pancreatic polypeptide.

Insulin release trigger

Glucose is the primary stimulus. It enters β cells via GLUT-1/GLUT-3, triggers ATP production → closes ATP-sensitive K+ channels → depolarization → opens voltage-sensitive Ca2+ channels → calcium influx triggers exocytosis of stored insulin and amylin.

Proinsulin processing

Proinsulin is packaged into vesicles with cleaving enzymes that split it into insulin and C-peptide.

Insulin signaling

Insulin binds its receptor (a protein kinase receptor), triggering an intracellular enzyme cascade that translocates GLUT-4 to the cell surface for glucose uptake.

Insulin's non-glucose effects

↑ amino acid uptake and ↓ release by skeletal muscle (protein synthesis, prevents muscle breakdown). Promotes triglyceride storage by preventing fat breakdown and inducing lipid formation. Stimulates IGF-1 (somatomedin) secretion, contributing to growth.

Amylin

Cosecreted with insulin by β cells. Acts on the area postrema in the brain to inhibit gastric emptying, induce satiety, and prevent postprandial glucose spikes. Also suppresses glucagon release via a local (paracrine) pancreatic effect — no brain involvement needed for that part.

Two phases of insulin secretion

First phase: brief spike from initial glucose/GI hormone stimulation. Second phase: sustained insulin synthesis with continued glucose elevation.

Incretin effect

GIP and GLP-1 (from intestinal cells) stimulate insulin production, promote β-cell proliferation, and inhibit apoptosis — explaining why oral glucose provokes more insulin than IV glucose (parenteral nutrition shows no incretin effect). GLP-1 also delays gastric emptying, inhibits glucagon, and increases satiety.

Fasting-state glucose production

Glucagon-stimulated glycogenolysis + gluconeogenesis account for up to 75% of glucose production during fasting.

Counterregulatory hormones (fasting state)

Corticosteroids stimulate gluconeogenesis, opposing insulin. Growth hormone increases peripheral insulin resistance and blocks insulin's suppression of hepatic glucose output. Catecholamines drive hepatic glycogenolysis and gluconeogenesis.

Neural Regulation & Exercise

First-phase insulin release (neural)

Food in the mouth stimulates the parasympathetic nervous system (vagal pathway), triggering β-cell insulin release before glucose is even absorbed.

Glucose-sensitive brain cells

Glucose-sensitive receptors exist in the brain, mouth, pancreatic β cells, and hepatic portal vein. Some neurons activate with falling glucose (glucose-inhibited), others with rising glucose (glucose-excited).

Glucagon regulation

Predominantly sympathetic-driven, in response to hypoglycemia. Hepatoportal vein glucose-sensitive nerve fibers release norepinephrine; combined with adrenal epinephrine, this activates α cells to release glucagon.

Exercise physiology

At exercise onset: insulin drops, glucagon/catecholamines rise → increased free fatty acid production (resting muscle's primary fuel) and stimulated glycogenolysis. Under catecholamine influence, muscle shifts fuel sources as exercise continues.

Classification of Glucose Intolerance Disorders

The big picture

"Diabetes mellitus" isn't one disease — as many as 30 different disorders fall under this umbrella. The 2011 Expert Committee criteria sort them into prediabetes categories and four clinical classes.

Prediabetes categories

Impaired glucose tolerance (IGT) and impaired fasting glucose (IFG) — intermediate stages between normal metabolism and diabetes onset.

Four clinical classes

Type 1 diabetes mellitus, type 2 diabetes mellitus, other specific types (genetic defects, pancreatic disease, endocrinopathies, drug-induced, infections, other genetic syndromes), and gestational diabetes mellitus.

"Other specific types" is a big bucket: genetic β-cell defects (e.g., MODY subtypes), genetic defects in insulin action, exocrine pancreas disease (cystic fibrosis, pancreatitis, hemochromatosis), endocrinopathies (acromegaly, Cushing syndrome, pheochromocytoma), drug/chemical-induced (glucocorticoids, thiazides, pentamidine), infections (congenital rubella, CMV), and genetic syndromes (Down, Klinefelter, Turner, Prader-Willi, and others).

Pathophysiology

Complex relationship between elevated glucose and developing insulin resistance. Glucose release is stimulated by metabolically active tissue mass (fat-free + fat mass); gluconeogenesis-stimulating signals outweigh counterregulatory effects, causing hepatic insulin resistance.

Downward spiral

Persistently high glucose supersaturates fat-free tissue (like muscle), which then downregulates GLUT transporters — accelerating systemic insulin resistance.

Type 1 vs. Type 2 Diabetes

Core comparison

Mind Map — Type 1 vs. Type 2 at a Glance

Type 1 Diabetes Mellitus

Epidemiology

~10% of all diabetes; 1.3 million in the US, ~10-20 million globally. Incidence 1 in 300-600 children/adolescents. Peaks at ages 2, 4-6, and 10-14. More common in White populations. Little sex difference under 15, but more males affected overall.

Type 1A vs. Type 1B

Type 1A (more common): immune-mediated. Type 1B (rare): idiopathic — β-cell destruction with no autoimmune markers or HLA association; etiology unknown.

Type 1A subtypeDetails
PolygenicTwo or more genetic loci; 80-90% of type 1 cases
MonogenicRare; associated with IPEX syndrome
LADA (latent autoimmune diabetes in adults)T-cell reactivity to islet antigens + GADA65 autoantibodies; 2-12% of all diabetes; diagnosed after age 35; often initially mistaken for type 2

Genetic link

Strong MHC association on chromosome 6 (HLA genes). DR and DQ loci confer predisposition; other loci may be protective.

Trigger

Viral infection or toxic exposure may trigger the autoimmune process in genetically susceptible individuals.

Pathogenesis

Macrophages and T lymphocytes (CD4 via cytokines, CD8 via direct cytotoxicity) infiltrate and destroy islet β cells. Autoantibodies (GADA65, insulinoma-antigen 2, insulin) often appear early — sometimes up to 13 years before diagnosis. Multiple antibodies present = highly predictive of type 1A.

When symptoms appear

Hyperglycemia signals that autoimmune β-cell destruction has progressed far enough that insulin secretion is now inadequate.

Core mechanism: Absolute insulin deficiency → glucose can't enter muscle/adipose tissue → unopposed hepatic glucose production → glucagon overproduction drives further glycogenolysis/gluconeogenesis → blood glucose rises → exceeds renal tubular reabsorption capacity → glycosuria → osmotic diuresis → hypovolemia. Meanwhile, insulin-dependent tissues are starved of glucose, and the brain triggers eating behavior. Result: the classic triad — polydipsia, polyuria, polyphagia.
Type 2 Diabetes Mellitus

Core defect

Resistance to insulin action on peripheral tissues, affecting 90-95% of US diabetics.

Prevalence by ethnicity

7.4% non-Hispanic White; 12.1% non-Hispanic Black; 11.8% Hispanic/Latino. Native American: 5.5% (Alaska Native) up to 33.5% (American Indians in southern AZ).

Age patterns

~29.2% of US adults over 65 have diabetes. Under age 20: affects ~283,000 (0.35%) — adolescent Native Americans are the highest-risk group in this age bracket.

Risk factors

Aging and sedentary lifestyle contribute, but obesity is the single most powerful predictor — especially excessive abdominal (visceral) fat, which poses a greater diabetes/cardiovascular risk than lower-body fat.

Subtle onset: Classic symptoms (polydipsia, polyuria, polyphagia, weight loss) may be mild or entirely absent in type 2 diabetes — a key reason it often goes undiagnosed for years.

Acute Hyperglycemia

Common causes

Alterations in nutrition, inactivity, inadequate antidiabetic medication use, or combinations of these.

Dawn phenomenon

Persistent fasting hyperglycemia from early-morning increases in growth hormone, cortisol, glucagon, and epinephrine.

Infection risk

Skin, urinary tract, and vaginal infections are most common. Elderly patients are particularly susceptible to malignant otitis externa, necrotizing fasciitis, and persistent candidal infections. TB reactivation is a concern in extended care facility residents.

Other symptoms

Nausea, fatigue, decreased sense of well-being, blurred vision — these recede as euglycemia is restored.

Diabetic Ketoacidosis (DKA)

Mind Map — DKA Pathophysiologic Cascade

Trigger

Continued insulin deficiency plus elevated catecholamines, cortisol, glucagon, and growth hormone (from hypovolemia, physical stress, or the insulin deficiency itself) drives lipolysis.

Ketoacid production

Fatty acids from lipolysis are converted to ketoacids in the liver; hepatic gluconeogenesis (responding to tissue glucose deprivation) also boosts ketoacid production.

Potassium dynamics

Metabolic acidosis pushes extracellular H+ into cells. Since the Na+/K+ pump (which normally moves potassium intracellularly) requires insulin, absent insulin causes transient hyperkalemia — but excess K+ then spills into urine, eventually causing net potassium LOSS.

Other electrolyte/lab changes

Sodium, magnesium, phosphorus losses occur as total body water falls (though serum levels may look normal/high due to hemoconcentration). Hypovolemia/dehydration raise Hct, Hb, protein, WBC count, creatinine, serum osmolality. Lactic acidosis can also occur from hypovolemia and reduced hepatic lactate uptake.

Kussmaul respirations: Deep, labored, fruity-smelling breathing — the respiratory compensation for metabolic acidosis, lowering PCO2 (compensatory respiratory alkalosis). DKA may be the very first sign of new-onset type 1 diabetes. Untreated hypovolemic shock can be fatal. Hospitalization need is greatest in patients under 45 (39.7 per 1000 diabetics).
Nonketotic Hyperglycemic Hyperosmolar Syndrome (NHHS)

Why no ketoacidosis in type 2

Endogenous insulin (still present, just relatively insufficient, in type 2) suppresses the lipolysis that leads to ketone body/ketoacidosis formation.

Clinical picture

Severe hyperglycemia with no or slight ketosis, plus striking dehydration. Symptoms of polyuria/polydipsia/polyphagia may be present but subtle.

Who's at risk

More common in type 2 diabetes, especially older individuals; more likely in institutionalized patients, particularly those unable to recognize or respond to thirst.

Mortality

Can be life-threatening, especially in the elderly. 70% of all NHHS coma cases occur in people over 64. Mortality rate ~11%.

Complications of Chronic Hyperglycemia

Vascular & neuropathic

Chronic complications split into two big categories: vascular (further divided into macro- and microvascular) and neuropathic.

Mind Map — Chronic Complications Overview

Macrovascular Complications

Definition

Damage to large vessels supplying the brain, heart, and extremities. Diabetes accelerates the otherwise age-dependent process of atherosclerosis.

Burden

Cardiovascular disease and stroke affect 38.1% of diabetics over 35. Heart/blood vessel disease risk is 2-4x higher with diabetes, responsible for more than half of all diabetes deaths. Stroke is more common and has worse outcomes in diabetics — accounting for ~1% of diabetes hospitalizations.

Intensive glycemic control research

ACCORD study: only study to suggest increased overall mortality, but significant reduction in nonfatal coronary events. Other studies: improved mortality with HbA1C ~6.5%, and a 14% cardiovascular event reduction per 1% HbA1C drop — but severe hypoglycemia risk rose significantly.

Contributing risk factors

Dyslipidemia, hypertension, and impaired fibrinolysis are present in uncontrolled diabetes and improve with better glucose control — the fibrinolysis link may relate to compensatory hyperinsulinemia in type 2 diabetes.

Key nuance: Improved glycemic control is thought to help microvascular complications more than macrovascular ones — reducing insulin resistance (caloric restriction, exercise, possibly drugs) plus standard risk-factor control (lipids, BP) matters most for macrovascular risk.
Microvascular Complications: Retinopathy & Nephropathy

Shared mechanism

Abnormal thickening of the capillary basement membrane, worsening with longer diabetes duration and persistent hyperglycemia. Hyperglycemia disrupts platelet function and basement membrane growth; advanced glycation end products (AGEs) also contribute. Thickening improves with better glycemic control.

Other risk factors

Hypertension and smoking.

Retinopathy burden

<10% affected after <5 years with diabetes; >50% after >20 years. Up to 21% of newly diagnosed type 2 patients already have it (reflecting long undiagnosed disease). The leading cause of new adult blindness in the US.

Retinopathy stages

Background: microaneurysms, small hemorrhages — usually doesn't affect visual acuity. Preproliferative: further capillary damage. Proliferative: capillary neovascularization — new capillaries are prone to hemorrhage; treated with laser photocoagulation.

Nephropathy burden

Affects 20-40% of type 1 patients (fewer type 2 patients progress to ESRD). Accounts for 40% of all US end-stage renal disease cases. African American, Hispanic, and Native American diabetics have higher ESRD rates than White patients.

Nephropathy mechanism

Characteristic lesion: glomerulosclerosis (basement membrane thickening/hardening). Progression: ↑ glomerular flow rate → hyperfiltration → glomerular damage → glomerulosclerosis → capillary blockage/leakage → protein in urine (starting as microalbuminuria, progressing to gross proteinuria) → falling GFR → chronic kidney disease.

Hypertension is an important contributing factor to diabetic nephropathy — ACE inhibitor therapy reduces the rate of chronic kidney disease, ESRD, and mortality.
Neuropathic Complications

Burden

Produces symptoms in 60-70% of diabetics; responsible for 6.8 hospitalizations per 1000 diabetics.

Two categories

Autonomic: GI disturbances, bladder dysfunction, tachycardia, postural hypotension, sexual dysfunction (35-50% of diabetic males have erectile dysfunction). Sensory: carpal tunnel syndrome, paresthesias/sensory loss in feet and lower legs.

Foot complications: Neuropathy drives a major increase in serious foot problems. Lower extremity amputation rate is 15-40x higher in diabetics; over 60% of all nontraumatic US amputations occur in people with diabetes.

Myoinositol & the polyol pathway

Diabetic neuropathy is linked to decreased myoinositol (a nerve membrane component) in peripheral nerves. Glucose competes with myoinositol for cellular transport; hyperglycemia/insulinopenia also activate the polyol pathway (glucose → sorbitol/fructose) in nerves, further reducing myoinositol. Focal ischemic nerve lesions may also contribute.

Pathologic findings

Degeneration or loss of nerve fibers, causing decreased nerve function.

Modifiable risk factors

Hyperglycemia, hypertriglyceridemia, obesity, smoking, hypertension.

Non-modifiable/demographic risk factors

Male sex, White race, older age, height >175.5 cm; elderly, non-Hispanic Black, and Mexican American individuals are disproportionately affected.

Landmark trial: DCCT

9-year prospective study of intensive insulin therapy (3+ injections/day or pump) in type 1 patients. Results: 76% reduced incidence of initial retinopathy, 54% reduced progression of existing retinopathy, 39%/54% reduction in microalbuminuria/albuminuria, 60% reduction in symptomatic neuropathy. Trade-off: significantly more severe hypoglycemia in the intensive group. The UK Prospective Diabetes Study extended similar findings (decreased morbidity/mortality) to type 2 diabetes.

Complications in Pregnancy

Risks with type 1 diabetes

Increased risk of perinatal infant mortality and congenital anomalies. Good metabolic control during pregnancy brings perinatal mortality close to the general population rate, but congenital malformation risk remains elevated.

Why preconception control matters

Affected organs develop early in the first trimester, so excellent glycemic control BEFORE conception is recommended.

Consequences of untreated maternal hyperglycemia

Macrosomia, shoulder dystocia, preeclampsia, fetal death, fractures, and nerve palsies.

Treatment & Education

Management

ADA goals: preprandial glucose 70-130 mg/dL, postprandial <180 mg/dL. Achieved through diet, exercise, medication, and hygiene practices — always individualized.

Nutrition, Obesity & Exercise

ADA nutrition therapy goals

Promote nutrient-dense eating patterns to hit individualized glycemic/BP/lipid goals and weight goals, delay complications; address individual cultural/personal needs; keep eating enjoyable; give practical day-to-day meal planning tools rather than obsessing over single nutrients.

Obesity definitions

Normal BMI: 18.5-24.9 kg/m². Increased health risk begins at BMI ≥25.2. Obesity = BMI >30. Obesity is the strongest risk factor for type 2 diabetes and a female cardiovascular risk factor.

Weight management

No single strategy works for everyone; recommendations include a nutritionally complete diet, maintenance routine, and exercise program. A modest 2-8 kg weight loss can meaningfully benefit type 2 diabetics.

Eating disorders

Bulimia and anorexia may be more common in females with type 1 diabetes; some patients induce weight loss by reducing insulin doses — clinicians must assess carefully.

Exercise benefits

Lowers BP and dyslipidemia, increases work capacity, reduces stress, prevents bone loss, improves reaction time, aids weight loss. Reduces tissue insulin resistance → net blood glucose decline. Can decrease insulin needs (type 1), reduce/eliminate drug needs (type 2), lower heart disease risk, and even prevent type 2 onset in genetically at-risk people.

Exercise risks

Type 1: hypoglycemia and ketoacidosis risk. Type 2: hypoglycemia, cardiac dysfunction, orthopedic injury, complication worsening. Post-exercise hypoglycemia can occur up to 24 hours later (glycogen replacement + continued insulin sensitivity).

Exercise recommendations

CDC: moderate intensity ≥30 min, 5+ days/week, OR vigorous intensity 20 min, 3+ days/week (no more than 2 consecutive rest days). Reduce sedentary stretches >90 minutes.

When to avoid exercise

Avoid if ketosis is present (signals acute insulin shortage — exercise can worsen hyperglycemia). Exercise is safe with glucose >250 mg/dL only if ketosis is absent.

Metformin — the first-line drug

Suppresses hepatic gluconeogenesis and enhances peripheral glucose uptake, WITHOUT causing hypoglycemia. First-line for type 2 (if tolerated/not contraindicated). Improves dyslipidemia and promotes weight loss.

Metformin risks

Common: nausea, diarrhea. Serious: acute renal injury and metabolic acidosis (lactic acidosis is rare, mostly linked to acute kidney injury). Use caution with liver disease. Hold 24 hrs before / 48 hrs after IV iodine contrast (depending on creatinine clearance) due to acute renal injury risk.

Treatment choice should be patient-centered: consider efficacy, cost, side effects, weight effects, comorbidities, hypoglycemia risk, and patient preference. Current guidelines favor timely titration of oral agents and insulin to reach euglycemia.
Pediatric Considerations

Epidemiology

~176,500 diagnosed under age 20. 1 in 400-600 children/adolescents has type 1; 18 new cases per 100,000 yearly. Vast majority have type 1 (only ~5% have genetic β-cell defects instead).

Presentation

Often presents acutely as DKA when insulin secretion drops below needs.

Honeymoon period

If diagnosed during a period of high insulin demand (e.g., viral illness), insulin needs may later drop below residual production once the illness resolves, allowing temporary normoglycemia without exogenous insulin. Rarely lasts more than 1 year, usually shorter.

Goals of therapy

Normal growth/development, avoiding acute/chronic complications, addressing psychosocial issues, and self-care education.

Acute complications

Hyperglycemia → glycosuria → dehydration risk (especially severe with DKA). Test for ketonuria when glucose >240 mg/dL or during concurrent illness, to catch DKA early.

Hypoglycemia in young children

Can be hard to detect — watch for behavioral changes: lethargy, pallor, sleep disturbances.

Chronic complications

Rare during adolescence, but regular screening for neuropathy/nephropathy and lipid checks should still occur. Pregnant adolescents need counseling on maintaining excellent metabolic control.

Insulin treatment

~1.0 U/kg/day; intensive regimen of 3+ injections/day recommended to prevent chronic complications. Infants/young children may need diluted insulin for accurate small dosing. Needs rise during illness or puberty. Abdomen is the LEAST preferred injection site in children (insufficient subcutaneous fat).

Nutrition

Base: 1000 calories/day + 100 calories per year of age until age 11. Ages 11-18: +100 more calories for females, +200 more for males. Growth should be plotted at every visit. After age 2, macronutrient ratios match adult guidelines.

Self-management & education

Kids typically start injections/monitoring (with supervision) at school age, varying by child. All caregivers (parents, babysitters, teachers) need age-appropriate education on hypoglycemia prevention/recognition/treatment.

Psychosocial factors matter clinically: Peer pressure during adolescence can worsen adherence; weight concerns can lead to insulin omission or eating disorders. Disturbed family functioning is linked to increased DKA hospitalization frequency.
Genetic β-cell defects are usually diagnosed under age 25, rarely cause ketosis, and are managed like young-adult type 2 diabetes.
Geriatric Considerations

Epidemiology

Adults over 60 make up nearly 50% of the US diabetic population. Prevalence ~21% at age 60+, more than double the 40-59 age group.

Why risk increases with age

↑ adiposity, ↓ lean body mass, ↓ activity (all → insulin resistance), plus diminished insulin secretion with age. Also increased by surgery, illness, and drugs like steroids/diuretics.

Diagnostic challenges

Fluctuating glucose values and inconsistent/absent hyperglycemia symptoms make diagnosis harder. Chronic complications (neuropathy, retinopathy) are often already present at diagnosis, signaling long-standing undiagnosed disease.

Goals of therapy

Prevent acute complications, manage chronic complications, address psychosocial issues, provide self-care education.

Atypical hyperglycemia presentation

May be asymptomatic or show polyuria/fatigue; polydipsia is LESS common due to decreased thirst perception. Can cause increased pain perception and slowed cognition. Infection risk rises above glucose 200 mg/dL — elderly diabetics are 2x more likely to be hospitalized for kidney infections.

Dehydration & falls

Chronic hyperglycemia causes mild-moderate dehydration, worsened by age-related kidney/water-conservation changes — leading to postural hypotension, electrolyte imbalance, and increased fall risk.

NHHS in the elderly: Not prone to ketosis, but at high risk for nonketotic hyperglycemic hyperosmolar coma. Risk factors: impaired thirst recognition, polypharmacy, dementia, concurrent illness. Mortality: 10-50%.

Atypical hypoglycemia

Can present as lethargy or focal neurologic dysfunction rather than classic symptoms — age-related blunting of counterregulatory response, or inability to report symptoms, may be responsible. Glycemic targets may need to be set higher for safety.

Foot care challenges

Orthopedic deformity, loss of protective subcutaneous fat, and atherosclerotic change complicate foot care; visual/joint changes can hinder self-inspection — assistance is often needed.

Drug selection

Favor shorter-acting oral agents; evaluate renal/hepatic function first. Avoid agents metabolized by impaired organ systems. Metformin is inappropriate with decreased liver/renal function (lactic acidosis risk).

Insulin in the elderly

Safe with caution — thin elderly patients can be very insulin-sensitive and need small doses. Daily/twice-daily long-acting insulin may be preferable. Visual/dexterity/cognitive changes may require magnification devices or prefilled syringes.
