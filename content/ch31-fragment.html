Key Terms & Definitions

Start here
Cryptorchidism
"Hidden testis" — a testis occupying an extrascrotal position (intraabdominal, inguinal canal, or prescrotal).
Ectopic testis
A testis misdirected outside the normal path of descent, most commonly into a superficial inguinal pouch — distinct from simple incomplete descent.
Orchiopexy
Surgical procedure to bring an undescended testis into a normal scrotal position — first-line treatment for cryptorchidism.
Hypogonadism (ADAM)
Subnormal testosterone with or without altered sperm production; age-related form is called androgen deficiency in the aging male.
Hydrocele
Fluid collection around the testicle/spermatic cord within the tunica or processus vaginalis; communicating or noncommunicating.
Spermatocele
A painless, sperm-containing cystic mass at the upper pole of the testis, seen only in sexually mature testes.
Varicocele
Abnormal dilation of scrotal venous structures (pampiniform plexus) from dysfunctional spermatic vein valves — the classic "bag of worms."
Testicular torsion
Twisting of the spermatic cord compromising testicular blood supply — a true surgical emergency.
Cremasteric reflex
Elevation of the testicle on stroking the inner thigh; its ABSENCE is highly sensitive for testicular torsion.
Epididymitis
Inflammation of the epididymis/testis, usually bacterial (STI or non-STI organisms).
Leukospermia
Elevated white blood cells in ejaculate — a sign seen in asymptomatic prostatitis.
Benign prostatic hyperplasia (BPH)
Nonmalignant nodular hyperplasia of the prostate transition zone causing bladder outlet obstruction — unrelated to prostate cancer risk.
Transurethral resection of the prostate (TURP)
The gold-standard surgical procedure for BPH — resecting prostatic tissue via a resectoscope through the urethra.
CP/CPPS
Chronic prostatitis/chronic pelvic pain syndrome — the most common prostatitis category; noninfectious, cause unclear.
Ureteral colic
Sharp, spasmodic, wave-like pain from a stone stretching and triggering spasm in the ureter.
Lower Urinary Tract Calculi

Terminology

Urolithiasis = stones anywhere in the urinary tract (kidney, ureters, bladder, urethra). Ureteral stones are usually ones that passed from the kidney through the ureteropelvic junction (UPJ), not ones that formed in the ureter itself.

Ureteral colic

Sharp, spasmodic pain in paroxysms lasting 20–60 minutes, from ureteral stretching/spasm. Upper ureter obstruction → flank pain radiating toward the umbilicus. Distal ureter → pain radiates to the ipsilateral groin, testicle, or labium. Often accompanied by gross/microscopic hematuria, dysuria, urgency, nausea, vomiting.

The size thresholds that drive management

≤5 mm: ~68% probability of spontaneous passage — managed conservatively with observation, hydration, and pain control (NSAIDs preferred — they directly reduce ureteral smooth muscle tone/spasm, not just pain perception). >5 mm and ≤10 mm: medical expulsive therapy — α-blockers, calcium channel blockers, or antispasmodics to facilitate passage.

Diagnostic workup

Urinalysis (hematuria/infection) + basic metabolic panel (kidney function). Low-dose noncontrast CT is preferred imaging for most adults. Ultrasound is preferred in pregnant patients or anyone needing to minimize radiation exposure.

Cryptorchidism

Incidence & risk factors

0.7–1.0% of male infants at 1 year; usually unilateral. Cause uncertain (possible intrinsic testicular defect or hormonal growth factor misregulation). Risk factors: breech presentation, low birth weight, prematurity, maternal factors. Bilateral cases are frequently linked to genetic syndromes/disorders of sexual development.

Why it matters — two distinct risks

(1) Fibrotic tubule changes → deficient spermatogenesis → infertility. (2) Increased testicular malignancy risk — this is the more clinically urgent concern and the main driver of early treatment timing.

Treatment timeline: spontaneous descent occurs for most congenital cases before 6 months of age. Because of the malignancy/infertility risk, treatment (orchiopexy — first-line) is recommended by 18 months of age. hCG administration to stimulate descent is no longer recommended.

Diagnosis

Morning serum testosterone on three separate occasions. May add prolactin and LH; MRI of the pituitary if severe androgen deficiency is suspected.

Treatment & fertility implications

Testosterone replacement (IM injections, patches, gels, subcutaneous pellets) — monitor for prostate cancer and polycythemia. Testosterone replacement does NOT restore spermatogenesis. Gonadotropin-releasing hormone therapy can optimize fertility in SECONDARY hypogonadism only — in primary hypogonadism, infertility is generally irreversible.

Hydrocele — secondary causes & treatment

Can follow scrotal injury, radiation, epididymal infection, or testicular neoplasm — but cause is often uncertain. Treatment only if symptomatic (discomfort, or rarely, circulation compromise) — surgical resection/plication, or aspiration + sclerotherapy (fluid may reaccumulate).

Varicocele — the fertility connection

Prevalence >15% in males (median age 19). Among infertile males, ~40% have a varicocele — a striking overlap worth remembering. Graded subclinical through grade III. Management: observation for mild cases; surgical repair or spermatic vein embolization guided by pain severity AND reproductive factors (abnormal semen quality/sperm function).

Testicular Torsion

Mechanism & population

Twisting of the spermatic cord → vascular compromise → ischemia → infarction. Most common in prepubertal males (can occur neonatally); extremely rare after age 35. Usually from a congenital malformation allowing free intrascrotal testicular movement.

Presentation

Acute severe unilateral testicular pain + scrotal swelling ± lower abdominal pain, nausea, vomiting. In infants/neonates, restlessness may be the ONLY sign. Absent ipsilateral cremasteric reflex: ~100% sensitive, 66% specific.

Mind Map — Torsion: Time Is Testicle

Distinguishing torsion from epididymitis

Both cause scrotal swelling — the key differentiator is Doppler ultrasound: torsion shows NO blood flow (ischemic testis); epididymitis shows INCREASED vascular flow (hypervascularity from inflammation). Testicular nuclear scanning is another diagnostic option.

Treatment

Urgent surgical detorsion is the goal — restore blood supply. Manual detorsion if surgery is delayed. Surgical detorsion includes "pexing" (securing) the affected testis to the scrotal wall — AND pexing the contralateral testis too, since the risk of contralateral torsion is also increased.

Male Infertility

Scale of the problem

~15% of couples can't conceive after 1 year of unprotected intercourse. A male factor is solely responsible in ~20% of cases and contributory in another 30–40%. An abnormal semen analysis is present in almost all male infertility cases.

Correctable vs. not

Often correctable: ductal obstruction, varicocele, hypogonadotropic hypogonadism (a hormone problem). Not correctable: bilateral testicular atrophy and similar structural/irreversible causes. Categories overall: acquired, idiopathic, congenital.

Workup

Complete history (infertility, sexual, surgical, medical) + gonadotoxic exposure history. Labs: two semen analyses + endocrine evaluation (testosterone, FSH). Further options: postejaculatory urinalysis, transrectal/scrotal ultrasound, specialized semen tests, karyotyping.

Treatment ladder

Varicocele → surgical repair/embolization. Ductal obstruction → sometimes surgically correctable. Advanced options: microsurgical epididymal sperm aspiration or percutaneous testicular sperm extraction (for IVF/ICSI). Endocrine therapy can improve semen parameters in some. Intrauterine insemination for some couples; donor sperm or adoption for others.

Epididymitis

Causes

Can follow trauma or reflux of sterile urine up the vas deferens, but most cases are bacterial. Sexually transmitted organisms: Neisseria gonorrhoeae, Chlamydia trachomatis. Non-STI organisms: Pseudomonas, E. coli.

Presentation

Scrotal enlargement and erythema. Remember: distinguishing this from testicular torsion is a genuine clinical emergency skill — Doppler ultrasound showing increased (not absent) vascular flow points to epididymitis, not torsion.

Benign Prostatic Hyperplasia (BPH)

Scale & a key clarification

~80% of men over 60 experience some degree of BPH. BPH and prostate cancer are NOT related — evidence shows BPH does not predispose to prostate cancer development. This distinction is worth memorizing explicitly since the two are so often confused as related conditions.

Pathogenesis

Cause is multifactorial; aging-related testosterone/dihydrotestosterone activity plays a role. Hyperplasia occurs in the transition zone (stromal + adenomatous nodules) → displaces/compresses the central zone toward the prostate base → compresses the prostatic urethra → bladder outlet obstruction.

Clinical manifestations

Progressive symptoms: ↓ force of urinary stream, hesitancy, interruption of stream, up to complete obstruction/retention. Incomplete bladder emptying → residual urine → infection risk.

Diagnosis

History/physical + digital rectal exam (enlarged prostate). Postvoid residual via catheterization/ultrasound. CT or abdominal ultrasound for hypertrophy/obstruction evidence. Urodynamics can confirm BPH as the LUTS cause. PSA is NOT a routine BPH test — it predicts prostate volume but can be elevated for many reasons and isn't diagnostic for BPH specifically.

Prostatitis

Causative organisms (bacterial forms)

Usually E. coli; less commonly Proteus, Klebsiella, Enterobacter, Pseudomonas, Serratia, Staphylococcus, Enterococcus. Infection routes: ascending urethral, reflux of infected urine into prostatic ducts, hematogenous spread, direct/lymphogenous extension from the rectum. Indwelling urethral catheters are a common periurethral-infection trigger.

The four NIH categories

Acute bacterial, chronic bacterial, CP/CPPS (chronic prostatitis/chronic pelvic pain syndrome — further split into inflammatory/noninflammatory), and asymptomatic inflammatory prostatitis.

Prostate Cancer

Epidemiology

Second most prevalent cancer in males worldwide. ~288,300 new US cases in 2022; ~34,700 deaths — the second-leading cause of cancer death among US males. Rare before age 40; incidence rises with age, peaking between 65–75. 95% are adenocarcinomas arising in the peripheral zone (note: this is a different zone than where BPH nodules form — the transition zone).

Etiology — what's actually implicated (so far)

Precise cause unknown; significant genetic influence suspected. Sex hormones were long suspected but recent research does NOT support an association — and testosterone supplementation for hypogonadism does NOT appear to raise prostate cancer risk. Modifiable risk factors: diets high in animal fat, smoking, sedentary lifestyle, obesity, and environmental carcinogen exposure.

Prostate cancers vary widely in aggressiveness — classified by structure, internal tumor architecture, and proliferation pattern, with correspondingly varied prognoses.
