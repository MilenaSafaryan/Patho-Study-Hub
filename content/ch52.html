How skin changes across the lifespan, how to systematically evaluate a lesion, the major infectious skin disorders, and how systemic disease shows up on the skin. Built from your Chapter 52 material.

Key Terms & Definitions

Start here
Elastosis (solar elastosis)
Cross-linked, thickened collagen bundles from chronic sun exposure, producing a weather-beaten/tanned appearance.
Senile purpura
Age-related hemorrhages from increased dermal vascular fragility.
Primary lesion
A skin lesion in its original, unmodified appearance.
Secondary lesion
A skin lesion whose appearance has been modified over time or by external factors (e.g., scratching).
Verrucae (warts)
Benign papillomas caused by human papillomavirus, invading only human skin/mucous membranes.
Herpes simplex virus (HSV)
HSV-1 (usually above the waist) and HSV-2 (usually genital); persists latently in the trigeminal or other ganglia after initial infection.
Herpes zoster (shingles)
Reactivation of latent varicella zoster virus in a dorsal sensory ganglion, causing a dermatomal vesicular eruption.
Postherpetic neuralgia
Persistent pain (months to years) after a shingles rash resolves — the most important complication in patients over 50.
Tinea
Dermatophyte fungal infection, named by body location (capitis, corporis, pedis, cruris, etc.).
Onychomycosis
Dermatophyte infection of the nails, causing white/yellow discoloration and thickening.
Hirsutism (hypertrichosis)
Intensified, male-pattern hair growth in females — upper lip, chin, chest, linea alba.
Mongolian spot
A benign area of selective pigmentation, usually buttocks/sacral area, common in Asian American and African American infants.
Strawberry hemangioma
A bright-red, raised vascular birthmark that typically resolves by age 5–7 without scarring.
Port-wine stain hemangioma
A flat, reddish-purple vascular birthmark, usually face/neck, that does NOT resolve with age.
Milia
Small white bumps in newborns from plugged sebaceous/eccrine glands.
Cradle cap
A harmless, self-limited scaly scalp condition in infants.
Rubella (German measles)
A mild childhood viral illness with major risk only if transmitted transplacentally to a fetus early in gestation (teratogenic).
Roseola infantum
A viral illness in children under ~4 (usually ~1 year) with high fever followed by a rash — NO lymphadenopathy, unlike rubella.
Rubeola (measles)
A more severe communicable viral illness with Koplik spots and a facial-onset rash; risk of otitis media, pneumonia, encephalitis.
Koplik spots
Small red spots with a bluish-white center on the buccal mucosa — pathognomonic for measles.
Age-Related Changes — Epidermis & Dermis

Mind Map — The Aging-Skin Cascade

Epidermis

Generalized thinning (though average cell-layer count is unchanged); mitosis decreases, cellular variation increases (irregular size/shape). Melanocyte number decreases in White individuals, with declining/less uniform pigment production on sun exposure.

Dermis

↓ Elastin and ↑ collagen-to-elastin ratio → loss of skin strength/elasticity. Collagen becomes cross-linked into thicker bundles (elastosis, especially solar elastosis from sun exposure) → weather-beaten appearance. ↓ Vascularity → senile purpura, cherry angiomas, venous lakes on ears/face/lips/neck.

Clinical consequence: drug absorption

Decreased dermal/subcutaneous vascularity slows absorption of subcutaneously administered drugs, prolonging their half-life — a practical prescribing consideration in older adults.

Sun exposure — the dominant modifiable factor

Skin habitually covered shows little age-related change; blue-eyed, fair-skinned individuals are more susceptible to solar damage than more heavily pigmented individuals.

The protective water/chemical barrier function of the stratum corneum weakens with age even though its thickness stays the same — a good example of function declining before structure visibly changes.
Age-Related Changes — Hair, Nails & Glands

Hair color & growth

Gray hair: autosomal dominant trait, from decreased melanin production at the follicle base; begins at the temples, extends to the vertex. >50% of people over 50 have ≥50% gray body hair. Adults reach full terminal hair pattern by age 40, followed by progressive loss in reverse order of development.

Sex-specific hair patterns

Postmenopausal White females: lose trunk hair first, then pubic/axillary; unopposed adrenal androgens cause coarse facial hair (chin/lips) in 50% of women over 60. Males: general thinning + longer/coarser eyebrow/ear/nose hair; frontal hairline recession in 80% of older females, 100% of older males. Male baldness is X-linked (inherited from the mother), requires testosterone, and shows an M-shaped or vertex-thinning pattern.

Nails

Become dull, brittle, hard, thick — mainly from diminished vascular supply to the nail bed. Growth rate drops 30–50% (0.1 mm/day at 30 → 0.07 mm/day at 90). ↑ Longitudinal striations → splitting. Toenails especially prone to hyperkeratosis/thickening; onychomycosis affects ~20% of people over 60.

Glands

Sebaceous: little structural change but declining function (↓ sebum) — minimal in males, more pronounced in females after menopause, plateauing by the 7th decade; fewer glands overall (tied to hair follicle loss) → drier, coarser skin. Sweat (eccrine): ↓ size/number/function, uneven secretory cells, lipofuscin accumulation, fibrous replacement in advanced age → raised sweating threshold, impaired thermoregulation. Apocrine: function declines though number/size don't.

Clinical bottom line: young children and older adults BOTH have fewer functional sweat glands and rely more on blood flow changes for thermoregulation — putting both age extremes at similar risk for temperature-regulation problems, for different underlying reasons (immaturity vs. degeneration).
History-Taking

Key history categories

Family history (hereditary skin disease), personal history (age of onset, psychosocial impact, ethnic origin), geographic origin/residence history, seasonal pattern, occupational exposures, leisure activities, accompanying diseases, previous treatment, and detailed lesion history (onset, course, symptoms, psychological triggers).

Why occupation/leisure history matters so much

Chemical/dust/gas exposure, heat, lighting, hygiene conditions, and even hobby-related exposures (gardening, woodworking, fishing) can all be the actual cause of a presenting skin problem — sometimes a contact of only 5 minutes is enough to acquire certain conditions.

Primary vs. Secondary Lesions

Primary lesions

Retain their original appearance, unmodified by time or external processes.

Secondary lesions

Appearance modified over time or by external agents (e.g., scratching) — may look quite different from the original lesion. Correctly telling primary from secondary is central to reaching an accurate diagnosis.

Lesion Descriptors

What to describe once a lesion is classified

Size, symmetry of color/shape, and distribution pattern if multiple lesions are present: symmetric, sun-exposed, intertriginous (warm/moist apposed skin), acral (distal extremities/ears/nose), genital, or flexor/extensor predominant.

Color as a diagnostic clue

Red-salmon pink, brown-black, blue-purple, bone white-slate gray, yellow — each color pattern suggests certain diagnoses, so color description isn't just cosmetic detail.

Verrucae (Warts)

Cause

Human papillomavirus — infects only human skin/mucous membranes (not found in other animals). Histologically similar across all wart types despite varied appearance by location; the stratum corneum is irregularly thickened.

Natural course

May resolve spontaneously if immunity develops, but this can be delayed for years and isn't reliable — 95% of untreated warts resolve within 5 years, though they may multiply to hundreds of lesions in the meantime.

Treatment options

Laser removal, liquid nitrogen/acid chemicals, cryotherapy, salicylic acid paint/plasters, topical blistering agents, immunomodulators, intralesional injections.

Herpes Simplex Virus

Two types, two typical locations

HSV-1: usually above the waist (can spread via occupational exposure in dentistry/medicine/athletics). HSV-2: responsible for most genital infections.

Lesion progression

Burning/tingling → vesicles + erythema → pustules → ulcers → crusts → healing (10–14 days). Most common on lips, face, mouth.

Latency & recurrence

Persists latently in the trigeminal (or other) ganglia after initial infection. Recurrence triggers: stress, sunlight, menses, injury. Most patients have ≥1 reactivation; some have 10+ outbreaks/year. Viral shedding can occur even without visible lesions.

Treatment

No cure — mostly palliative (lidocaine/diphenhydramine, aspirin, cold compresses). Acyclovir, famciclovir, or valacyclovir shorten active outbreaks; can be used as daily prophylaxis in some cases.

Herpes Zoster (Shingles)

Mechanism

Reactivation of latent varicella zoster virus (same virus as chickenpox), which has resided in dorsal sensory ganglia since the original childhood infection. During reactivation, virus travels from ganglion to the corresponding dermatome's skin.

Presentation

Vesicles on erythematous bases, restricted to a single dermatome (unilateral) — most often thorax, trunk, face. New vesicle crops erupt for 3–5 days; lesions deeper/more confluent than chickenpox. Clears in 2–3 weeks. Severe pain/paresthesias common. In immunosuppressed patients, lesions may extend beyond the dermatome.

Postherpetic neuralgia — the most important complication in patients over 50: pain persisting long after the rash resolves, sometimes for months to years. Eye involvement (herpes zoster ophthalmicus) can cause permanent blindness. In the elderly specifically, shingles can be more severe and lengthy (episodes up to a year).

Treatment

Antivirals (acyclovir, famciclovir, valacyclovir) — start within 48–72 hours of onset for best effect. Topical Burow compresses/aqueous alcohol shake lotions, pain medication. Systemic corticosteroids can help severe pain in healthy patients over 50, though use remains controversial.

Prevention

Shingrix — a recombinant vaccine approved for adults 50+ and for adults 18+ at increased risk from immunodeficiency/immunosuppression.

Fungal & Yeast Infections

The three dermatophyte genera

Microsporum, Trichophyton, Epidermophyton — cause "tinea" infections named by location: capitis (scalp), barbae (beard), faciei (face), corporis (trunk), manus (hand), cruris (groin), pedis (foot).

Presentation varies by site

Often erythematous macules/plaques with peripheral scaling and central clearing; vesicular lesions common on the feet. Can be mistaken for eczema or impetigo when weeping/crusted. Nail infection (onychomycosis): white/yellow opaque discoloration progressing to thickened, crumbly, deformed nails.

Treatment

Localized: topical antifungals (miconazole, clotrimazole, econazole, ciclopirox, terbinafine) — 4-week twice-daily course usually clears symptoms. Extensive/hair/nail/resistant infections: systemic therapy (griseofulvin, itraconazole, terbinafine). Duration ranges from 3–4 weeks (tinea corporis) to 12 months (onychomycosis) — a striking contrast worth remembering.

Yeast infections

Candida albicans — presents as thrush (white oral lesions) in newborns, and as intertrigo in infants/bedridden patients from moisture-trapped skin folds.

Special Characteristics of Dark Skin

More common in darker skin

Postinflammatory hyperpigmentation (after inflammation/injury/infection), seborrheic dermatitis, keloids, and traumatic (traction) alopecia from tight hair plaiting or hot oil/tension on the scalp.

Rarer in darker skin

Squamous/basal cell carcinoma, senile keratoses, and psoriasis. When psoriasis DOES occur, the classic bright-red color and silvery scale are often absent — plaques instead appear blue/violet from melanocyte stimulation, making it harder to detect.

Assessment technique adjustments

Assess color in sclerae, conjunctivae, buccal mucosa, tongue, lips, nail beds, palms, soles. Pallor: yellowish-brown tinge (brown skin) or ash gray (black skin) — absence of underlying red tones. Petechiae: best seen over lighter-pigmented areas (abdomen, gluteal, volar forearm) or palpebral conjunctiva/buccal mucosa.

The blanch test

Pressure over an area blanches erythema but does NOT affect petechiae or ecchymosis — a fast way to distinguish true bleeding from simple redness regardless of skin tone.

Normal variants frequently mistaken for disorders: Mongolian spots (infants), Futcher/Voigt lines, linear nail pigmentation — know these are normal so they aren't misdiagnosed.

Sensation, Texture, Temperature & Hair — Systemic Clues

Sensation (pruritus)

Can be purely dermatologic (atopic eczema, contact dermatitis, scabies, dermatophytosis, psoriasis, varicella) OR a sign of systemic disease: carcinoma, diabetes, thyroid disease, uremia, obstructive biliary disease.

Texture

Loose/wrinkled/toneless skin may indicate dehydration — best confirmed by palpating oral mucous membranes (gum/cheek junction; if dry, finger doesn't slide easily). Pitting edema on hands/feet/ankles/sacrum indicates fluid excess. Induration/lipodystrophy at injection sites is common with repeated insulin injections.

Temperature

Warm + dry + febrile = rising core temperature (sweating not yet compensating). Warm + wet = temperature about to drop (sweating working). Hypoglycemic sweating: distinguished by accompanying weakness, tachycardia, hunger, headache, mental irritability/confusion. Generalized coolness: ↓ metabolism (e.g., post-anesthesia) or shock if severe. ↑ Temperature: hyperthyroidism or sun exposure.

Hair changes

Fast scalp hair growth makes it especially vulnerable to systemic disease, toxic drugs, radiation, stress. Hirsutism/hypertrichosis: male-pattern hair growth in females — from excess androgen OR simply ethnic background (Mediterranean ancestry). Hypertrichosis lanuginosa: congenital (autosomal dominant, linked to spina bifida) or, notably, an acquired paraneoplastic sign of certain internal carcinomas.

The core selection principle: weeping/exudative lesions need drying agents (wet dressings, gels); dry/chronic/thickened lesions need occlusive, penetrating vehicles (ointments). Delivery system choice follows the lesion's moisture state, not just the medication being delivered.
Corticosteroids

Duration classes

Short-acting: cortisone, hydrocortisone. Intermediate-acting: prednisone, prednisolone, methylprednisolone, triamcinolone (most commonly used systemically). Long-acting: dexamethasone, betamethasone.

Systemic use

Usually oral (dosage adjusts quickly if needed) or IM (e.g., triamcinolone acetonide, effective >4 weeks — useful for unreliable patients). Since dermatologic courses are typically short, the classic long-term corticosteroid complications are uncommon — EXCEPT with prolonged use in diseases like pemphigus/SLE, where cushingoid features (round puffy face, dorsal fat pad), fatigue, weakness, and acne can appear.

Topical potency: divided into 7 groups by vasoconstrictive capacity — Group 1 is most potent (augmented betamethasone dipropionate, clobetasol dipropionate), Group 7 is least potent (1% hydrocortisone). Topical steroids reduce erythema/edema/pruritus and promote healing, but don't cure the underlying disease.
Infancy

Why newborn skin is more vulnerable

Dermis/epidermis are thinner and loosely connected (rete pegs, which anchor the layers together, aren't yet developed) → susceptible to damage from minimal friction. Less melanin at birth → lighter skin → more UV vulnerability. Small, nonfunctional apocrine glands (don't develop until puberty) → drier skin.

Milia mechanism

Maternal androgens stimulate sebaceous glands prenatally (producing vernix), which then become plugged after birth. Eccrine glands (most active on newborn palms, activated at a HIGHER temperature threshold than in adults) can also obstruct sebaceous glands — both pathways converge on producing milia.

Common congenital/early lesions

Mongolian spots: buttocks/sacral pigmentation, common in Asian American/African American infants. Strawberry hemangioma: resolves by age 5–7. Port-wine stain: does NOT resolve; laser surgery or cosmetic coverage are the main options.

Common irritative conditions

Prickly heat: midepidermal sweat gland obstruction/rupture from prolonged warm/humid exposure — treat by removing excess clothing, cool baths, powders, avoiding hot/humid environments. Diaper rash: from ammonia/alkaline urine breakdown products — prevent with frequent changes, gentle detergent, air exposure; avoid plastic pants. Cradle cap: managed with mild shampoo + gentle combing.

Baby powder caution: avoid talc (serious respiratory risk if inhaled) — cornstarch-based powder is the safer alternative.

Rubella's real danger isn't the child — it's the fetus: teratogenic effects from early-gestation transmission include cataracts, microcephaly, intellectual impairment, deafness, patent ductus arteriosus, glaucoma, purpura, and bone defects. This is exactly why MMR immunization (infancy + booster at kindergarten/middle school entry) is required in most states — protecting future pregnancies, not just the vaccinated child.

Distinguishing roseola from rubella

Age (roseola is younger, ~1 year) and absence of lymphadenopathy are the fastest clinical differentiators; antibody titers confirm if needed. Rubella itself rarely occurs before age 6–9 months because of protective maternal antibodies.

Measles severity factors

Can hemorrhage into skin/other surfaces in severe cases ("hemorrhagic measles"). More severe in malnourished children specifically. Diagnosis confirmed via antibody titer, checked during illness and again 6 weeks after symptoms resolve.

Chickenpox (varicella) is caused by the same virus (varicella zoster) that later reactivates as shingles — the material transitions into chickenpox next but cuts off at the introduction. Send the rest whenever you have it.
